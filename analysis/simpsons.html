“No More Simpsons” and what came of it

There is a The Simpsons fanfic titled No More Simpsons (Não Mais Simpsons in Portuguese), which happens in a world where Homer is a lazy drunk, Marge is significantly disturbed by her husband’s lack of care for her and the rest of the family, Bart is a sex-crazed maniac, Lisa has a best friend, and Maggie (herein Meg) is a boxer. It’s probably the longest Simpsons fanfic in terms of chapters, standing at a whopping 218. Its author, a journalism student named Brendon, died in 2023, leaving the story without an ending. This led to “Just Another Simpson”, essentially my version of the story’s three remaining chapters, intended as a homage to the original author.

The initial version of this story, titled only as Lisa, Bart and Maggie (alternatively called Lisa, Bart and Meg), was created at some point during 2017 and mentioned by Brendon in one of his other stories, Gravity Falls: Different Ways, in a chapter where he also highlighted a few of his other works. The only noticeable detail about this story, aside from Meg's name change, was the fact the story was supposed to happen six years after the show's timeline — something that was kept in the final version.

Note: The content in this analysis has been toned down, albeit not censored, for better clarity and viewing. The original story includes content such as major & recurring character deaths (if you also count original characters), canon-typical violence, an unbelievable amount of sex scenes (from blowjobs to orgies), homophobia, abuse of power, nuclear disasters, (non)consensual usage of alchohol and drugs, self-harm, sexual harrassment, and many more.

Act 1

The story is divided into three sagas, or acts, with appearances from all Simpson family members, but also several other characters from the show. The first saga, or Act 1, contains several plot lines — some of which are only relevant later on. The prologue shows Lisa meeting her best friend, a black-haired girl with albinism named Cristianne, at a library, while Bart tries to join the “Indelinquents”, the name given to Springfield Elementary’s group of bullies — Dolph Starbeam, Jimbo Jones, Kearney Zzyzwicz, and Nelson Muntz, although the latter seems to have departed the group temporarily at the time. Jimbo, as leader of the group, challenges Bart to tell one nearby girl to touch his private parts; if she did, he’d be part of the group. The sheepishly honest Bart was upfront about this to the girl in question, named Claire, who told him not to do this and instead kissed him. This would start off a long but important relationship throughout Act 1.

By the start of Act 1, seven years have passed since this moment, and the Simpson family is quite different from last time. Maggie — now 7 years old and only referred to as Meg —, relates less and less to her father, seeing local bartender Moe Szyslak more and more as her own father, something she’s felt ever since the day he saved her, an event that happened two years prior to NMS’ events and five years after the prologue. Her brother, Bart, is described as a slob who’s only interested in making sweet, sweet love with his girlfriend, Claire. Homer, even more of a slob than usual, frequently enrages Marge, who believes her own stress to be caused by him. Lisa’s the only one in the family who hasn’t significantly changed, although she remains affected by her parents’ constant arguing.

Marge in particular got very enraged after Homer misses Lisa’s school recital, instead sleeping in the family’s pink sedan. She wasn’t the only one; that same day, Bart and Claire would have a quickie in another school area. When the family came back home, they found themselves being awaited by police chief Clancy Wiggum and a group of soldiers, who invited Meg to a military school in Jefferson City, the capital of the state of Missouri. Though she would later consult Moe about this, who would give his consent, she was already willing to do so; one big reason for this was her lukewarm feelings toward her family, in particular Homer and, to a lesser extent, Bart.

The next big event in the story occurs when Lisa receives a letter from billionaire and Springfield Power Plant owner Charles Montgomery Burns, a.k.a. Mr. Burns, inviting her to a hospital visit. Burns, as it turns out, is dying; connected to at least a dozen different cords, the ailing Burns is only aided by his trustworthy aide, Waylon Smithers, who sits hopelessly next to him at the hospital every day. Knowing that Lisa, who once served as an assistant to him, would be one of the few people he could trust in this day and age, he admitted to her that he was never a good person. To redeem himself, even if just for a bit, he agreed to list Lisa in his last will — as the receiver of his trustworthy teddy bear, Bobo, and also 80% of his fortune; conservative estimates valued the inheritance at least $796 million (dollars), though likely much more. The only requirement was that Lisa would need to spend it from time to time; she agrees to it.

In the meantime, Lisa would also join her friend Cristianne’s band; an alternative unnamed band, they would feature Jimbo on drums, Lisa on saxophone, Mat — a friend of Cristianne’s — on guitar and Cristianne on vocals. They had only one rehearsal throughout the story, partially due to several breaks and vacations other characters would take. Shortly after this, Meg finally departed Springfield for Jefferson City, where she would remain through the rest of Act 1. The Simpson family took this as an opportunity to take a break and go to the beach, inviting Bart’s childhood best friend Milhouse as well.

Milhouse’s background here is something to notice; he’d shown signs of depression and had been self-harming for at least a while prior to the story’s events. Part of this is hinted to have been caused by the bullying from the Indelinquents, in particular Nelson.

A common theme in this story is the occasional appearances of other minor characters in the protagonists’ point of view, even if briefly. Generally, these characters would appear later on, either in a more major role or in their usual minor roles. One of these happens to be the Simpsons’ next-door neighbor, Ned Flanders, whose lack of appearances in the story is explained by Homer in another chapter; two years prior to the story’s events, his two children went to study in another state and moved away, and so did he. Marked for sale this whole time, he ends up receiving an offscreen proposition from Collin’s father to buy the house, which he accepts, and returns to Springfield for about two months to assure the sale goes smoothly and that the house is in perfect condition. Act 2

Act 2 starts with Bart and Lisa playing chess, seven months after the events of Act 1 and the death of Cristianne — who ended up forgotten. She’s barely mentioned in the story after this, a common occurence with past and future dead characters. This is a particular characteristic of Brendon, who seems notorious for killing many characters in his stories — such as in his original story “In the assassin’s shadow” (Na sombra do assassino, 2018), where half of the story’s cast of characters ended up dead. In No More Simpsons this does not occur, but he is much more creative in this Act.

In this Act, two characters are introduced. One is Professor John Frink, albeit not the one in the show — it’s actually his son, John Frink Jr.; the original Frink reportedly was eaten by a shark while working on a blood-based sunscreen lotion. Frink Jr. has a son, John Frink Junior Junior — though he is frequently called “Grandson” —, who can be best described as a deeply curious Ritalin kid. His POVs are very interesting because he has a tendency to repeat himself very often, especially when it comes to separating himself from his father, Frink Jr. For example:

The car continued for 500 meters, until it reached the SBRC — Squabbletown Biochemical Research Center. It may be very possible that he wrote the ending to the story somewhere — this story was made in 2018, and he noted even back then that he already planned everything out in advance. After some thinking, I decided to give it a shot and write a version of how the endings of this story would have be, around late December 2023-early January 2024.

Obviously, I don’t think this is perfect. In fact, I think I was too lenient on some of the characters here; Brendon would’ve definitely killed more characters than I did here (only one, technically), though I tried my best to make sure this wasn’t a "happy ending for everyone" because, even if they were alive, they’d still be suffering, given everything that happened and how many of their friends perished. Even then, I made some mistakes, which I realized over the course of writing this analysis. There is a good reason for this: when reading this story, I wrote mini-summaries using text-to-speech (TTS) so I could read them back eventually, especially to write the last three chapters of the story. However, after reading chapter 182, I more or less skimmed through the remaining 36 chapters for the sake of reading them all, and also so I could get to writing the first chapter of the "final three". Marge had every reason to complain about Homer: he did nothing, didn’t stay home, wasn’t responsible with the salary he received, and fortunately never knew about Lisa’s wealth — otherwise, they would have fallen into poverty much earlier, and none of them in the city would be alive now. It was very difficult for her to make the decision she made. She saw her husband, whom she thought was a careless man, cheat on and beat her (she would later learn he had been drugged by Sideshow Bob), and understood there was no way forward. Asking for divorce wasn’t easy, because despite the process being lengthy, a part of her still loved him. Maybe for his playful ways, maybe for his goofy mannerisms, maybe precisely for being Homer. And Homer could say the same about Marge, because despite not agreeing with everything she did, and admitting he himself was irresponsible, he continued loving her. After so many drinks at Moe’s, so many moments in that poor apartment with his father, Abraham, so many dreams… his mistake was not realizing sooner that he was making the same mistakes his father made, and wanted his son not to make.

Each of the people crossing that tunnel had their problems. Lenna, for example, had pain and grief in her heart for letting her daughter — Cristianne — die. For not being so helpful, for having put her under a lot of pressure, for maybe not having been a good mother. Todd, Ned Flanders’ son who was under her care, had pain and grief for another person: Ralph, his friend who, despite finding a bit strange, didn’t deserve the death he got, buried by meters and meters of toxic nuclear waste, released by that disgusting plant. He loved him, even with the doubt of what the word love meant in this case. And Lenna would always continue loving her daughter. Claire and Milhouse, despite everything in their relationship, felt fear. Fear for their friends, fear that something would happen to them, fear they wouldn’t be able to move forward, but also confidence in themselves. Carl and Lenny felt the same way, along with uncertainty about whether they could finally live in peace, besides hesitating about leaving the city where they were born, lived. Springfield didn’t deserve that end.

For Laura, some people shouldn’t have had that end. Rod, for example, despite being a complete perverted alien, didn’t deserve to die there — and she hoped he was okay. Her son, little Andrew, whom she hoped was safe in his mother’s hands, didn’t deserve to die. For Lisa, Fat Tony, despite his antagonistic ways, shouldn’t have died. At least not at that complicated moment — she knew well that, if it wasn’t for him, no one would have escaped from there. Despite the idea being hers, the execution of the plan was his: providing fake passports for the citizens, who, after escaping the city, would spread throughout the countries of the world. It was an ambitious idea, maybe bold, but surprisingly effective. She just didn’t want things to have reached that point.

Jeffrey, the director of that squadron, followed behind the group of inhabitants in the tunnel, providing cover. He had seen so much suffering, so much confusion, so much madness… a few minutes ago, he had killed Hugo, his childhood friend. It wasn’t an easy choice — not that he had been making many easy choices along the way — but that was one that, despite everything, he thought was necessary. It was either that or he would be killed instead, to be fair. Springfield wasn’t perfect, but he came to nurture a special affection for the city, for its inhabitants, for those who lived there, grew up there. It wasn’t easy having to almost destroy it to move forward. He hoped far fewer people would die — but his heart prevailed. As he passed through the tunnel and left the city, breathing that fresh air, not very polluted, the group was received by several helicopters. The vast majority were from the Army, but some appeared to be from both local and international television channels. In other words, the press. It was something Jeff didn’t want, at all, to happen, but it happened. My God.The whole world would now know what they tried to cover up in there: that they tried to destroy Springfield, tore the city from the Earth, and tried to cover that hole with dirt.

"Soldiers… lower your weapons and leave them on the ground."

Upon landing, some helicopters with supplies ran to the numerous wounded, tired citizens, who were only not in worse conditions thanks to the fortune Lisa obtained in Mr. Burns’ will. From other choppers, this time Army choppers, armed guards came out, wanting Jeff to go with them. It would happen sometime, he thought. Even following orders, the noise he caused was too loud.

And not far from there, a few minutes later, the Simpson family, for the first time in a while showing a kind of smile, gave each other a hug. Marge, Lisa and Bart reunited with their youngest daughter Meg, after almost two years without seeing her.

"Meg!" Marge shouted. "I don’t know how you’re here, but… thank goodness you’re here."

"Me too, mom…"

The family quieted down, maintaining the hug for a few more minutes. Not far from there, Homer — already with both arms and hands bandaged, also ran to the family. Meg gave him another hug, this time longer, and he soon looked at Marge. She smiled.

"Homer… look, thank you for saving us that time in there, with Tony and everything."

"I did what I had to do, Marge," he said, looking at his fingers. "I wasn’t going to let anyone hurt my wife- I mean, e-ex-wife. Or my family."

Bart and Lisa, both emotional about the situation, hugged Homer.

"Oh, children… you’re not even children anymore, but you’re kinda making my arm hurt even more…"

On that day, several of Springfield’s citizens, in various physical states — some badly hurt, others in shock, had to be taken to hospitals in the state capital, Jefferson City, to be better treated. Still, Lisa’s plan continued at full steam. Several of the citizens, carrying very few things besides their own documents, passports and the skin on their bodies, went in helicopters, and some in buses and cars, toward the city’s airport, ready to go to their new places, and assume their new lives. No one stopped them; at that point, there was nothing left to stop. The entire country already knew what had happened in Springfield.

Exhausted, she disembarked from one of the planes, along with her family at the airplane window, which had started to take off. She looked at the world around her. Wow, the world was indeed huge. When the plane passed over a small city, surrounded by a wall, she tried not to look down too much. That place, which had already given her so much happiness, had already brought her so much hatred, had already given her so many moments, now only gave her sadness.

Chapter 2

The news out of Springfield shocked the world. A nuclear disaster, in the 21st century? Well, it wasn’t exactly unheard of, but in the world’s greatest nation (as they themselves would say)? It was certainly a surprise. And worst of all: it was a nuclear disaster caused by a man with no connection to the plant, who had somehow managed to get inside, sabotage its operations, and push a single button that led to the near-complete destruction of the city.

This news broke through an anonymous tip, published in one of America’s major newspapers. The leak was clear: the U.S. Army, along with the Department of Defense, had taken advantage of the incident to eradicate the city, wipe it off the map, and pretend everything was fine. The supposed “cholera epidemic,” mentioned by some sources, had never occurred; it was a complete fabrication, intended to cover up the incident. And trying to reconcile that story with the nuclear contamination narrative, which other sources had reported, was no easy task for them. Along with this leak, several photos emerged. These photos, taken with a long-distance camera attached to some kind of telescope, showed just how grave the situation in Springfield was.

In short, they depicted moments of human rights violations committed by the military during their occupation of the city. One photo in particular showed a soldier, described by the informant only as “Hugo,” brutally assaulting a teenager who walked past him smoking. Another image showed him entering a local business and leaving a short time later, followed by a photo of the local shopkeeper, covered in scrapes, leaving the establishment. The informant was adamant: the shop owner was beaten because they thought he was an illegal immigrant.

Other images in the same vein showed other soldiers abusing their power, evicting residents, and forcing them to perform degrading activities. Although the photos were low quality (they had a grainy, black-and-white look, and many other photos were taken at night), they were still sharp enough to capture the nuances of the cruelty that had taken place in Springfield.

The press immediately began to work the case. Despite differing opinions within the army, especially regarding how to handle the situation, the head of the Department of Defense had to resign. Even the governor of Missouri had to deal with the fallout — since Springfield was in his state, he had to face the consequences regardless of whether he was aware of the events. But the top of the military and governmental hierarchy wasn’t the only level to be investigated; several soldiers who participated in the Springfield operation were also investigated for possible abuses of power. Boll, who was in charge of the military group that included Meg and Lexanna, was an exception; by stating that he wished no harm on the city, as he was born there, and had worked to restrain the actions of other soldiers, he came out looking like some kind of hero. The situation was more complicated for Jeff, who was accused of abuse of power and homicide. The trial would drag on for some time, but he was beginning to feel certain he had made the right choice.

Not long after a group of citizens escaped — through a fake tunnel near the wall surrounding the city — the state government, this time trying to show that the situation was under control, formed a crisis committee. It dispatched a special team of civilians, trained in nuclear safety, to the city with the goal of answering a few questions by the end of their assessment:

1. What are the environmental conditions (such as air, soil, and especially water quality) in Springfield? Was the city’s decontamination process appropriate? Is there a need to continue, or improve, this process?

2. What is the general condition of the Springfield Nuclear Power Plant and public buildings? What are the general conditions of the neighborhoods? Are there serious infrastructure problems in the affected areas?

3. Is it possible to detail the number of deaths that occurred within the city limits? Of these, how many were from natural causes versus non-natural causes?

The process took about a month, during which the city was, ironically, also "walled off"; no one entered or left without authorization. The head of the report — a Finnish scientist with an unfamiliar name (Alberti Boon, which didn’t even seem like a Finnish name!), — jotted down important descriptions of the city in its new, post-military era.

“[…] the nuclear incident released radioactive material into the soil, material that was being stored behind a type of barrier. Part of this waste was supposed to be treated and the other part stored, but this process had not been carried out for some time, since shortly before the death of the plant’s director, the late Charles Montgomery Burns… with the treatment plant under renovation, security failures at the plant, and a long restructuring process, evidence indicates that a private citizen, name [REDACTED], obtained a piece of experimental, high-tech, long-range equipment, causing the opening of the floodgates…”

“The air quality in Springfield is within reasonable standards set by the World Health Organization, although the same cannot be said for the quality of the soil, which is extremely vulnerable and susceptible to erosion and other changes in its composition, or the water quality. On a simple scale of 0 to 100, where 0 is most unsuitable or polluted and 100 is least, the water of the river that cuts through the city would receive a 23, and the city lake a staggering 4… although measures were taken by the military to prevent a greater disaster, part of this water remains trapped in the city’s subsoil, and with military control of the local water treatment plant, there was further disorganization regarding the place’s new controllers… […] physical removal of certain contaminants or the alteration of their chemical nature is necessary…”

“[…] a war zone. There are several destroyed buildings in the city, like the town hall, or with serious structural risks, like the police station. There was a large fire that was brought under control a few days after our arrival. Searching the neighborhoods, we came across the decomposing body of a teenager. He had been shot with a rifle, likely by a soldier. Other such bodies can be found in various states on the city streets. […] we even found a group of people in an abandoned, crumbling house, begging for a scrap of food and water […] a total breakdown of the supply chain.”

Part of the document, however, was kept for confidential use. Especially the sections about the life of Sideshow Bob — the government, of course, didn’t want anyone to know that, at some point, there had been a superhuman being among them. Curiously, no one in that document could pinpoint how he had transformed like that. One witness from the day Bob was arrested at the police station, who hadn’t left the city, swore that the superhuman was indeed him, and investigations at the Springfield plant confirmed a break-in. But how did he become that person — or thing?

Again, nobody knew. Except for a handful of Springfield citizens, now countless countries away… and a certain scientist and his son. It had been about five or six months since the incident. The two had caught a plane in the early hours of the morning and arrived, still in the morning, in the city of Dover, in the southwest of the United Kingdom. Following the directions on the small scientist’s phone map, they arrived at a medium-sized, two-story house in an ordinary suburb. It looked like any other house, but that wasn’t important. He rang the bell on the black gate and waited for someone to answer. When the gate opened and they entered the garden, they stood before the house’s (also black) front door. When the door opened, a girl, perhaps 16 years old, with blonde hair and a distinctive red-orange dress, greeted them.

“F-Frink?”

“—Junior,” the little one pointed to his father. “And I’m Frink Junior Junior, not to be confused with my father Frink Junior or the original Frink, no Junior, who was also a Frink, but not a Junior—”

“I already know you, Frink JJ.”

“Call me Grandson. Everyone does.”

Upon entering the cozy house, Frink Jr. and Grandson got straight to the point. They asked to gather the whole family, as they had something to discuss with them. The family had been living in that place for about five or six months, already with false identities. Lisa was Olivia Moore, Meg was Niki Moore, Marge was Livia Moore, and Bart was Eddie Moore. The IDs produced by Fat Tony were of the highest quality!

The other four soon came to the kitchen table, Bart sleepy and Meg not much in the mood to talk. Zero and Pearl were upstairs, asleep after several minutes of playing. The surprise was when another man came down the stairs, visibly more tired than the others, and sat in one of the chairs. The man, according to his passport, was named Colin Dawson. But anyone who knew him, even at a glance, knew his real name: Homer Simpson.

“Well… I think I have a lot to tell you, and you have a lot to tell me, I believe,” Frink Jr. said, handing Lisa an envelope. As she opened it and read the documents aloud, she quickly realized what it was: the technicians’ report on the state of Springfield. Grandson, observant as always, swore he saw a tear or two escape from Lisa’s eyes. Or maybe it was just a bit of dust in his own?

“I… I can’t believe all of this happened. And it’s all my fault…”

“Why?” Marge tried to calm her daughter. “No one could have predicted what happened—”

“It’s not that, Mom. It’s just… if I hadn’t tried to interfere, m-maybe none of this would have happened and the town would have been f-fine—”

Grandson, who had a much greater familiarity (or rapport, as his mother would say) with Lisa than his father, Frink Junior (not to be confused with the original Frink), smiled.

“Lisa, we’ve been doing some digging. And we found out that this—" he pulled a piece of paper from his pocket, “—Ter… willi… ger… Terwilliger. That guy. He had already put his plan into motion long before you discovered it.”

“Yeah, I already knew that. It wasn’t the first time he’d tried something from the top.”

“Actually…” Frink Junior decided to interject, “You only knew part of what he did, in this case. I’ll try to explain as best I can. For who knows how long, the test subject—ahem, Terwilliger, after being arrested so many times and after so many plans went down the drain… an idea lodged itself in his mind that… the boy Bart here was responsible for all of it, all the chaos in his life.”

Everyone present looked at Bart, who grimaced out of the corner of his eye.

“What? I’m not that evil…”

“But he was, though that is hardly news to anyone, not was this the first time he tried to kill you. But for Robert, he had to have his revenge. And the best way to eliminate you was, first, to divert your family’s attention away from you. With… Homer, well, we know he was drugged, which was the final straw in any relationship between him and Mrs. Bouvier, wasn’t it?”

“Y-yes,” Marge agreed, a little hesitantly.

“And that’s not all. Apparently, he got someone to sabotage the military admission exam… to put… Meg’s name, right? And… make her stand out, as if she were a prodigy. From there it was only a matter of time before they contacted her, made the invitation, and sent your daughter to Jefferson City.”

“But wait a minute,” Meg said, surprised, trying to understand. “If they put my name in the running, then how did nobody at the police department know I’d been ‘enlisted’?”

“Because no one told them. They probably received incorrect information, and no one bothered to correct it. It led to what it led to. But there’s more…”

As he was sharing this information, another boy came down the stairs. The boy, the same age as Lisa with brown hair, had the name Wallace on his passport. But like the others, it was a fake name: this was Collin, Lisa’s boyfriend. (Perhaps having two Collins in that household, the real Collin and a fake Collin, was quite confusing.) He stood behind Lisa, a little confused by Frink’s appearance, but said nothing.

“Lisa,” Frink Jr. pointed to the girl. “You know the old Flanders residence? The one that was for sale a few years ago?”

“Yes.”

“The one who moved into it was the boy… I forgot your name.”

“Collin.”

“Collin! And his father. So far, nothing unusual… except we discovered that he manipulated several conversations so that the news would reach Collin’s father.”

With a hand on her chin, Lisa struggled to grasp the situation.

“But there’s nothing wrong with that, is there? It’s strange, but… why?”

“Let’s just say…” Grandson intervened, “It was on purpose. Again, he wanted you to be distracted, of course, so it would be easier to assassinate Bart. But supposedly he discovered a secret of yours, and that’s why he went ahead with his plan.”

“Something… of mine?” She thought for a moment, and when she realized what he was talking about, she covered her face with her hands. “Oh… do you have anything else to talk about?”

“Sorry. But… actually, we came here to find out if you want to go back to Springfield.”

The shock was universal. It was a simple, innocent question, but it was a stark reminder of how much the family had suffered in recent years. Both mentally (Marge, Lisa, Meg) and physically (Bart) — not to mention people like Homer, who wasn’t quite ex-family yet, Collin, who was a step below, and their pets Pearl and Zero.

Frink Junior, trembling slightly, spoke again.

“I… I’m not going to say the place is beautiful, it’s quite the opposite. But the survivors, and also the committee that was tasked with assessing the city, have tried their best. But you could just visit if you’d prefer—”

He was interrupted by Lisa’s index finger, which stopped inches from his mouth. Her eyes were shining, almost welling up with tears.

“I… was born there. And the place was never great, it was almost always a dump. We’ve suffered so much these last few years. I watched my best friend die and thought it was my fault. I watched Ralph die and thought it was my fault. I’ve seen so many disasters right in front of me… but that’s my home. Springfield is my, it’s our city. And we’re going back.”

No one else said a word. It was almost funny… but it showed exactly how much affection they had for the place. Frink Junior, who was born there, and Grandson, who had lived part of his life there, agreed. Before they could say anything else, another person came down the stairs: a girl, slightly taller than Meg, no more than 11 or 12 years old. Some might call her Monica, but others knew her real name was Lexanna.

“Morning everyone… I didn’t miss anything, did I?”

Grandson, confused by the number of characters entering this scene, questioned:

“Lisa, you didn’t bring the whole city into your house, did you?”

“Well…” Bart chimed in. “She tried, didn’t she? But there was only room for about ten. There were four of us, five with Homer, six with Collin, seven with Lexanna, nine with Zero and Pearl… but we’ve got a spot for you—”

The others began to boo him playfully. To ease the tension, Frink Jr. asked for a glass of water while Lisa considered her next steps. Grandson, who was by her side, also had an idea in his head. One he wouldn’t tell Lisa, but that she might just like.

Chapter 3

Lisa wasn’t going back to Springfield alone. Even with her family coming along, she knew other people would want to join them. That afternoon, she called several people who had fled Springfield with her family, asking if they wanted to go along with her new plan. There were countless contacts, some closer to her, others farther away. For example, Claire, Milhouse, and her father were in Calais, France — luckily, the ferry service made travel between their cities possible, which made it easier for them to meet up (especially for Bart, since he had always been closer to Milhouse than Lisa was to Claire). Milhouse’s own parents lived in the same city, not far from their son. It was funny to imagine that, despite everything, they somehow stuck together this time.

But other citizens were farther away. Carl and Lenny, if she wasn’t mistaken, were enjoying a long honeymoon in Malta, the island in the middle of the Mediterranean Sea, living a much less chaotic life compared to their time in the States — especially with their forged documents making it clear they were a married couple. Lenna and Todd, who had also escaped with them (she never found out what happened to Ned or Rod Flanders, though), were living not too far from there, in the Netherlands. Poor Todd had suffered so much… she hoped that, in the last six months, he’d had the chance to learn what he never got to in school. Laura was with her mother (or grandmother?) somewhere in the south of Scotland, Apu was with Manjula and the octuplets in Lisbon, and she could spend a whole afternoon recounting the stories of the other residents… there were so many of them.

In the not-so-distant past, she could have hardly gotten the whole city (or, in this case, part of it) to rally together for anything that wasn’t about money. It was almost funny — they were only alive because Mr. Burns had passed on and left a mountain of endless money to Lisa. But the truth was that many of them shared the same sentiment: the place was a dump, but it was their dump — if anyone was going to clean it up, it would be them. After many days of discussion with the group she’d gathered, she agreed, once again, to pay for everyone’s tickets. Although she still wanted to follow through on her promise to Burns — to spend all his money — she was seriously considering letting at least part of it accrue interest, depending on what state the city was in. That way she could keep something for her family while also keeping true to her word. She was already doing her best to take care of Bobo, Burns’ prized teddy bear, too.

With everything ready, she arrived with her family (plus Collin and Lexanna) at an airport near Jefferson City in the dead of night. Five days had passed since their conversation with Frink Jr. and Grandson, and it had been exactly six months and 12 days since they’d left Springfield. The seven of them piled into a rented (and rather cramped) car, heading down one of the highways that led to the city. Marge held her younger daughter in her lap, while Lexanna sat on Lisa’s. As they approached the city and passed through a checkpoint that had probably been there since the military arrived, they began to take in the startling new reality. The air was much cleaner, since some of the city’s industries had been shut down when the accident at the plant occurred.

But it was a strange feeling. As they looked at the houses on the streets they passed, they saw some destroyed, others vandalized, and even the remnants of homes that had once stood there. It was a post-apocalyptic city, even though a fair number of people still lived there. Marge, who had swapped driving with Homer midway, decided it wasn’t time — not yet, at least — to go downtown. A few soldiers were still spread throughout the neighborhoods, not marching as before, but in a few isolated corners. Meg swore she saw a soldier in a blue helmet, from the United Nations, but could it really be that bad? She wasn’t sure. Since some neighborhoods were still restricted due to the numerous potholes in the asphalt, the car carrying the Simpsons and their companions stopped in a suburb near their old house.

“Alright,” said Marge, getting out of the car, “since we have to stop here, let’s split up and meet who we need to meet. When you’re done, we can meet up at Moe’s bar… or what’s left of it. Frink said it would be okay with the military here.”

Everyone agreed and went their separate ways. Marge decided to go with Homer to find her family; Meg and Lexanna went to see if Laura had arrived in town, or if Rod had come back from the dead (they didn’t know his whereabouts). Bart, Collin, and Lisa continued down the street ahead, since just a few streets and blocks away was a house familiar to them all. It wasn’t their house, but it was a place they needed to check on.

Homer and Marge walked for a few minutes to a part of the neighborhood with a larger number of people — that is, greater than zero. Some time well before the incident, Patty and Selma had moved from their apartment to the old family home. Ah, that house with the light-purple hue… it was well-preserved, thankfully. After getting out of the car and walking to the front door, she knocked and waited a moment. The door opened, revealing Selma, with her magnificent smoke-colored hair and a cigarette in her mouth, which fell to the floor when she saw Marge.

What followed was an almost comical mix of happiness and emotion. Marge hugged her sister, then ran inside to hug Patty, who had been sitting motionless and in complete silence on her living room armchair for about five minutes. Bored out of her mind, she couldn’t believe what she was seeing. Homer also entered the house, giving lukewarm handshakes and much shorter hugs, when he heard a voice from another room.

“I thought you weren’t gonna remember me anymore…”

And there he was. His father, Abraham, looking exactly the same as when they had last seen each other. Except, of course, for a few injuries and the exaggerated amount of foundation that had been applied to his face. He was alive, somehow. There was no helping it: he ran immediately to his father and wrapped him in a hug that knocked them both to the floor. In response, Homer got a whack on the head with Abe’s cane.

“If you knew how to shoot better, we would’ve solved this problem much sooner! I can’t stand watching these… what do you call ’em? These ’entertainment’ shows!” he said, making air quotes with his fingers. “In my day, we did it better and with much less money!”

Marge had to explain what had happened to them, wanting to know if everyone was okay (or at least in decent shape). Homer, trying to recover from the lump forming on his head, went to the kitchen freezer to get some ice to soothe it.

It was curious, but Meg and Lexanna didn’t have a general sense of where they could go. Lexanna had a reasonable knowledge of Springfield, but she was just following Meg. Meg, for her part, did have someone she could meet, but since they were all heading to Moe’s later, she could wait a little. Her friend, who had been quiet for most of the trip, began to speak perhaps her first words of the day.

“We never really talked about that, did we?”

“You’re right, we didn’t,” Meg said, focusing more on the houses and the worn asphalt than on Lexanna. “Or well, not as much. I’m not good with words… but I have to thank you. If it weren’t for you, we wouldn’t be walking here today. I… I should have been less selfish with you.”

She smiled. “That’s all I wanted to know. Seriously, Meg, you’re one of the best people I’ve met in all my years of existence. Don’t ever change.”

As they walked down the deserted street, Meg eventually noticed a small dot on the horizon (or, in this case, the end of the street). Maybe two dots. Wait: there were four dots! Four people. Soldiers? They could be, but they didn’t seem to be in uniform. At least, not some of the dots, she thought. As she got closer, she realized that yes, there were soldiers. Two of them, in fact, and also two people who didn’t look like soldiers (how many times had she thought that word?). In front were Ann Brie and Rodric — better known as the two soldiers she and Lexanna had to kidnap (politely, of course) to put their plan to infiltrate the city into action — and behind them, Laura and Rod, whom they knew a little better. (The two of them tried their best to not recall that, technically, Lisa’s plans of escaping almost failed because of Rodric leaking them.) And to top it off, the four of them looked a bit alike…

She hadn’t imagined their reunion would be in the middle of a random street in a random neighborhood, but it would do. Lexanna, who had been smiling ever since her conversation with Meg ended, smiled even wider. Could she have known they would be passing by? Honestly, Meg didn’t even want to know. She just quickened her pace into a run and hugged the first person she saw. The hug, which soon became a group hug, didn’t knock anyone to the ground this time.

“W-where have you two been?” asked Rod, who was wearing a light gray sweatshirt. “I almost died here, you know? They left me locked up in the police station. Lucky I escaped, otherwise I would have burned to death…”

“You have no idea how hard it was to find this boy,” replied Laura, who had apparently arrived in Springfield before the family. “And I took the opportunity to invite our new friends, Ann and Rod 2, so we could have our reunion…”

Fortunately, everyone seemed to be in good shape, and even Lexanna, who had always thought she had few friends in life, looked at the sextet and thought: maybe she wasn’t so alone in the world after all. They would spend the next few minutes talking about the city, and about some other events, but they still had a lot to catch up on.

Walking through the deserted streets in a neighborhood of the city, Lisa, Collin, and Bart didn’t say much. It was strange to be there after being away for so long. She had thought about leaving the city a few times, but she never thought she would be gone for so long, or that she would return to find the city in such a state. It was a miracle Springfield was still standing; the fires set in the city had caused a good deal of destruction, from what little she had seen.

But at some point, they would have to talk, especially with Bart knowing exactly where they were going.

“Bart, I—” Collin tried to find the words. “D-do we really have to go there? We don’t even know if they’re really there—”

“Dude,” he interrupted her. “They’re there. I’m sure of it. And if they’re not, well, there are plenty of other places for us to look. This is the first stop.”

Lisa couldn’t hide the worry on her face. “So Bart, that’s the problem. Remember when I got passports for everyone, that whole story with Fat Tony and all?”

“I remember.”

“Yeah… well, I kind of didn’t pay for that many people. I didn’t know everyone’s whereabouts, so… the Indelinquents kind of got left out. And I know *nothing* about what happened to them — Kearney, Nelson, Jimbo… I don’t know.”

“Relax, we’re almost there. We’ll get there, we’ll see them, and everything will be okay. Alright? Don’t worry.”

She sighed, and silence reigned once more. A short walk later, they arrived at their destination: an abandoned house, as decrepit as the last time they’d seen it, more than a year ago (for Lisa, at least). Still, it was a little different, with several barricades here and there and even a barbed-wire fence covering part of the front yard. Not that the situation in Springfield was the best, but did they really need all that?

“Lisa, are you sure you didn’t tell anyone?” Collin asked, quite anxiously.

“I told Kearney. He was the one who took me to Fat Tony, but I don’t think he heard about the passport story. And he didn’t tell anyone about our meeting either, so that’s the problem.”

The three of them crossed through the wire in the small yard and went to the front door, where Lisa gave three small knocks. Knock-knock-knock. A man answered, a little taller than Collin, wearing a green jacket, a worn-out black shirt, even more worn-out jeans, and a pair of black boots — not to mention a characteristic beanie that had once been red (or purple?). It was Jimbo, whose eyes widened in a way she had never seen before.

“L-L-Lisa?”

“Can we come in, Jimbo?”

“Sure. Uh…”

The three of them entered the house that, honestly, had seen better days. Bart could clearly see the difference. It was much more fortified, but also much more run-down; the paint on the walls had peeled off years ago. The floor was covered with bottles, boxes, and general filth. There was a tube television in the corner, which for some reason was working, even though she thought it was unlikely to have electricity in that house. There was also a small green sofa, where a man with long brown hair, a green shirt, and black shorts was lying down — Dolph.

Perhaps tired, he almost didn’t notice Lisa and Collin’s presence, but he nearly fell over backward when he saw Bart. Staggering a little, he went to the entrance to give a hug to Bart, who was practically a VIP member of the Indelinquents. Bart, embarrassed, didn’t prolong the hug. Dolph soon went to call the other members of the group to the living room, all of them very surprised. Kearney, plus the sisters Sherri and Terri (despite everything, Lisa still couldn’t easily tell who was who). Only one person was missing…

“Where’s Nelson?”

Everyone fell silent. Lisa, finding it strange, tried to change the subject.

“He’s not here? No problem. Anyway, I wanted to know—”

“Nelson’s dead,” Jimbo said, looking down.

“DEAD? How did—how did Nelson die? W-wasn’t he here with you?”

Bart was in shock. Besides Sherri and maybe Terri, Nelson was the group member closest to him. They were practically friends, even after everything they had lived through in their childhood and part of their teens. Collin, who barely knew him, didn’t react.

Terri intervened. “He saw the soldiers on the street, running, walking, marching… and he sacrificed himself, for all of us. Because we were a family to him, right? He left… and you left us here.”

“Not on purpose!” exclaimed Lisa. “We—there were so many people to get passports for, I-I had to get the money, Fat Tony was threatening me, I had to find my dad and—”

“It was always easy for you, Lisa. You were always the perfect one, you’re never wrong, everything is everyone else’s fault…”

“Enough!” Bart shouted, getting everyone’s attention. “We’re not doing this now. Calm down! Sure, my sister isn’t perfect, she can be arrogant, too rigid, and have a busted armpit,” he recounted, to the horror of Collin and his sister, “but she’s my sister. And we didn’t come here to fight, we came here to see if you’re okay, so we can get back to our lives.”

“Nothin’s ever gonna be the same again!” Kearney commented, breaking all standard rules of the English language.

“I know it won’t be. We’ve already lost so many people, and we have to stick together so we don’t lose any more, I don’t know, rebuild this place. What’s the point of us fighting now, after everything?”

The five Indelinquents present nodded in agreement. Collin and Lisa, wanting to prevent her brother from getting any more worked up, asked everyone to meet at Moe’s bar, where there would be a gathering of the survivors and the old visitors. They agreed.

As they left the house, Bart asked for a moment alone with Sherri. He hadn’t forgotten that, months before they left, she had told him she was pregnant with his child — but he didn’t know what had happened since. Her belly wasn’t that big.

“Sherri, about my, I mean, our chi—”

“I lost him.”

“Oh. Uh… um… wow.”

She put a hand on his shoulder.

“He would have suffered a lot these past months. I think it was for the best. I don’t want to talk about it now. Maybe later?”

“Maybe. I guess. Maybe…”

An hour or two later, the separate groups gathered at Moe’s bar, which was surprisingly intact, all things considered. In theory, the city center had been one of the hardest-hit areas, but there was an effort by the downtown shopkeepers to renovate the place. It looked much greener, for sure!

Lisa’s plan had worked: several of the residents who had gone abroad were reunited with their friends, family, and also the remaining residents of the city. The only part of the plan that hadn’t worked out was, ironically, everyone arriving well before she did. On the street in front of the bar, people were mingling with all sorts of drinks. Over there, Smithers — a figure the family had practically not seen since the incident at the plant — was there, chatting normally with some of his aides and plant employees. He seemed slightly embarrassed. Cleverly, Homer took the opportunity to politely ask (beg) for his job back. Knowing that the plant would now be in need of employees, and still believing that Lisa was cool enough to help with the reconstruction of the factory, he reluctantly agreed.

Also there, Bart reunited with Milhouse, although the meeting wasn’t much of a surprise; they had kept in frequent contact. Claire, Milhouse’s girlfriend, was there too. He couldn’t deny that, perhaps, he still had some kind of feeling for her — but he pushed it aside, understanding that his best friend was a better boyfriend for her than he had been. Much better, in fact. The two exchanged a brief smile and went back to talking about other things.

They weren’t the only ones to reunite, of course. In the crowd, a breathless Ned Flanders, who had refused the recommendation to flee the city, was looking for his two sons. Staying in the city had perhaps not been the best of choices, even though he had survived and so had his son Rod. He saw, from a distance, little Todd, along with Lenna, the woman he had been dating for almost a year, who had taken his son far away when he refused to leave. Well then. He hugged his son briefly, but noticed that Lenna wasn’t so enthusiastic. The conversation they would have later wasn’t going to be a good one, it seemed.

Among the many people there, Meg, now without Lexanna or anyone else, walked along the sidewalk (with difficulty — it was so crowded!) and pushed open the swinging door that led into Moe’s bar. Inside, a familiar figure in an apron. Ah, good old Moe. Unlike perhaps all the other encounters that had happened that day, this one had no running or falling to the ground. It was a simple walk that led to a long, and heartfelt, hug.

“Meg… it’s been a while…” Moe said, trying to start a conversation. But Meg, after so long without seeing the man who was practically her second father, preferred the silence. He quickly got the message; he did not need to belabor the point, after all. She cared about him anyway.

Several minutes later, outside, on a kind of small platform, Mayor Quimby was finishing some speech that few were paying attention to. Lisa, taking the opportunity, climbed onto the platform and began to get everyone’s attention.

“Everyone! I know things haven’t been the easiest, a lot has happened in recent years… but I know that together, we can make Springfield a better place… or at least, better than it is now.”

Some in the crowd murmured, others smiled, but those present applauded her loudly. She didn’t even have time to finish the speech she had planned, which was funny. This wasn’t exactly how she wanted to be recognized, and who knows how much money they’d ask to borrow now that the whole city knew she was high society, to an extent. Marge was happy for her daughter — how much she had learned! Amid the applause, she looked at some of the faces. She felt sad for those who had left them. She was worried about some people, like Lenna. She was happy her family was there, safe and sound. Some were talking amongst themselves; Frink Jr. and Grandson were intently discussing some experiment of theirs involving dead people with Lexanna. At this point, he was probably going to revive half the town in an experiment that would definitely go badly. (Or perhaps, go very well… just look at how it went for Sideshow Bob.) Collin was talking with his father, while Laura showed her son to Apu. Looking around at all the smiling faces, she realized Homer wasn’t there. She looked behind her, and he was leaving, moving away from the crowd and heading to the opposite side of the party. She went after him.

“Homer!” she shouted. He stopped in his tracks, giving her time to catch up. “What is it, are you leaving?”

“I’m going home. You know, live my life, with my dad—”

“But our house is a total mess!”

“Oh, right, well, it’s—” he tried not to stutter. “It’s my house, my apartment, you know? The one Lisa was paying for us…”

“But who says you’re going there?”

Homer was surprised. What was she trying to say?

“I mean, I’m very grateful that you and our kids let me live with you, but… I don’t know if you’ll still want me, you filed for divorce, the lawyer—”

Marge laughed. “What divorce?” She moved closer to Homer, taking his hands, now showing few remnants of the events of six months ago.

“You’re the father of our children. You’re still my husband.”

“I-I know, but… in the end, I’m just another Simpson.”

“Yes. The Simpson who didn’t let go of our hands. You saved our lives, you know, one way or another…”

Homer smiled. He couldn’t contain himself, and soon he wrapped her in a hug that, naturally, led to a kiss. It was so good… not just the kiss, but this tender affection, her scent… and also a wonderful smell of whiskey from the window next to them. Moe really was back in business.

Maybe this night wouldn’t be so bad after all… he made a mental note not to overdo it on the drinks. Not just because he wanted to, but also because fate willed it. How much he would have to fix, repair, buy, correct, formulate, how many verbs he’d have to invent just to get the sofa and the TV back in place… he decided to keep his eyes closed a little longer, even after the kiss ended.

Tomorrow would be a new day…
