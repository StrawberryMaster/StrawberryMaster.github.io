Chapter Summary

After being rescued from a particularly evil Rick's lair, two rather different Mortys have a chat.

In the murky silence of a vessel, a Morty sat among his duplicates.

Rows of narrow seats stretched before him, filled with other Mortys — wow! — whose faces told variations of the same tired story: exhaustion, defeat, flickering thoughts of “I want to die” and also some whispered wishes of “I want to go home.” Some hunched forward, elbows on knees, lost in thought or desperate for sleep — and God knows how much they needed that. Others, wrapped in fraying blankets, leaned against the cold walls, while a few exchanged hushed whispers under the watchful eyes of the rather stone-faced Guard Ricks posted along the aisle.

They’d all suffered enough. Some twisted Rick had managed to capture an unbelievable amount of Mortys, enslaving them for his own benefit — something about using them as camouflage while he carried out his schemes, or whatever it was. It was bizarre, but also very revolting. He counted himself among the lucky ones; he’d been kept inside with fifty other Mortys instead of suffering whatever fate befell those outside. It was genuinely surprising, at least for him. The treatment wasn’t kind, but it could have been worse. He could've been dead.

The vessel itself reflected their status: expendable, replaceable. Stripped of luxuries, it offered only a grudgingly functional water dispenser in the corner, sputtering out lukewarm water that none of the Ricks would even dare touch. At least I’m safe here. As Morty watched, another of his counterparts — who he didn’t seem to recognize, unbelievably — shuffled through the rows, finally slipping into the seat beside him. This one’s face was as unreadable as the rest. An awkward silence enveloped them.

He almost felt bad for the guy — he looked so disheveled he couldn’t even imagine that he was once a Morty. His yellow shirt was completely tattered — like he’d been using it for over a year straight without switching to something else. (Understandably, the guy also stank, but to be fair, so he did he.) Though not the most conspicuous among the vessel’s passengers, if one could say so, his neck-length ruffled hair and slightly smaller stature set him apart.

“Hey, um, hi there,” Morty mumbled, unsure of himself, unsure of how to start a conversation with another human being that also happened to be himself. The other Morty met his gaze with distant eyes.

“Hey,” came the curt reply, as though he’d already checked out of the conversation. If only…

The taller Morty forced a weak smile, trying again. He hoped some small talk might ease the tension, though he privately prayed this Morty hadn’t been among those kept outside. That’d be terrible. “A-are you, um, okay? It was… it was rough in there. For all of us, I mean... i-it’s just, y’know—so-so stressful and everything—”

The Morty beside him gave a half-shrug, his gaze fixed somewhere beyond the metal wall, as if he could see beyond it. (Space, probably.) “Yeah. I don’t wanna talk about it.”

“S-sorry.”

Morty fell silent, his hands twisting awkwardly. He really wanted to continue that conversation — or at least steer it to somewhere more manageable. That’s something he was good at, he thought.

“So, uh, w-what happened to your Rick?” he ventured after a pause, voice barely audible.

The Morty beside him hesitated, his expression flickering. “He’s… not around anymore.” He stared at his hands, voice trailing off.

Morty let out a shaky sigh. “I-I’m… sorry. Mine’s… gone too. I don’t even know what happened. One minute I was at some stupid party at school, and the next… I wake up with a bunch of Mortys… and here I am.”

The other Morty quietly nodded, a glimmer of sympathy in his otherwise blank expression. It seemed genuine. “At least it’s over. I mean… that’s something, right?”

“Y-yeah…” Morty’s voice faded into silence as they sat, and the faint whir of the vessel’s engines helped fill the lull. Eventually, the cleaner Morty cleared his throat, gesturing to a small, shabby icebox tucked between the seats. “You, uh, wanna grab something to eat?”

The smaller Morty gave a small nod, reaching over and popping open the icebox. Inside, they found nothing too interesting: a few nutrient bars, some stale-looking crackers, bottles of water with Bextissimus® labeled in it. Not a feat, but it would do. He grabbed a bottle, eyeing it with much distaste.

“Man, this stuff’s terrible,” he muttered, holding up the Bextissimus® like there was a mystery to be solved. (Thankfully, there wasn’t.) “It’s just like Dasani, except it’s somehow worse. Like jeez. The guys on uh, C-410 don’t know what water’s supposed to taste like. I mean, Bextissimus®? The fuck does that mean? Such corporate bullshit…”

Morty let out a small chuckle, picking up his own bottle but not opening it. According to the fine print, it was supposed to mean “exuberance”, which would’ve been understandable if this wasn’t just a generic water bottle. It looked like one, for sure. He just watched as the smaller Morty continued.

“All I know is my Jerry loved it. He drank it all day long, thought it was great. One day, he drank like five of these, then asked Rick to build him an aquarium.”

“Out of nowhere?”

“You ask me.”

“So, uh, did Rick do it?”

“Yeah, for about an hour. Then he flooded the entire living room, ruined the floorboards, and gave Summer hepatitis E.”

“Oh.” Morty grimaced, unsure whether to laugh or cringe. “That’s… uh, that’s pretty Jerry of him.”

The other Morty shrugged, cracking open his bottle. It was just water anyway — it’s not like the Ricks were trying to kill them inside. They both took tentative sips, grimacing slightly, but neither said anything more.

As the two of them settled into the cramped space, a Morty a few rows ahead started muttering nervously, his words growing louder and more erratic. Guard Ricks, who had no idea what the hell was going on, glanced at each other before one of them, with a slight roll of his eyes, lumbered to the front of the vessel.

“Alright, listen up, belch Mortys! We’ve got a special treat for you today!” one Guard Rick announced, in a very enthusiastic tone. He jabbed a finger toward a rickety screen that slowly descended from the ceiling. Whoa, technology.

As the screen flickered to life, the first few minutes of the hit 2006 movie Click popped up. The Guard Ricks took their posts along the aisles, clearly hoping the film would keep the Mortys distracted for the rest of the trip. The two Mortys, confused, shared a glance — maybe it would be a good movie? Little did they know there would be another 196 minutes left for them to watch.

Two long, agonizing hours later, the vessel finally docked at the Citadel, mercifully cutting the movie short. The Mortys were herded into two lines, surrounded by a row of stern-faced Guard Ricks. A massive floating monitor flickered to life between the lines, and another Guard Rick appeared on-screen in a pre-recorded message.

“Welcome to the Citadel, Mortys and uh… other Morty lookalikes,” he announced, punctuating his words with sloppy belches. Many, too. “You’re gonna love it here. Probably. You’ll get used to it. Anyway, you’ll be separated into two lines. Line A is for Mortys who need a little belch extra… refinement! You’ll be enrolled in Morty Academy, where you’ll learn how to be… better Mortys. Or at least less pathetic human beings; you know the drill, I don’t care. Line B is for everyone else.”

The more serious, smaller, and unfortunately stinky Morty, who apparently had more knowledge on the Citadel than the other, less serious, taller, and slightly less stinky Morty, told the latter that Line A, or whatever it was named, didn’t matter at this point. Every Morty there, assuming they were alive at the end of the process, would be made more “Morty” until they were assigned their very own Ricks — who may or may not be as “Rick” as they expect them to be. This was confusing. The fact they were skipping that process was greatly appreciated, but it didn’t really change the outcome; they would be directly assigned a Rick of their own.

After some shuffling forward, they each received a flimsy paper ticket with their assigned Rick’s ID scrawled on it. Neat-but-not-so-neat Morty’s ticket read: Rick C-481. Scrunkly Morty scanned his own ticket and sighed as he read: Rick E-295. The two exchanged a quick glance. The Citadel, with its almost endless maze of sectors and thousands of Ricks, suddenly seemed impossibly vast. There must’ve been at least zillion Ricks in there — dimensions were of little reference to them at that moment. It almost sounded ridiculous!

“It says my Rick’s in… Mortytown?” The taller Morty squinted at the small text. “Wait, are all the places named after us and Ricks?”

“Probably,” the silent Morty murmured, scanning the Citadel’s surprisingly sprawling skyline. “That’s what ego does for ya. But hey… at least we’ve got places to go now.”

Morty shifted on his feet, a bit hesitant. “S-so… can we, uh, keep in touch? Y-you might need help getting around, and maybe I… might- no, wait, I-I-I definitely need some help too?”

Silent and Definitely Sweaty Morty let out a small sigh, then dug into his pocket, producing a crumpled scrap of paper and a stubby pen. Damn. He had that all this time? He scribbled down his ticket information and handed it over. “Sure. But don’t overthink it.”

Taking the note with a grateful nod, Morty slipped it into his pocket. They reached the front of the line, where rows of so-called interdimensional taxis (ironic, given that they weren’t really going through any dimensions here — except for those within the Citadel?) were waiting to take each Morty to his destination. The Empire State Building-tall Morty waved as his new friend was ushered into a cab, and then found his own, headed toward the twisting streets of Mortytown.

Mortytown, turns out, was more like a maze of narrow streets bordered by shabby buildings and walls covered in peeling paint and graffiti. It definitely had Mortys, but it didn’t look like a town. The air was thick with a strange, unidentifiable odor, making Morty wrinkle his nose as he navigated the winding pathways. Finally, he arrived at his assigned destination — a rundown apartment with faded red paint and cracked windows, just one of many in the endless stretch of Citadel blocks. At least it was still standing.

Morty dragged his feet up the narrow, worn staircase of the Mortytown apartment building, still feeling the damp chill of the cramped ship in his clothes. (That and sweat — he really needed a shower.) He found himself at the second floor and checked the paper slip: 204. A faint odor wafted from beneath the door as he knocked. It creaked open to reveal a tall, rather thin Rick wearing a dusty mail carrier’s hat, his face set in a weary but vaguely sympathetic expression.

“I gave you five bucks already, kid.”

“No- what? No, I was sent here by the guards at the Citadel… or whatever you call them.”

“Oh. You must be the new Morty,” the man said in a raspy voice, nodding with an air of disinterest. “I guess I’m your, uh, well, your Rick. It’s, uh, Mailman Rick, if you need to call me anything.”

Morty looked him over, blinking in confusion. Well, this definitely wasn’t what he pictured his new Rick would be like. Back home, his Rick was… well, not this. He was definitely more of a scientist, but a mailman? “Uh, hi…? So, wait, you’re a Rick? And a mailman?”

Mailman Rick squinted, letting out a long sigh. Uh-duh. “Yep, that’s right. Mortytown’s finest. The Citadel’s, technically. And well, only, almost.” He paused, sniffing. “And Jesus kid, you reek. If I wanted a Morty from the sewers I would’ve gotten one much earlier. H-how about you take some time to get used to the place — I’ve already dealt with enough crap today from the neighbors. I’ve got routes to finish.”

“Oh, okay… s-sure, no problem,” Morty muttered, clutching his small paper a little tighter. He wasn’t sure what he’d expected, but this wasn’t it. It really wasn’t. It felt… weird. Maybe it was the fact the only Rick he’d seen — and barely — in previous months was perhaps the most evil of Ricks. The mailman gave him a curt nod and shuffled out, closing the door behind him with a rattling click.

And so Morty found himself alone in an apartment he’d never even been to. Things were developing very fast, apparently.

Despite its exterior, the place was surprisingly tidy. The floors were mostly clear of clutter, and the furniture looked worn but well-kept. Damn, they even had interdimensional cable! Nothing like the chaos of his old Rick’s garage — always scattered with inventions, half-empty beakers, and strange glowing objects he wasn’t supposed to but definitely did touch. Here, there was only a narrow coffee table, a modest sofa, and a basic kitchen with a few mismatched dishes stacked neatly in the sink.

He stepped further inside, his eyes scanning the small living room, when something caught his attention near the couch: a small framed photograph on a side table. Morty leaned closer, squinting at the image. It really was a photograph, nothing exciting at that. But it showed Mailman Rick, looking younger — but unmistakably him —, smiling with a boy who looked… a lot like Morty.

Oh yeah. This Rick had a grandson. In fact, many Ricks did — this was something his own Rick told him once when he was drunk on a Friday night. Sure, he didn’t really understand this at first, but eventually he was able to see it for his own. Maybe his grandson was around here somewhere… Morty pictured himself meeting him, maybe even finding a friend in this strange new place. Another Morty could be nice… someone who’d understand what he was going through.

He set by the couch and, for a moment, just let himself imagine it. A friend, someone he could talk to, maybe even trust… though he was vaguely familiar with some of the Mortys he found in whatever he could call that hellhole, it was hard to remember everyone because they were all named Morty. At one point even he ran out of adjectives to use. On Earth — his Earth, at least —, the closest friend of his in school was some guy named Bruce, and even they didn’t talk all the time.

And sure, he also made a friend back in the ship — the serious-looking smaller Morty who really needed a shower —, but who knew if they’d ever cross paths again. Hopefully, though — he had the paper with the other Morty’s contact information, and he assumed the other Morty would likely know where to find him. Besides, he seemed to know more about the Citadel than he did, so maybe this was a good thing.

Morty sat down and let out a deep breath. He really, really, really needed a shower.

How long had it been since he’d had a proper bath? That was a good question, and honestly he’d lost track of time since… well, everything. But, at least for now, he could wash it all away.

In the bathroom, he found a neatly folded set of clothes waiting for him — a long-sleeved yellow shirt, soft blue sweatpants, and sandals. Looks like every Morty around here has to wear something like this, huh. Nothing fancy, admittedly, but after what he’d been wearing, these clean clothes might as well have been designer fashion. Tossing his grimy, sweat-stained clothes into a pile beside the sink, he turned on the shower. Steam soon filled the small space, curling up around him like a comforting embrace as he stepped under the warm spray. Ahh. The water hit his skin with blessed relief, carrying away layers of dirt, dust, and bone-deep exhaustion he’d been practically wearing like armor.

As he washed, Morty caught sight of himself in the mirror, his reflection wavering through the foggy haze like a ghost. His hair — darker when wet, naturally — clung damply to his forehead, and it felt funny too, given how long it was since actual water had touched his hair. But underneath it all, it was still him. Just another Morty. A little weathered, maybe, a little more worn around the edges, but still him. He smiled.

Once dressed in the clean clothes, he padded back into the living room and nearly jumped out of his skin. Mailman Rick was lounging on the couch, one arm draped across its back, staring idly at the wall as if he’d been there forever.

“Oh! U-uh, back already?” Morty blurted, self-consciously running fingers through his hair.

Mailman Rick’s eyes rolled skyward. “W-what do you take me for, kid? Some irresponsible dumbass?” He shifted, crossing his legs. “I wasn’t about to leave you here for six hours without a clue what to do — t-t-that’s just dumb. Do I look dumb?”

“N-no-”

“Good. There are dumber Ricks out there, so at least that’s a relief.”

“W-well, right. Sorry,” Morty mumbled, still trying to get a read on this somewhat mellow Rick. “And, uh, thanks for the clothes and everything.”

Rick waved it off with a lazy hand. “Look, kid, I’m not exactly running a Morty daycare here… obviously it would’ve been easier if they just threw everyone into the Mortyboree, but nooo, they insisted we should all hang out and hug each other and sing and all that crap. But… you do seem pretty green. This your first time here?”

“In your house?”

“No-no, the Citadel.”

Morty nodded, his eyes wandering around the apartment as if trying to convince himself it was real. ”Yeah, I just got here. They said you were, uh, my new Rick? So, u-uh… here I am.”

“’New Morty’ wasn’t exactly on my bucket list either.” He snorted, but there was something almost gentle in his tone. “Still, this place gets lonely sometimes and you’re not half as annoying as the last one I dealt with, so there’s that.”

“Oh?” Morty perked up, curiosity getting the better of him. “You’ve had other Mortys? Did they stay here too?”

Something flickered across Rick’s face as he gazed at a spot somewhere past Morty’s shoulder. “A few. None stuck too around long though. T-the Mortys tend to come and go around here, you know? Though I gotta wonder why they dumped you on my doorstep of all places.”

Morty shifted his weight, suddenly aware he had no answer for that. Well, he technically did — he could just say he was randomly be selected to go there — but he also technically didn’t have an answer. He wasn’t fully sure what separated him from the other future Morty Academy alumni, what separated someone like the other Morty he knew from another Morty who would need to be “trained”. What was the difference that separated them?

“Yeah… me too. I guess I’ll figure it out eventually.” Hesitating slightly, he gestured toward the side table. “U-um, if you don’t mind me asking… that picture there — is that one of the, uh, past Mortys?”

Rick’s expression softened almost imperceptibly. “Yeah… was. Don’t see much of him these days. He’s probably around kissing another Jerry’s ass or whatever.” He cleared his throat. “Anyway, you need directions or… uhh… whatever it is you Mortys do, I’m around.”

He glanced back at Rick, who was still staring off, slightly lost in his own thoughts. Smiling, he nodded. “Thanks,” Morty said, the word carrying a little more weight than he intended.

Mailman Rick blinked, his eyes snapping back to Morty, his grandson — well, a lookalike of one. But in theory it might’ve very well been one. They were almost identical, for God’s sake! He gave a noncommittal grunt, but there was something in it that almost resembled acknowledgment. Maybe even agreement.

“Yeah, kid, sure. I’ll tell you what you need to know later, but right now I actually have to work.” Rick pushed himself up from the couch with a rather exaggerated groan. “Some of us have real jobs around here, b-believe it or not. Clean up our living room while I’m gone, will ya? Ciao.”

Morty glanced around at the practically spotless room, then back at Rick with raised eyebrows. Without thinking, he flipped him off.

To his surprise, he might’ve heard a slight chuckle from him, but he didn’t say anything else as he closed the door. The sound of his footsteps faded down the hallway, leaving Morty alone.

“Well,” he thought. “I’m in it for another ride, I guess.”
