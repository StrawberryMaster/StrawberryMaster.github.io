Chapter Summary

Mailman Rick reflects on Morty's first week in their apartment. Meanwhile, Morty goes to meet his only other friend in the Citadel.

The Citadel society was essentially a huge spectacle, one which explaining it to little Morty proved harder than it seemed. Rick knew this very well.

When he first moved here, he thought he’d love it — seeing like-minded people who were just as smart as him and getting away from eccentric wackos like Jerry in the process. The idea strongly appealed to him, maybe for about 30 days or so. His initial enthusiasm faded when he discovered he was allocated the position of mailman — which he didn’t exactly hate at first, but grew to despise shortly after realizing there was no Rick designated for that position.

“Couldn’t they have hired a Morty?” he asked. No, this isn’t a position for a Morty, they told him. “Did they seriously need only one mailman for the whole place?” Yes, we only need one mailman. He realized he was asking many, many questions and getting no substantive responses. Some time later, they finally acknowledged the “one mailman for the entire Citadel” idea was obviously impractical, even though few residents were actually using and receiving mail, and hired an extra two mailmen. Of course, he rarely saw them and couldn’t really say he was close to either. At least he got paid — the juice was worth the squeeze.

Being allotted a rather basic apartment in Mortytown — overwhelmingly filled with Mortys, naturally — he made few close friends besides the people in his building. His next-door neighbor was a drunken Paralegal Rick, who also happened to be the other Rick in the entire building. The rest were just Mortys and, ironically, he was now living with one. What a mechanism! This kid didn’t seem bad, even for a Morty, but damn, he wished his grandson had actually come with him.

Or, well, that he had never even left his world in the first place.

He’d learned some interesting details about this particular Morty. The kid came from dimension C-491, which was fortunate enough because it was probably one of the most boring dimensions a Morty could come from. Not necessarily because nothing happened there; last time he checked, jazz fusion had become mainstream there, and the President of the United States was Rosario Dawson. Funny, maybe a tad weird, but nothing too unusual. Stranger things had happened in other worlds. He was just three dimensions away from the world where people had butts on their faces and faces on their butts. Rick had never really heard of Rick C-491, though. Must’ve been a funny guy.

But Morty C-491, well, he now knew. In fact, he actually stood out a bit from most Mortys he’d seen (although not the ones in Mortytown, of course). He seemed very data-driven, surprisingly — he knew countless random facts that Rick found incredibly irrelevant, even by his standards — although he still maintained the appearance of your average Morty. He was rather shy, naturally, and his sense of humor was terrible, but he had gone through so much that he seemed like a shell of a Morty at times. Rick was actually kinda relieved to know this Morty at least had his head in the right place. He once saw a walking, talking, headless Morty and couldn’t sleep for days.

But hey: the addition of a government-mandated Morty would give him some nice tax credits. Niiiiice. Really nice, too. The Mortduction Tax Relief Act, as they called it, gave him deductions for Morty-related education expenses, though admittedly, he had no idea what this meant. This kid’s access to the Citadel internet was as close to “education” as he could get.

Still, Rick had to explain the basics to Morty — where they were, his role in society, places he could travel to, places he should avoid, and more — which he did over the course of a week. For starters, most of Mortytown was a no-go zone for him, at least for now. If he stayed out there in the open for more than 5 minutes, there was a non-zero chance the entire place would come chasing after him and turn him into a flattened pancake, or worse. Curiously, Rick had never encountered a Morty in Mortytown who didn’t respect him, probably because he was a mailman and no one would dare disrespect a mailman. Never ever. Absolutely not.

Even then, people here knew him. They didn’t necessarily know the other Morty, however, and since there were a zillion of them here, it was hard to say if he’d fit in. In fact, going from Nothingburgerville to Mortytown was one of the worst things that could happen to someone. Amazingly enough, this Morty seemed to know at least some things about this place too. He often rambled about another Morty he had known and befriended, which was honestly not that exciting to think about.

In fact, he must’ve been hanging out with him right now.

Rickburg Heights was a part of the Citadel known for their ugly blobjects and snobbishness. It was the complete antithesis of Mortytown; not only were there few of the so-called “yellowshirts” — initially a pejorative term for lower-class Mortys, although this was used by a few to refer to any “non-behaved” Morty — but it was full of the more so-called strong and stable Ricks. It made a perfect place for the upper Citadelian class, including a guy named Private Sector Rick — who just so happened to have been allocated a Morty of his own. Morty E-495, apparently, or whatever dimension he was from.

And Rick’s house stood in stark contrast to the somewhat more modest dwelling of other Citadel citizens, perhaps for obvious reasons. Sleek, polished surfaces gleamed under bright, artificial lighting. The house was meticulously arranged, every piece of furniture exuding a sense of a rather cold luxury. Sure, almost everything there was imported from another dimension — the Citadel had some issues with an Intergalactic Federation outpost a while back, and Rickburg Heights was one of the most affected areas —, but did anyone really care at this point? Well, this Rick did.

The Morty who’d been allocated to this house stood in the corner of the living room, pretending to be interested in an elaborate holographic news feed Private Sector Rick had left running. It was all bluster about Citadel politics, but his host seemed to revel in it. He was thinking of running for President — ridiculous considering his lack of political expertise, understandable considering his own influences and contacts. Unfortunately for him, many other Ricks would run — and his connections could not just buy him a way into the Citadel Palace. At least not the way things were going…

He turned his attention to the doorway, where another, familiar Morty shuffled awkwardly into the room. The disparity between the two Mortys was stark; while one was neatly groomed, fitting rather seamlessly into this pristine environment, it was obvious that one of them still hadn’t taken a shower. Morty E-495 raised a brow, unimpressed.

“What’re you doing here?” he asked flatly, leaning against the wall.

“I-I just… wanted to check in on you,” the other Morty stammered, glancing around nervously. He fidgeted with the hem of his long-sleeved yellow shirt, now with a green jacket over it. “Y’know, see how you’re doing. This place is, uh, way nicer than my Rick’s, at least…”

Morty E-495 sighed, running a hand through his hair. “I’m fine, okay? Like, what’s it to you anyway?”

The other Morty hesitated, shuffling from one foot to the other. “I just… I dunno, thought maybe we could stick together. L-like, help each other out. I mean, don’t you wanna go home? Back to, uh, you know… our families?”

Hearing those words just made his expression darken. E-495 — who was now starting to consider a shower would really be a good idea — pushed off the wall and strode over, his tone dropping into a quite dangerous calm. “Go home? Really? You’re still clinging to that?”

“Well, yeah! I-I mean… this is just temporary, right?”

And it should’ve been — right? Morty liked Mailman Rick, but he knew he couldn’t stay there forever. He had a loving mother at home, a father that deeply missed him, and a sister he had so much to talk about. Admittedly, he knew things weren’t great; Beth and Jerry had been sorta divorced for a while, albeit living in the same house, and Summer was rarely seen at home, but he was the thing that united the family. That and Rick — who, despite everything, really must’ve missed seeing his grandson. He could make things better.

But Morty E-495 just thought this kid was dumb.

“Stop.” His voice cut through the air like a knife, almost. “Jeez, just stop with the small talk and the ‘when this is over’ scenarios. It’s never gonna be over.”

“But my Rick is-”

“Your family? Your Rick?” a rather bitter laugh escaped the other Morty’s throat. “Come on. Wake up and smell the coffee. They’re not coming back for us. They stopped caring — if they ever cared at all.” His eyes darkened as he continued. “And these new Ricks? Private Sector Rick? Fucking… what was your Rick again?”

“M-Mailman Rick.”

“Fucking Mailman Rick! Is that what you deserve? Is that what we- I deserve? We’re just tax breaks to them. Government-mandated burdens.”

The visiting Morty’s face crumpled, tears welling up in his eyes. He couldn’t believe what he was hearing. “That’s… that’s not true. It can’t be…”

“Your Rick’s probably dead or something. And even if he wasn’t, he clearly doesn’t care anymore if he’s not going after you. What, did you actually expect Jerry to show up in a space you to save you? A-a-acting like he’s two, three seasons ahead of us? That’s fucking meta, Morty.”

Unable to hold back anymore, the visiting Morty just broke down, his small frame shaking with sobs. He curled up on the other Morty’s floor — not a very comfortable place — face pressed into the linoleum as if trying to hide from the harsh reality he’d just been forced to confront.

What if he was right? If he’d never be able to go home, he’d have to be able to meet his family ever again — or at least for a good time. For years he’d been witnessing his parents fights, which led him to not have a healthy idea of what a “familial relationship” even was, but he knew they loved him, and so did he. At least that’s what he always tried to believe in. As Dr. Wong once told him — in one of their few therapy sessions before, well, everything —, the Smith family was built on many flaws, and it turned into a key part of how their dynamic worked. And that he’d been very susceptible to being in a codependent and toxic friendship with a person that was way older than him and with many emotional issues — his own Rick.

But he didn’t want to be a burden to the family. A constant fear of his, admittedly, was coming back and realizing people were better off without him. It was something he put away in a corner of his mind and tried his best to not believe it, but the longer he went without seeing them, the more this idea made sense. And if Rick’s not coming after him… then maybe he doesn’t need him anymore. Maybe he was replaceable at all.

That’s what hurt him the most.

The shorter Morty rolled his eyes. This kid had barely entered his home and it took him less than 5 minutes to start crying? This was pathetic. Everyone around here was pathetic. Yet, honestly, he hated to see a Morty like this — broken because of a goddamned Rick over all things. “Oh, come on. Don’t do this,” he muttered, though there was no real venom in his tone.

When the muffled sobs continued, he actually felt a bit guilty. Morty E-495 sighed heavily, sitting on the floor. Understandably, it was cold. After a moment of hesitation, he awkwardly placed a hand on the other Morty’s shoulder, giving it a light squeeze. “Look,” he said, his voice softening. “I didn’t mean to make you cry, okay? B-but I’m just being realistic. This place… it’s all we’ve got now.”

The taller Morty sniffled, turning his tear-streaked face toward him. “S-s-so what do we do? Just… stay here forever?”

His hand hesitated for a moment before gently resting on the crying Morty’s shoulder. “Listen, I… I have a plan. Something that could change everything, probably.” His fingers traced small circles on his counterpart’s shoulder, a rather awkward attempt at comfort. “I just need to know if you’re willing to help. You seem like a nice Morty, believe it or not. I don’t want you to be just another pawn in their game or whatever.”

“W-what… what kind of plan?”

A small smile curved the other Morty’s lips. “The kind that will make sure no Morty ever gets abandoned again.”
