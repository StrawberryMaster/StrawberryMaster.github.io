Chapter Summary

One Morty goes forward with a special, national announcement.

Morty adjusted the knot on his long red tie for the third time, tugging at the fabric until it sat mostly straight. Jesus, this tie is long. He’d worn a few ties back on Earth, although not usually, but in hindsight, he really should’ve bought a smaller tie. How could he have known it was sized for the taller Mortys? At least it matched his light white dress shirt. Still, his reflection stared back at him with that unimpressed face he knew too well.

Beside him, the other Morty — thankfully cleaned up after what felt like a lifetime of grime — stood planted in front of the mirror. His slate suit didn’t scream “presidential” so much as “slightly better-dressed Morty,” but it was the best they could scrounge up on short notice. His blue tie, though, actually worked pretty well. Morty E-495 stood there with his fists on his hips, staring at his reflection like he was going to wrestle it.

His friend squinted at him. “Uh… you okay, dude?”

“Uh-huh. Why?”

“You’ve been doing that… uh… thing for like e-eight minutes. What’s the deal?”

The neatly dressed Morty E-495 shrugged, still locked onto his reflection. Not even a bath could get rid of his scruffy-looking hair, but at least he looked presentable. “Uhh… why not? P-p-power stance or something. Gotta look confident, you know?”

Morty rolled his eyes but didn’t press further. He knew this was a big deal for the other Morty — and for himself too. Heck, it was probably a bigger deal for all Mortys, even if they didn’t know it yet. He glanced down at his oversized tie again. It really was too long.

“Just… nerves, I guess,” Morty E-495 eventually mumbled. For all his bravado, Morty could tell he was feeling the weight of this moment. A Morty, running for President? Of the goddamn Citadel of all things? It was unprecedented, and honestly ridiculous. He didn’t stand a snowball’s chance in hell of succeeding, even though a part of him knew he could do it.

It hadn’t even been two months since they’d landed here, and now one of them was running for President. Time passed fast on the Citadel — everything felt sped up, like 2x playback. The reckless Ricks wanted everything efficient and immediate, whether it made sense or not. And here they were. It almost seemed off.

A harried-looking Morty with oversized glasses and a clipboard that was practically bigger than him poked his head around the curtain. “Sir, you ready? T-They’re, uh, waiting for you.”

Morty turned to his friend. “Hey, uh… good luck out there.”

The taller and now clean Morty E-495 looked at him, his sharp blue tie catching the flickering backstage lights. A hint of a smile crept onto his face. “Thank you,” he said simply. “Keep your eyes open out there. Might learn something.”

And with that, he stepped out from behind the curtain and onto the stage.

The modest setup — a small stage flanked by a dozen Mortys from various corners of the Citadel, each dressed in their best attempts at professionalism — seemed honestly underwhelming. A handful of Reporter Ricks and smaller press Mortys had shown up, though mainstream media had barely noticed. To them, this was just another Morty stunt. He was just a nobody, one of countless insignificant Mortys that the Citadel was already brimming with. Hell, his own Rick would think of it this way.

If he was nervous — which he was, even if just a bit —, he didn’t show it. He stepped up to the microphone, taking in the small crowd of Mortys and scattered reporters before him. Adjusting the mic, he began his speech, his voice rather steady and deliberate. A rarity for a Morty like him.

“Good evening, Mortys — and yeah, Ricks, too. I know you’re listening.”

The faint chuckles and murmurs from the audience didn’t really faze him. He let the words hang in the air for a moment before pressing on.

“I’m here today because this Citadel has a problem. A big one. One that’s been swept under the rug for far too long… and it’s time we stopped pretending it’s not there.”

Damn, he’s good. Behind the curtain, Morty watched with his heart pounding. Was his friend really this good, or had he just never noticed? Well, he’d soon find out; he’d barely started his speech and he was already adoring him. Maybe this was a good sign…

“For too long,” E-495 continued, “the Citadel has operated under a system that keeps Mortys like you and me at the bottom. We’re told it has to be this way — that Mortys follow, and Ricks lead. But who decided that? Was it us? Because I don’t remember voting on it.”

A ripple of uneasy laughter passed through the room.

“This place was supposed to be a haven. But for whom? While Ricks sit in their high-rises, Mortys scrape by in the city streets, begging for some change. This isn’t just unfair — it’s unsustainable. I may have been here only for a few months, but I know exactly what the issues Mortys in here face because I am one of them. We do the jobs they don’t want. We’re the sidekicks, the labor, the punchlines. And for what? Scraps. Scraps! And we’re supposed to be grateful for them?”

E-495 paused, letting the silence amplify his words.

“Well, I’m not grateful. I’m angry. And you should be, too.”

A few Mortys in the crowd clapped tentatively, then more joined in. Morty gripped the sides of the podium.

“I stand here today before you not as a politician, or as a mere Morty, but as someone who believes in the power of change… who believes in the power of the Citadel… someone who believes in you! It’s time for a Citadel that works for all of us. Not just Ricks. Not just the elites. But us — Mortys. And yeah, I know what you’re thinking: ‘What can a dumbass Morty even do? We’re just sidekicks. It’s just like that movie- erm, we’re just… Mortys.’”

He leaned forward slightly, his voice dropping.

“No. We’re not. We’re more than that. And it’s time we started acting like it.”

Cheers broke out across the small room, louder this time. Morty even found himself clapping, too, despite the knot of uncertainty in his stomach.

E-495 stepped back from the podium, his sharp gaze sweeping over the crowd as the applause washed over him. This may have been a short speech — it was the very start, after all — but he’d electrified the small group he’d kindly arranged for this announcement.

Perhaps he could run a good campaign after all.

The two Mortys were sitting in a dimly lit dive bar on the outskirts of Mortytown. Morty had the idea to take Morty E-495 — kindly dubbed by the press as Candidate Morty, which was true — for a brief celebration.

They sat sipping drinks that teetered dangerously close to glowing. They’d definitely drunk too much, partially as a pastime and partially in response to the media’s reaction to the campaign launch. One particularly vicious online headline had branded their campaign as “just plain dumb,” and most outlets hadn’t even bothered to cover their announcement. Still, E-495 told himself it was something. Attention — any attention, honestly — was better than none.

Morty E-495 slouched on his stool, nursing his glass, and let his mind wander. He’d read about this before. Napoleon (well, the Napoleon from his dimension at least) had been dismissed as a fool when he first started clawing his way to power. They’d called him small, ridiculous, delusional. But he’d kept going, and eventually, they couldn’t just ignore him. Sure, he’d later been exiled twice and sent to some island whose name he thankfully couldn’t remember, but that wouldn’t happen to him, right? E-495 figured the same principle applied here. Public opinion was fickle; today’s mockery could easily turn into tomorrow’s support — if he played his cards right.

“Dumb, huh?” his dumb Morty friend snorted, slumping against the bar and waving his nearly empty glass at the bartender, who ignored him. His words came out thick and sloppy; the alcohol was hitting him hard. “Dumb is running for president when every Morty out there hates your guts anyway.” He was drunk, yes.

“They don’t hate us,” he countered, his own words slightly slurred. “I just announced today. I-I know we just gotta believe in the other Mortys sometimes, y’know? Give them a chance and all that crap.”

“Pfft. Do you think a Rick will just let a Morty vote for whomever they want? Hell, would they even let a Morty vote? L-like, I get what you mean, and I looove this campaign, but I’m not sure we can make it. I mean, what’s your platform? ‘Vote for me because I’m not a Rick?’ That’s gonna go over real well.”

E-495 shot him a glare, his eyes slightly bloodshot and heavy-lidded. This kid was so irrational at times it was borderline annoying. Too dense, maybe. He really should’ve picked a better campaign manager. “It’s bigger than that, Morty. You wouldn’t get it.”

“Bigger than that?” Morty snickered, leaning closer. “What, like your ego? Or like your chances of puking all over this bar?”

He grunted in response, pushing his drink away. He was already feeling queasy, but he wasn’t about to give Morty the satisfaction of seeing him lose his composure. Not here, not ever. “You’re an idiot,” he muttered, sliding off the stool. His boots hit the floor with a thud, and he swayed, gripping the bar’s sticky edge to keep him from toppling. “Come on. Let’s get out of here.” The other patrons — ranging from worn-out Mortys slumped over drinks to a few Ricks staring into their glasses — barely glanced up.

Morty followed, still laughing as they stumbled out into the cool, damp night. The alley beside the bar was dimly lit by a flickering neon sign that said “Mortville”, which was probably the worst name possible for a bar. The fact almost everything here had these fucking Morty-themed and Rick-themed names really did want to make him puke. They weren’t even good names. Maybe if there was an extra pun or two, but no, they were the most boring names ever. More corporate bullshit.

It was terrible. Mortys begged for three-fifty on the streets while corporate Ricks plastered their soulless branding across every corner of the Citadel. God, there would be so much he’d need to clean up…

He leaned heavily against the wall, clutching his stomach, while Morty stood a few feet away, hands in his pockets, watching him with an amused smirk.

“You know,” Morty mused, his voice light and teasing, “you’re kinda cute when you’re like this. N-not drunk, I mean. Just pensive, y-yeah. Pensive. Chill.”

“Do me a favor and shut up.”

Yet Morty laughed harder, doubling over as if Morty E-495’s misery was the funniest thing he’d seen all night. (It was.) E-495 rolled his eyes and straightened up, wiping his mouth with the back of his hand; he could still taste the bitter dregs of his drink there. “We’re calling a cab,” he said sharply. “I’m not walking back home with you acting like this.”

Morty shrugged, waving a hand and still grinning. “Suit yourself, Presidente. Why don’t we go to my house?”

“I don’t— your— ugh. I’m not even in the mood to argue. Fine.”

As they waited for the cab, the alley stretched quiet around them. As the Mortys stared down the street, the Morty candidate’s mind was already racing with plans for the next step in their campaign. He didn’t care what the newspapers said. He didn’t care what his squeaky clean and now drunk Campaign Manager Morty said — at least not everything. This was just the beginning. Eventually, they wouldn’t be able to ignore him, they’d have to listen. After all, what was the worst that could happen to a Morty at this time of night?

A lot, honestly. But not to a candidate Morty. Especially not one who’d have special security once the campaign really got going. Still, as the two of them piled into the cab and the driver sped off into the haze of Mortytown, he kept an eye out to make sure no one too weird was going after them.

When the cab hovered to a halt in front of Morty’s apartment complex — which E-495 didn’t think was that bad, considering the circumstances — they made their way inside, climbing the only creaky staircase to Morty’s unit. The hall smelled faintly of coffee and burnt toast, but it was quiet. When Morty opened the door to this politician friend, Morty E-495 was rather surprised. The apartment was tidy, almost unnervingly so — not too fancy furniture, naturally, but everything had its place. There was even a potted plant on the windowsill, its leaves just beginning to wilt. How fanstastic.

“Well? What d’ya think?” Morty asked, holding the door open with a grin. “Betcha didn’t expect a Morty to have a setup this nice, huh?”

“It’s… fine,” E-495 muttered, stepping inside cautiously. He took in the warm lighting. Huh. How homely.

From the couch, Mailman Rick looked up from his Sudoku book, squinting at the pair as they entered. The hell was this kid doing. His face relaxed when he recognized Morty, though his gaze quickly shifted to the other Morty.

“There you are, kid,” Rick said, setting the book aside. “Was startin’ to think you got mugged or abducted or somethin’. Happened to me once. Guess you brought a friend, huh?”

Morty laughed rather nervously. Although Rick vaguely knew about the other Morty, they’d never met until today. In fact, he probably didn’t even know that Morty was running for President. “Yeah, uh… Rick, meet… well, I think you all know each others’ names, so um. Mailman Rick, E-495; E-495, Rick? He’s… you know. My Rick.”

Uh-duh.

Mailman Rick shrugged, shaking the other Morty’s hand. “I’m not even gonna ask about the fancy suits and your smell of alcohol. B-believe me, I was a drug dealer once, I know what it’s like.”

“Uh-”

“Yeah yeah. You know those seeds? Those seeds you stick up your ass. Well, I sold many of them, a-a-and now I’m working as a fucking mailman. Pathetic-

“ANYWAY”, Morty cut in, rolling his eyes but smiling. “W-what’s for dinner? We’re starving.”

Rick jabbed a thumb toward the kitchen. “There’s some spaghetti there. Probably cold by now, but you can heat it up. And please don’t eat it in the bathtub, t-this isn’t like Gummo. Oh, and if your, uh, buddy needs anything, tell him to knock. I’ll be in my room. Busy morning tomorrow. I have many letters to deliver…” He stood, stretching with a groan before grabbing his Sudoku book and heading down the hall. “About nine of them, precisely. Nice meeting ya, uh… whatever Morty you are. Night.”

The two Mortys gave him a faint nod. That was… something.

“Alright,” Morty announced, clapping a hand on his friend’s shoulder. “Make yourself at home. I’m gonna eat somethin’ before I pass out.”

E-495 shrugged him off, mumbling, “I’m just gonna crash for a bit.” He dropped onto the couch with a heavy sigh, rubbing his temple. The alcohol churned unpleasantly in his system, and his cybernetic implants clearly were not taking well to the substance. Aw jeez. He made a mental note to scale back his intake until the campaign was over. Politics demanded a clear head, and he couldn’t afford to lose control.

At least not yet.
