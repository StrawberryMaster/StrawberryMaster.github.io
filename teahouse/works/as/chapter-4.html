Chapter Summary

Another day on the campaign trail for a Morty. Meanwhile, his campaign manager reflects on the campaign at home.

Campaigning sucked.

Morty E-495 hated to admit it, but campaigning really sucked. It seemed like each morning, he’d wake up in either his house or his campaign manager’s apartment, shove down whatever was in the kitchen counter, and head out to tackle another rally, another interview, an endless loop of handshakes and slogans.

And God, did he hate it.

His speeches felt like garbage. Every word that tumbled out of his mouth sounded stiff and rehearsed, no matter how much effort he put into making it sound authentic. He just had next to zero enthusiasm in his delivery, yet everyone seemed to love him. He thought some of his ideas — radical as they were, compared to the average Rick’s — might catch on, but none of it seemed to matter. Instead, the crowds adored him for his so-called energy, his simple calls to unity and change, and for the damn slogans. What the hell.

They’d crowd around him after every speech, their identical (wow!) faces beaming with an enthusiasm he couldn’t comprehend. They’d quote his most mundane statements as if they were profound philosophical insights. “Yes we can!” they’d chant, completely missing the irony that he’d stolen the slogan from some Earth president he’d read about. What was his name again? Obama or something. He’d be a better candidate than him, that’s for sure. Morty wasn’t that great. The fact that none of them seemed to notice — or care — was both convenient and unsettling. Maybe fame did kill.

Walking down any street meant being bombarded by waves of yellow shirts and brown hair, which wasn’t exactly unprecedented — again, he was the Morty candidate. Who else would Mortys support? He looked exactly like all of his voter base, unless you counted someone such as Hammer Morty. That guy was a bit of an odd fellow. There were so many Mortys following him that sometimes he’d catch his reflection in a store window and momentarily forget which Morty he was — the candidate or just another face in the crowd. He really needed to draw the line before they saw him as one of those figures the so-called Mortyists worshiped. “The One True Morty” or whatever crap they made up — he heard a lot about it when he was at Evil Rick’s lair.

This also meant his actual policy proposals, which he carefully crafted during sleepless nights with his surprisingly competent manager from Mortytown, barely registered with his supporters. It was simultaneously frustrating and relieving — frustrating because he’d actually put thought into those policies, relieving because some of them were admittedly half-baked. Maybe it was better this way.

Even then, he had something else to take care of, too: the Ricks.

The campaign had narrowed to 6 candidates, 5 of whom were Ricks. Among them, the so-called intellectual Reverse Rick Outrage; the amusing, to say the least, and eccentric Juggling Rick; the angry old man Retired General Rick; the loony Rick Guilt Rick; and the worst candidate of them all, Private Sector Rick. His very own Rick, his quote unquote “grandpa”. Disgusting.

They led the charge in what could only be described as a smear campaign: during the few debates they held (which most Ricks purposely boycotted, as they didn’t wanna debate with him), they would either talk over him or discuss him as if he were just a lunatic who didn’t know who he was or why he was there.

“Look at this Morty,” Private Sector Rick had sneered during their last debate, not even bothering to address him directly. “H-he thinks he can reform the Citadel. Ha. belch Probably can’t even reform his own bedroom. I’m Private Sector Riiick!” Of course, the other Ricks had laughed, and their laugh made E-495’s blood boil. Motherfuckers.

Worse, the electoral commission was staffed entirely by Ricks (naturally) and treated these jabs as “healthy political discourse.” Usually, he would smile through these moments. Let them laugh. Let them dismiss him. Let them think this was just some cute rebellion that would fizzle out like all the others. Their complacency was his greatest asset, someone once told him.

But the fact he was going through all of this sucked.

During one particularly low moment, his campaign manager had asked him if everything they’d been doing so far was worth it. “T-they’re never gonna take you seriously at this point,” Morty had said, sorting through another stack of hate mail from Rick supporters. Of course, he should’ve hired a private email server instead of a goddamn Rickgle email. They screwed themselves up. “Maybe we should just—”

“That’s exactly why we’re going to win,” E-495 had interrupted him, setting down his glass in their couch. “Because they’re not taking us seriously. And by the time they do…”

He never finished that sentence. He didn’t need to; the gleam in his eye said enough. Or so he hoped, at least.

The apartment was unusually quiet. Mailman Rick was out on some errand, likely delivering packages across Mortytown (or wherever he usually went to…), leaving Morty alone to his thoughts. He sat slouched on the couch, legs sprawled out in front of him, staring at the ceiling. A faint hum from the ancient refrigerator filled the silence, broken only by the occasional creak of the floorboards as he shifted. Just a few minutes prior he was listening to a song on the radio — for whatever reason, Citadel radios really liked Robin Thicke. Rick too, but he wasn’t in the mood for it; he shut it off.

His thoughts drifted back to his planet, to the life he’d (unintentionally) left behind. It had been months since he’d last seen his family. Months since he’d sat at the dinner table, bickering with his dad about something stupid he made or sneaking a smile at his mom when Jerry inevitably made a fool of himself.

Morty chuckled softly. He could feel the memories coming back to him as if the happened yesterday! One time, many months (years?) back, Jerry had insisted on fixing the lawnmower. “It’s just a spark plug, Morty,” he’d said, only to have the machine sputter and smoke the moment he turned it on. Honestly, this wasn’t even a great memory — but seeing how his dad easily broke things made him laugh.

And Beth… mom… God, he missed her too. The way she’d always been so strong, so sure of herself, even when things were falling apart. She had this way of grounding him, of making him feel like everything would be okay, even when he doubted it. Out of everyone in the house, she was the one he liked the most — even despite Rick. He remembered the time she’d let him help her with a horse surgery at the clinic she worked in. She’d walked him through every step, even when he’d nearly dropped a scalpel and almost killed a horse. Morty remembered how she still smiled at him when it was all over, ruffling his hair like he was a kid again. “You’re smarter than you think, Morty,” she’d said. “Don’t let anyone tell you otherwise.”

And maybe he still was.

Of course, there was also Summer. Sum-Sum, as Rick would call her — she’d always been the one to push him out of his comfort zone, dragging him to parties or forcing him to stand up for himself when he wanted to just stay quiet. Once, she’d convinced him to sneak out to see some stupid concert downtown; it was for some weird band she liked and the songs were, understandably, terrible. (He totally didn’t sing a song or two during it.) They’d gotten caught by Beth on the way back, but even then, she just laughed it off and said it was worth it. And it kinda had been.

Morty sighed, rubbing his hands over his face. The Citadel felt like another universe entirely — well, technically it was. In fact, he’d been thinking about (and bringing up) the fact he was surrounded by endless versions of himself and Ricks for months now. This also made it so easy to forget who he used to be. So easy to feel like he was losing himself in the noise.

Being exposed to so many Mortys almost made him feel unrecognizable. Not so much physically — he’d never discriminate against a Morty, probably — but mentally. The fact Mortys did so many things he never did was enough to make him feel… weird. It’s like if he was doing it too, and he couldn’t shake that off his head. It was one thing he didn’t want to get involved with, but had to because he was the head manager of a campaign. Finding out that Mortys were in love with Ricks seemed… off. Finding out there were Mortys in love with other Mortys confused him. Not that he was against it, of course, it’s just that… nevermind.

He reached for his phone, unlocking it with a flick of his thumb. He opened his gallery app, looking at pictures he took over his stay in the Citadel. Among them, pictures from the start of the campaign. In one of them, he and Morty E-495 were standing side by side, arms around each other’s shoulders, grinning like idiots. It almost seemed funny; he’d rarely seen him have this big, genuine smile on this face. And it made him so happy, too. It had been a good day, and was right before the weight of the campaign had started to bear down on them.

Morty stared at the photo for a long moment. As cliché as it sounded, it almost looked like they believed they could actually change the world — or at least the Citadel. Cute, kinda.

Closing the gallery, he leaned back on the couch and let the phone rest on his chest. Maybe they needed a day like that again. Just the two of them, no speeches, no debate preparations, no campaign nonsense — and especially no people throwing pies at them. He wasn’t sure where that Rick protester came from, and although the pie was delicious, being hit by it hurt a bit.

Hmm, yeah. I think we can do this.

He made a mental note to bring it up tomorrow. Maybe they’d hit a quiet spot in a quieter part of town, grab a drink — or something non-alcoholic, considering how things went last time — and just… talk. De-stress. Reconnect. That’d work, right?

Something told him he’d probably decline, at least for now; there was a big debate coming up next and Morty in particular called it especially important. His numbers right now were around… 4%, and he was 15 points behind the fourth place candidate. Despite all the Morty enthusiasm, few Ricks had budged. (They were flopping, as Summer would say.) He probably had something up his sleeve to take care of that, being a great orator and all, but this was a ladder that was getting harder and harder to climb.

Turns out appealing to humans in general was easier than Ricks, especially since all the so-called smartest people in their universes were splitting votes for other so-called smart Ricks, who also happened to have been running terrible campaigns. He wasn’t saying that because he was jealous, no, he was saying that because they were terrible campaigns.

But hopefully he didn’t. Deep inside, he knew he was also a Morty just like him — one that suffered a lot and had used his feelings to power something great. Sure, he’d probably need to use more of his feelings because the power wasn’t enough, and if they lost he’d have no idea how his friend would even react… they had to time everything just right and hope for the best.

Except the best wasn’t enough. They’d need to try much harder, and God knows how that’d end up.
