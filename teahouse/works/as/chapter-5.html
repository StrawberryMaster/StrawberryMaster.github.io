Chapter Summary

It's debate night.

The state-issued limo was cramped, as expected. Today was a special day: it was the day of the final debate, the last chance to prove he was more than just a Morty with a grudge and a catchy slogan. He had no illusions about how high the stakes were.

Time went by pretty fast, too. It was the final stretch of the campaign; the next day, everyone would cast their vote, so he really had no time left to spare. Of course, neither did the others; weeks of relentless attacks from the other candidates — especially Private Sector Rick, who’d painted him as some kind of delusional child with a God complex — had worn him thin. They lived in the same house, for Mort’s sake, and yet he still had the courage to say things like that? Pathetic. Maybe some Ricks were all the same. But he knew that campaign debates, in general, were nothing more than elaborate theater. He could probably shit on everyone’s faces and still rack up votes. A good amount of them, too. Not tonight, though.

He sat wedged in the far corner of the back seats, head pressed against the tinted glass window, his eyes half-closed. Morty E-495 wasn’t sleeping — far from it. Just maintaining what little composure remained.

Keep it together. Just one more push. You’ve made it this far, haven’t you?

The limo jolted slightly as they hit a bump in the road, jarring him out of his thoughts. Sighing, he clenched his fists briefly before releasing them.

Across from him, his Campaign Manager Morty was talking, his voice enthusiastic and full of life as he rattled off reminders about talking points, gestures, and which candidate to ignore entirely. Morty nodded along half-heartedly, though most of the advice blurred together. Sure, he appreciated the advice, but he knew the drill already: keep your composure, stick to the issues, don’t let the Ricks bait you into losing your temper, profit.

This seemed easy for him. It’s not like he was gonna go ape-shit on another Rick and go back home with a -20% approval rating. The hard part was not knowing what else could happen that night. Every so often, that anxiety gripped him hard enough to consider popping something to take the edge off. He’d probably need to do this soon if it got worse, too.

Morty would soon shift gears and approach him. “Hey, uh… I just wanted to say something, y’know, before we get there.”

“What is it?” he raised an eyebrow.

“Well…” Morty hesitated, scratching the back of his neck. “I’ve been thinking, y’know, about how crazy this whole thing’s been. I mean, me? A campaign manager? And you, like, running for president of the Citadel? I-I never thought I’d be a part of something like this. Never thought I’d actually, like… matter, I guess?”

He frowned.

“What are you talking about? Of course, you matter. You’ve been keeping this whole thing together while I—” he stopped short, realizing his tone was slipping into something far too earnest for him. “I-I mean, don’t sell yourself short or whatever. You’ve done fine.”

“Thanks,” he grinned awkwardly. “But, uh, what I mean is… it’s been nice, see? Like… you and Mailman Rick, this whole campaign, it’s kinda become, like, a second home for me. I’ve never really had that before.”

E-495 froze, unsure of how to respond. Though he’d heard many similar things from the other Mortys, he hadn’t heard it from anyone inside the campaign — at least from his closest advisors. Heat crept up his neck, and he immediately turned to look out the window. “Uh, okay. Cool, I guess. Glad you’re enjoying yourself.”

Morty chuckled at his obvious embarrassment. “You d-don’t have to be so weird about it, dude. I’m just saying I appreciate it. I’m not gonna bullshit you about that.”

“Yeah, yeah,” he muttered, brushing him off with a wave of his hand. “Just don’t get sentimental.”

Still, the words lingered a bit. As much as he tried to brush them off, he couldn’t ignore the strange sense of gratitude (maybe?) simmering under the surface. He hated to admit it, even to himself, but it felt good to know someone else was invested in this — invested in him. But it also felt strange.

The limo began to slow as they approached the debate venue. The sprawling hall was surrounded by crowds of Ricks, Mortys, to be expected. He even saw a few Jerrys holding signs- wait, what? Oh wait, these aren’t normal Jerrys, they’re just paper cutout Jerrys. Huh. That’s cool. Most people out front were holding campaign signs, which was expected — some in support of him, most not. Cameras flashed like strobe lights; his eyes would probably hurt if he stayed there for too long, which he realized a couple of days back after one of his rallies. Morty E-495 straightened his tie, each movement calculated — sorta. And his heart thudded in his chest as the limo came to a stop, the driver opening the door for them to step out.

His campaign manager patted his shoulder lightly. “Alright, this is it. Just do what you do best, alright? You’ve got this.”

Stepping out into the pavement, the cool air hit his face. It was probably gonna rain soon, he thought. If only he hadn’t left his umbrella at home… he straightened his blue tie again, adjusted his jacket, and took a deep breath. The crowd erupted into a mixture of cheers and boos as cameras flashed in every direction.

He glanced back at Morty, who gave him an encouraging nod.

This is gonna be fun.

The final debate was a circus, to say the least — only something the Citadel could produce. It was hardly surprising; there was only so much you could do when there were other five Rick candidates and all they did was either ignore you, mock you, or fart. Many times. An unbelievable amount of times. He really hoped they hadn’t saved it all for tonight.

The moderator’s smirk was practically burned into his memory by now, a look that screamed you don’t belong here. And maybe he didn’t — why the hell would a Morty run for President? Most Mortys his age would be jacking off or going after infinite Jessicas, probably both, yet it had genuinely been a long time since he even thought about the Jessica from his dimension. At some point, he just stopped caring. And maybe it was better this way.

Besides everything else, the debate had so far gone about as well as Morty E-495 could have hoped, though that wasn’t exactly a high bar. He thought he was doing alright, but he knew this wouldn’t be enough. In fact, he felt somewhat drained — it was like he was scraping the bottom of a barrel already long-empty — but he knew he needed to stand out from the rest, especially since he was running out of time.

Sitting backstage in the cramped space for the other candidate’s managers, Campaign Manager Morty had been glued to the screen the entire time. He’d seen the pattern before; they all had. Five Rick candidates, each more ridiculous than the last, stood at their podiums, spewing nonsense and, in one case, juggling literal balls.

Juggling Rick was cool, he had to admit. He wished he could juggle as well as he did. In fact, he’d probably be a good Rick for an upcoming administration. One they wouldn’t be a part of… and this made him worried.

“Are you kidding me? They’re eating this up.”

This is the end, he thought. He wasn’t doing bad, but… it didn’t seem like enough. There would be no runoff, no ranked choice voting, no fancy mathematical voting systems that could even favor him here. He needed to come out on top of everyone, regardless of how much, and the numbers just weren’t there for him. If this debate ended now, he’d probably end up with less than 5% in the polls — maybe a bit higher, maybe a bit lower, but still far enough from fourth place.

Back to the campaign stage, the candidate Morty tightened his grip on the edges of his podium as the moderator turned to him.

“Candidate Morty,” Moderator Rick began, attempting to remain serious. “The number of displaced Mortys is soaring, while Rick satisfaction levels are plummeting, and the divide between the two groups has never been wider. Solve that one real quick.”

The audience burst into laughter, but Morty didn’t flinch. He stood there, hands steady, eyes locked on the moderator like he was staring down a predator. Maybe he was. He wished he could just kill this man already and end this election here.

“I don’t see a divide between Ricks and Mortys,” he said, his voice even.

But maybe he could.

Retired General Rick, the oldest and probably the crankiest of the group, snorted. “Oh, shocker.” He leaned into his microphone and let out a rather, erm, exaggerated fart noise, drawing uproarious laughter from the crowd. So funny.

“Gentlemen, gentlemen,” Rick Guilt Rick chimed in, raising his hands before punctuating his words with two, also loud, deliberate farts. “I think we can all agree on one thing — whoops, make that two things.”

Another Rick joined in, then another. By the fourth or so fart noise, Morty could feel the blood rushing to his head, his jaw tightening. His hands gripped the podium harder, fingers twitching with the urge to rip the mic out and throw it at them. Worse, he could just grab his chair and throw it at them, just like they do in most modern democracies. But he didn’t. He knew he couldn’t. Instead, he just waited for the laughter to die down.

“You guys finished?”

The audience quieted. The other Ricks exchanged glances but didn’t interrupt him this time.

“The division I see,” he continued, his voice steady but rising with each word, “is between the Ricks and Mortys that like the Citadel divided, and the rest of us. I see it everywhere I go.”

He sighed.

“I see it in our schools, where they teach Mortys we’re all the same — because they’re threatened by what makes us unique.”

And it was true — the very idea of rescuing Mortys from Evil Rick was admirable, but to what avail? Sending them to the Citadel and essentially forcing them to be there, pushing them into so-called “good Ricks” was deplorable. Some may have liked it, yeah, especially since he was pretty sure some of them had no Ricks anymore. But it didn’t make sense. Why not send them some of them back to their original dimensions, their original families (or Ricks…), instead of just adopting them and forcing them to live the same oppressive lifestyle they once had? At least it’d be cheaper…

“I see it in our streets, where they give guns to Mortys so we’re too busy fighting each other to fight real injustice.”

It almost sounded hypocritical — Ricks fighting to keep Mortys from gaining influence while also making sure they had just enough influence to end themselves. It was inhuman. Once, during a Mortytown rally, he saw a Morty get gunned down just a few feet away from him. And yet he knew no Rick would do anything about this; the more they did that, the better for them. He even wondered how Mailman Rick hadn’t been killed by a Morty yet.

“I see it in our factories, where Ricks work for a fraction of their boss’ salary, even though they’re identical and have the same IQ.”

Smartest man in the universe, his Rick once said. All of them, technically. The dumbest Rick was still smarter than the smartest Morty. And yet this never stopped some lower-class Ricks from being explored, exploited and subjugated to the very same system they helped build.

“And I’ve got a message for them,” he said, his voice rising just enough to carry the weight of his words. It was working. “From the Ricks and Mortys keeping this Citadel alive, to the Ricks and Mortys feeding off its debt… a message to the ones who think they can keep us divided forever.”

Morty E-495 paused, letting the silence stretch just long enough to make the room hang on his next words. He thought someone would interrupt him, but no one did. This was great.

“You are outnumbered.”

Letting the silence hang, he stepped from the podium and started walking back to the curtain backstage. He was done. He did all he could’ve done. The initial silence actually hurt him a bit because of how deep it was, but as he walked to the curtain, he could see it rising up.

He smiled for a moment. The applause was deafening.

Backstage, his Campaign Manager Morty couldn’t believe what he’d just seen. He had done it. He had fucking done it. Standing frozen in front of the huge monitor in the room, mouth agape, he clutched the clipboard (his knuckles were white already!) like it was the only thing anchoring him to reality. But it wasn’t — he had just witnessed history.

“Holy shit,” he muttered to no one in particular as his friend walked off stage, the roar of the crowd still echoing in the background.

E-495 didn’t stop, too. He walked past the campaign staff, past a few cheering Mortys and even a few stunned Ricks from other campaigns, and straight toward his campaign manager.

“I-I don’t believe it!” Morty stammered. “I- I can’t believe it! That was amazing!”

And it was! He was practically bouncing on his heels when his friend came off stage. It was incredible. He didn’t even think he could do it, but he did it. Truly impressive, he thought.

When he stopped in front of him, he seemed happy. Why wouldn’t he be? He may have just fundamentally altered the current status of this race. This would also change his calculations, too; maybe he wouldn’t even need to make a few extra phone calls. Perhaps, if he got just high enough turnout among Mortys, alongside the other Ricks splitting their votes, he’d be able to squeak a win. And that’s presuming the Ricks also wouldn’t change their votes, which the lower-class Ricks definitely would.

But he didn’t say anything. Instead, he placed a firm hand on Morty’s shoulder, fixing him with a gaze so intense it stopped him mid-ramble. The silence stretched uncomfortably long — thirty seconds, maybe more. It was funny at first, but the longer it went on, the stranger it felt. Morty wasn’t sure if this was a good thing or bad.

“That’s why you’re fired,” he said flatly, before turning and walking away.

Wait, what? Fired? Fired?!

The words hit Morty like a slap. His face fell, his excitement drained in an instant, and one could even think he died there. He barely had time to react when another Rick — Juggling Rick — sidled up beside him until the sound of a zipper snapped him out of his daze.

“Sounds like you’re looking for work,” Juggling Rick said, pulling out his juggling clubs with a theatrical flourish. “You know, I can offer you a very enticing compensation package. First, let’s talk benefits...”

Morty watched Rick juggle for about half a minute before dismissing his services. By the time he looked across the room, his boss was gone. He had no idea what the fuck was going on.

There was a bar not too far from the debate center. Morty E-495’s now-former campaign manager sat hunched over a glass of something brown and strong enough.

“Calm down,” he muttered to himself, though the words felt like a lie. And to be fair, they were. His hands were trembling. His head was spinning. He was not thinking clearly at all. He didn’t get it. He couldn’t get it!

Why?

Half an hour ago, he’d been on top of the world — or as close to it as a Morty like him could get. Things were going so well — sure, he thought the campaign was dead in the water just half an hour ago, but things weren’t bad. He may not have liked all of the other Mortys in the team, but he liked the main one; he trusted him. And then… that happened.

Why had he dismissed him after such a triumphant moment? They’d worked so hard together; they’d built this campaign from nothing! He was probably the first person he told about the presidential campaign, and helped him build the connections with the Mortys most likely to vote for him, in Mortytown and beyond.

And now, with the finish line in sight, he was being cut loose like he was nothing. Was it something he said? It didn’t add up. He stared into his drink, as if the answers might be swirling in there somewhere. Naturally, they weren’t.

By this time it was almost midnight, and the bar was nearly empty, except for a few Mortys and Ricks scattered among the stools. A TV mounted on the wall broadcast Candidate Morty’s — the new media darling, apparently — press conference, with a crowd of reporter Ricks shouting questions as he approached the podium.

“Gosh,” he said, his voice disarmingly casual. “You know, we moved around so much it’s hard to remember. I see every Rick as my Rick…”

Ha. Funny. He wouldn’t say that about the Rick taking care of him… although, of course, he knew he couldn’t hand it to Private Sector Rick. The guy sucked, but even the lowest of Mortys would be better than him. Morty shook his head, taking another sip of his drink. He always knows what to say, doesn’t he? The thought was bitter, but even now, Morty couldn’t help but admire him.

The bartender — yet another Morty, like almost everyone else here — slid him a fresh drink. “Hey, cheer up, pal!” he said, motioning toward the TV. “A Morty’s gonna be president. Imagine this kisser gettin’ blamed for everything!” He chuckled to himself before shuffling off to wipe down some glasses.

Sighing, Morty took a long sip from his glass, the alcohol burning its way down his throat. Jesus. He’s never gonna stop doing this now, right? Great. Fantastic.

“I guess I shoulda had more faith,” he muttered to himself, but the words rang hollow. Perhaps he was too negative at times. Maybe a bit too pushy. Maybe too… caring. It couldn’t be it, right?

From a few seats down, a rather gravelly voice chimed in. “It’s not faith you need,” said a Rick wearing a long trenchcoat and fedora. Huh. “It’s fear.”

Morty turned his head and blinked. It really was a Rick in a long trenchcoat and fedora. “H-huh?”

The shady-looking Rick reached into his coat and pulled out a thick dossier, sliding it across the bar to him. He noticed the bartender gave them a curious glance but quickly returned to wiping down the glasses — he didn’t really care whatever happened at the bar as long as it didn’t make him lose customers. Hesitating at first, Morty picked up the document.

“What’s this?”

The Rick downed the rest of his drink in one gulp and smirked. “It’s secrets. Wh-wh-what do you think it is?” he tapped his coat lapel with a gloved hand. “Like come on, look at how I’m dressed.”

Fair point.

Before Morty could press him further, the Rick got up, adjusted his hat, and strolled out of the bar like nothing even happened, leaving Morty alone with the dossier. What the hell is happening. For a moment, Morty just stared at it. The weight of the folder felt heavier than it should have. He hoped this thing didn’t have anthrax inside.

Curiosity, or maybe desperation — either which one came first — got the better of him. He flipped it open and started sifting through the contents. At first, it was just a mess of documents — some surveillance photos, a few scattered reports, notes scribbled in an unfamiliar (and illegible, too) Rickian scrawl. None of it made sense, honestly. But then he turned the page and froze.

The face in one of the photos… the notes scrawled beside it… the text leaping out at him. It all started to click.

Wait. I recognize this guy.

He could still hear his voice from the TV screen. Morty’s hands trembled as he clutched the documents, his gaze darting between the papers and the screen.

“I’d rather live in hope than fear,” the candidate Morty told one of the dozens of particularly loud Reporter Ricks. “If I had to fear anything, I’d fear other people being afraid. Of fear. Itself.”

He smiled.

And that’s when it clicked.

Hard.

“Oh, shit.”
