Chapter Summary

It's Election Day. A great day for everyone — except Rick.

Evil Morty.

How did he not see this.

When he was sent to Evil Rick’s lair alongside half a million Mortys (or around that…), there was one weird-looking Morty who always hung alongside that Rick. He seemed like a normal Morty — slightly smaller, slightly longer hair, but virtually the same as one. The only difference is that this Morty wore a black eyepatch on his left eye.

One hell of a serious guy, too. He was in charge of putting him — and others — in the Morty cubicle, or whatever it was named. The place sucked, was unbelievably crowded, and he almost converted to Mortytheism on the way. If only… if a Morty saved them all, it would’ve been very appreciated… but, of course, no Morty was going to save them. That wasn’t how the universe worked.

Every so often, some Mortys would get pulled out of it — sometimes just a few, sometimes in big batches. And no one really knew where they went. Morty himself had no idea if the ones who left were the same ones who came back. At some point, he stopped trying to figure it out. Everyone looked the same anyway, and worrying about it just made the whole experience worse.

Still, Morty never completely gave up hope. It didn’t make sense for hundreds — maybe even thousands! — of Mortys to rot away like that without someone doing something about it. But why was no one coming? Why was no Rick or even a hero Morty swooping in to rescue them? Had anyone even tried? He figured some people probably had, but if they got caught or killed, no one in that soundproof hellhole would ever know. Morty was pretty sure the place was designed so you couldn’t hear a Morty scream without losing your mind. He lost his a few times along the way.

And yet, somehow, he hadn’t completely lost his own mind. Though honestly, the idea of dying in a room full of Mortys, literally dying with them stacked around him, ending up in a black body bag while everyone else watched and stared was so disturbing he avoided thinking about it for too long. It felt like something out of a bad dream (a nightmare!), but it was all too real.

Apparently, he wasn’t the only one who thought about it.

One day, he happened to be one of the Mortys pulled out of the cubicles. He never fully understood why, but since they were just calling random people, he didn’t think much about who was called, but instead why they were called. Maybe it was a test, maybe they were looking for something specific — he never knew. And when they called his name, or rather his identification number (he’d forgotten what it even was by then!), he figured his luck had finally run out.

A few guards — some weird robotic action figures, it was hard to describe — led him down a dimly lit corridor and escorted him to a small room at the end of the hall. It was quite stark and clinical, with a single table, two chairs, and one occupant already seated.

The eyepatch-wearing Morty.

Back then, most people called him Eyepatch Morty. It wasn’t too hard to find out why; he was a Morty and had an eyepatch, so Eyepatch Morty. How innovative! But the existence of an Eyepatch Morty there, out of all places, with a Rick they couldn’t easily describe (though Black Shirt Rick remained a popular nickname — BS Rick, as they’d humorously call him.) made this trickier for everyone. Eventually they settled on Evil Rick and Evil Morty — not only because they were a Rick and a Morty, but because they were evil. Yeah.

Evil Morty leaned back in his chair as Morty was shoved forward and plopped into the seat across from him. For a moment, neither of them said a word. The eyepatch-wearing Morty just watched him, his single visible eye sharp and unrelenting, while Morty tried to figure out if this was the part where he got experimented on, or worse. He had no idea; no one sent him the script for that scene.

“Been scratching yourself?” He seemed bored.

Uh?

Morty glanced down at his arms. Oh. Yeah. He’d been scratching himself a lot lately. Well, maybe not a lot, definitely not a bit, but just enough. Stress, nerves, probably a little bit of everything. The whitesmoke to slightly red marks on his skin were proof. “Uh… yeah, I guess,” he mumbled.

For whatever reason, Evil Morty decided it would be a good idea to inspect him like a piece of meat at a butcher shop. It was weird, but he kinda understood why; maybe it was less to see if the Mortys were okay, and more to see if they weren’t hiding something inside.

The other Morty shrugged. “Figures.”

His inspection continued. Evil Morty ran his hands over Morty’s arms, his neck, even the back of his head, as if he were, unsurprisingly, searching for something. It gave him the chills. It wasn’t until Morty, desperate for a distraction of any kind, spoke up that the silence broke.

“So, uh… w-what do you do for a living?” he’d asked, his voice cracking slightly. Oops. He tried to laugh it off, but it sounded pathetic even to him. “I mean, like, b-besides… y’know, checking us for uh, something or whatever. And uh…”

Evil Morty paused, his eyebrow raising slightly. For a moment, Morty thought he’d get an answer, but the other Morty just went back to work, silent as ever. Nope, no answer for him.

“Okay, cool, n-no big deal,” Morty muttered; his nerves were fraying at this point. He tried again. “But how’d you even get here? Like, you’re a Morty, right? They just… gave you this job? You’re evil or something? L-like how evil on a scale of 0 to 10?”

No reply. Just the faint scratch of a pen on paper as Evil Morty jotted something down on his clipboard. This was going nowhere; why was he even making so many questions? This could’ve been very well the person who took you here, who ruined your life, Morty thought. It’s not like he could just Stockholm syndrome his way out of this.

Finally, out of sheer desperation, Morty blurted, “Do you… uh, do you even like being a Morty?”

That stopped him. For the first time, Evil Morty looked up, meeting his gaze with an intensity that made his stomach twist. “I wish I was a Morty,” he said flatly, his voice low and calm. “Instead, I just look like one. But I’m more like him…”

It took a moment for the words to sink in. Morty had chuckled awkwardly — he had no idea what he actually meant, and assumed “him” was referring to their father, Jerry. Well, his Jerry — it’s not like they came from the same womb. “H-ha, yeah, I get that. My Jerry’s not so bad, though. Kinda useless, but harmless, see? What about you? Anyone in your family that bad?”

The room went quiet again. Eyepatch Morty didn’t reply — and he likely never would. Giving up, Morty just sat uncomfortably on the examination table, wishing he hadn’t asked. A minute or two later, Evil Morty finally turned away, scribbling something on his clipboard. “We’re done here,” he said curtly, motioning for a nearby guard to escort him out. That was their only face-to-face encounter.

At least until, well, they met again…

Now, sitting in the bar with the dossier spread out in front of him, the memory burned in his mind. I wish I was a Morty. The words echoed over and over. At the time, they hadn’t made any sense. Now, they terrified him. Because that Morty had meant every word. He wasn’t like other Mortys, yes, he never had been. Maybe once, but that was in the past.

Morty slammed the dossier shut, his hands trembling. He felt sick. All this time, he’d thought he was helping someone good, someone who could make a difference for Mortys everywhere. Instead, he’d been aiding a monster. He’d enabled him.

And now that monster was about to become president.

Staring back at the dossier, he noticed there was something glued to the back of it. A smaller envelope — he didn’t even need to open it to see what it had inside: a gun. Did that Rick want him to-

Oh. That.

What a beautiful day.

It was a crisp morning on the Citadel, and Mailman Rick had planned to spend it doing absolutely nothing. That was the beauty of his day off — no packages, no Mortys whining about deliveries, and thankfully no Ricks yelling at him for “misplacing” their experimental interdimensional garbage. Just peace, quiet, and maybe a nice nap on the couch. Amen.

But no. The elections were today. Which meant he had to drag himself out of his house and participate in what arguably was the dumbest process in the multiverse. Ugh.

Mailman Rick sighed as he stared into his half-empty mug of coffee. “Why couldn’t we just go full dictatorship? Save everyone the trouble," he muttered to himself. It didn’t take him long to realize that, technically, they already did — it was that way under the Council of Ricks, which still ran things. And now, with a new President soon, he couldn’t help but laugh. A new President and the Council of Ricks squabbling for power like two children. Can you imagine? Who would kill each other first?

He grimaced at the thought. Unless one of ‘em gives me a promotion, I couldn’t care less.

Rick scratched his unshaven face and slumped deeper into his chair. Politics was a joke. Lenin once told him — personally, by the way, via a phone call (a long story he loved to tell but no one ever wanted to hear) — that there were “decades where nothing happens and weeks where decades happen.” Sure, no one may have listened to him in their dimension, but he was damn right. Citadel elections were the latter: chaos, scandals, and many, many, many promises no one cared about. Post-election Citadel life, though? Indeed, decades where nothing happens. Bo-ring.

The mailman finished his coffee, grumbling as he went through his morning routine. He fried some eggs, yelled at one of his neighbors for leaving their trash in front of his door again, and finally threw on his signature postal jacket — because even on his day off, he couldn’t be bothered to wear anything else. After all, it was comfortable, and who was he trying to impress?

He ordered a cab to take him to his designated voting center, deep in the heart of the Citadel’s Financial District. Financirick. God, what an awful name. He groaned at the thought. Couldn’t they come up with something less forced? Whatever. Still, he counted himself lucky he wasn’t stuck voting in Mortytown. The place had only one polling station, meaning it was practically a war zone, swarming with Mortys and packed tighter than an orgy. No thanks. He didn’t even want to imagine what that would look like.

In the car, he recalled he hadn’t seen his own Morty at home. Eh. He was probably hanging out with that other buddy of his, some guy also named Morty. They talked a lot about the election or whatever candidate they were rooting for, but Rick really didn’t care about that. Couldn’t they talk about something more engaging — like jazz? Everyone loves jazz. (Jazz fusion was very popular in his dimension, too.) He liked his Morty, but at times he felt he was too constrained, sticking with other Mortys instead of seeing the wonders of the external world (although arguably ignoring the Citadel and everything within it was much, much more appealing).

And this friend of his, by now, must’ve been madly in love with that kid. Or anyone else, really — Morty got so attached to every other Morty he could find that he’d probably fused with his friend or something. Depending on the Morty, this could be bad — he didn’t want to wake up to 10 police officers on his door and have to tell Morty that, maybe, just maybe, he tested positive for STDs. All of them.

Syphilis, chlamydia, gonorrhea, hepatitis… A, B and C. Uh… what was the rest again? Realizing he was being too out of his mind, he dropped that idea.

By the time he arrived at the Financirick polling station, the streets were already bustling with Ricks and a few Mortys. Inside, the place was humming with the annoying buzz of voters — upper-class voters, too, who would willingly vote for a Hitler Rick if they had the chance. In fact, they’d done it so much already that a Hitler Rick now would be moderate by comparison. They’d be unemployed in this economy! Rick shuffled through the line, ignoring the cheery Morty volunteers handing out "I Voted!" stickers like they were worth something. Losers. When he finally reached the voting machine, his patience was already running thin.

“Alright,” he muttered, scrolling through the holographic ballot. “Let’s just get this over with.”

He smirked to himself as he hit the “write-in” option. If he had to vote, he was gonna do it his way. Screw Candidate Morty, screw the other Ricks. He was putting himself down as the ultimate protest vote. President Mailman Rick. Yeah, that’s got a ring to it. He’d do much better than these fuckers, too…

…except, as he hit “confirm,” the screen flashed: THANK YOU FOR YOUR VOTE FOR CANDIDATE MORTY.

“Huh. What the-?” Rick blinked in disbelief. “I didn’t vote for that smug little bastard!” And it was true; he’d never vote for a candidate that was 5’3”. He jabbed the screen again, trying to go back. It just beeped at him. THANK YOU FOR YOUR VOTE FOR CANDIDATE MORTY.

“Oh, come on!” He tapped furiously, smashing the “write-in” button again and typing his name. Nothing changed. He hit confirm and the machine displayed the same smug message: THANK YOU FOR YOUR VOTE FOR CANDIDATE MORTY.

They’ve gotta be joking with him. He was Simpsons’d. He was fucking Treehouse of Horror’d. Again.

“Son of a—!” Rick kicked the machine, causing it to wobble dangerously. A Morty volunteer scurried over to him, visibly alarmed. “Uh, sir? Is there a problem?”

“Yeah, there’s a problem!” Growling, Rick continued jabbing his finger at the screen. “This stupid piece of crap won’t let me vote for myself! I thought this was a free country! W-W-where’s the pen and paper?”

“Uh… we don’t have that option, sir,” the Morty stammered. “It’s all digital now.”

“Then find me a voting machine that works or I will be wrecking all this shit.”

It took a minute or two, but it worked. They didn’t get him a voting machine, but they got him a pen and paper. It would suffice. He now had one vote!

With that done, he slapped his hands together, brushing off some imaginary pixie dust, and stormed out of the polling station. He was indeed done here. As he pushed through the doors, he couldn’t help but smirk. One vote for me, he thought. That’s one more than any of those clowns deserve.

Satisfied, he flagged down another cab and hopped in, ready to spend the rest of his day off the way he’d planned: doing absolutely nothing.

Not too far from where Mailman Rick voted, a certain Candidate Morty also cast his vote.

Taking a small break in a café, he was relieved. The campaign was virtually over at this point, and although polls hadn’t closed yet, he knew that he had essentially won. He would win, there was no way he wouldn’t.

That didn’t stop a cluster of Mortys (but also a few Ricks) from gathering and cheering him on as he left the place. He knew well how this worked; smiled for some pictures, made small talk, posed for other photos, shook many hands, yadda yadda yadda.

Among the many people in there, he saw one that he recognized very well — his former campaign manager, standing near the edge of the crowd. The man was pale, trembling, his eyes darting nervously as if weighing whether he should even be here. To be fair, it’s not like he shouldn’t have been, erm, there. He may have fired the man yesterday, despite everything, but was glad he was still sticking out to support him. He just hoped he was alright.

“Hey, buddy!” he called out, striding toward him. “You look like you’ve had a rough couple of days. You okay? Got a new job yet?”

The former manager froze, his heart pounding in his chest. He couldn’t breathe. Couldn’t think. He really, really didn’t want to do this. Morty always thought of the other Morty as a friend; they worked together and, if he lost, they’d fall together. In fact, he always thought their friendship would come out on top, regardless of what would happen to them. If he lost, they’d have a lot of free time in their hands to do virtually anything. If he won, maybe he could’ve helped him in the Presidency or something? As an advisor or whatever. And if not, well, at least he could be happy knowing he was one of the people who voted for him.

Oh boy. How innocent he was. If only he hadn’t been clearly lied to… it hurt.

He couldn’t stop his hand from slipping into his jacket and pulling out the gun.

The rest was history.

Mailman Rick’s head pounded as he was jolted awake by the sound of frantic banging at his front door. It was early morning, too. What did he do yesterday again? Uhhh. He forgot. Groaning, he swung his legs out of bed, cursing under his breath as he shuffled toward the noise.

His first thought was that it might be one of those idiot delivery Ricks, here to drop off another package that wasn’t his. Again. He swore, if one more of those morons confused his apartment with Storick Street, he was going to-

BANG BANG BANG.

“H-hang on! I’m coming!”

He yanked the door open to find a shy-looking Cop Rick standing there, shifting uncomfortably on the porch. The cop gave him an awkward nod, holding up his badge with one hand while the other toyed nervously with his belt.

“Uh, morning sir,” Cop Rick said, stepping inside uninvited. “We need to talk.”

Rick rubbed his temples, still half-asleep and hungover from last night’s drinking spree — which, well, totally didn’t happen. He didn’t need to tell this cop about it, at least not now. He didn’t expect the guy to care either way, considering at least 11 in every 10 Ricks did it. “About what?”

“Your Morty.”

That got Rick’s attention. His Morty? What the hell was this about? “What’d he do now?” he asked, annoyed. “I could’ve sworn he was… uh… here. He is, right?”

Cop Rick didn’t answer immediately. Instead, he motioned toward the kitchen. “Mind if we sit?”

Narrowing his eyes, Rick just shrugged. “Fine. Whatever.” He led Cop Rick to the kitchen table and slumped into a chair, gesturing toward the coffee maker. “Make it quick. My head feels like it got hit by something, and I don’t wanna know what ‘something’ means.”

“There was an assassination attempt on a Morty last night.”

Rick blinked, the words taking a second to register. “Uh…. who? There are like… uh… a billion Mortys here, probably. I didn’t kill him, right?”

It was true — sure, a Morty being (almost) assassinated was bad, but what could he do? It’s not like he had control over it. In fact, he probably wouldn’t even know it happened unless it was a Morty he was particularly close to. There weren’t many that fit these criteria. If it was a Rick though, he could pinpoint at least a hundred of them, including half a dozen that probably needed to die anyway.

“Morty E-495. You might know him as Candidate Morty. He was running for President of the Citadel, as you might’ve heard, and also won the election last night,” Cop Rick clarified.

Rick’s jaw dropped. “Wait, that punkass bitch won? W-when the hell did that happen?”

“What? You didn’t know?” he raised both eyebrows. “The election was yesterday-”

“No- yes- well, yeah I know, I voted too. But I was drunk and passed out all night,” Rick said defensively. “I couldn’t care less who wins, I-I’m not that kind of guy interested in elections and stuff. But what the hell does this have to do with my Morty?”

Cop Rick sighed, pulling a small notebook from his pocket. “Well, see, the Morty who pulled the trigger… witnesses identified him as your Morty. We’re gonna need to ask you some questions.”

Rick sank back into his chair. Unbelievable. He had no idea what was going on, but he realized his morning had just gone straight to hell.
