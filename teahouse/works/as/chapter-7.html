Chapter Summary

Two familiar Mortys encounter themselves once again.

Morty almost didn’t remember how he ended up there.

He woke up in a white room, and the harsh fluorescent lights above him were making his head pound. Funny, he thought — he half-expected them to switch to the more efficient LED bulbs already. His entire body ached, and as he groggily shifted his position, it didn’t take him long to notice the bruises spreading across his arms and legs, purple and yellow splotches blooming just about everywhere. Aw, jeez.

It was all so fast, so sudden — he barely had time to react. What did he do again? Hmm… oh yeah.

He shot him.

Unbelievable, almost — he actually did it. He just couldn’t remember how he succeeded. Did he actually succeed? This was something he didn’t know. He definitely got shot, he knows that — but that’s it. He had no clue how long he’s been out, and judging by the way his body feels, he’s probably been like this for a while. In fact, he had no clue what “a while” could translate to.

His vision swam as he tried his best to sit up. Being in a room that was whiter than the color white almost made it seem like it was spinning around him. Somewhat dizzy and disoriented, he leaned back against the wall, trying to steady himself. That’s when the smell hit him.

Oh, God…

He stinks. He really, really stinks. The sour stench of sweat clings to his body, mixing with something metallic—blood. It wouldn’t take a fool to notice his clothes — the same clothes he wore on the night of his own firing; he didn’t even have time to change them — were stained with dark patches of what only could be blood streaked across his shirt and pants, mixed with a few smears of dirt. Morty winced, wiping his hands on his pants, only to realize it didn’t help. Ironically, after many days of insisting Morty E-495- erm, that Evil Morty needed to desperately cleanse himself, now he was the one who needed a bath. Just fucking great.

Well, he was all jumbled now. Morty felt this numbness that he couldn’t properly describe — and of course, there was also the elephant in the room. Did he succeed? Did he fail? Does it even matter? He didn’t know. What he did know is that he hated himself — for succeeding, for failing, for even trying. He didn’t want to do it. But he also had to do it.

But to be fair, it wasn’t just about Evil Morty. Not really. That shot had been about everything — just about everything Morty had bottled up over time, everything he’d pretended wasn’t eating away at him. Years of frustration at home and in school, primarily, made him feel like he was a sidekick in his own life.

Sure, he didn’t hate his life or his family. He loved them, and they loved him, he hoped. But the littlest things that built up over time came back right in the moments he needed them — or felt like he needed — the most. And part of this he blamed on his own Rick.

Technically, they’d switched families twice by now. Maybe thrice, he honestly couldn’t recall. And yet, he just got accustomed to it. At times, Morty feared he had already been replaced by a previous Rick, and just couldn’t remember it. At times, he feared he’d already been replaced back at Earth. The fact no one came to rescue him was more than enough. Maybe his Rick wasn’t a thing anymore. Maybe his family wasn’t, either. Who did he have by his side?

Mailman Rick, I guess. Rick was more of a friend than a grandpa to him at this point — he just couldn’t see it otherwise. He already had a Morty of his own in his dimension; as nice as it was, he’d leave him in the first opportunity he’d get just to go back to his family. Once he found out about what happened… he’d never want to look at his face again.

And there would be no one else left.

When Evil Morty came along, promising change, Morty had dared to hope for something better. And he thought things would be alright — all the daydreams he had about them hanging out, doing something, changing the world, and so on and so on. Yet all that hope soured into something darker when he saw the truth. That other Morty wasn’t a savior. He was just another version of the same cycle Morty had been trapped in his whole life.

So he did it. He pulled the trigger.

In his dimension, the BAFTA-award-winning hit game Big Rigs: Over the Road Racing would’ve sent him a “CONGRATS! YOU’RE WINNER!” message after his incredible stunt. He’d be hailed as a hero, would’ve gone back to his home, to his family, to his Rick, and they would’ve all had a good day out at Blips and Chitz. Yet there was no winning message.

His head fell back against the wall with a soft thud. Jesus. It’s really over for me. His throat feels dry, so maybe there was a glass of water somewhere in the room. He didn’t even know what would happen next. Well, they definitely found him, but what now? It’s not like they were gonna let him there forever, right? Right?

Morty closed his eyes, letting the silence of the room press in on him. For the first time in what feels like forever, he whispered, “What the hell am I even doing?”

No one answered.

Three hours later, Morty stirred awake. He wasn’t sure if he’d actually slept or just drifted in and out of consciousness; his head still throbbed, his body ached, and his mind felt like it had been put through a blender. It didn’t seem like a bad alternative considering his current state. At least he wouldn’t have to feel anything else. He wiped at his face with his filthy sleeve, trying to shake off the fog in his brain when he noticed something — a figure standing on the far side of the room.

Evil Morty.

Morty froze. His breath caught in his throat as he sat up against the wall. No, no, no. There he was, alive, standing there in that stupid slate vest and trousers like nothing had happened. He might’ve been shot, but he looked like he’d just gotten dressed for a special occasion. Did he even get the show right? There was no way he misremembered it. No way.

The only other figure in the room, Evil Morty gestured toward the center of the room. Morty followed his hand and saw a white chair sitting there — as if it had been there all along. Maybe it had; he genuinely could not remember. His stomach twisted into a knot.

For Evil Morty, it wasn’t easy to be there, either.

This guy — this Morty — was just like everyone else. Not so much mentally, a bit more physically, but essentially a Morty. Childish, weak, subordinate to one of many infinite Ricks… it was funny to think that, for a while, he thought otherwise. He really thought this Morty was different from the others — well, the others he’d met, at least — because he seemed more… willing to be different, something like that. He didn’t want to do the same half a dozen things every Morty did, he wanted to do more.

What “more” meant for him might’ve been a mystery to him, but he, for a genuine moment, saw him as a friend.

Well, at least before he shot him.

Morty swallowed hard. His limbs felt like lead as he pushed himself off the ground and stumbled toward the chair. He sat down slowly, trying to see what exactly he could do or say next. He was scared. And as Evil Morty stepped closer, his gaze rather sharp and unrelenting, Morty couldn’t hold it together anymore.

He broke down.

Tears streamed down his face as he choked on sobs, his words tumbling out in jagged fragments. “Y-you’re alive,” he stammered, his voice cracking. “I… you… I didn’t… I thought…” He buried his face in his hands, shaking as he tried to piece together a sentence. He couldn’t.

He failed. He hated that he failed. Again. Morty could imagine himself begging for his life — the same life he at times despised, the same life he once wanted to desperately end. Things had been hard.

Evil Morty didn’t budge. He just stood there, with his arms crossed, watching Morty with that same cold intensity. He didn’t know if he felt bad for him — or just pity — but it was all he could think of. After a moment, the other Morty unbuttoned the top of his vest and pulled it aside, revealing a neatly bandaged wound on his chest.

“Private Citadel hospitals,” he said, his voice dripping with disdain. Morty wasn’t sure which of these three words he hated the most, but he assumed it definitely wasn’t the third one. “So efficient, so expensive… the kind of care a Morty only gets if he’s useful to the system. They’ve learned nothing from America, right?” He buttoned his vest back up. “You’re just like the rest of them. Just like the Ricks. You don’t care about Mortys.”

The words hit Morty like a slap to the face. He stared at Evil Morty, his eyes wide and bloodshot. “T-that’s not true!” he shouted. He could almost sense his voice cracking, too. “I trusted you! I-I put everything into you! I thought you—”

“You thought what?”

This was getting pathetic, Evil Morty thought. He’d used that word to describe so many events in his life over the last months that his usage of the word itself was, ironically, pathetic. “You thought I was your savior? Your hero? You thought I’d make everything better?” He stepped closer, his face only inches from Morty’s. “Don’t kid yourself. You didn’t trust me, you never did. Hell, you didn’t even know me. You just wanted someone to fix your pathetic little life for you, right?”

Morty flinched, tears streaming down his face. “That’s not fair,” he muttered. And it wasn’t; Evil Morty was trying to play into his head. The worst part is that it part of it was working. “I didn’t… I just wanted… I just wanted to have a friend. Someone who actually cared. Someone who…” He trailed off, his chest heaving as he tried to catch his breath.

Evil Morty’s expression didn’t change, but something flickered in his eye — something Morty couldn’t quite place. The light in the room, maybe?

“I didn’t hate you,” Morty said, his voice breaking. “Not even after everything. I just… I just hoped that for once, that you- that we felt like Mortys. Or-or at least just like normal people. Like we weren’t just… just tools for someone else’s bullshit.”

It was true. He loved Mailman Rick, he loved (and hated, at times) his new Morty acquaintances in the campaign, he loved meeting the so many Morty voters who trusted their candidate, but also trusted the people working with him — hence him and many others. It’s like he was being loved by himself — fair, considering how many Mortys that place had — and it was such a warm feeling that he wouldn’t easily forget.

“Y-you… you were one of my only friends… you made me feel welcomed, I-I-I don’t know, loved? And when I f-found out you were different, I just, ugh, got reminded… of my failures… a-and…”

Evil Morty opened his mouth as if to say something, but quickly closed it. His lips pressed into a thin line as he watched Morty break down completely, sobbing uncontrollably, his words dissolving into incoherent babble.

If he didn’t feel bad for the man during their conversation, he now had.

For several minutes, the only sound in the room was Morty’s sobs. He just stood silently and let his arms hanging at his sides. This was disconcerting. When Morty finally stopped after what seemed like ages but was only 3 minutes, his chest hitching as he stared at the floor, Evil Morty stepped forward.

To Morty’s surprise, the other Morty just placed a hand on his head — a small, almost hesitant gesture. He didn’t say anything else. He just patted Morty’s head once, maybe twice — though a few seconds longer than the first time — before stepping back and gesturing toward the door.

When Morty looked up, his tear-streaked face full of confusion, he didn’t get it. I’m alive, he was. But why? “Wait,” he blurted, though his voice was still slightly hoarse. “I-”

“Get out.”

Morty blinked, his mouth opening to protest, but the look in Evil Morty’s eye stopped him cold. This guy was serious; he’d probably have never been so serious in his life before. He stood up shakily, his legs barely holding him, and made his way toward the door. Despite this, he hesitated at the threshold, glancing back one last time.

“Sorry-”

“Leave!” Evil Morty shouted, his voice echoing through the room.

And so Morty flinched and stumbled out, the door slamming shut behind him. He wasn’t even sure where he was headed to this time.

How he got home was beyond him.

The last thing he recalled after leaving the station — a police station? Downtown? How had he even gotten there? — was the air feeling cold against his bruised skin and the dull hum of distant traffic. There was a carefully placed cab waiting at the curb, its door open like it had been summoned just for him. It felt off.

Maybe Evil Morty was never going to kill him after all. Or maybe this cab wasn’t even meant for him? He didn’t have time to linger on the thought. Morty noticed a few passersby on the sidewalk, staring at him with obviously confused expressions. His clothes were still somewhat bloody, his face was still bruised and puffy, and he smelled like he spent one year living in the sewers. Morty ducked his head and scurried into the cab as fast as his somewhat sore legs could carry him.

The taxi was one of those fancy automated vehicles — so no driver, no awkward conversations, and just silence and the faint hum of the engine. Maybe it was better than nothing, but at least they could’ve turned on the radio… and so Morty leaned back in the seat. He didn’t know where the cab was taking him. Home, he guessed. He hoped.

Oh God. What am I gonna tell Rick when I get there?

That would’ve been an issue. How would he tell his Rick what happened? Yeah, I just shot the guy running for President. Hm, no. Yeah, I just shot my ex-best friend. No…

The ride took about 20 minutes. By the time the cab turned into the dimly lit streets of Mortytown, he noticed something unusual: the streets were practically empty. Did something happen? Sure, it must’ve been around… uh… six, seven in the morning? But that was no excuse for the streets to be practically deserted. It looked like a cardboard city, to be honest. Eerily silent. He even wondered if everyone else just moved on back to their original dimensions and he was one of the few who — involuntarily — stayed. Would be funny, but also quite tragic.

When the cab pulled up to his apartment, Morty stepped out cautiously. The place also seemed empty, too. He trudged up the stairs to his apartment door, half-expecting it to be locked or barricaded or something. But when he reached it, the door swung open with ease. It was unlocked!

And inside, the apartment was quiet. Too quiet.

“Rick?”

No answer. In fact, there was no sign of Rick anywhere, inside or outside their apartment. This by itself wasn’t an issue — he wasn’t called Mailman Rick for no reason! — but his lack of presence there gave him the chills. He thought something happened to him. This feeling only got worse when he noticed the documents that had been sent to him by that Rick investigator vanished. They weren’t in his room, weren’t on the couch… nothing. Nada. It’s like they never even existed.

“Dammit,” he muttered under his breath. “He screwed me over.”

Typical. Did he just think Evil Morty would just send him to his home and pretend nothing happened? As much as it looked like it, obviously this wasn’t the case. He knew information about him that, if revealed, could bring down his entire career. Not having any evidence complicated this; everyone else would just see him as “the weird guy who shot the President”.

What he did find was a handwritten note left on the kitchen counter. It was from Mailman Rick, so no surprise here. (He was relieved to know his handwriting was more legible than that of the average Rick’s.)

“Had to settle some things with my bosses. Don’t wanna get caught up in your mess, so I’ll be laying low for a while. Don’t expect to hear from me. Uhhh. And don’t forget to unclog the toilet. I, uh. Well. I got a bit silly. -R”

Morty crumpled the note and threw it to the ground in frustration. Motherfucker. Of course, everyone bailed on him when he needed them most. He turned to the living room, noticing the faint flicker of the TV screen. It was the only thing still on.

Fantastic. Now he’d have to pay the bills for that, too. He wandered over and collapsed on the couch. On the screen, the news anchor’s voice was calm but serious.

“…as we take you live to the inaugural address of President Morty.”

What.

Morty’s heart sank as the camera cut to what looked like a grand stage. It vaguely reminded him of the convention center the last debates were held in. And there he was, Evil Morty, standing behind the podium, wearing the same suit he was wearing just hours ago. The crowd roared as he raised a hand in acknowledgment before stepping up to the microphone.

Oh yeah, he thought. He won. He couldn’t believe it took him so long to admit this, but it was true. He won. And somehow bailed him out of jail, too. But for this to have happened, he had to win. And it’s what he did.

He just stared at the screen, unable to look away. He should’ve turned it off. In fact, he should’ve gone to shower — something he desperately needed —, to sleep, to do anything else. But he didn’t.

“Today marks the beginning of something new. Something Mortys everywhere have dreamed of but never thought possible…”

The crowd cheered wildly, but Evil Morty held up a hand to quiet them.

“…for too long, Mortys have been overlooked. Used, abused, treated as less than. But today, I stand before you as proof that we are more: that we can be more. You know, I didn’t get here alone. Along the way, I had many allies, many friends, just like you! Some of them aren’t here to see this moment, but I carry them with me in my heart. This victory is as much theirs as it is mine.”

Now he wants to talk about having friends. Ridiculous. He felt Evil Morty was staring right at him through the screen. Which was true, since he was staring directly at the camera — but maybe this comment wasn’t aimed at him?

“I promise to be a President for all Mortys… and for all Ricks… even for those who didn’t believe in me. Even for those who doubted me… because that’s what it means to lead. To represent, that is. To break the glass ceiling and shatter expectations…”

The crowd erupted into cheers again, chanting his name. Maybe that explained why everyone wasn’t in town; they were at his inauguration. They loved him.

“This is just the beginning,” he said, smiling a bit. His voice almost a whisper now, carried only by the microphone. “The future… our future… starts now.”

And the screen cut back to the cheering crowd, one of the many cameras panning over the sea of faces. That was a… rather short speech. That didn’t stop Morty from sitting frozen and gripping his knees.

What have we done…?

Sometime around 2 a.m., Evil Morty jolted awake in his luxurious new presidential suite.

Okay, it wasn’t much of a presidential suite, since the Citadel didn’t really have a President before; it was more of a refurbished suite for one of the many members of the Council of Ricks. Which one of them didn’t really matter, he forgot who it was.

Sweat was soaking the collar of his crisp, perfectly-pressed purple pajamas. He was kinda surprised to know there was one that fit him perfectly! The room was fairly dark except for the faint glow of city lights seeping through the floor-to-ceiling windows. The Citadel was busy even at night.

For a moment, he lay there, staring at the ornate ceiling, trying to shake the haze of whatever had clawed at him in his sleep. There was something about a zebra for sure, but he couldn’t remember what exactly. Unfortunately, the feeling in his stomach was impossible to ignore. It churned quite violently. This was bad.

He bolted upright and stumbled toward the bathroom, gripping the marble walls for support. The suite itself was luxurious enough, even though it seemed designed to mock him: gold trim on everything, a bed big enough to drown in, and a bathroom with a mirror that stretched across an entire wall. The Rick who once lived here was one hell of a guy. But right now, he couldn’t care less.

Evil Morty barely made it to the toilet before the contents of his stomach came up. Well, there goes my salad…

When he finally stopped, he slumped against the cold porcelain, his forehead slick with sweat. For a moment he even thought he was gonna die, before realizing he knew how that felt already. Ugh. His hand trembled as he reached up to wipe his mouth.

“Great,” he muttered. “Just what I needed.”

His limbs felt distant, disconnected; it was a strange feeling. Forcing himself to stand, he shuffled over to the sink. He turned on the faucet and splashed some much-needed cold water onto his face. It didn’t help much. He looked up, meeting his own reflection in the mirror.

And for a moment, he just stared at himself.

He’d done it. He’d won. He was the President now. The first Morty to ever hold the position. He didn’t only shatter the so-called glass ceiling that he mentioned — he obliterated it.

Yet he still felt something weird.

Not the slight pain in his chest from the patched-up bullet wound, no no. Maybe it was some of his implants? Hm, probably not. Maybe it was because he had killed over half of the Council of Ricks just a few hours earlier? Probably. But maybe it was the weight of the next step — the knowledge that his real work had only just begun.

He gripped the edge of the sink tightly, his knuckles turning white. He tried to shake the feeling off, to convince himself it didn’t matter. But his own reflection betrayed him — there was a bit of sadness there, even if he hated admitting it.

Sometimes he wished he just ran away from everything, moved on to a world with a fairer Rick. And then he realized none of them cared for him. His own Rick didn’t! And despite everything he did, he couldn’t just get rid of him. It’s not that he didn’t want to, it’s just that… he couldn’t bring himself to end the man personally. Rick was his grandfather, after all. He couldn’t just… do it. Not by himself. The rest, well, was history.

“This is what you wanted,” he whispered to himself. “This is what you worked for.”

And this was true — it’s not like he ran an entire presidential campaign for nothing. He did what Al Gore couldn’t — at least in his dimension. 5, 10, 100, 500 ballots? Yup, he got them. He got more than necessary to win, yet just enough to make it seem narrow. He won. He worked hard and got graciously awarded for it. People loved him; he was their gentleman. Yet there was only so much a gentleman could stand. His words felt hollow.

Evil Morty sighed.

He turned away, leaving the bathroom. Although he still felt somewhat ill, he realized he just needed some rest. Maybe it’d help a bit. His footsteps echoed in the suite, which were met by silence; there was no one at night he could chat to about his own feelings. If only someone could answer his phone, then maybe…

His bed was too large, too soft, too much like quicksand. As he climbed back into bed — it almost swallowed him —, he couldn’t shake one of his many thoughts.

What I just witnessed could’ve been the end of a particularly terrifying nightmare.

He pulled the covers up to his chest and stared at the ceiling, his eye wide open. He felt like a child.

It isn’t. It’s the beginning.
