Chapter Summary

The beginning of everything. Or well, almost.

Chapter Notes

I'm not sure why this is a thing, but I guess it'll do. When writing this, I unintentionally realized that I made Cartman sound a lot like a somewhat more coherent Donald Trump. Well, uh. I guess that happens.

It was a beautiful day.

The afternoon sun filtered through the blinds of Butters’ bedroom, and cast a few stripes across his homework-cluttered desk. At seventeen, he’d grown a few inches taller and his voice had finally settled into something that didn’t crack every other word, but he still maintained that same nervous energy that had defined him since childhood. (Well, at least that’s what his parents’ friends said about him.) He was hunched over his algebra textbook, pencil tapping anxiously against the paper as he worked through a particularly troublesome equation. He wasn’t too good at them.

It had been many years since his last room renovation, and it didn’t look particularly exciting, to be fair. If anything, ever since he’d grown up, it felt more and more bland. Given how much time he spent inside it, perhaps this needed to change soon. Yet the motivation for it never happened — and so he went back to the things he actually liked doing. Or, well, had to do.

The peaceful silence he was in was rapidly shattered, however, by the sound of heavy footsteps thundering up the stairs, followed by what could only be described as, erm, a funny door-opening attempt. If anything, that was an understatement.

That door didn’t just open — in fact, it pretty much exploded off its hinges, and its top hinge snapped clean off with a metallic ping that sent the door careening wildly to one side. Hell, it swung with such momentum that it should have taken Butters’ head clean off, but by some miracle of physics and rather poor carpentry, it missed him by mere inches before slamming into the wall behind him with a crash that shook the entire room. Wow.

“BUTTERS!”

Of course, the person who turned the handle (or tried to) was his beloved friend, Eric Cartman.

Shit.

Butters jerked back in his chair, and his pencil flew from his hand as his heart hammered against his ribs. “Jesus Christ!” he yelped, spinning around to see Cartman standing in, well, what used to be his doorway, looking mildly surprised at the destruction he’d just caused.

“Huh,” Cartman said. For a moment, he briefly examined the dangling door. “Well, that’s weird.” He shrugged and stepped over the door’s lower corner, which was now scraping against the floor. “Whatever, Butters, we’ve got more important things to worry about.”

At eighteen, Cartman had grown into his bulk rather than out of it, though there was no arguing that he was, undeniably, fat. (Not that he meant that in a bad way, no no…) His face had lengthened slightly, giving him a more adult appearance (for good or worse), but his eyes still held that very same glint that had gotten half the town into trouble over the years. It was terrible. He was wearing what appeared to be an expensive-looking jacket — it was leather, with way too many zippers —, and some brown beanie that he probably found buried somewhere in his closet. Probably.

“Cartman, what the hell?” Butters stammered, still trying to process the fact that his door was now hanging at a forty-five-degree angle. “My dad’s gonna kill me! How am I supposed to explain this?”

“Dude, forget about the door,” he waved dismissively. Instead, he grabbed his phone from his pocket and showed it to Butters. His screen showed what looked like a banking app with zeros. A lot of zeroes. An unbelievable amount of zeros. He’d never seen this many before. What the fuck?

“Check this out, Butters. You’re looking at a future millionaire.”

Okay, perhaps he exaggerated a bit. There was a normal amount of zeros there. But not for someone like Cartman, who to the best of his knowledge hadn’t done anything to even obtain such money in a short time. Unless, well…

Butters squinted at the screen. “Holy h-h- wait, what? Eric, where did you get all that money?”

“Well, I can neither confirm nor deny my methods,” he grinned, “but let’s just say I’ve been making some very, uh, smart investments in the cryptocurrency market. Very smart, unbelievably smart.” Of course, the way he said it suggested he had absolutely no idea what cryptocurrency actually was. He probably didn’t.

“That doesn’t sound legal—”

“Butters!” Cartman interrupted, grabbing him by the shoulders with surprising intensity. “Forget about legality for one goddamn minute and listen to me. I’m about to let you in on the opportunity of a lifetime. We’re going to Scotland.”

“Scotland?” he blinked rapidly. “Like, Scotland the country?”

“Uh-duh. That’s the one,” Cartman nodded enthusiastically. “And we’re going there to photograph the Loch Ness Monster.”

There was a long pause as Butters processed this information. What?

He opened his mouth, closed it, opened it again, then finally managed, “The… the what now?”

Cartman sighed. “The fucking Loch Ness Monster, Butters! Nessie! The big-ass dinosaur thing that lives in there, you know?” He began pacing around the small room, stepping carelessly over many scattered homework papers. “Think about it, man. Nobody has ever gotten a clear picture of that thing. But if we could — if we could get just one good photo —, we’d be rich. Unbelievably rich. Never will anyone be as rich as us—”

“But Cartman, people have been trying to find that thing for like, decades—”

“Exactly!” Cartman spun around, pointing at Butters triumphantly. “Point is, they were all amateurs and losers. Are you an amateur and a loser?”

“Well… no?”

“Exactly. You don’t fail, I don’t fail, we won’t fail. Plus, I’ve got a secret weapon.” He reached into his expensive jacket and pulled out what was clearly a professional-grade camera. It was the kind that nature photographers used, with a lens that looked like it could spot wildlife from several counties away.

Butters couldn’t recognize its brand at first, however. Before he could react, Cartman hurled the camera directly at him. He fumbled to catch it, and nearly dropped the expensive equipment in his panic. “Cartman, shit— you can’t just throw— this thing probably costs more than this entire room!”

“Relax, it’s insured,” he joked. “The point is, you’re coming with me to Scotland to be my photographer.”

Huh.

“Me?” Butters clutched the camera to his chest rather protectively. “B-but I don’t know anything about taking pictures of monsters! I mostly just take photos of flowers… and stuff for my mom’s garden blog!”

“Which is exactly why you’re perfect for this job,” he nodded. “You’ve got that… uh… artistic eye. That’s what it’s called, right? I dunno, fuck it. Plus, you’re the only person I know who won’t screw this up for me.”

“What about the other guys? What about Kyle and Stan? They’re your friends!”

Cartman’s expression darkened immediately. “Fuck those guys. Stan isn’t even here and I’m obviously not gonna ask his mopey pathetic boyfriend Kahl. Even if I wanted to, it wouldn’t make sense because they’d just try to talk me out of this… erm, brilliant plan.”

“Kyle’s not Stan’s boyfriend—”

“Whatever, Butters, the point is they’re dead weight. I need someone I can count on, someone who won’t question my methods or try to be all moral and shit.” He fixed Butters with what he probably thought was a persuasive stare. “That’s you, buddy. You’re my guy. My pal, my buddy, my friend, my whatever. You get it.”

Butters shifted uncomfortably, still holding the camera. Although he was still trying to process everything Cartman was telling him, there was a grain of truth to what he’d been saying. A couple of months ago, for whatever reason, his friend Stan decided he had enough of his dad’s antics, and decided to burn his prized marijuana farm.

This would’ve seemed fairly easy, if it weren’t for the fact the two of them almost died during that process. It didn’t take long for him to be sent to some facility upstate. He forgot exactly where it was. Hell, he hadn’t even called or even visited. What a good friend he was…

Naturally, that hit Kyle the hardest. Without his super best friend around, he’d become far more withdrawn and bitter, spending most of his time holed up in his room or at the library. They’d still chat from time to time, but Butters knew that it wasn’t the same as before.

Kenny was… well, uh, nobody really knew where Kenny was these days. At least he didn’t. He’d been increasingly absent from school, and when he did show up, they spoke to maybe once or twice at most. It was odd. Some people — okay, just Clyde — said he’d gotten a job at a truck stop outside of town, and others claimed he’d dropped out entirely, which didn’t always make sense. Either way, he wasn’t often around to hang out with. It sucked.

That left Cartman as pretty much the only option for social interaction, and even then, Cartman had been spending most of his time with Scott Malkinson lately. Not that Butters was jealous or anything, but it was weird seeing Cartman actually try to maintain a friendship with him. Maybe times had changed…

“I can’t just go to Scotland, Cartman,” Butters said finally. “I’m grounded, remember?”

This was true. About two days ago, he accidentally left the toilet seat lid up, an act so deadly it was enough to anger his mom but also his father. He wasn’t allowed to leave the house except for school for two weeks. And there were still twelve more days left…

“Butters, Butters, Butters,” he chuckled and shook his head. “Do I look like I care if you’re grounded or not?”

“W-well, I don’t think so.”

“See? Look, you’re thinking too small. I really, really don’t care if you’re grounded or not. But you gotta understand this is about seizing destiny by the balls and squeezing until it gives you what you want.”

“B-but that’s a really gross metaphor, Eric.”

“The point is,” he continued, ignoring his interruption, “your parents won’t even know you’re gone. We’ll be back in like, a week, tops. You can tell them you’re staying at my house for a school project or something.”

“But what if they call your mom?”

“My mom thinks I’m already in Scotland!” Cartman laughed. “I told her I won a contest. She was so proud she cried and gave me her credit card. It was very cool and epic and wholesome.”

Butters stared at him. “You lied to your own mother about winning a contest?”

“You say that like you’ve never lied before.”

“But I—”

“It’s not lying if it’s going to be true eventually,” he replied. “I thought you were smart enough to know that, Butters. Besides, she’s used to it by now.”

There was something quite depressing about that statement, but Butters pushed the thought away. It was Eric after all — what else did he expect? He looked down at the camera in his hands, then at the broken door hanging uselessly from its remaining hinge, then back at Cartman’s expectant face.

The truth was, he was desperately lonely. He didn’t like admitting it, hated even thinking it, but it was true. Because his main friend group was all over the place, he had to find other friends to talk to. Even though he knew some of these people for years, he wasn’t particularly close to them, and so found it much harder to glue himself to them.

For example, Craig and Tweek were inseparable these days, and he just couldn’t find a way to interact with them without seeming like the third wheel. Tolkien seemed like a fine choice, but he had gotten heavily involved in student government and spent most of his free time organizing fundraisers and writing policy proposals. Clyde, well, he was still good ol’ Clyde, although lately he’d been particularly insufferable, especially after getting dumped by his latest girlfriend, whoever the hell that was.

Sure, there was always Scott, but even then he had his girlfriend Sophie, and while he was nice enough, it just didn’t feel quite right. Not that he was jealous, no— it’s just that, well, he always felt vaguely uncomfortable about being someone’s second choice.

The other kids at school had their own established friend groups, and Butters had never been particularly good at inserting himself into existing social dynamics. He was friendly with most people, but not really close to anyone. The main boys had been his primary social circle for so long that he wasn’t sure how to function without them.

And here was Cartman, offering him an adventure. Sure, it was probably going to end in disaster — as did the other 572 times he was also involved — but hey, at least it would be something different. It would be much more exciting than sitting alone in his room and doing homework while listening to his parents argue about property taxes downstairs. Maybe it was a good thing…?

“I don’t know, Cartman,” he said slowly, though they both knew he was already wavering. “Scotland’s really far away, and what if we don’t find anything? What if the monster isn’t even real?”

“Then we’ll make it real,” Cartman replied. He was already losing the little patience he had with his friend. “Look, Butters, I’ve thought this through from every angle. I’ve got the money, I’ve got the equipment, I’ve got the plan. All I need is someone to document our success, and that someone is, well, you.”

“But why not Scott? Why not anyone else, why does it have to be me—”

Cartman’s expression soured slightly. “Scott’s a pussy. He said the whole thing was ‘impractical’ and ‘probably illegal’, which obviously isn’t true. Plus that bitchass girlfriend of his doesn’t want him traveling without her, which is an interesting proposal, but one I’m obviously not agreeing to.”

“So you did ask him first—”

“Blahblahblah. You’re my first choice for this specific job,” Cartman corrected smoothly. “Scott’s good for hanging out and playing video games, especially because he always loses, but this requires someone with real skill. Someone with, uh. Vision? Yeah yeah, vision. I’m a leader and I needed, uh, you know, a visionary. That’s, uh, you.“

It was obvious flattery (and a bad one, too), but Butters found himself responding to it anyway. When was the last time anyone had called him a visionary? Probably never. His parents still treated him like he was twelve, his teachers saw him as a quiet kid who did his work and didn’t cause trouble, and his former friends… well, they weren’t really around to see him as anything at all.

This is why, despite everything, a part of him still liked Eric.

“When do we leave?”

Cartman’s face lit up. He smiled. “Tomorrow night. I’ve already got the plane tickets!”

“Tomorrow?” Butters’ voice cracked slightly. “But that’s so soon! I need time to prepare, and pack, and figure out what to tell my parents—”

“Nah. Already handled,” he pulled out his phone again and showed Butters what appeared to be a very official-looking email. “You’ve been selected for a special photography exchange program with a school in Edinburgh. Congratulations, Butters, you’re about to become an international student.”

Butters examined the email more closely. It definitely looked legitimate, complete with official letterhead and everything, though he was pretty sure the signature at the bottom just said “Scotland Guy.” Couldn’t he have been more creative with that?

“This is fake, isn’t it?”

“Oh, it’s as real as it needs to be,” Cartman replied. “Your parents will buy it, and that’s all that matters.”

He was fucked, right? This would definitely be another terrible idea that would probably end with him in jail, grounded for life, or possibly eaten by an actual monster. Maybe all three at once. But another part of him — that is, the part that had been sitting alone in his room for weeks while his friends scattered to the four winds — was desperate for something, anything, to break up the monotony of his current existence.

And so he said yes.

“I guess…” he said slowly, “I guess it might be kind of cool to see Scotland.”

“That’s the spirit!” Cartman clapped him on the back hard enough to make him stumble forward. “Pack light, bring the camera, and be ready for the adventure of a lifetime. Or close to it. But we’re going to be so rich, so unbelievably famous, so cool that we’re definitely gonna be knighted by the King or whatever they have up there.”

“I don’t think Scotland has a separate king—”

“Oh shut up, we’ll figure the details later.”

Cartman headed toward the broken doorway, then paused and looked back. “Oh, and Butters? This stays between us, okay? I don’t need Kyle or anyone else trying to sabotage this operation.”

“I mean… it’s not like he’s talking to anyone these days anyway,” Butters muttered.

“Perfect. Then it’s just you and me, buddy. My buddy. My pal. My partner. My… you know what, forget it, I ran out of words to use here.”

And with that, Cartman squeezed through the angled doorway and thundered back down the stairs, leaving Butters alone with a broken door, an expensive camera, and the growing realization that he was about to do something incredibly stupid.

He looked down at the camera again, turning it over in his hands. It was a nice camera, at least. When was the last time someone had trusted him with something this expensive? When was the last time someone had needed him for something actually important?

From downstairs, he could hear his parents arguing about whether they should refinance the house. And upstairs, his room felt smaller and more suffocating than ever, especially with the door hanging at that weird angle. He really needed to look into that.

Maybe Cartman was right. Maybe it was time to seize destiny by the balls, gross as that sounded. Though perhaps not literally. Butters set the camera carefully on his desk and started planning what he was going to pack for Scotland.

And of course, how to fix that broken door.
