Chapter Summary

Despite his hesitance, Butters goes along with Cartman's plan. Their first stop: Denver.

Chapter Notes

If you told me from 10, 11 years ago that this would be my first South Park story, they would think I'm insane. That would be a good thing, though. I can finally cite the half a dozen or so characters from the early seasons that at least one person in the word is fond of!

The next day passed in a blur. Despite Butters’ own unwillingness to go along with the plan, he knew he couldn’t just leave his house and pretend nothing had happened. He couldn’t even imagine what his father would do in this case. He’d be grounded for a million years, if not more!

At dinner, he managed to present the fake Scotland exchange program email to his parents. It was ridiculous, and he didn’t expect them to even believe it, but, to his amazement, they’d bought it completely. His mother, Linda, had even gotten teary-eyed about her “little photographer” getting such an amazing opportunity, while his father, Stephen, had grumbled something about “damn liberal education programs” but ultimately signed the permission slip that Cartman had somehow procured.

His father had always been a bit of a bleeding-heart conservative, at least in some way. He recalled that, when younger, his family had been one of the few in the entire city that supported McCain. And Romney, for that matter. That changed a bit with Trump, but he’d always been the most conservative one in their family. At least Linda was beginning to see the light; she wrote in Daffy Duck last year.

By evening, Butters was exhausted from the stress and had crashed hard around eight p.m., sleeping fairly deeply until his phone alarm buzzed at 11:47 p.m. Yup, it was time. He jolted awake in the darkness, momentarily confused about why the hell he was setting alarms for midnight, before the reality of the situation came flooding back.

Oh yeah, Scotland. Right.

Moving as quietly as possible, Butters gathered the supplies he’d laid out earlier: his handheld camera, for simpler photos; the fancy one Cartman had thrown at him, now hanging around his neck; some basic clothes; toiletries; some other useful stuff; and what little money he had saved from his allowance. Everything fit into an old camping duffel bag of his. It was funny, too, because he genuinely could not remember the last time he went camping, but he was pretty sure this one was actually his dad’s, which explained a lot.

Of course, getting dressed in the dark proved trickier than expected. What a genius idea! Still, he’d planned his outfit earlier that day: a black turtleneck for warmth, dark pants that looked good enough, and a khaki photographer’s vest he’d found at a thrift store that had more pockets than any human being could reasonably need. It had an unbelievable amount of pockets. To complete his look, he also wore a black beanie. Unfortunately, it was too big for him, though, and it kept sliding down over his eyes.

With that done, the hardest part was getting out of the house without waking anyone. Although in these circumstances he would usually climb out his window (and faceplant the snowy floor), there was just no way he could do that with all the gear he was carrying. Every floorboard in that damn house seemed to creak, and the front door’s hinges chose that very moment to squeak and almost ruin everything. Somehow, though, through a combination of luck and very, very careful tiptoeing, Butters managed to slip outside into the night.

The cold hit him immediately, making him shiver so violently that his teeth chattered. October in South Park was no joke, especially at midnight. Even though he was already used to this weather, well, it wasn’t like he usually hung around in the streets at midnight. He found himself questioning his clothing choices (and also his life choices). The turtleneck wasn’t nearly as warm as he’d hoped, and the photographer’s vest was clearly not insulating anything.

Ahem.

Did he hear something? No, probably not.

AHEM.

Okay, maybe he did.

Butters spun around to see a car idling at the curb. Specifically, it was a gray 2002 Honda Civic, although Butters had no way of actually knowing that. Hey, at least the thing was running and appeared to have functional headlights, which was more than he had expected.

Of course, inside it was Cartman.

“Jesus Christ, Butters, you look like you’re about to shoot up a school,” came Cartman’s voice from inside the car. “Didn’t you have anything better to wear?”

“I do not look like a school shooter!” he hissed, hurrying toward the car while trying to keep his voice down. “I look like a professional photographer! You know, like those guys who take pictures of eagles and stuff from mountain tops!”

“Uh-huh,” Cartman said, though rather skeptically, as Butters climbed into the passenger seat. “Well, at least you’re dressed for the cold. Kind of.”

Butters was too busy marveling at the car’s interior to respond to the jab. Okay, the interior wasn’t too fancy, but its seats were quite comfy. There was also an actual CD player built into the dashboard, which was hardly revolutionary, but it was pretty neat. Most modern cars had stopped coming with these years ago.

Yet there was one question that immediately popped up in his head.

“Cartman, since when can you drive? And whose car is this?”

“I’ve been able to drive for like, six months now,” he replied as he pulled away from the curb. “You know, unlike our loser friends, I actually got my license the day I turned eighteen. And the car… uh, let’s just say I have my ways.”

“You rented it?”

“No. Yes. Erm, something like that,” Cartman said evasively. Obviously, he was not going to tell Butters all of his great, amazing, wonderful plan. No. Not yet. “T-the important thing is that it runs and it’s got good music. Okay? Good music.” He gestured toward the CD player, where something was already loaded. “Check it out.”

Butters pressed play and, after a few moments of silence, was somewhat surprised by the soulful voice that filled the car. It was Amy Winehouse, he realized after a moment — specifically this was her first album, “Frank.” He’d heard a few of her more popular songs on the radio, but never a full album, and certainly not on a CD in a car he’d just seen for the first time. But still.

“Hmm. Oh yeah, this is good. Really good. I didn’t know you listened to stuff like this.”

“Pfft. I’m a man of sophisticated tastes,” Cartman replied, though he seemed a tad pleased by Butters’ reaction. “Besides, we’ve got a long drive ahead of us. So uh. Yeah.”

They drove in comfortable silence for a while. Butters found himself relaxing despite the rather, uh, unusual circumstances. The Rocky Mountains were beautiful at this time of year, too. When younger, his parents would take him on trips to places across the state and he’d stare at those mountains for hours. Some of them looked a lot like sleeping giants, he’d tell mom. Usually, she’d agree.

“So,” Butters said eventually, “what’s the actual plan here? I mean, besides ‘go to Scotland and find a monster.’”

Cartman grinned, clearly having waited for this question for the previous 34 hours. “Finally! Okay, so uh, so first we drive to Denver — t-that’s about four hours from here. Then we catch a flight at six a.m. to London Heathrow. That’s like, an eight-hour flight or something ridiculous. Then from London, we get another flight to… what was it called again?”

“What?”

“The city?”

Butters thought for a moment. “I don’t know, Glasgow?”

“No, the other one.

“Hmm. Ohh, you mean Edinburgh.”

Cartman grinned. “Yes! Yes, Edinburgh, just like I was saying. That’s the capital of Scotland, in case you didn’t know.”

“I know what Edinburgh is, Cartman.”

“Good, because that’s where things get interesting. From Edinburgh, we take a smaller plane to Inverness. Which I think is the closest airport to Lake Ness—”

“Loch Ness.” Butters corrected.

“What?”

“It’s Loch Ness, not Lake Ness.”

“Whatever. Same difference.”

”It’s actually not. ‘Loch’ is Scottish for—”

”Butters, I swear to God, if you don’t shut up about Scottish geography, I will turn this car around.”

He fell silent.

After a while, Cartman spoke again. “Anyway, uh, where was I? Oh yeah. Once we get there, we’ll set up camp in a five-star chalet near the lake—”

“Loch,” Butters mumbled.

“—near the FUCKING LOCH,” Cartman emphasized with a glare, “and stake it out. According to my research, the best time to spot Nessie is at dawn or dusk, when the light is low. That’s where you come in with your photography skills. Got it?”

Butters absorbed this information, trying to calculate the logistics in his head. “Uh… w-well… it’s a lot of flights, Eric. A-aand… Jesus, how much is this all costing?”

“Don’t worry about money,” he shrugged. “I told you, I’ve got it covered. Besides, think of it as an investment. Once we get that picture of Nessie, we’ll make millions selling it to National Geographic. Or Discovery Channel. Or whoever pays the most, fuck it, I’m selling it to the highest bidder.”

“But what if there’s no monster? What if it’s just a hoax, or—”

“Butters!” Cartman interrupted him. He didn’t look too happy. “Let me tell you something about success. Success isn’t for people who sit around asking ‘what if?’; success is for people who see an opportunity and grab it. At least I think that’s what JFK said, I forgot the full quote. But this, well, is the opportunity of a lifetime.”

“Ah.”

He honestly didn’t know how to reply to this, and found himself staring out at the passing landscape. At this point, understanding Cartman was something not even he could do. They were climbing now, heading up into the mountains that surrounded South Park. The familiar sights of his hometown grew smaller in the side mirror until they disappeared entirely, replaced by endless forest and rocky peaks, as to be expected, naturally.

Part of him couldn’t believe he was actually doing this. Twenty-four hours ago, his biggest concern had been finishing his algebra homework. Now he was in a questionably-acquired car at like, one in the morning, heading toward an international flight with Eric Cartman of all people to hunt for a mythical creature. His parents thought he was asleep in his bed, his few remaining friends had no idea where he was going, and his remaining brain cells were freaking out.

“You know what the best part is?” Cartman continued, apparently taking Butters’ silence as some sort of attention. “We’re going to be the guys who proved all those loser scientists wrong. Hell yeah, and all those rich assholes who said the Loch Ness Monster was just a hoax. Nah. We’re gonna show them it’s for real.”

“For real real?”

“For real real, not for play play.”

“B-but what if the photos come out blurry?” Butters asked. “I mean, I’m not exactly a professional photographer. What if I screw it up?”

“You won’t screw it up,” he insisted. “You’ve got good instincts, Butters. Better than you think, at least. Plus, I’ve done my research! I know exactly where to look, exactly when to look, and exactly how to get close enough for the perfect shot.”

There was something in Cartman’s tone that made Butters glance over at him suspiciously. What the hell was he doing? “What kind of research?”

“Oh, you know, the usual. I mean… forums and documentaries and stuff.” His grip tightened slightly on the steering wheel. He was not very interested in talking about that, apparently. “Come on, you know how that works, I don’t need to belabor the point.”

Butters shrugged. “You sure?

“Ugh. Can you stop being so fucking skeptical? I mean, yeah, I know, ‘going to Scotland to hunt for things’ sounds dumb, and may be dumb, but it’s a pretty good idea. But come on! This is a, erm, carefully planned expedition with professional equipment. We’re going to succeed where everyone else has failed because we’re not everyone else. Are you like everyone else?”

“N-no—”

“Then we’re not. I’m not gonna comment on this again.”

And so they went back to silence.

Of course, Butters still couldn’t believe they were really doing this. They were really driving through the Colorado mountains at nearly two in the morning, heading toward an international flight that would take them halfway around the world to hunt for a creature that might not — probably not — even exist. It was insane. It was probably illegal in several different ways. It was definitely going to end badly.

It didn’t even make much sense. If Cartman already got a comical amount of money, regardless of how he did it, why was he going along with this plan? Butters started thinking that, perhaps, he was not doing it for the money, but perhaps for the fame. But the idea didn’t make sense to him.

Plus, the Simpsons already did it.

But as Butters watched the moonlit landscape roll past and listened to the music filling the car, he found that he didn’t really care. At least not as much as he was indicating. For the first time in months, he felt like he was part of something bigger than homework and groundings and sitting alone in his room. Even if that something was completely ridiculous, at least it was an adventure.

He just didn’t know if it was a good one.

A couple of hours had passed since that conversation. The sky was fully light now as they approached Denver. What a ride that was. Butters was honestly surprised that no one had come in to check on them yet. He soon realized this was a dumb line of thinking — who would check on him at two in the morning?

Well, I guess that could’ve happened. But not today.

“Did you bring your passport?”

“Huh?”

“Your passport.”

Butters patted his vest pocket. “Oh, yeah, my passport. I got it.”

“Good. Uh. Good.”

As they pulled into the long-term parking lot at Denver International Airport, Butters noticed Cartman checking his phone repeatedly.

“Who are you talking to?” he asked.

“No one.”

“What are you doing then?”

“Tweeting,” Cartman replied vaguely, shutting off the engine. “Yeah yeah. Tweeting. On my favorite social media, X the everything app. W-what are you staring at? Grab your bag. Our flight leaves in two hours.”

They soon unloaded their luggage — that is, Butters’ modest duffel and Cartman’s surprisingly large suitcase —, and headed toward the terminal.

“Ah, one more thing,” Cartman said, stopping Butters with a hand on his shoulder. “When we go through security, if anyone asks, we’re cousins traveling to visit our sick grandmother in Scotland.”

“Why would we need a cover story?”

Cartman’s eyes narrowed. “Because international travel as unaccompanied minors can raise flags.”

“We’re not minors anymore, Eric. You’re eighteen, and I’m seventeen.”

“Technically you still are, but whatever. Just stick to the script.”

As they entered the terminal, Butters couldn’t shake the feeling that Cartman wasn’t telling him everything. There was something off about this whole trip, an unease he couldn’t name for, well, obvious reasons. He had no idea what it was! The only thing he was sure of is that Eric wouldn’t kill him. At least not in this manner, of course. If he wanted to, he would’ve done it down in South Park instead of… Scotland, for God’s sake.

They approached the check-in counter, and Cartman handed over two first-class tickets to London Heathrow.

“W-what? First class?” Butters whispered. “How much did these cost?”

“A lot.”

The airline attendant smiled professionally. “Passports, please.”

Butters held his breath as they handed over their documents. The attendant scanned them, the seconds stretching into what felt like hours. I mean, they were valid, right? His was, at least, he was sure of it. But Cartman’s…?

“Enjoy your flight, gentlemen,” she finally said, returning their passports and boarding passes.

As they walked away, Butters exhaled in relief. Well, one less issue to deal with. Up next was the security line, and right as they were about to enter it, Cartman suddenly grabbed Butters’ arm. “Oh, and one more thing. If anyone asks what’s in my suitcase, you don’t know anything about it.”

“Is that a good or bad thing?”

“Depends…”

“What IS in your suitcase?”

Cartman’s smile was unsettlingly wide. “Monster bait.”
