Chapter Summary

Butters and Cartman finally reach their destination: a prized cabin near the Loch Ness.

Chapter Notes

I'm alive.

Their transatlantic flight had been surprisingly comfortable in first class — a luxury Butters had, admittedly, never experienced before. He’d gone on a couple of flights before, but it was never anything this fancy; he’d always gone on economic class. While Cartman had spent most of the journey either sleeping or demanding special attention from the flight attendants, Butters had just stared out the window in wonder, watching the vast Atlantic pass beneath them. What a beautiful ocean that was.

As they emerged from Heathrow Airport, London sprawled before them in all its glory, or at least something close to it. A few double-decker buses rumbled past, and the distinctive architecture made Butters gasp in delight.

“Wow, Eric! It’s just like in the movies!” Butters exclaimed, spinning around to take it all in. “Can we see Buckingham Palace? Or the Tower of London? Or—”

“No, no, and absolutely not. We’re not here for sightseeing,” Cartman cut him off, checking his phone for messages. “We’ve got a connecting flight to Edinburgh in three hours, I’m not gonna waste my time on souvenirs I can get from Wish at a much lower price.”

Butters’ shoulders slumped. “Oh… I was hoping we could explore a little.”

I mean, they had a little time to go out and check places, but he doubted Cartman would let him go anywhere anyway. Oh well. That might’ve as well been it.

In fact, it would’ve obviously been much, much easier if they had a contact down here. At least he’d get some actual conversation with someone they may or may not have known, instead of sitting in the airport lobby for hours doing absolutely nothing. Butters didn’t have any parents in the U.K., but he did recall someone he knew who was, in fact, British.

Pip.

Oh, little Pip. They went to third grade together. Well, fourth grade too! Ah, what a bright and shiny little kid. He was from England, although he couldn’t recall if he was from London, its outskirts, or somewhere else entirely. This place was huge. He could’ve lived anywhere! It was a shame he was gone; he’d have been a good tour guide. Butters had always been kind to Pip, unlike most of their classmates.

“You know, I wonder if Pip had family around here,” Butters mused aloud. “He was a nice fella. I really liked him.”

“Who?”

“Pip.”

“Who?”

He sighed. “Do you have to be this rude?”

“Who cares about that French piece of crap?” Cartman scoffed. “He’s like, dead dead. He’s seeing stars and bars in wherever the hell he is now.”

“He was British, not French.”

“He was?”

“Yeah!”

“Same difference.”

“It’s really not, Eric.”

Cartman ignored him, flagging down a taxi. He was not gonna waste his time commenting on dead people. “Terminal 5, domestic flights,” he instructed the driver as they loaded their luggage.

During the ride, Butters pressed his face against the window, trying to absorb as much of London as he could in their brief transit. Their taxi wove through traffic, passing monuments and historic buildings that Butters recognized from books and TV. Or at least those he thought he recognized.

“So, uh, where are we staying in Scotland?” he asked, finally tearing his gaze away from the window.

“I’ve already said we’re gonna be near Inverness.”

“No— no, I get that. I mean where in it.”

Cartman, who had been furiously texting again, looked up. “Jesus, you have a terrible memory. See, I got an old acquaintance of ours to set us up in a chalet near the lake.”

“An acquaintance?”

“Yep.”

“Of ours?”

“Yeah.”

“Who?”

Cartman sighed dramatically. “Remember Gregory?”

“Who?”

“Oh come on, that weird blonde kid.”

He looked at Cartman. “Eric, we’ve met many weird blonde kids. Who’s that again?”

Cartman sighed. “Think harder.”

“Hmm… wasn’t it… wasn’t it that Mormon kid?”

“No, that was one of Stan’s gay boyfriends.”

“They dated!?”

“The fuck would I know? It’s not him though.”

“O-oh. I mean… uh… well… didn’t we have that blonde kid in our class? Like, seventh grade—”

“Well yeah, we did. But that’s not him either. Come on, it’s not like your brain is that small.”

It’s funny, but Butters genuinely could not remember who it was. Not that Cartman was making it easier on him, but still. If you removed the obvious people (Tweek, Kenny, Bradley, uh… probably someone else too) from the equation, and also a couple of odd-balls, there just wasn’t anyone he could realistically name. Unless…

“Wait. Wait wait. I think I remember the guy. Wasn’t it… uh…”

“Hm?”

“How about that kid who swore a lot… it’s him, right?”

“Ugh. No. Where the hell are you pulling out these people from?”

Butters blinked. “B-but you two were friends! Y-you two watched Rashomon in my house a few years back!”

“That’s not how I remember it.”

After thinking for a minute or two, finally, another person popped up into Butters’ head.

“Wasn’t there that kid who helped us back in third grade? With uh, Canada and stuff.”

“Ding ding ding!” Cartman smiled. “That’s Gregory.”

Butters remembered Gregory, at least quite a bit. He’d never really spoken to him, or at least he wasn’t aware of any conversation the two of them had. He didn’t stay in their class for long.

“Ohhh. Well, uh. I mean. I don’t think I ever spoke to him… so he’s more of an acquaintance of yours than mine.”

“Hmpf. Fair. But that’s not the point. See, that pretentious asshole… he’s washed up now. Unbelievably washed up. There has never been anyone so washed up in history before.”

“Oh?”

“He was living here last time I met him. Well, the chalet was his I think. I don’t know, he’s high on crack most of the time.”

“…on crack?”

“It’s amazing, ain’t it? Life comes at you fast,” Cartman shrugged. “Anyway, he’s got a place near Loch Ness that he’s letting us use. Had to agree to some conditions, but whatever.”

“What conditions?”

“None of your business. Just shut up about it.”

Butters fell silent, but his mind raced with questions. From the little bits that he remembered and were going through his mind right now, Gregory had been one of those kids who seemed destined for greatness — hell, he helped them lead the fight against Sheila’s Mothers Against Canada. He seemed brilliant and charismatic enough. Maybe even principled? The idea of him becoming a drug addict was disturbing.

“It was a great war, though,” Cartman smiled. “He and his ridiculous French friend really helped us out.”

“Who?”

He sighed. “The Mole.”

“I don’t remember him.”

“Of course you don’t. But whatever, he’s just as washed up, if you can believe it.”

“Ah. Hmm. That’s sad,” Butters said quietly.

“Life’s sad, Butters. Deal with it.”

The taxi pulled up to Terminal 5, and they unloaded their luggage again. As they checked in for their flight to Edinburgh, Butters noticed Cartman seemed increasingly tense, checking his phone every few minutes and surveying the terminal with suspicious eyes.

“Is everything okay, Eric?”

“Fine,” Cartman snapped. “Just had to make a few… amends to even get here. Had to do things I hate discussing, so don’t ask.”

“What kind of—”

“I SAID DON’T ASK, GODDAMMIT!” he shrieked, earning them several startled glances from fellow passengers and a stern look from a nearby security guard.

Butters flushed crimson and mumbled a quick apology to the air. Of course, the air didn’t reply.

Their flight to Edinburgh was quick and uneventful, luckily. From there, they took another small plane to Inverness, the gateway to the Scottish Highlands and Loch Ness, just as Cartman had suggested. By the time they landed, the day was fading into evening, the Scottish sky painted in a palette of purples and really beautiful oranges.

Cartman, after a brief, aggressive negotiation, rented a rugged-looking, mud-splattered Jeep at the airport. He tossed their bags into the back with a grunt, before going back to consulting a map on his phone.

“Gregory’s cabin should be about an hour from here,” he said, starting the engine. “Right on the shores of Loch Ness. Haha… yes!”

The drive took them through the stunning Scottish Highlands. Although slightly worried about Cartman’s bizarrely competent driving ability, in the fading light, Butters just spent his time appreciating the hills and forests around him. The road wound alongside a few rivers and through valleys, occasionally passing small villages that looked, but were not, frozen in time.

“It’s beautiful here,” Butters said softly. “Peaceful.”

“Yeah, great, whatever,” Cartman replied, not taking his eyes off the road. “Just keep an eye out for the turnoff. Should be coming up… uh… soon.”

After nearly an hour of driving, they turned onto a narrow dirt road that snaked through a somewhat dense forest. The Jeep bounced over potholes and ruts, jostling them uncomfortably.

“Jesus Christ, what kind of road is this?” Cartman complained.

Eventually, the trees opened up to reveal a small clearing, and there it was: Gregory’s chalet. Except it wasn’t much of a chalet, it was more of a cabin. And, uh, well, it wasn’t really a good one on the outside. It was a dilapidated shack, with its windows patched with plastic sheeting, and a front porch that looked one step away from collapse. Huh. How familiar…

Cartman slammed on the brakes, and the Jeep skidded slightly on the gravel. He stared at the cabin in disbelief, then at his phone, then back at the cabin. What the fuck.

“No… no, no, NO! NOOOOOOO!” he shouted, smacking the steering wheel with both fists. The horn let out a brief bleat. “This CANNOT be the place! What in the actual fuck!?”

“Uh, maybe we took a wrong turn, Eric?”

Cartman thrust his phone at Butters. “This is the address that pretentious asshole gave me!”

Butters squinted at the GPS, which confidently confirmed that yes, they had reached their destination. A little red animated pin dropped directly onto the site of what was supposedly their shack.

“Well,” Butters offered weakly, trying to find a silver lining in the rapidly darkening cloud of Cartman’s fury, “maybe it’s… nicer inside?”

But he didn’t listen.

Cartman stormed out of the Jeep, slamming the door behind him with such force that the vehicle almost rocked on its suspension. When did he get so strong? Butters hurriedly followed as Cartman stomped up to the cabin’s front door, which hung slightly askew on rusty hinges.

“GREGORY!” he bellowed, pounding on the door. “YOU BRITISH PIECE OF SHIT!”

The door, unwilling to deal with Eric Cartman of all people, creaked open, seemingly of its own accord. He pushed it wider, revealing an interior that, understandably, matched the exterior’s state of disrepair. Dust covered every surface, cobwebs hung from the ceiling, and the air smelled of something really similar to mildew.

“This is horseshit!” Cartman exploded, his face turning an alarming shade of red. “I paid that crackhead five thousand dollars for this?!”

“Wait, what? Five thousand dollars?!” Butters echoed; he was genuinely shocked. “Eric, t-that’s a lot of money!”

“No shit, Sherlock!” His hands clenched into fists. “I knew I should have gone with that French bastard instead. At least he’s competent when he’s high!”

Butters cautiously stepped inside, surveying the cabin. Despite the neglect, he could see the bones of what was once a charming place. It had a stone fireplace, large windows that would offer views of the loch (although they were currently obscured by grime), and a loft area accessible by a ladder.

“It’s not so bad,” he said, trying to be positive. “We could clean it up a bit.”

“Clean it up?!” Cartman’s voice rose to a near-shriek. “I… I paid for a luxury cabin, not whatever the hell this is! There is no way we’re staying here!”

“But where else would we go? It’s getting dark, and we don’t know the area…”

Cartman pulled out his phone again, furiously tapping at the screen. What a resistant screen that was. “Oh, I’m calling that limey bastard right now. If he thinks he can scam Eric Cartman, he’s got another thing coming!”

As Cartman stalked outside to make his call (presumably for better reception), Butters continued exploring the cabin. Looking at it now, it wasn’t that bad. There were two small bedrooms off the main living area, each containing a bare mattress covered with a rather questionable sheet. The kitchen area had a rusty sink and a not-so-ancient refrigerator that hummed ominously. Plus, there was the bathroom, although Butters decided immediately he would rather not inspect it too closely. At least not yet.

Despite it all, there was something almost… cozy about the place. With some cleaning supplies, fresh linens, and maybe a trip to the nearest store for provisions, it could be made habitable. It wasn’t hell on Earth, for sure. It’s not like they’d be staying there forever…

From outside, Butters could hear Cartman shouting into his phone. He wasn’t sure who he was talking to, and he didn’t seem very happy, of course, but that was just the average Cartman. He sighed, setting his camera bag down on what appeared to be the least dusty surface available.

Well, they had come all this way. They couldn’t turn back now. Somehow, Butters would have to convince Cartman that this ramshackle cabin was better than nothing. That wouldn’t be impossible, but it’d take a while.

Either way, they were here for the Loch Ness Monster, not a five-star resort. And if there was one thing Butters had learned over the years, it was how to make the best of bad situations.

The issue is that many of these situations involved, well, Eric Cartman.

Yeah, he was fucked.
