Chapter Summary

After a few hours of sleep, Cartman and Butters go search for Nessie. It doesn't go well.

Chapter Notes

Psst: I can assure you this story isn't about the Loch Ness Monster. At least, well, it's not the important thing here.

Cartman’s rage, it turned out, had a surprisingly short battery life. After another ten minutes of fuming about the so-called “limey bastard Gregory” and the “false advertising” of the cabin, he finally seemed to run out of steam. He kicked a loose floorboard, grunted when it didn’t so much as splinter, and then declared he was going to sleep.

“Don’t touch my stuff, Butters,” he’d mumbled, before stomping into one of the small bedrooms and slamming the rickety door behind him. Butters heard the sound of a large body flopping onto a dusty mattress, followed by silence.

Finally.

Left alone in the main room, Butters let out a long, slow breath. He looked around the dilapidated space. It was a dump, no question about it; this place made Kenny’s house look like a mansion, especially given the reforms that had been happening there from time to time. But as his eyes adjusted to the gloom, he saw what he’d noticed before: it had potential… well, just like a lot of things in his life. At the very least it could be a useful cabin, he thought, especially if they could patch some of the holes here and there.

With a sigh, Butters set his duffel bag on the less-grimy of the two armchairs. He couldn’t just sit here, no. Pulling a crumpled-up plastic bag from his pocket — his mom always told him to be prepared! — he began to clean. He started small, wiping down the kitchen counter with a damp handkerchief he sacrificed for the cause. He swept the worst of the dust and debris into a pile in the corner with a funny-looking broom he found by the door. The good news is that, during his chores, he even managed to find a couple of old, musty-smelling but dry blankets in a wooden chest, shaking them out vigorously on the porch before laying one on the other bedroom’s mattress.

It wasn’t much, but it was definitely something. As he worked, a strange sense of calm settled over him. It was quiet here. Sure, this would last until Cartman’s unbearable snoring began, but still. It was calm, especially in comparison to his home. No parents arguing downstairs, no pressure to finish homework, and definitely no feeling of being an awkward third wheel. There was just the gentle hum of the refrigerator and the sound of his own breathing. Maybe, just maybe, this wouldn’t be so bad. Maybe this was the adventure he needed after all.

He finally laid down on his own makeshift bed, fully clothed, and fell into an uneasy sleep. He didn’t even bother eating, as he’d eaten a few snacks in the car. Plus, Cartman almost certainly bought plenty of them for the trip anyway… assuming he hadn’t eaten them all already. The expensive camera that was accompanying him rested on the floor; he just hoped he wouldn’t wake up in the night and accidentally step on it. That would’ve been bad.

It felt like only minutes later when his world exploded into noise.

“BUTTERS!”

Oh no.

“WAKE UP! We got shit to do!”

Butters shot upright, his heart hammering against his ribs. Of course, there was a dark, bulky shape silhouetted in the doorway, and it had to be Cartman. It was. He was already dressed in his leather jacket, looking absurdly out of place in the rustic setting. Still, given how cold it must’ve been outside, maybe he wasn’t exactly wrong.

A brief glance through the grimy windows confirmed it; it was pitch black outside and freezing cold. A glance at his phone showed the time: 4:02 a.m.

“G-gosh, Eric, what time is it?” he mumbled.

“It’s go-time, you moron! This is our first opportunity and I’m not gonna go waste it. Now grab the camera and let’s go!”

Shivering, Butters pulled on his too-large beanie and the photographer’s vest, and the cold seeped into his bones instantly. Yikes. He grabbed the camera, ignoring how cold its metal body was, and followed Cartman out into the biting Scottish air. The sky was a deep, star-filled indigo, though he could see that a faint line of gray was beginning to form on the eastern horizon.

“The boat’s this way,” Cartman whispered, pointing him towards a small, rickety-looking dock that jutted out into the water. Tied to it was a small, green rowboat that honestly looked like it had seen better days. “Mr. Shitass said it came with the place. It’s probably infested with tetanus, but hey, it floats.”

“Mr. Shi—”

“Gregory.”

“Ah.”

Soon, they were on the water. The loch was vast and unnervingly still. Its dark surface looked a lot like polished obsidian, Butters thought, and it reflected the stars above, understandably. A thick mist clung to the surface, and swirled around their little boat as Cartman rowed them towards the center. The only sounds he could really hear were the creak of the oars and the gentle lap of water against the hull. It was both beautiful and quite terrifying. It didn’t help that it was cold outside.

“Okay,” Cartman hissed, stopping the boat. “This is it. Be ready.” He pulled out a pair of cheap, plastic binoculars — couldn’t have he spent more on that? —, and began scanning the water with an intensity that was almost frightening. “Keep your finger on the trigger, Butters. The second you see a ripple, you shoot. Don’t think, just shoot.”

Butters raised the camera, although the long lens was somewhat heavy and unwieldy. He pointed it toward the rather misty expanse. The two of them sat in silence for what felt like an eternity. Not only was Butters on alert for the monster, but he was fucking freezing. He’d barely slept and now here he was, chasing a monster that might have not been real, but he liked to pretend it was. At least it would keep his brain busy…

“There!” Cartman suddenly shouted. He pointed a trembling finger at the water. “Right there! Did you see it?”

Butters’ head snapped around. Huh. He squinted, trying to pierce the gloom, and then he saw it — there was a long, dark shape breaking the surface. He fumbled with the camera, frantically trying to focus. “I-I see it! I see it!”

He pressed the shutter button, and the camera let out a series of satisfying clicks. Great! He kept shooting. No way, this was it! They were going to be famous!

Cartman was practically vibrating with excitement. “Yes! YES! We got it! We fucking got it!”

He rowed furiously towards the shape. As they drew closer, however, their triumphant mood began to curdle. The long, dark shape didn’t move. It didn’t submerge, either. It just… floated.

It was a log.

A very large, vaguely monster-shaped log.

But it was a log. No monster or anything like that.

Cartman stared at it, his face falling. The silence that followed was heavier than before.

“Well,” Butters said, trying to sound cheerful. “It was a very convincing log, r-right?”

But Cartman didn’t answer. He just slumped in his seat, the binoculars dangling from his neck. They sat there for another hour as the sun slowly rose, painting the sky in shades of really nice pale pink and orange. The mist eventually burned away, revealing the stunning, rolling green hills that surrounded the loch. It was breathtakingly beautiful, but the magic was gone. There were no monsters here.

That may have been good for some, but it was terrible news for them.

By 6 a.m., they were rowing back to shore. The mood in the boat was as murky as the water below.

“Well, whatever,” Cartman finally said, his voice flat. He didn’t look at Butters. “It was our first day anyway. Who knows, that thing is probably… shy. Yeah, shy. We’ll try again tomorrow.” He grunted as he pulled the boat onto the shore. “But if it doesn’t show up soon, I’m moving to Plan B.”

“Plan B?” Butters asked nervously. “What’s Plan B?”

“You don’t need to worry about Plan B,” he replied, before stomping back towards the cabin to, presumably, sleep off his disappointment.

Left alone once again, Butters felt a familiar hollowness spread through his chest. Whatever adrenaline he’d felt hours ago had faded, leaving behind only the cold, the damp, and the crushing weight of their failure. Their cabin was much cleaner now, but it still seemed sad. And pathetic. He imagined it was probably laughing at them; what they were even doing here? Why couldn’t they just get this over with?

He needed to talk to someone. Someone normal.

Pulling out his phone, he was momentarily surprised to see two bars of cell service. He knew the cabin had a weak, password-protected WiFi signal — Gregory wasn’t that much of a monster, apparently —, but having actual service out here felt like a small miracle, at least. His fingers hovered over his contacts, and he scrolled past a few names before landing on one in particular. It would’ve been much smarter to just text him, but Butters preferred to call his friends rather than text them. It felt more natural, at least. He pressed the call button. It rang twice before a familiar voice answered.

“Hello?”

Ah, Scott.

Scott’s lisp had faded significantly as he’d gotten older, to the point where it was barely noticeable most of the time. It hadn’t, of course, stopped Cartman from making jokes about it, though now he’d mostly just stuck to the ones about Scott having diabetes.

“Hey, Scott! It’s me, Butters.”

“Butters? Holy cow, where are you? Your mom called my mom asking if I knew where you went. She said something about a photography program? Or whatever that was.”

“Oh, yeah! That’s right!” Butters forced a cheerful tone, pacing the small living area. “I’m in, uh, Scotland! It’s, uh, great! We went out on a boat this morning. The scenery is amazing.”

There was a pause on the other end, long enough for Butters to hear the faint sound of a video game in the background. “We?” Scott asked. “Who’s ‘we’?”

“Oh, you know. Me and uh… Eric.”

The silence that followed was so profound Butters thought the call had dropped. He could picture Scott on the other end, controller paused, and very confused at what was going on. Finally, Scott spoke. “Butters… what are you doing with Cartman in Scotland?”

“We’re on an adventure!” he replied, a little too quickly. “We’re… we’re looking for the Loch Ness Monster! He needed a photographer, Scott. A real one, a-and he said I have a visionary eye!”

“A visionary eye? You?” He sounded incredulous. “I mean, not that you aren’t— no, it’s just that uh, well, he’s clearly exploiting you.”

“No, he’s not! It’s a partnership! See, he has the plan and the money, and I have the artistic talent. So it’s just like an expedition!” Even as he said it, the words sounded quite flimsy and ridiculous. If anything, it sounded like a lie he was trying to sell to himself more than to Scott.

Scott sighed. “Butters, when was the last time an actual expedition you went on with Cartman ended well?”

“See—”

“Did he tell you he asked me to go first?”

Butters froze mid-pace. He remembered Cartman’s words clearly. Something something Scott’s a pussy, something something illegal, yup. “Yeah,” Butters said slowly. “Yeah. He said you turned him down.”

“That’s because he never asked me, dickwad!” Scott exclaimed. He wasn’t angry, but he wasn’t quite happy either. “He knew I’d tell him it was a stupid, illegal plan that would get us both arrested! Fuck, do you think he likes people who question him?”

“Hmm… w-well no, not really-”

“Of course, he just wanted someone who would do whatever he says. He wanted you, Butters, because he knew you were lonely and would say yes to anything.”

No. It wasn’t like that, right? I mean, he wasn’t lonely. Not as lonely as Scott was suggesting, no. That was clearly an exaggeration, right?

…or maybe he was right. Maybe he was lonely, maybe he would say yes to (almost) everything. Maybe Cartman had lied. Of course he had.

“But… he has all this money,” Butters mumbled. It was his last, desperate defense as he sank onto the edge of his cot. “He showed me his bank app.”

“And you believed him? After everything? Hell, he probably borrowed it from Tolkien again.”

“…again?”

“Look, Butters, whatever you’re doing is clearly not an adventure. It’s just… Cartman being Cartman. And you’re letting him drag you down with him.”

Butters stared at the opposite wall, the phone feeling heavy and cold in his hand. The now-clean but still-shabby cabin, the broken-down boat, the non-existent monster — well, he could say it all coalesced into a single, ugly truth. Scott was right. There was no grand opportunity for him.

“I… I gotta go, Scott,” he whispered, his throat tight.

“Butters, wait—”

He ended the call. Butters stared at the blank screen, the silence of the cabin pressing in on him. Outside, a bird sang a cheerful, oblivious tune. It might’ve been a bird, he wasn’t sure. For the first time since he’d arrived, Butters didn’t feel like he was on an adventure. He just felt very, very far from home.

And utterly, completely screwed.
