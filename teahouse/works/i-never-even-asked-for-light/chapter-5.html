Chapter Summary

Another day, another hunt. With his patience gone, however, Cartman initiates "Plan B," forcing Butters to confront the rather humiliating nature of his role in their expedition.

Chapter Notes

I get it, you're bored of Loch Ness stuff. So am I. It's only gonna get better here, I promise.

The rest of the day passed in a rather suffocating silence. Cartman, true to form, had stomped back into his room after their failed morning expedition and — as usual, apparently — promptly fallen asleep. Jesus, was that all he could do? His snores seemed to shake the flimsy walls of the cabin; it was ridiculous. Butters was left alone, with not much of a friend to talk to.

He couldn't bring himself to do anything. His camera just sat on the table looking like a ridiculously expensive paperweight. He picked it up, turned it over in his hands, and felt nothing. It was a neat camera, sure, but what else? He didn’t feel like a visionary, nor much like a photographer. In his hands was the cold, dead weight of an object he had no right to hold. He was a fraud. He was not worthy of getting a camera in… well, the conditions he had been subjected to.

Butters just spent hours staring out the grime-streaked window at the loch. The water was, of course, very beautiful. It was water. It was, well, wet. Perhaps not the wettest from the standpoint of water, but still. Besides that, it looked just like any other large body of water: gray, cold, and empty. Sure enough, scrolling on random sites and watching random videos was fun, but it wouldn’t cut it, at least not with everything going on in his mind.

He thought about calling Scott back, maybe about apologizing for being so stupid. He thought about calling his parents, maybe making up some story about the exchange program being a bust so he could come home. But what would he say? How could he possibly explain this without admitting the full, pathetic scope of his own desperation? The grounding he’d receive would be truly legendary, and not in a good way. He’d probably be grounded forever, or worse, if that was even possible. If Butters made up something else, well, maybe he could get away with it, but it wouldn’t leave his mind so easily. It sucked; he was trapped.

As the afternoon bled into evening, the sky began to bruise with the colors of sunset, or something close enough to it. The light softened, casting long shadows from the trees and painting the surface of the loch in hues of orange and deep purple. It was quite stunning.

Inside their cabin, a loud yawn from the other room signaled Cartman’s return to the world of the conscious. It was impressive. He either slept for many long hours or stayed up all night on his phone. Was he ever this boring? He emerged a few minutes later, stretching his arms over his head and surveying the cabin.

“Alright, Butters,” he announced. “We… uh… nap time’s over. It’s dusk, so time for round two. Let’s go.”

Butters didn’t move. “I don’t know, Eric. I don’t think anything’s out there.”

Cartman’s eyes narrowed. “What?”

“I mean, I-I really don’t think we’re gonna find anything on that lake. Maybe some fish but uh—”

“Pfft. Are you giving up? Losers give up, Butters. Are you a loser?”

“Maybe I am,” Butters muttered, so quietly he wasn't sure Cartman heard.

But he had.

“No, no, no,” Cartman said, walking over and clapping a heavy hand on Butters’ shoulder. “We don’t have losers on this team. We have visionaries. And leaders. Like me, duh, I’m the leader. And you’re the visionary. Now grab your equipment and let’s get back on that floating piece of shit we call a boat.”

Of course, any argument right now was pointless. In fact, any argument with Cartman would be pointless because even if you won, he’d always find a way to make it seem like he won, and this process would be very, very, very annoying. With a heavy heart and heavier feet, Butters picked up the camera and followed him back down to the dock.

The evening air was even colder than the morning’s had been. As Cartman rowed them out into the center of the vast, dark loch, they barely even heard anything, which was to be expected, perhaps. Hell, Butters didn’t even bother raising the camera this time. He just sat there, watching the last sliver of sun disappear behind the hills, leaving them in a deep, starless twilight. What a fucking ride.

They waited. Ten minutes. Twenty. Thirty. After a long moment of silence, Cartman tried making small talk with Butters.

“You know, Chef’s father used to live here.”

“Here?”

“No, dumbass, in Scotland.”

“Oh.”

He sighed. “Yeah, and he actually met that fucking monster once. It was beautiful, really. He and his wife were out on the lake and suddenly this huge, giant crustacean from the Paleolithic era popped up.”

“Ah, wow.”

“Yeah! And they asked the creature what it wanted, and it said ‘Uh, I need about tree-fitty.’ Can you believe it?”

Butters was silent for perhaps a minute or two.

“’Tree-fitty’?”

“Three dollars and fifty fucking cents.”

“Oh.”

“Yeah.”

They didn’t speak much after that. If anything, Butters could feel Cartman’s impatience radiating off him in waves, even in the moments when they briefly talked. He fidgeted, he sighed, he tapped his fingers on the side of the boat.

Finally, after what were probably fifty minutes or so, he exploded.

“FUCK THIS!” he roared, his voice echoing across the still water. “This is horseshit! This monster bitch isn’t coming here. It’s too stupid or too scared or too… not-real. Huh” He glared at the dark water as if it had personally offended him. “Uh, well, yeah. I mean. If the mountain won't come to Muhammad…”

He turned to Butters, and his face was illuminated by a terrible, familiar grin. Oh no. It was that grin that had preceded ninety-nine percent of the worst moments of Butters’ life.

“It’s time for Plan B,” Cartman declared.

“W-what’s Plan B?” Butters asked.

Without a word, Cartman reached into the large, surprisingly waterproof duffel bag he’d brought onto the boat. He grunted with effort and pulled out a large, lumpy object. He shook it out, and in the gloom, Butters could just make out what it was. It was a suit. But not just any suit, no. It was a full-body costume made of cheap, shiny green fabric, with a long, drooping neck, a flipper-like tail, and two enormous, cartoonish googly eyes sewn onto the head. It was a ridiculous, pathetic-looking Loch Ness Monster costume.

“Ta-da!” Cartman said, holding it up. It smelled faintly of plastic and mothballs. “If we can’t find a monster, Butters, we’ll make one. Put it on.”

Butters stared at him, horrified. “What? No! Eric, you’re fucking crazy! I’m not putting that on!”

“Don’t be a baby,” he scoffed. “It’s brilliant. You put on the suit, I throw you in the water, you splash around a bit, I take some blurry pictures, we sell them to the tabloids, and get filthy rich. It’s foolproof, you moron.”

“You want to throw me into the loch?” he shrieked. “Eric, it’s freezing! I could get hypothermia! I could drown!”

“Oh, relax, I’ll pull you out. Eventually,” Cartman dismissed him. “This is how you create art, Butters. You have to suffer for it. Well, at least it’s what most major artists did before they died. Now get in the goddamn suit before I start shitting all over this boat. I really should’ve gone to the bathroom earlier.”

“No!” For one brief, shining moment, Butters held his ground. Or tried to, at least. “I won’t do it!”

Cartman’s smile vanished. His expression hardened into something cold and ugly. “You know, Butters,” he said, his voice curiously low. “I brought you here because I thought you were my friend, see. You weren’t like those bastards… well, not like Kyle, Stan, Kenny, or that diabetic asshole Scott.” He leaned forward a bit more. “Wow. I guess I was wrong. I guess you’re just like all the rest of them. A useless, backstabbing…”

He let the words hang in the air. Of course, he was being manipulative. His words were a lie. Butters knew they were a lie. And yet, for some reason, Butters chose to believe them. Maybe it was because he didn’t outright hate Cartman. Maybe it was because they were on a boat in the middle of a lake on the other side of Earth, perhaps.

But maybe it was because, despite everything, a tiny, pathetic part of him still wanted to prove Cartman wrong. He wasn’t like the others, right? Eric actually liked him as a friend, right? Right?

Butters sighed. Defeated, he reached for the cheap, humiliating costume. “Fine,” he whispered.

Putting it on was a bit of a nightmare. The zipper caught, the fabric was clammy and cold against his skin, and its oversized headpiece kept flopping over his eyes, making him feel like he was suffocating. Where the hell did he even buy this? He felt tears of humiliation pricking at his eyes, but he blinked them back fiercely. No. He would not cry. Not in front of Cartman, not like this.

Once he was fully encased in the ridiculous green suit, Cartman looked him over and nodded. He seemed satisfied. “Perfect. You look… heh, monstrous. Haha, get it? Monstrous… monster… heh. Alright.”

Before Butters could protest or even brace himself, Cartman grabbed him by the scruff of the suit.

“Say ‘cheese,’ asshole,” he grunted.

And then he shoved him into the water.

The shock of the icy water rapidly stole Butters’ breath. It was cold, unbelievably cold. It was colder than he could have possibly imagined, which definitely helped to leach the very warmth from his blood. Panic, pure and primal, surged through him. He was fucked.

That cheap costume Cartman bought was not working at all. Its flimsy fabric was soaking up the frigid water and weighing him down like an anchor — which, admittedly, would be a smart tactic to kill someone. Kinda funny, too. The difference is that Butters did not want to die, and did not find this funny either. His arms and legs tangled in the soggy flippers and tail. Wow, he was very fucked. His head broke the surface, and he sputtered, choking on a mouthful of peaty, black water.

Shit.

Through the plastic eyeholes of the monster head, he could see Cartman in the boat. Of course, he wasn't rowing to help him. He was actually standing up, capturing every second of his friend’s terror with the camera that Butters was carrying not 5 minutes ago.

“Yeah, that’s it! Look more… uh… I don’t know, monstrous? You know how they’re supposed to look like!” Cartman shouted. “Flap your little flippers! Haha, fuck yes! This is gold, Butters! Gold!”

Rage, hot and sharp, cut through Butters’ cold-induced haze. If he wasn’t sure before, oh boy, he definitely was now. It all seemed so clear. He was no partner, visionary, sidekick or whatever Eric had told him over the past couple of days — or months, even.

He was bait.

Yet another prop in Cartman's pathetic, delusional fantasies. Scott was right. Scott was right about everything. And he almost couldn’t believe it.

The worst part is that this realization didn't empower him; if anything, it hollowed him out. They were friends, for God’s sake — but why now was he realizing that, maybe, Cartman didn’t like him much at all? No friend would make them go through this, of all things. It’s not like Eric was better when they were younger, of course; the amount of crimes he had done certainly had him on a federal watchlist of any kind. But how could someone like Butters live through a lifetime of this, and still want more?

No. He couldn’t do this anymore.

The fight went out of him. He stopped flailing and just floated there. A miserable, shivering green blob in the vast, dark water. It would’ve been funny if it wasn’t tragic.

It was only then, when the flashing stopped and Cartman seemed to realize his star prop was about to actually drown, that he finally acted. With a groan, he reached over the side of the boat, grabbed the back of the costume, and hauled Butters out of the water. That seemed easy.

Butters collapsed onto the floor of the boat. An unbelievable amount of water streamed from the costume, pooling at his feet. He was shaking so violently his teeth chattered like castanets. He couldn't feel his hands or his feet. Hell, he almost thought he’d go see Pip in heaven… if he was there.

Their ride back to the cabin was silent. Butters didn’t say anything, perhaps for obvious reasons, and neither did Cartman, who spent his time navigating their boat, and also glancing down at the camera's display screen with a smirk. Butters just lay there. Not only was he cold and wet, but he was also tired.

Back in the cabin, he managed to peel off the costume — which he hoped, honestly, he’d never see again —, as his numb fingers fumbled with the zipper. That suit now lay in a puddle on the floor. He stumbled into his room and crawled onto the dusty mattress, pulling his wet clothes and the thin, musty blanket around him.

Yet the shivering wouldn't stop. It wasn't just from the cold anymore, he had to admit. It was from the humiliation he’d just witnessed and the rather brutal clarity of his own stupidity.

He curled into a tight ball, facing the wall. And in the dark, silent cabin, miles and miles from anyone who cared about him, he sighed. He really, really wanted to cry, even if just for a moment. But he didn’t even have the willpower to do that.

If he wanted confirmation, he definitely got it.

Scott was right. He was just being used.

He always was.
