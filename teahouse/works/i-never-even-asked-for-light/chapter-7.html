Chapter Summary

After their failed adventure, Butters and Cartman agree to go home.

Chapter Notes

What a season premiere, huh? It was really fun to watch, I liked it.

The first thing Butters did after packing was take a bath. He stank, for God’s sake. It turns out that the combination of sweat and loch water can create unbearable smells that could, maybe, probably, qualify as a war crime under the Geneva Convention. Although the cabin’s bathtub was ancient and stained, it had hot water, and that was all that mattered.

He sank into the steaming water with a sigh that came from somewhere deep in his soul. For the first time in days, he felt almost human. It’s not that he’d felt like something else, exactly, but being constrained, humiliated, and maybe even abused by Cartman really let him down. “Man is born free, and yet everywhere he is in chains,” a great philosopher once said. (It was Jean-Jacques Rousseau.) Not quite literally in his face, but it was still said. And he hated how true it was. Eventually, the warmth seeped into his bones, washing away the cold that had settled there since his misadventures. He scrubbed at his skin until it was pink, trying to wash away more than just the dirt.

When he finally emerged, wrapped in a towel (one of his, in fact) that had honestly seen better days, he found Cartman in the main room, stuffing his belongings into his suitcase with some violence. His laptop was closed; apparently, he’d given up on the fake monster photo idea. What a pity; this entire voyage now had no meaning.

“You done primping, princess?” Cartman muttered without looking up.

“Hmm. Yeah,” Butters said quietly. He went to his room and finished packing in silence. He had no time for arguing.

The drive back to Inverness was awkward as hell. Cartman drove with white knuckles, muttering under his breath about his “backstabbing friends” and other things. But Butters barely heard a thing; he’d put in his earbuds and let some new wave wash over him — Duran Duran, The Cure, and stuff that felt appropriately melancholy for the occasion — well, at least according to the script. That is, whatever the Spotify algorithm was feeding him. God Bless the ‘Is It New Wave?’ playlist, he thought. The Scottish countryside rolled by, all gray skies and green hills that looked nothing like the majestic wilderness Cartman had promised. And yet, it was still beautiful.

Butters thought about pulling out his phone, maybe texting Kyle or Kenny or anyone really, asking what the actual fuck had just happened. In fact, he should have done this days ago instead of marinating in bed and pretending nothing happened. But every time he started to type something, he’d delete it. What would he even say to them right now? “Hey guys, heard you threw a party celebrating my death, that’s cool I guess?” He didn’t even have a good response to this. The whole situation was so absurd it felt like something out of a fever dream.

What a ride.

They returned the jeep to a rental place in Inverness without incident, thankfully, although the clerk gave them some suspicious looks. He was probably wondering why two American kids looked like they’d been through a blender, Butters assumed. Their puddle-jumper flight back to Edinburgh was mercifully short, and Butters spent most of it staring out the window, watching Scotland shrink away beneath them. Soon it would all be over.

But it was at Edinburgh Airport, while they were waiting for their connecting flight back to Denver, that things got interesting again.

“Eric? Eric Cartman?”

They both turned. Standing there, looking remarkably less disheveled than Butters remembered, was Gregory. In retrospect, he hadn’t aged as badly as Cartman had suggested. If anything, he vaguely reminded Butters of Kenny now — well, something about the bone structure at least. His hair wasn’t as long, though, so he looked more like, uh, Bradley, Henrietta’s brother. He hadn’t seen him for a while, even disregarding the time he’d spent away from his friends on this trip.

The last time he saw him, he was working with Tweek at his family’s coffee shop. He was probably having the time of his life there! Ah, the wonders that cocaine can do…

“Oh,” Cartman said, his voice taking on that fake-polite tone he used when he was about to do something awful. “Gregory, wow. Ah. Fancy meeting you here.”

Gregory smiled, and it didn’t quite reach his eyes. “I was just flying back from a business trip. Saw you two and thought I’d say hello. How did the, ah, expedition go? Did you like the cabin?”

“Wonderfully,” Cartman said through gritted teeth. “Just fucking wonderfully. Great cabin, amazing cabin. Hey, Greg, could you do me a favor and go into the bathroom for a minute?”

He raised an eyebrow but shrugged. “Sure, I suppose.”

What happened next was… well, it was something. Cartman followed Gregory into the men’s room, and about thirty seconds later, there was a lot of shouting and what sounded like someone getting shoved into a bathroom stall. Well, at least that’s what Butters interpreted, because he had no idea and was too scared to check. Then more shouting. Then a loud crash. Wait, was that the sound of an anvil falling? Huh. Well, uh. Good question.

Butters couldn’t quite make out what was going on in there, aside from the fact that some sort of fight definitely happened. Maybe I should intervene, he thought, but didn’t.

Cartman emerged five minutes later looking defeated and with what was definitely going to be a black eye. Ouch. Gregory followed, straightening his shirt and looking entirely too pleased with himself.

“Well,” Cartman announced to the airport at large, “that was…. uh… a complete fucking disaster. I’m done. Screw you guys… I’m going home.”

And with that, he grabbed his suitcase and stormed off toward his gate, leaving Butters standing there with Gregory in awkward silence.

Uh…

“That went about as well as I expected,” Gregory said cheerfully. “Huh. So, uh, Butters, was it?”

Butters blinked. “Uh, yeah. You remember me?”

“Of course I do. We didn’t talk much back in elementary school, but I remember you. You were always… well, nicer than the rest of them.” Gregory checked his watch. “I’m guessing Eric left you to fend for yourself?”

“Does it look like he’s coming back?”

They waited for about two minutes in complete silence to see if he would come back. He did not, in fact, come back.

“Hm, nope.”

“Pfft, typical. Well, I’m flying back to Denver, actually; I need to meet some old friends in Colorado. Care for a ride? I’ve got first-class tickets and no particular attachment to traveling alone.”

Butters stared at him. After everything that had happened, this felt like either a miraculous stroke of luck or the setup for an even worse disaster. Probably both. “I… uh… you’d do that? Why?”

Gregory shrugged. “I mean, the ticket was for your fa— ahem, your slightly obese friend. But now he’s gone, and besides, you look like you’ve been through hell. Well, consider it my good deed for the day.”

“Fair enough.”

When they settled into their seats — and holy shit, first class was nice —, Butters couldn’t help but ask one question that had been bugging him.

“Um, G-Gregory? Can I ask you something?”

“Sure.”

“How did you… I mean, why did you even agree to help Cartman with this whole thing? The cabin, uh, this flight… I mean, it’s nice and all, but it seems so elaborate for someone you barely know.”

Gregory was quiet for a long moment, swirling the complimentary whiskey he’d ordered. Butters even wondered if he forgot to read his lines from the script or whatever. When he finally answered, his cheeks were distinctly pink.

“Well,” he said, clearing his throat, “this is rather embarrassing, but Eric and I… we had a bit of a, shall we say, encounter at an establishment in San Francisco. Have you ever been to a ‘whorehouse’?”

Butters nearly choked on his ginger ale. “You… wait, what?”

“Yeah. Uh… it was a moment of poor judgment, you see,” he continued hastily. “I was, uh, feeling nostalgic about America, he was there for… God knows what reason… there was alcohol involved… and, well, one thing led to another. It’s not what you think it is, though. No, no. Anyway… when he called me up talking about some grand adventure in Scotland, I suppose I felt obligated to help. Guilt, you might say.”

“Oh,” Butters managed, his face burning. “Oh wow. Okay. Um. Let’s maybe talk about something else?”

Gregory laughed. “Gladly. So tell me, how have you been holding up?”

“I’m doing good, I think.”

“I mean, really. You’ve been traveling with that guy for several days. That can’t have been easy.”

And it wasn’t. In fact, it was surprising that, despite everything, Butters found himself actually talking. Really talking, too. About how he’d ended up on this stupid trip, about the monster suit and the freezing loch water (although he tried to minimize some of what happened), and rather briefly, about the phone call that revealed his friends’ pseudo-memorial party. At the end of that conversation, he felt very much like everyone in his life either used him or forgot about him entirely.

Gregory listened without judgment — or at least that’s how it looked to him —, occasionally nodding or making a few small hums. When Butters finished, he was quiet for a while.

“You know,” Gregory said finally, “I left South Park when I was quite young. Emancipated myself from my parents and struck out on my own.”

“How’d you manage that?” Butters asked.

Gregory’s expression darkened slightly. “My father was… well, let’s just say he had some rather unsavory business practices.”

“Hm?”

“Arms dealing, mostly.”

“Oh.”

“Yeah. When I discovered he was selling weapons to terrorist organizations, I turned him in to the authorities and petitioned for legal emancipation. The courts were surprisingly, heh, accommodating when presented with evidence of his war crimes.”

“Jesus,” Butters breathed. To be fair, he honestly didn’t think all of it was real, but given that he had seen worse things, he opted to believe it.

“Quite. My mother was… well, she chose him over me, I’m afraid. So I’ve been on my own since I was fourteen. It’s given me a, well, a certain perspective on friendship and loyalty.” He took another sip of whiskey. “I’ve traveled the world, made money, had adventures. But you know what my biggest regret is?”

Butters shook his head.

“Not staying around long enough to make real friends. I hate it. I fucking hate it. ‘Oh, but you have Christophe,’ they say—”

“Who’s that?”

“You might’ve heard of The Mole.”

He sort of did — barely. He definitely heard Cartman bring him up a few times on their way to Scotland, and the name (rather, the nickname) seemed somewhat familiar, but he couldn’t really remember who he was. Was there even a student at South Park Elementary with that name?

Thinking about it, he did recall there being a guy named Chris there, who’d played with them a few times. But he wasn’t particularly close to him, so it couldn’t be that one, unfortunately. He was pretty sure this Chris was blonde, too; if only he’d remembered his name a couple of days ago…

“…but we’re both rather damaged individuals, and I think we bring out the worst in each other sometimes. There’s something to be said for having people who knew you when you were young and stupid and still care about you anyway. I don’t know anymore.”

“Even if they throw parties when they think you’re dead?”

Gregory chuckled. “Uh, especially then. Look, Butters, I don’t know your friends well, but I remember them from our brief time together. I wish I did, though. They were children then, just as we all were. Children do thoughtless things; the world is full of them, some taller, some smaller. And to an extent, we still are children, though that’s for another discussion. But the fact that they felt the need to memorialize you, even in such a, uh… um…”

“…bizarre?”

“A bizarre way, yes, suggests they care more than you might think.”

Butters considered this. “So what should I do?”

“Well,” Gregory said, “if I were you, I’d show up to this party. See how they react when you walk through the door, very much alive and well. I think their response will tell you everything you need to know about whether they actually missed you or were just going through the motions.”

He sighed. “And if they were just going through the ‘motions,’ whatever that means?”

“Then you’ll know where you stand, and you can make decisions accordingly. But I suspect you might be pleasantly surprised. I don’t think they hate you, really.”

The plane hit some turbulence, and Butters gripped his armrest. Gregory, who was probably more than used to this, ignored it and continued talking. He was making direct eye contact with Butters now, who felt uneasy not because of Gregory himself, but because this conversation had reached levels he was not expecting to reach.

“The thing about friendship is, well, it’s messy and complicated and people fuck it up constantly. I’ve done my fair share of fuckups and I deeply regret them. But the difference between real friends and people who are just using you is that real friends feel bad when they hurt you, even if they don’t always know how to show it.”

“Real friends.” Well, Butters wondered, who was he actually a real friend to? He hadn’t visited Stan, he hadn’t actually spoken to Kyle or his other friends to tell them that he was, in fact, alive (something which, honestly, he should’ve done earlier, if he had the courage too), he wasn’t even sure if Scott appreciated their friendship, and he didn’t even know what to say about Cartman. Well…

“What about Eric?” Butters asked quietly.

“What about him?”

“He… I mean, he’s awful, but sometimes I think he’s the only one who actually wants to spend time with me. Even if it’s just because I’m easy to manipulate.”

Gregory leaned back in his seat, considering this for a moment. Cartman was, after all, not the sharpest tool in the shed. He couldn’t even be called a tool at this point, and was too far away from the shed for this analogy to even make sense. “Butters, let me tell you something I learned the hard way. I think that, someone who only values you for what you can do for them isn’t a friend, they’re a user. And the longer you let yourself be used, the harder it becomes to recognize genuine affection when it comes along.”

“But what if it doesn’t ever come along?”

“Then you’ll have learned to be comfortable with yourself, which is more valuable than you might think.” Gregory gave him a sad smile. “You’ve got time to figure it out. I’m not very good with analogies, honestly.”

Butters buried his head in his hands. He couldn’t even imagine what awaited him back home. For starters, there were his parents, who would probably ground him until he was thirty once they realized this had been one of Cartman’s schemes; his friends, who might hate him for being associated with Cartman, or worse, might not care enough to hate him at all; and Scott, who might never speak to him again after getting caught in the crossfire.

“It’s not a fair world,” he mumbled through his fingers.

“No,” Gregory agreed, “it’s not. But it’s the only one we’ve got, so we might as well make the best of it.”

They flew in silence for a while after that, Butters watching the clouds drift by below. Admittedly, he had no idea what to think. A lot of weird shit had gone down and he was almost starting to think it was all some twisted dream. Yeah… maybe he was just in his home, sleeping, and none of this had happened. It would’ve been pretty scary, but also quite fantastic…

If only.

Maybe Gregory was right. Maybe the only way to know for sure was to show up and see what happened.

“Gregory?” he said eventually.

“Yes?”

“Thanks. For the ride, and for… you know. Uh, listening.”

“Don’t mention it,” Gregory replied. “We outcasts have to stick together.”

When their plane began its descent toward Denver, Butters felt something he hadn’t felt in days: curiosity about what came next. Well, it wasn’t hope exactly, and it wasn’t despair either, but he felt very curious about what would happen next. Chances are that he would end up very disappointed, grounded, or both. But who knows?

It’s a living.
