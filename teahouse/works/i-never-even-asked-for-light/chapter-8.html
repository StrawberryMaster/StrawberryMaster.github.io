Chapter Summary

Cartman heads back to South Park, and has an idea of who he should visit first.

Chapter Notes

I'm pretty bad at determining when a story will end. From what it seems, though, I think the next chapter will be the final one in this story. Wow - that was fast.

Cartman was pissed.

After ditching Butters and that smug British asshole Gregory at the Edinburgh Airport, he’d moved through the next eighteen hours not so much as a person but as some sort of embodiment of spite; it was hard to explain. The transatlantic flight he’d been on had been torture, and not because of cramped seats or airplane food or whatever, but because his mind wouldn’t shut up. Every few minutes, another humiliation would bubble to the surface like some toxic gas leak in his brain. There were so many things he had to be worried about. The money, the monster who was probably fake, that tabloid editor, the party… Jesus Christ, the fucking party.

He’d sat rigid in 73F (about 23C), white-knuckling the armrests while some business douche next to him tried to make small talk. “First time to Scotland?” the guy had asked. Bitch. Cartman had fixed him with such a glare that the man had spent the rest of the flight pretending to be asleep.

The thing is, he was in no mood for such bullshit. If anything, he had enough of it from his so-called friends, and was trying his best not to think about it. He was surprised he was even getting home. Well, not because he wouldn’t, he just thought it wouldn’t happen so fast. But oh well — nothing a little whining couldn’t do. God bless those airport employees!

By the time he arrived at Denver International Airport, he hauled his oversized suitcase through the terminal and tried his best to keep his head down. The shuttle to long-term parking took forever and was filled with chattering tourists and their stupid vacation stories. Oh mom, what about that time we went to Ireland… oh dad, you took such a great photo at the Louvre… oh sister, it was great to throw you from the top of the Burj Khalifa… it sucked. The fact he understood everything they were saying just made it worse.

When he finally spotted the gray Honda Civic tucked between a pickup truck and a minivan, he almost felt relieved. It was intact, luckily. The funny thing was, this car was not his. It was Tolkien’s mom’s old car, actually; she’d replaced it a year ago with a newer Honda Civic and left this bad boy to her son, who had his own car and barely used this thing. The thing is, getting access to it had cost Cartman a few major favors — it was a complicated arrangement of debts he'd rather not think about. Of course, these details weren’t particularly important; they didn’t swear for absolute secrecy about the whole arrangement, but it wasn’t like Tolkien was gonna snoop around anyway. He had better things to do.

It’s wasn’t like had anyone else to do a deal with, anyway. Tolkien was the only one with enough money to fund his journey to Scotland, even if he wasn’t fully aware of the details. Who else would he ask?

Cartman threw his suitcase in the back and slammed the trunk harder than necessary. He got behind the wheel, closed the door, and just sat there for a moment in the quiet of the parking garage. The rage within his body felt like it might split him open from the inside.

“Ugh.”

He leaned forward and pressed his forehead against the steering wheel. To his surprise, instead of a normal car horn, well, uh, a rather electronic-like version of ”La Cucaracha” echoed through the vehicle.

BEEP-beep-beep-beep, BEEP-beep-beep-beep, BEEP-BEEP-BEEP-BEEP-BEEP-BEEP-BEEP!

Well, something like that. He lifted his head, stared at the steering wheel for about half a minute, then slammed his forehead down again. And again. And again. And again. What was Tolkien thinking?

“Oh, you piece of shit!” he screamed at the dashboard. How did that happen?

The absurdity of it all almost made him laugh. Almost. Instead, it just made him angrier. How did he not notice that? Hmm, well, it wasn’t like he had actually tested it, to be fair. He’d barely used the car until a few hours before he went to pick up Butters. Maybe that was his fault after all.

After a few moments of laughing and sighing (sometimes both), he started the car and peeled out of the parking garage with more aggression than the little Civic’s engine could really handle. The two-hour drive back to South Park stretched ahead of him, but at least it gave him time to think. Time to plan and, of course, time to figure out exactly who needed to pay for this clusterfuck.

The highway unwound in front of him while his mind churned through the list of traitors. Heh. Butters was the obvious starting point — that little blonde moron couldn’t keep his mouth shut to save his life. But as much as Cartman wanted to blame him entirely, he had to admit he’d never explicitly told Butters to keep quiet about the trip. That was a tactical error on his part. You couldn’t get mad at a dog for being a dog.

No, the real betrayal came from Scott Malkinson. Of course it had to be him. Scott, with his stupid lisp and his stupid diabetes and his stupid irrelevance. Well, not that Cartman ever used this against him, no…. uh, he could never do such a thing. He had many friends with diabetes; he could never hate someone for it. If anything, he actually liked Scott, but for him to be the one who ran to Kyle… well, that was the ultimate insult. It was betrayal from someone who should have known better, for crying out loud.

Okay, yeah, it’s not like Cartman told Scott about the plan. He might’ve told Butters otherwise, but that was just to nudge him into participating in their winner-take-all plan, where they wuld be the winners, and would take, uh, all that money. In the end, they got nothing.

If it had been Craig or Clyde or even Jimmy, Cartman might have understood. They were peers, sort of, equals in their little circle of dysfunction. It wasn’t like they would’ve cared anyway. Jimmy would’ve just made a crappy joke about it, and that would’ve been it, but even then he was trying to be the newest Jimmy Fallon. This had its advantages and many, many, many drawbacks.

But Scott? No, no. He was essentially a bottom-feeder, and someone who should have been grateful just to be included in anything. It sucked, and Cartman sort of understood that, but what else could he do? For him to go crying to Kyle the second things got complicated, no, that was unforgivable.

By the time the familiar mountains came into view and the “Welcome to South Park” sign appeared on the horizon, Cartman’s plan had crystallized into something simple and direct. He was going to find Scott Malkinson and beat the living shit out of him. Maybe that would send a message to the rest of his loser friends…

About 20 minutes later, he screeched to a halt in front of the Malkinsons’ house. It was a tidy little place with a well-maintained lawn and flower boxes. In other words, just like every other house in that town (unless you were someone like Tolkien). It was annoyingly middle class — although he couldn’t really complain about it, having once lived inside a former hot dog store.

The Honda’s horn gave one final toot of “La Cucaracha” as he killed the engine, and he stomped up the walkway like he was marching to war. (Which, in a way, it was, thinking about it.) His fists hammered against the front door with enough force to rattle the frame.

“SCOTT! GET YOUR ASS OUT HERE, YOU LITTLE BITCH!”

The door opened a crack, and Scott’s pale, terrified face appeared in the gap. His eyes went wide behind his glasses when he saw Cartman standing there, looking like he’d crawled straight out of hell. This was half-true.

“E-Eric? What are you doing here? I thought you were in Scotland or—”

“You thought wrong!” Cartman roared, shouldering the door open and sending Scott stumbling backward into the foyer. He grabbed the front of Scott’s polo shirt — when did he even start wearing these things? It was some preppy thing in light blue that made him look even more pathetic than usual — and yanked him close. “You couldn’t keep your mouth shut, could you? You had to go crying to Kyle like some kind of little bitch! You ruined everything!”

Scott’s voice came out in a panicked stammer. “I-I was worried! Butters called and he sounded really scared and I thought—”

“Butters is always scared! That’s his default setting! Like, come on.” Cartman shook him hard enough to make his glasses slip down his nose. “Your job was to be a friend, and that means keeping your goddamn mouth—”

“Eric, stop it.”

The voice cut through his rage like a blade. It was quiet but firm. Cartman froze, his hands still gripping Scott’s shirt, and turned his head slowly toward the front door.

Of course it was him.

Butters stood on the porch, his duffel bag slung over one shoulder. He looked just about the same, which was to be expected. Not that Cartman would’ve expected something else, it’s just that, well… there was something that immediately set him off.

“H-How the hell did you get here?” Cartman demanded. He was genuinely thrown off balance by this. If he had a coin every time a blonde kid he met had mysterious teleportation powers, he’d have…. uh… one coin, at least.

“Uh, Gregory gave me a ride.”

Well, make that two coins.

“All the way across the ocean!? What, does that kid have wings or something?”

Butters stepped inside and let the door close softly behind him. He didn’t answer that question.

“Look, Eric,” Butters continued, trying his best to meet Cartman’s eyes without flinching. “I-I’m sorry I told Scott about the trip without asking you first. That was wrong, and it’s my fault. I should have known better.”

For a second, Cartman felt a flicker of satisfaction. Finally, an apology. At least someone here could take responsibility. Well, besides himself, but it’s not like he did it often…

“But this?” his friend gestured at the scene. Cartman’s hands were still twisted in Scott’s shirt and Scott’s face was white with terror. If anything, to Butters, it looked like he was about to kill him. ”This is exactly why they’re having that party, Eric. You don’t get it, b-but they’re scared of you. And-and… you can’t just treat the rest of us like we’re just toys for you to break when you get bored.”

The silence that followed was deafening. Scott had gone completely still in Cartman’s grip, too terrified to even breathe loudly. Somewhere outside, a lawn mower started up. Probably one of the neighbors?

Something shifted in Cartman’s head. Perhaps not guilt; he was sorta incapable of that particular emotion. Not remorse, either. To be honest, he was partially confused over how Butters even got there. Even if they took a flight around the same time, he’d still need to drive here, and he was pretty sure Butters would not remember the directions back home.

Either way, he had to at least partially admit that Butters was right. Although Cartman didn’t always expect for his usual tactics (from intimidation to manipulation to brute force to many more!) to work, it felt as if they had backfired on him more often than not. But was it true? Had his actions driven away the few people who might have actually helped him? If so, well, those same people were throwing a party to celebrate his absence. His predictability had become his weakness, one could say.

He needed a new strategy.

Slowly, and deliberately, Cartman released his grip on Scott’s shirt. He even smoothed out the wrinkles with a pat that was almost gentle; it was a gesture that was somehow more unnerving than all his shouting had been.

“Huh. Well, uh. The party,” he said, his voice now eerily calm. “Is it still happening?”

Scott nodded, still shaking. “Y-yeah. Tonight, at Tolkien’s. It starts at seven.”

“Good.” Cartman turned to Butters, and there was a new, calculating glint in his expression, whatever it actually meant. “We’ll be there.”

The “we” wasn’t a question or a request. It was a statement of fact. They would be there, like it or not.

He turned back to Scott, and his whole demeanor shifted again. By that point his rage was gone — or at least not as visible. “Scott,” he said, his voice dropping to an almost pleading tone. “You can’t tell anyone we’re back. Not Kyle, not your girlfriend, not anyone. This has to be a surprise. I… I don’t think I can handle having my trust broken again right now. It’s uh, not very cool. You get what it’s like not being cool, you know.”

It was, of course, manipulation. Playing the victim, making Scott feel guilty for his “betrayal,” and all that while setting up whatever scheme was percolating in Cartman’s head. Perhaps he truly hadn’t learned anything. But underneath his performance, there was a sliver of genuine pain. His ego, that precious and fragile thing at the center of his identity, had taken a real beating. Worse: he didn’t know if it was because he had been proved wrong or because other people (like Butters) had been proven right. It was all confusing.

Scott looked between Cartman’s wounded expression and Butters’ rather stoic face, clearly out of his depth. “O-okay, Eric,” he stammered. “I won’t tell anyone. I promise.”

“Thank you,” Cartman nodded. To him, it felt like Scott had just done him the greatest favor in the world. He reached into his jacket pocket and pulled out a folded piece of paper, which he handed to Butters. “Here. That’s for your parents.”

Butters unfolded it carefully. It was some kind of certificate, printed on cheap paper with a garish, curly font. At the top it read: “The Edinburgh Institute of Visionary Photographic Arts.” Below that, it certified that a certain Leopold Stotch had successfully completed a “week-long intensive course in Advanced Cryptid Documentation.” And of course, at the bottom was an illegible signature attributed to someone called “Scotland Guy.”

He sighed.

“What? I wasn’t gonna just let you off with a lie that would get me in trouble.”

It was the most ridiculous, pathetic thing Butters had ever seen. Well, it was in the top 10 of them for sure. But somehow, looking at it, he understood something about Eric that he’d never quite grasped before. Maybe this wasn’t just about ego or manipulation. Maybe this was about someone so desperate to be important, so terrified of being ordinary, that he’d create elaborate fantasies just to feel like he mattered…?

The only thing is that this person was Eric Cartman, and God knows how many things he’d done to fulfill his evil desires.

“I’m going home to plan,” Cartman announced, heading for the door. “Butters, I’ll pick you up here at seven. Be ready. I will.”

He paused at the threshold, his hand on the doorknob. “And Scott? Thanks for being a real friend. I know I can count on you.”

With that, he was gone, leaving behind only the faint sound of “La Cucaracha” as the Honda started up and pulled away from the curb.

Butters and Scott stood in the quiet of the foyer, both of them trying to process what the fuck had just happened. Scott was still slightly pale and shaky, and his polo shirt was wrinkled from Cartman’s grip. Maybe he didn’t like the shirt? And Butters was staring down at the fake certificate, turning it over in his hands like it might reveal some hidden truth. It did not.

“Jesus,” Scott whispered finally. “What was that?”

Butters looked up at him, and there was something almost sad in his expression. “That, uh…” he said quietly, “…well, I’m not sure, and I’ve probably said this before, but I’m starting to think things will never be the same again.”

“You sure?”

“I don’t know, yeah, probably.”
