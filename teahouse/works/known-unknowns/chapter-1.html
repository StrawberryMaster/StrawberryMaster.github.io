Chapter Summary

It's night at Bullworth.

The dorm was rather quiet that night. Besides the occasional sounds from the arcade machine in the common room, that is, or the half a dozen students huddled around the TV, annoyed because a poor soul managed to mess with it in a way that it stopped receiving any sort of signal; when they did get something, it was all audio and no video. In Bullworth terms, it was a rather pleasant evening.

At some point during that night, the creak of the dorm room door and a soft thud of footsteps jolted Pete awake as he, bleary-eyed and confused, stirred beneath his blankets. It was about 2:17 am, and the room was mostly pitch dark, besides a small area of the room that was bathed in a slight blue glow of moonlight. He cracked open an eye to see Gary, his roommate, now as a dark silhouette against the glow through the window, standing in the middle of the room. What was happening?

“Gary…?” he half-murmured and half-mumbled, sitting up and rubbing his eyes. “Wh-what are you doing, man?”

Although the sharp contrast of light and shadows obscured his features, it couldn’t mask his raspy, weary voice.

“Petey,” Gary whispered. “Pretend you didn’t see me.”

Pete groaned. He was dumb but not this dumb. “I’d have to be blind not to see you.” he mumbled. Squinting through the darkness at Gary, he could now barely make out his roommate’s features in the dim light. If it wasn’t for his posture (and the cadence of voice), he could’ve easily mistaken him for the half a dozen roomgoers that wandered around the dorm. “What time is it?”

“Late,” he replied, his gaze fixed on the window. “Late enough, I guess. Prefects are gone, figured I’d take a walk.”

Pete’s brow furrowed. He shifted, pushing himself upright. The covers slipped down his chest, exposing him to the cool night air. “A walk? W-what are you talking about?”

“Just taking a walk,” Gary said, his voice clipped, almost defensive. “Needed some air.”

“Some… air? At this time of night? You know, I didn’t think you liked stargazing-”

Gary scoffed. “Not stargazing, dweeb. Fresh air. You should know what that looks like.”

“Right, right.”

This wasn’t exactly a new routine for Gary. Periodically he would go out the dorm and wander into the outside world, usually looking for something and coming back with none of what he’d actually wanted. (A bummer.) Still, sneaking out for a bit usually yielded a prize for him — from bags of chips from vending machines to issues of comic books he found dumpster diving (and that neither of them ever heard of!), or books with ripped covers that someone else had tossed in the trash. Mostly snacks, though, but it was far better than whatever Edna had been cooking these past few weeks. If he was lucky enough, he’d come back with some soggy fries and a not very happy-looking hot dog, like he did last week.

But lately, Gary’s nighttime adventures had become few and far between. Pete usually knew when he was missing, even if he didn’t always catch him returning. Last night, for example, Pete was three chapters deep into a book about the Yugoslav Wars by the time he realized Gary wasn’t in his bed. Weird, sure, but nothing to lose sleep over; he slept at 10 o’clock, just like most days.

Now, though, with Gary looming by the window like a brooding gargoyle, Pete couldn’t help but nudge for an explanation.

“So, w-where’d you go?” he pressed.

“Around,” Gary mumbled, barely audible. “Doesn’t matter. Just clearing my head. I heard the nerds buzzing about some new kid coming next week. Apparently, he’s got a face like a bulldog… what a moron.”

Pete frowned. “New kid? Who told you?”

“I heard them whispering in the observatory, that Cornelius kid,” he said, nonchalantly.

“Who’s Cornelius again?”

“The black-haired kid.”

“That’s his name?”

Gary shrugged.

“And why were they in the Observatory… at 2 a.m… out of all possible times?”

He was ignored.

“Anyway, this new kid, let’s say… he’s got some baggage — kicked out of his last school. How pathetic… didn’t even get a participation trophy! Should be fun to break him in—”

“…at this time of night.”

“And when we’re done… we throw him onto a vat of acid. You writing this down?”

Pete almost winced. Gary’s definition of “fun” was usually synonymous with someone else’s misery. The half-dozen other “new kids” who’d come and gone over the years (assuming Gary wasn’t embellishing as usual) could probably attest to that. Unfortunately, there were few of them around to tell the story.

Gary let out a harsh cackle. “You know, I wouldn’t be complaining if I were you… unless you’re really a fan of Edna’s cooking. Mystery meat surprise coming up tomorrow, femme-boy!” he gestured vaguely towards the ceiling with his thumb. “Probably leftover gym socks and shit, for all I know.”

He stood frozen, a statue bathed in the cool moonlight. It kinda reminded Pete of the mascot statue near the fountain. He continued staring outside for maybe a minute or two, and then, without another word, he turned away from the window and climbed back into his bed, the springs creaking in protest.

And Pete stared, still confused, at whatever just happened. What the hell was Gary even doing out there? Unless he was teaming up with Earnest and the Nerds on something — they would love his presence in their G&G club — but it didn’t seem likely. Though he could never really rule out anything, knowing how he was, why now out of all times? Either they were having some sort of special event there and Gary just so happened to be passing by when he heard the news, or he was lying. Though the latter was most likely, Pete couldn’t fully rule out the former either. It was confusing.

“Good night… I guess?”

Sleep seemed impossible now, he guessed — but it’s not like Gary would reply back, right? With a sigh, Pete pulled the thin sheet tighter around himself. Closing his eyes, he tried his best not to focus on the snores now emanating from the other bed. Again, why now, out of all times?

He didn’t reply back.

Turns out Gary was right. A new kid had joined later that day — some copper-haired guy named Jimmy Hopkins. Granted, he did not have a particularly friendly face, though he seemed far more open to talking to them than most of the school, which was already a net positive. Unfortunately, Gary was still being Gary.

“Oooh,” he drawled, leaning back dramatically in Jimmy’s dorm chair, “Look out. Here comes Jimmy. ‘I’m feeling self-important now that I finally hit puberty!’”

Of course, Gary, bless his twisted little heart, couldn’t let a new face walk in without being disturbed. He was being true to his essence, perhaps. His cruelty easily defined him, unfortunately.

Pete blinked. “I’m sorry?”

Gary scoffed. “What’s your problem, Pete? I’m just being nice to the new kid – as he passes through Bullworth on his inevitable journey to prison.”

Undeterred, Jimmy raised an eyebrow as he scanned the room. “Prison, huh? Sounds exciting. Do you guys wanna give me some time to settle in and get unpacked?”

“You are none too interesting yourselves, friend.” Patting Jimmy on the shoulder a little too hard, Gary rose from the chair and walked out of the room, while also looking at Pete. “Jimmy can’t stand you already.”

He sighed, giving Jimmy a tired but friendly smile. “Jimmy, if you want to hang out w-we’ll wait in the dorm downstairs.”

Following Gary out, Pete caught a glimpse of the amusement flickering in Jimmy’s eyes before the door shut. Gary, already halfway down the hallway, tossed a comment back over his shoulder. “Did you see how he pummeled that prick Wade? Oh my God, this is gonna be so fun!” he chuckled as he scurried upstairs, probably back to their dorm. Pete, just a few feet behind, nodded with his head. By the time he finished nodding, Gary was already gone.

Pete didn’t see much of Jimmy that morning. He was very busy being introduced to the school by Gary, apparently, so that must’ve gone well. Sure, he wasn’t the first to get a front-row seat to a three-ring circus, but he hoped he would be the last one. Making friends at Bullworth was easier said than done. The Nerds thought Pete was a dork and that Gary was a psycho, which admittedly… was not far off from the truth. The Preps would never accept them, despite both being Vale residents themselves. It was actually funny — they all lived in the same street and yet were treated so differently because they were less rich than the others. The Jocks were a no-go, the Greasers were kinda scary, and there’d be no way they could fool the other Bullies (besides Russell, of course) into joining them.

This also made their options much more limited. The girls? Forget about it — Pete could maybe manage a strained conversation with Beatrice about the weekend’s chemistry homework, and maybe some of them would listen if he offered them the answers to Mr. Galloway’s latest tests — but that was about as far as it went. The rest of the student body just… ignored them. Not even the rats and cockroaches in their dorm room (who’d occasionally scuttle across his drawings in the dead of night) would befriend either. Oh well.

As he left the Kafkaesque nightmare known as Shop class that afternoon, ignoring the slight smell of oil in his hands, a blur in a white vest — a Bully, no question —careened into him at considerable velocity, sending Pete and his books sprawling towards the floor. Ouch. As he gathered himself from the linoleum, wincing at his brand new set of bruises, he spotted Jimmy approaching from behind, evidently in pursuit of the other guy. Fortunately, he decelerated to avoid a similar collision, looking down at Pete with a mix of amusement and something that might have been concern.

“I saw him, he ran towards the fountain!”

“Thanks!” Jimmy said, throwing a quick grin Pete’s way as he darted off down the fountain.

“Can I help—” Pete started to say, but he was already a blur around the corner.

Goddammit.

Later that evening, now in his dorm room, Pete hunched over his sketchbook, a faint smile playing on his lips. Unlike his usual battlescapes, tonight’s drawings were simpler — a wonky robot battling a stick figure dragon, a lopsided house with a bright yellow sun peeking over its roof. Ms. Phillips once told him his drawings would fit in a children’s book, and maybe she had a point; they were kinda cute. Lost in his own world, he barely registered the rhythmic tapping of Gary’s pen against his desk.

The earlier events of the day replayed in his mind. He lingered for a moment, a strange feeling settling in his gut, but thought Jimmy seemed much nicer than, well, the rest of the other students. (An easy line to cross, given how the average student there was.) Maybe that was a good sign? Some of the other new kids that came over time seriously scared him. Jimmy, well, just a bit — but not as much as the others.

Pete had learned to read people quickly at Bullworth, and while Jimmy definitely had a certain edge, it didn’t come off as mean-spirited. If anything, it looked like he was someone who’d been through some stuff. He seemed decent.

Back to the present, the background tapping stopped, replaced by a heavy sigh. “Hey, Petey,” Gary’s voice, a touch too loud, broke the stillness.

Pete looked up, startled. He hadn’t realized how long he’d been in his own head. Oops. There was a dark smudge of charcoal on his cheek, though he didn’t notice it. “Yeah?”

“So… what do you think of Hopkins?” Gary asked, his gaze fixed on the star-dusted night sky. It was a beautiful night outside.

Pete paused, considering his answer. Jimmy seemed like a riddle — seemingly tough, but with a surprising glint of something else in his eyes. He didn’t seem bad, for the lack of a better word. At worst he’d just be a slightly less annoying Bullworth student.

“Different,” Pete said finally, choosing his words carefully.

Gary scoffed. “Different? He’s probably just another punk with a chip on his shoulder. Thinks he’s clever or some shit. Not smarter than me, that’s for sure…”

He didn’t reply. He felt a dull pressure in his gut, an unease that made him close his sketchbook more carefully than necessary. Thing is, this wasn’t like their usual back-and-forth. Gary hadn’t yet decided whether to manipulate, provoke, or ignore Jimmy — although he already seemed to be aiming for the first option —, and that made Pete nervous

“Maybe,” Pete said softly. “But maybe that’s not a bad thing.”

Silence soon followed, and not the comfortable kind. After a minute or two, Gary finally replied back.

“You know,” he mumbled, barely above a whisper, “sometimes I wish things were different.”

The raw honesty in Gary’s voice sent a shiver down Pete’s spine. He wasn’t sure how to respond, honestly, given this glimpse into a part of him that he rarely ever saw. He could count on one hand the number of times Gary had ever opened up about anything personal – especially if it was sincere, which this felt suspiciously like. Well, recently, that is — if this were happening five years ago, it’d have been a completely different story…

“You okay?”

Gary turned from the window, his face hidden in the shadows. “Forget it,” he muttered. The tapping resumed, faster this time. Much faster.

Huh.

Pete lay back on his bunk, staring at the ceiling, chewing on the silence between them. He thought about pressing Gary and asking what he meant — but something stopped him; his brain. When Gary was in a certain mood, it was better to give him space rather than starting a conversation with him. It wasn’t something that worked always, but it’s what he had done for a good while now, and it usually worked. At least he wouldn’t get hurt if he didn’t touch him with a 10-foot pole. And yet, something about that moment lingered. It was a weird feeling.

As he drifted off, Gary let out a quiet laugh — at whatever he was thinking in his bonked head.

Pete blinked at the ceiling.

Yeah. Maybe, he started to think, this was just the beginning of a long, strange adventure.
