Chapter Summary

Pete gets some advice from an unexpected source. Meanwhile, Gary lives.

The library was a sanctuary from the usual Bullworth chaos.

It was no wonder why the nerds liked hanging out there. Hushed whispers, the scent of old paper… it was an atmosphere of almost complete tranquility, which compared to the rest of the campus almost seemed surreal.

Pete was hunched over a worn-out sketchbook, his pencil dancing across the page. As it was well known at this point — by himself — he had nothing better to do. Ms. Phillips, in one of her rare moments of lucidity, had advised him on a project idea — to draw the world from the perspective of a banana. It was absurd, but he couldn’t deny that it was a damn good idea. Why not?

As he worked, his eyes wandered around the room. The usual cast of characters was missing – curiously, there were not many nerds there. Sure, it’s not like he could’ve expected something different, as many of them preferred to not stay in the school during Christmas. But there were a few of them around.

Two nerds with glasses — one of which he recognized as Melvin, the other one he forgot — were seated at a table playing some board game. Grottos and Gremlins? If only. Two people couldn’t run a good campaign; being terrible at the game, Pete would rather not be the third wheel to whatever adventure there were having. Although he would not be opposed to a nerd friend, he was well aware doing this would destroy whatever is left of his reputation at school. Gary would definitely dump him for trashcan food, that’s for sure.

But his curiosity piqued and Pete moved closer. The board game was a rather-cheap copy of “Guess Who?”, the classic game of deduction where players tried to identify a mystery character by asking a series of yes or no questions. The nerd with glasses whose name was not Melvin was losing — badly — and evidence was clear in the empty glass on the table. It was filled to the brim with a murky brown liquid, which he just assumed to be chocolate milk, but even that was gone. Since Mrs. Carvin wasn’t on at this time of morning, they could practically do whatever they wanted! Besides the fact that, well, they were the only ones who hung around there often.

Pete couldn’t help but chuckle as he thought about this. Though he returned to his drawing, he couldn’t shake the feeling that he was missing out. Not the board game, of course — but something else. Maybe, just maybe, the idea that he complained too much about things and did nothing about them had finally gotten into him. And no drawing of a banana could fix that, as much as it pained for him to say this.

But he was gonna finish that drawing, for sure.

With his pencil moving with a rhythmic grace across the page, he had managed to capture what it finally meant to be a banana. A yellow peel contemplating its purpose in life, in complete existential crisis – he was just adding the finishing touches when a voice interrupted his creative flow.

“Pete, what are you doing, drawing pictures of bananas?” Melvin, the nerd from earlier, stood over him, his voice dripping with disdain.

Pete looked up, startled. “Oh hey, Melvin,” he managed, trying to hide his amusement. “Just, uh, working on something.”

He rolled his eyes.

“You should be hanging out with your loser friends, not wasting time on banana art,” he sneered.

But Pete couldn’t help but grin. Wasn’t he the one who got his ass beaten by one of the greasers earlier this week in the parking lot? At least his dignity was still there, he was no loser material. (He later realized he was now sounding too much like Jimmy.) “I’m good, thanks. And for your information, I don’t have any loser friends.”

Melvin’s face turned a shade of crimson. “Don’t be cocky, Petey,” he retorted, but his voice was much more uncertain than it was a minute ago. “You mess with me, and you’ll regret it. I know things you don’t.”

“Like what? How to not piss yourself?”

“Like where your ‘pen pal’ Gary is.”

“What do you mean, you know where Gary is?”

Okay, it was pretty funny that almost every time Gary was mentioned, it usually involved Pete chasing after him or discovering his whereabouts. Hmm. When did this turn into Carmen Sandiego-levels of weirdness?

Smirking, Melvin was clearly enjoying his newly found power dynamic. “I am everywhere and nowhere… but usually everywhere, fellow traveler. And I know where your little friend is hiding. But first, you gotta apologize for being a jerk.”

Pete hesitated. Apologize to Melvin? Come on, the guy was a walking encyclopedia of useless information, and he’d rather faceplant the sidewalk than do it. Since he was just going around in circles until that moment, however, he had to find a way to make Pete’s Very Own Adventure™ at least sound promising. If that meant going after Gary again, then so be it. But this time, he’d be smart to stick by him — and actually try it.

“Fine,” Pete muttered, his dignity taking a non-negligible hit. “I’m sorry.”

Melvin’s smirk widened. “Good boy. Now, listen up. You know that big, ugly building in the northeast corner of the city?”

“Which one? This place is huge.”

“That greaseball dump. Some hugeass building. It’s condemned, but people still live there. Your psycho friend has a room of his own there!”

The Tenements?

Well, that was honestly believable, given all the “Gary eats from the trashcan” references he had overused in his mind for half a year already. But what the hell was he doing there?

Ugh. He was asking too many questions again. Nodding, Pete just tried to process the information.

“Thanks, Melvin,” he said, more to himself than to the other boy.

Melvin just shrugged.

“Wait. How did you know where he was?”

He chuckled. “I’m his shrink.”

And he walked away, a triumphant look on his face.

Oooh, foggy.

The Tenements were kinda scary — the whole thing looked like a monstrous, decaying tooth, a blight on the already decaying New Coventry skyline.

In retrospect, Gary could’ve found a better place to stay. He should’ve. If he had just bribed a nerd here and a nerd there, he could’ve easily used half of the observatory to do his planning. Had he been just a tad more insistent, he could’ve changed his mind and stayed in the much cozier spare dorm room in Harrington House. If he had been just a bit braver… he could’ve stayed in his good old dorm room, and all he would need to do was find a way to get little Pete to shut up.

He stood at the cracked balcony door, his silhouette stark against the city lights.

Jeez, it was cold out there. Yeah dumbass, it’s winter, he thought, but it wasn’t his fault that his, erm, improvised outfit of choice wasn’t the best. Unfortunately for him, he left the majority of his good clothes on campus, meaning he had to live with his school vest and leftover clothes from Righty or whatever his name was. Even then, this made him easily better than Hobo Hopkins or future Teletubbies star Petey.

Hey, Gary.

Oh yeah, this voice was getting into his head again. Goddammit. Every time this kid showed up where he was, he’d try to make some contact. Sure, he didn’t mind, but could he have some fucking peace for once? Taking over the school was easier said than done, especially if he stopped interrupting his plans.

Can we talk for once?

He’d popped a Lunesta® just minutes ago, and it wasn’t working. He was not gonna concentrate if he didn’t sleep well, and femme-boy’s voice was not helping. “Um, I’m not sure about this!” or whatever he’d say. No, he was too busy with the school’s swim team or barnyard animals to help him out. Just like James.

Can you look at me for once?

When he turned back, mostly out of habit, well, there was a surprise. There, leaning against the crumbling brick wall, was Pete, his face illuminated by the dim glow of the hallway.

“You’re not a voice in my head after all?” Gary smirked. “Maybe you were right, Petey. I should’ve killed myself when I had the chance!”

Pete sighed.

“Look, we’ve been through this before. I don’t wanna go around-”

“How did you even find me?”

“Melvin?”

No way. Gary chuckled bitterly. “Melvin? You believe anything that guy says?”

Pete grinned. “No, but this time he was right.”

Gary made a mental note to take care of the nerd later. Unfortunately, Melvin was a good supplier on information around the school, so he couldn’t just get rid of him. But he’d find a way to deal with this.

“Oh, come on,” he said, walking back towards the edge of the room. “Are you his lost puppy now? You got tired of James and decided to torment me or something?”

“Because you’re my friend, dipshit. Of course I care.” Pete tried his best to ignore Gary’s cold demeanor, staying put beside him.

“I don’t need your pity, or whatever it is you’re trying to do.” he murmured, voice low and resentful.

“Y-you’re not gonna get anything done by moving left and right to Bullworth’s slums, dude. I don’t care what you do but you gotta stop with this Ozymandias mentality.”

Gary looked at Pete irritably. That might’ve actually offended him. It was one hell of a gamble, stepping up to him; he’d only done it a few times and usually backed down for fear of getting hit — or something worse.

“Oh, so now you’re giving me life advice?” he snapped, his irritation increasing. “And don’t tell me what to do. I’m bigger than all of these bastards in school. When time comes, they’re all gonna be a billion particles of sand in the fucking Sahara, and I am gonna be a statue erected in gold.”

“Dude, I don’t care about your plan or whatever you wanna do anymore. I just want us to be friends, I-I don’t know, hang out more often.”

“Look,” he muttered, his voice much less hostile now, as he came closer to Pete. “I don’t need your friendship, okay? I only need respect. You made your choices and you paid the price — can’t do anything about that.”

That made no sense, he should’ve known that. Pete never betrayed him! Nor did Jimmy, but that was hard to get out of his head. He was one tough nut to crack. Tired, he hesitantly reached out for Gary’s hand, trying to bridge the gap between them.

“Just… can’t we just-” Pete started, but before he could finish, Gary’s hand swiftly swatted his away.

Owww. That hurt.

“Just leave, Pete,” he said, his voice low and steady. “I don’t need you here. I don’t need anyone.”

He’d tried, hadn’t he? It was worth a try, but maybe Gary was right. Maybe they were too far gone.

And it was a shame, really, because they had a friendship that was hard to define. They might not have been friends forever, but they’d known each other for half their lives already. He was no perfect friend — he had his flaws and Pete knew this well; the amount of times they’d got in trouble together would probably surprise a couple of people. And even though there was a distancing as of late, he thought there was still something in there. Maybe there still is — but it has not shown any signs of coming back, at least for now.

With a heavy heart, Pete turned to leave. “Look, I get it,” he said, his voice rather quiet. “Just… just don’t be a stranger. You know, you can always stop by the dorm. We can… talk or something. I don’t know, try to be normal again.”

Gary didn’t respond. He merely watched Pete as he walked away, the sound of his footsteps echoing through what was once supposed to be an apartment. His doom didn’t have a door; when his figure disappeared from his eyesight, a heavy silence settled over the room.

Alone once more, he sighed and slumped against the wall.

Across the street, one young boy watched Pete’s departure with unease, a scoop of ice cream melting in his hand. Hmm, vanilla. His eyes followed Pete until he disappeared from view, wondering what he was doing there at this time of night. Eh, just Petey business, he thought. Mounting his bike, he swiftly pedaled away.
