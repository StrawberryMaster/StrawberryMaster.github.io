Perhaps unsurprisingly, things had developed pretty fast since Pete’s last encounter with Gary — albeit not in a good way.

For starters, Jimmy’s relationship with the greasers, which was doing somewhat okay up until then — with Jimmy mostly doing tasks for their head, Johnny —, collapsed soon after Jimmy told Pete that he’d personally sent them a message to show who’s boss, by tagging “Jimmy wuz here!!” near the overpass. God, that was horrible. Not so much the graffiti — though it sucked, quite frankly — but the idea of tagging it in the first place.

You said you were gonna make amends with them!, Pete thought. Of course, Jimmy wasn’t obliged to suck up to them like he’d been doing with the bullies and nerds up until then, but maybe he could’ve been more thoughtful. It’s the thought that counts, Jimmy would probably say, but he hadn’t realize he set back his own progress then — and this thought couldn’t easily be perished.

In hindsight, he later understood that, although dumb, Jimmy’s hands were tied; the preps were the 800-pound gorilla in the room that he conveniently tried to ignore, to no avail. Last year, when Gary was still around, he once remarked to Pete that Jimmy, although a brutus of his own, was the weakest link in their friend group because of his supposed inability to think properly; basically, he was their nothingburger.

“How can someone so dumb have been born?” Gary bashed him. It was after dark on a Saturday night, and he faintly smelled like booze. He wasn’t sure how that happened. “Well, fuck. That’s it. It’s just like what they always say… meat only falls on a vegan’s plate, Petey, and we’re the goddamn osturduckens. Shit.”

“Ostur-what?”

“Come on, osturduckens. It’s an ostrich stuffed with a turkey stuffed with a duck stuffed with a chicken. We ate it last year on my birthday…”

His bowel wished he didn’t remember this moment. Pete had no idea if he was drunk that night or if he was referring to something else he might’ve missed — he didn’t really pay much attention to what Gary was saying, honestly —, but maybe there was a grain of truth to what he’d said then. Jimmy was, erm, not the smartest human being out there. The preps basically ruled Bullworth, and if a beating couldn’t get them in line, well, not much could. They each had different interests, which clashed with each other, leading to Jimmy picking his allies over his future allies. Maybe not an entirely bad decision.

Now that being nice was out of the cards, Jimmy started doing the exact opposite. For some reason, this also included fooling around with Lola (maybe to mock the greasers?) and other people too, it seemed. What was his name again? Oh, Vance. What did they even have in common? Sure, these were just rumors… but there was no arguing that Jimmy was his very own Romeo.

At some point, things in New Coventry escalated pretty quickly into an all-out gang war. Good news for the rest of Bullworth; the only thing that would drive down real estate prices in this economy was a good-old fashioned gang war — apart from an outbreak of plague, but this was more of a Gary idea than a Pete idea. Kids whacking each other left and right, it was one hell of a chaos. And he was too curious to not poke around… when he realized, he was there, in a junkyard in a northern part of town, surrounded by a mob of angry greasers who wanted to take him down. Helping Jimmy take down Johnny, and maybe half a dozen others too. Despite everything, he was still there — by his side. Birds of a feather…

Jimmy didn't know, but Pete stood in that exact same spot for about 18 minutes before he arrived. How lucky!

Curiously, he hadn’t heard about Gary in a while. He hadn’t visited him, and likewise, he hadn’t visited him either. And maybe it was for the best after all; sure, he still needed a roommate, and he was basically begging to do something with Jimmy not related to fighting cliques or being the third wheel in whatever he was planning (that was still a work in progress, albeit one that was not easy.)

With Gary looming elsewhere, Jimmy now had control of all cliques except the jocks, which pretty much made him one step closer to being “king of the school”. Ironic, since that wasn’t exactly his goal — but it was easier than finding where Gary was, it seemed. Pete, of course, had aided him in this specific task by doing absolutely nothing. What else did he have to gain by concealing his location? Given how Gary changed his actual location every now and then, one would easily assume he’d be huddling with the jocks at this point. And yet he kept it secret anyway, not gaining any sympathy with his old-time friend but keeping his new friend in contention.

Ugh. He really wished for this to end.

One particular night, Pete was in the common room checking his notes. Art block had struck again. He’d already filled a page with potential subjects — mostly inanimate objects — as he’d realized his fruit obsession might be getting a little out of hand. (Last time he drew one, things didn’t go as well.) The TV droned on in the background, some random series about a married guy with a family playing as ambient noise. What was the name of it again? He forgot. There must’ve been at least five shows with this theme right now on the timeslot.

A shadow fell over his work, and he looked up, not surprised to find the devil himself — Gary — leaning against the couch. “Jeez,” Pete thought to himself. “I must’ve committed a pretty bad crime in a past life, because I don’t know why he keeps coming back.”

Although he was not in a bad mood, he really wanted to shut down the whole thing. The more he talked to Gary, the less things made sense. Maybe that was his intention… to trick his mind. Would be smart, but maybe it was just Gary being inconvenient to him. Pete sighed, setting down his notes on the couch.

“Look, Gary,” he started, his voice weary. “I-I don’t know why you keep showing up like this. You made it pretty clear you didn’t want anything to do with me.”

This was true — it came from Gary’s mouth, after all — but he looked taken aback by Pete’s response, clearly not expecting him to counterattack so soon.

“What? No, no. I just wanted to ask if you wanted to go for a walk. Chill.”

Pete shook his head. “You said you didn’t want to be friends. I don’t get why you keep coming back and acting like nothing happened. It’s kind of freaking me out.”

Gary ran a hand through his hair, a gesture that looked more like a nervous tic than a cool pose. He didn’t seem particularly confident, although confidence is not something he had been harboring (much) for a while. “Okay, yeah, maybe I said that. I was pissed. But I didn’t mean it. Can we just forget about it and go for a walk?”

But Pete hesitated. “You hurt me, Gary. I can’t just forget about it, act like nothing happened.”

A cocky smirk crept onto Gary’s face. “Oh, poor you. You’re such a drama queen. A little slap and you’re all wounded. I expected more from you, Petey.”

Yeah, this wasn’t going anywhere. He stood up, gathering his notes. “Maybe you should go for that walk alone, Gary,” he said quietly.

Just as Pete was about to turn and walk away, however, Gary’s hand closed around his wrist. “Come on, man, just a quick chat. Let’s go to ou- your room.”

With a heavy sigh, he nodded. Here we go again.

They walked to Pete’s room in silence; it was funny to imagine they once shared this same room! Many memories here, both good ones and bad ones. (Mostly bad ones.) He looked around — Pete had kept the room fairly clean, which was not surprising but still pleasant enough —, his usual energy replaced by a dullness that was almost unsettling.

“Look,” Gary began, his voice uncharacteristically quiet. “I know I’ve been an ass. I’ve got my reasons, you know why, but that doesn’t excuse it. I just… I need someone to talk to. And you’re the only one who might understand.”

Pete watched him for a moment before sitting down across from him. “Alright, Gary. Let’s talk.”

Gary sat on the edge of Pete’s bed — which was fair, given it was the closest to the door —, his posture slumped, and soon he flopped down there, watching as Pete closed the room’s only door. Damn, was his bed ever this comfortable?

Staring at the ceiling as if it held the answers to his myriad of questions, Pete patiently waited.

“I’ve been trying to figure things out,” Gary finally said, his voice low. “I thought I could handle everything on my own, you know? But it’s all just… spiraled out of control. So rapidly, too.”

“What do you mean?”

Pete assumed that Gary knew the obvious; Jimmy was just moments away from catching him, but Gary’s revenge never seemed close enough to actually getting him. Any time he did something close to revenge, such as the Hole incident or the “me and the preps made an omelette out of Jimmy” incident, it’s like they never made more than a splash. With any clique, Gary started one step ahead of Jimmy and would usually end at least ten steps behind him.

“I know I’ve been a dick,” Gary continued, though he seemed uncomfortable with this admission. “I just… I don’t know. I’ve been dealing with a lot of stuff, see.”

He trailed off, avoiding Pete’s gaze. No point in pushing his buttons tonight, he thought.

“It’s okay,” Pete replied, trying to sound reassuring. It was an attempt. “We all have our stuff.”

Gary nodded, though his eyes remained distant. “Yeah, alright. Can I stay here for a while? I just need to get away from… everything else.”

Perhaps unsurprisingly, Pete’s heart softened at the request. Despite everything that had happened, he couldn’t deny that he missed having someone to talk to, especially with Jimmy getting so busy with his new friends left and right. Gary sure had his flaws, and Pete had seen a lot of them over the years, especially recently… but he didn’t have the courage to say no. “Sure, you can stay,” he said, trying to keep his tone casual.

They rested in silence for a few moments; Pete took this opportunity to look at his notes and also at his former roommate, who still hadn’t left the left side of his bed. For some reason, he was sporting a dark navy-colored shirt of Dewey from Malcolm in the Middle, alongside a Bullworth Bullhorns sports jacket; they probably weren’t his, he never really liked the show. Nor the team. But it was a damn good shirt.

And honestly, the silence was better than virtually all of their conversations over the past months. Nice and quiet, just like Pete liked it. Arguably, it was better than nothing, even though part of him really wanted to move away from Gary for good. He didn’t want to be blinded by his own nostalgia, but he’d never get rid of a chance to preserve one of his longest-lasting friendships.

As the night deepened, Pete set aside his notes, carefully placing them in the drawer next to his bed. He thought of some good ideas, but decided that maybe just a simple landscape would be good enough. He stretched and let out a sigh, glancing over at Gary — who was now sprawled out on his bed, free and easy, eyes half-closed. Huh.

“You know you’re not sleeping in my bed, right?”

Gary just raised his hand and gave Pete the middle finger. Wow, what a shocker!

“Fine. Sleep wherever you want,” he said. Getting up and pulling out his pajamas, he changed quietly, trying not to disturb the poor man.

He chuckled softly to himself. The sight of Gary in his bed after all this time was at the very least amusing. It’s not like he had a hidden knife and wanted to kill him or something — or so he thought, at least. But huh. He turned off the lights, casting the room into darkness save for the faint glow from the lights outside his dorm window.

Settling into his own bed, Pete pulled the covers up and closed his eyes. This was strange, no other way to put it. He could very well wake up the next day to find him gone, but this wasn’t a concern anymore; in his mind, something strange was going on, and even though he had no idea of what it was — just like the other 50 times he also had no idea of what was happening —, or if it was just his brain pranking him, well, at least he had a few Nerd sources to maybe find out what was going on.

Jimmy was having some skirmishes with them, apparently. Or at least that’s what he made out of Gary’s mumbles.
