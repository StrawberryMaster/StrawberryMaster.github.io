Pete sat on the edge of Jimmy’s bed, trying to find a spot that wasn’t covered in discarded clothes or random junk. Jimmy’s dorm room was a mess, to say the least — it had gotten much worse as time went on — but Pete had gotten used to it. It was just part of who he was — he lived like he fought, with everything out in the open, nothing hidden. This, of course, had its drawbacks, but cloning him was not a realistic possibility. At least not yet.

The walls were plastered with his very own souvenirs: some ribbons; posters of movies and bands he honestly had no idea he liked; a rather unexpected pair of panties; photos and love letters from God knew how many people… he had a funny way of showing affection.

Jimmy was halfway through a bucket of fried chicken, talking between bites. “And you wouldn’t believe it, Petey. Earnest and the nerds went completely nuts. So I had to take those apeshit wussies down in the observatory.” He paused, wincing as he shifted in his seat. “And man, they had this… I don’t know… damn spud cannon thing. It shoots potatoes. Those potatoes hit hard. My ass still hurts from that thing…”

Although familiar with the nerds and their weapons of mass destruction — he’d be more likely to find them in the observatory than in Iraq — he hadn’t heard about any new inventions in a while. Something told him the spud cannon wasn’t made in mind to target Jimmy, but maybe to target the nearby jocks. Many months ago, the observatory had turned into a walled fortress; no one could get in, no one could get out, and unsurprisingly, no one in Bullworth cared. None of the other cliques ever came close to it. Crabblesnitch probably forgot it existed. Hell, even he did.

Even then, it wasn’t the nerds’ inventions or their betrayal — honestly, he’d be surprised if they didn’t betray him — that surprised Pete. Even though it was all so sudden, Earnest was one of the few clique leaders that Jimmy hadn’t topped yet, for good or worse. He was no T-Rex like Russell, nor a moneymaker like Derby, nor a Mr. Bad like Johnny was. But he had guns, heavy machinery, all kinds of stuff — probably arranged from materials they bought at the same store their mothers’ bought pants for them. And it was this, their sheer willpower to stand up to someone like Jimmy, while managing to put up a serious fight, that impressed him. Either he pissed them off, something he did upset them… or maybe someone else interfered in it.

He had no clue, so he just listened intently to Jimmy’s stories.

“Hmm, this explains the loud noises we were hearing from here last night,” Pete mused aloud, his thoughts drifting. “You know, you two could’ve been quieter.”

Jimmy shot him a look. “What?”

“N-nothing.”

...Mr. President, a second spud cannon has hit Jimmy Hopkins.

He shrugged, more interested in finishing his fried chicken than diving into whatever Pete was hinting at. “You know, this has gotta be Gary’s doing… yeah, he’s a psycho, but he’s smart— he knows how to get what he wants, we’ve seen that. The nerds were just another piece on his board. And I thought they were on my side. Damn.”

Pete nodded, still lost in thought. “Well… I don’t know. I mean, if he did it, wouldn’t you have seen more of him by now? Like you did with the preps and the greasers?”

Or at least heard from him. It was kinda funny — Jimmy didn’t know Gary had been hiding out in his dorm for days now, and there was no way he’d be telling him that. Pete had started to wonder how Jimmy barely crossed paths with Gary after the betrayal. They were at the same school, for crying out loud! They had to share at least one class, right? But somehow, they’d managed to avoid each other. Good news for Gary, bad news for Jimmy. At this rate, they’d never get to the game-set-match point of their rivalry.

Still, Pete wouldn’t be surprised if Gary was behind it all. Melvin was practically his consigliere, or at least one of them, man, and who knew how many others Gary had in his pocket. Especially someone dangerous — maybe Sheldon, if he was twice as tall.

“But it’s crazy to think about everything that’s happened since then, you know?” Pete said.

Jimmy laughed, tossing a chicken bone into the already overflowing trash can. “Yeah, well, they’re gonna think twice before messing with me again. It’s like that old saying… shame me once… fool me once… I forgot the rest, you get the point.”

He wiped his hands on a napkin, glancing at his smaller friend. “Oh, and nice jacket dude. Where’d you get it?”

Pete looked down at the jacket, feeling a bit awkward. It wasn’t anything special — just a green jacket, a size too big for him. What Jimmy didn’t know is that it was actually Gary’s, or at least something Gary had brought back to the dorm along with his stuff. Pete had picked it because it smelled the least like Gary, though there was still a faint hint of something, which he hoped was cologne. That and, well, he was right; it was really a nice jacket. He could’ve sworn his father once wore a similar jacket.

“Oh, this?” he tried to sound casual. “Just something I found lying around.”

And Jimmy grinned, clearly liking the jacket. “Think I could borrow it later? I’ve got a date tonight, and I could use something that looks decent. Can’t be showing up looking like this.”

Huh. A date. Although Pete had lost track of how many people Jimmy was romantically involved with, or worse, this actually was the first time he heard it straight from the source himself! It felt strange — sure, Pete didn’t really care who Jimmy was seeing, but they were friends. Why did he never tell him? He’d confided some of his most embarrassing moments to him, and vice versa. For Jimmy, it was that one time he went swimming in sunny Florida and came back to shore with a condom stuck to his face. As for Pete, well, he had many embarrassing moments to share — including some from last year.

He hesitated — the thought of Jimmy heading to the dorm and possibly running into Gary made his stomach churn. Unless, of course, he wanted to bring forward their much-anticipated big fight, there was no point in letting him find out in the worst possible way for him. Maybe later, perhaps. But not now, not while he was attempting to get along with his former roommate. “U-uh, sure, I’ll bring it to you later. No need to go t-to the dorm or anything.”

Jimmy raised an eyebrow, maybe sensing something was off, but didn’t push it. “Alright. But don’t forget, okay? I gotta look sharp for tonight.”

He made a mental note to grab the jacket as soon as he got back and hand it off to Jimmy as quickly as possible. No need to drag things out or let him anywhere near his room. Pete could handle this on his own, probably. He’d have to.

Later that night, Gary perched in the branches of an old tree near the football field. The field was mostly empty, save for a couple of jocks tossing a ball around in the fading twilight. He wasn’t exactly perched like a bird; his back slouched, earbuds in, listening to a funny Peanuts-like tune from a band he vaguely remembered. What was the name of it again? Velvet something something? Petey would know, but he didn’t care enough to try to remember.

He spooned some yogurt into his mouth, absently chewing as he looked at the stars peeking through the branches. Sure, the yogurt had the texture of loose connective tissue — at least that’s how a sucker like Melvin would describe it — but it was pretty good. He smirked at the thought. If he wanted to, he could probably find a new minion who’d be just as competent and at least 10% less dorky than him, but who was he kidding? There wasn’t time for that kind of thing anymore.

His thoughts drifted to Jimmy, the way he’d managed to charm the various cliques at school. Sure, a good portion of this was due to James’s unique “shoot first, ask questions second” strategy that usually finished with either him wounding a bunch of morons or riding the Bullworth cock carousel. It was a contrast to Gary’s more nuanced approach — telling people what they wanted to hear. That had always worked for him. Hell, it had worked everywhere: at school, in the city, even at home. But now, those strings he’d been so good at pulling were slipping from his hands.

It didn’t seem fair. He’d put in the time and effort, crafted intricate plans, and yet here was Jimmy-boy, so poor he was now barking by himself so he wouldn’t buy a dog. Youth is really wasted on the young.

And he thought Pete was good enough for him…

Maybe not for Jimmy. But well, he was good enough for him at least.

He hated to admit it, but he kinda missed having Pete around. In hindsight, it seemed comical; even after everything Gary had put him through, Petey was still there — quiet and corny as always, and, most annoyingly, still caring.

It was… strange, thinking about him in this way. The guy was obsessed with Jimmy for a while now, following him around like a lost puppy, eager to be of use, and still falling flat. Yet, despite all that, Pete still seemed to care about him. He didn’t understand it—why would he, of all people, still care about someone like him?

Things used to be so much easier in the past — it was always the two of them against the world, or at least that’s how Gary had seen it. They’d shared secrets, plotted countless pranks (mostly his ideas, but Pete could say he co-authored a few), doing the best to escape dullness of their everyday lives. He had always been the quieter one, the more sensitive one, but that hadn’t mattered. They were a team.

And then came Jimmy and ruined it.

“Fuck.”

He tossed the yogurt cup to the ground below, the plastic cracking against the dirt. What a shame — he’d already named it Smiggles. And while he couldn’t deny that the yogurt was good, he knew that sitting here and doing nothing wouldn’t be a very efficient strategy. To take care of James, hopefully for good, he’d need to strike just at the right time — and since he was having so much fun with whatever people were throwing at him now, why not leave him be for a while?

It wasn’t like the jocks or nerds didn’t already hate him. Even then, Gary still couldn’t believe someone as supposedly smart as Earnest had bought into his ridiculous barnyard animal theory. He could barely contain his amusement just thinking about it! The coming weeks were going to be a lot of fun. But for now, he’d wait, watch, and plan. That was his game, after all.

When he finally dragged himself back to the dorm a few minutes later, Pete was sitting on his bed, flipping through one of his books (probably one from the dumpsters). Maybe he had nothing else to do. He looked up as Gary walked in.

“Where were you?” Pete asked, his voice soft, almost hesitant.

Gary didn’t respond right away. He just collapsed onto the edge of his bed, running a hand over his face. He was mentally and physically exhausted — climbing that stupid tree had been way harder than it looked. Getting down hadn’t been much better; his hands were scraped, and his legs had a few shallow cuts from the rough bark.

“Out,” he finally muttered back, although his voiced sounded a bit flat. “You know, you should stop asking so many questions sometimes.”

Although this was true, he knew didn’t sound convincing — but what else could he say? ”Oh yeah, I just fell out of a tree and nearly broke my neck.” didn’t have a great ring to it. Saying he’d beat up Sheldon would’ve been easier. Everyone wanted to do that.

Before he could think of anything else to say, Pete was already next to him with the first-aid kit from the small cabinet. Ah, not this again. He grabbed some antiseptic wipes and bandages and started cleaning the scrapes with careful precision. Looks like he had some experience with this.

“Pfft. It’s not that bad. You don’t have to do this,” he snorted.

Sure, it didn’t look bad, right? Nah. But Gary watched him work anyway. Of course, he hated having someone take care of him — he was no baby! — but he couldn’t help but feel a little relieved that someone still cared.

“I know,” Pete replied, his voice gentle, not looking up from his task. “But I want to.”

He just hoped Gary wouldn’t ask him where his jacket was.
