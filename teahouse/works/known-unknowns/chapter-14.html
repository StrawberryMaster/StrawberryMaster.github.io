It didn’t take much convincing for Gary to settle for that plan.

He couldn’t hide that a part of him hated it; it almost felt contradictory. For months now he’d been going around, parading the academy (or, well, its students) saying that he was Mr. Big — that he’d take over the school and take out the scum known as Jimmy. This was working, sorta, but at times it seemed he was just running in circles. He did something, Jimmy undid it, and so on and so on. It was boring, borderline cliché — not even Pete could step in and disguise it as some sort of Korinesque fantasy that you’d need to think harder to understand.

It was getting frustrating.

The more Jimmy continued to gain influence over the rest of the school, the harder it was for Gary to do his own bidding. If, hypothetically, he left his dorm room right now and — metaphorically — shat on his front porch, that dumb kid would eat the shit and tell everyone that it was good. And then everyone would be eating shit and if he said it was bad, no one would believe him. (He makes a mental note to revisit this idea in the future.) He could joust Jimmy in front of the entire school — and he’d come back from the dead only to blame it on the black plague. And somehow, everyone would believe him.

At times, he couldn’t even believe that anyone was still on his side; though he boasted about Melvin — and Sheldon, to a lesser extent — privately to others, as a matter of fact, he despised both. Sheldon, for being the extremely annoying kid that he was; Melvin, for being a prick. He couldn’t care less about either. The good news is that Melvin was, at least, somewhat of an efficient lackey; he didn’t know how he’d be doing without his connections. But the very idea of picking someone he had next to no familiarity with to be an “ally” made him feel weak. But as he was risking it all to get far — he just shrugged.

The prospect of getting the townies involved in his plan was too enticing to ignore. Gary spent a good amount of time debating where to meet with one of the townies; they didn’t want to be seen in public, so the Carnival was out of consideration. The Vale was also off-limits — the rich kids wouldn’t tolerate their presence there, and he didn’t feel like being seen with one of those streetwalkers would be good for his already dangling reputation.

Gary also didn’t want to trek all the way to Blue Skies to make the deal happen, nor did he want to navigate through the grimy slums of New Coventry. Not this time and hopefully never again. The compromise, as suggested by Melvin, was to meet in the basement of a comic shop in Bullworth Town, where the nerds often gathered to play the demon-summoning game known as Grottos & Gremlins. The thought of that alone made Gary roll his eyes. Visiting the same comic shop whose dumpsters he used to ravage… pathetic. But it was the best option they had, so he reluctantly agreed.

The townies’ representative was a guy named Duncan. Gary vaguely recognized him as a former Bullworth student — a dropout, naturally. He had that rough, hardened look that came from living in Blue Skies, which was the closest thing to an actual hellhole on Earth — every man for himself or some shit. He looked like an idiot. Gary just hoped he would use that 1% of his brain that he’d never used up until then.

“We need to unite against a bigger enemy,” Gary said, his voice low but firm. “Jimmy Hopkins. That bastard’s got the entire school wrapped around his finger, and it’s only a matter of time before he comes after you guys next.”

Duncan leaned against a dusty shelf, his arms crossed, eyeing the younger kid. He just kept eyeing him. It was clear that while he didn’t trust Gary — not by a long shot — he was at the very least intrigued by the idea of getting revenge on his former school.

A beat passed when he finally spoke, his voice gruff. “And what’s in it for us?”

“You help me take him down, and Bullworth is ours,” Gary replied, trying his best to show his most confident smirk. “No more jocks, no more preps, no more hierarchy, none of that crap. Just us. We run the show, and you do whatever you want.”

“You really think you can pull this off?”

“I mean… what do I look like I’m made of? Pudding? Of course I will.”

Duncan nodded slowly. He was getting it — slowly, yes, but he was getting it. But Gary chuckled, because his plan was one step closer to happening.

After the final touches, Gary returned to the dorm around 1 a.m., having slipped past a few of the school’s prefects and some police officers on his way back. It wasn’t a difficult task; after all, sneaking around had become second nature to him by now. Slipping past the low-energy, half-bored enforcers was almost too easy… if he was a prep, he’d have easily bribed them — or let his dad do the task. Unfortunately for him, his dad was not the nicest guy, but that was something he didn’t like thinking about.

He opened the door to his dorm room quietly, expecting to find Pete already asleep. Unlike most of the school, Petey at least had some way of preserving his sanity — he couldn’t have easily gotten back if it was, say, someone like Jimmy, who’d turn their room into a crackhouse. By all circumstances, he was somehow retaining the littlest of his luck, which got things moving.

Instead, he found him sitting on the edge of his bed, fumbling with a roll of bandages. Pete jumped up the moment Gary walked in, clearly startled. Gary’s eyes narrowed as he took in the scene.

Pete jumped up the moment Gary walked in, clearly startled. Gary’s eyes narrowed as he took in the scene. “Uh… what did I miss?” he asked.

He quickly pushed his négligée sleeves down, covering the hastily bandaged arm as best he could. “Nothing,” he mumbled, avoiding Gary’s gaze. “I just… took a bath.”

It just so happens that Pete was a bad liar; his balls would probably agree. Okay, maybe the idea of him being awake at this time wasn’t entirely unusual, given how much love he had for the dozen or so books he read almost religiously. And he knew that him taking a bath at this time of night, although odd, wasn’t completely unrealistic; in order to avoid public humiliation, Pete would usually shower around 4-5 am, way before most students in the dorm woke up.

But he never went there this early. And that definitely didn’t explain the bandages. He was hiding something. And so he took a step closer. “You’re bluffing,” he said, reaching out to grab Pete’s arm. But Pete pulled away more forcefully he’d anticipated and stepped back.

“I said it’s nothing,” Pete insisted, flopping back onto his bed and turning away from Gary, clearly wanting to end the conversation.

Gary sat down next to him, rather puzzled. He opened his mouth to speak, but stopped after realizing he didn’t have a good retort to use here. It was fairly late, and he was not going to waste his time questioning things that he could very well ask hours later. But part of his brain insisted on saying at least something, and after a moment, he finally asked him a question.

“What are you so afraid of?”

Pete didn’t respond right away; knowing him well, Gary almost sworn he’d reply something like “you.” and talk about how he was bad, etcetera. Unfortunately, sometimes little Petey failed to realize that he wasn’t just running around like a chicken with its head chopped off, like a horse with no name — he had plans, and to make these plans happen, he needed to chop and screw with the very fabric of Bullworth.

When Pete finally turned to face him, his eyes were blurry — just as if he’d been holding back tears. “What do you want from me?” he asked quietly, his voice barely above a whisper.

“I don’t know. What do you want from me?”

“I-I wish we had an actual friendship,” he blurted. “We live together, sometimes talk together, but it hasn’t been the same for months. I just… I wanted some normalcy.”

As vulnerable as he felt saying this, Pete’s words were true. Although still wary, a part of him felt very relieved to know that Gary was okay and — somewhat — safe in their room. He hated seeing the guy walking around like a zombie, working on his “plan” to take over the school, which at this point was about anything but taking over the place. He hated when he saw him hanging around with people like Melvin, who leeched him and consumed him in exchange for a spot at the top when he’d win. And he wondered if that’d ever happen.

Maybe Pete was just being angry? No, that’s not it. Jealous? He didn’t know. Gary looked… disoriented, sure, but one could argue he was the happiest he had been in weeks, months. Focused, certainly, more than he ever was. And Pete was just there, revolving around everything and everyone, seeing where the winds took him next.

The world was turning… and he was left there.

Gary stared at him, feeling a pang of guilt that, truthfully, he didn’t quite know how to deal with. He gave his friend a sad pat on the head, a gesture that was meant to be comforting but felt annoyingly hollow. In silence, he stood up, making his way to his own bed, laying down and closing his eyes. He didn’t have time — or, perhaps, the willingness — to discuss the nitty-gritty of his plans right now.

But it’d only be a matter of time before he saw everything.

While Gary was engrossed in his scheming, Jimmy had been busy doing what could only be described as God’s work — at least in the eyes of the nerds.

Earnest had gone into full-on battle mode. His mission was simple: defeat the jocks, the nerds’ perpetual bête noire, even though “for good” only meant until the next day. Bullworth was no Neverland, and for most of them, the only way they’d truly escape the jocks would be by graduating and moving far, far away.

He did get the chance to test that spud gun, though. Both the smaller version — which he now owned — and the real deal, which was just as efficient, if not more. More than once, he found himself holed up in the observatory, reading the spud gun against waves of jocks who tried — and failed miserably — to invade.

This didn’t just happen once. It happened a few times. Then several times. Then… many, many times. An unbelievable amount of times. At one point, Jimmy found himself wondering where all these potatoes were coming from. Did the nerds have a secret stash in the Observatory — probably taken from the school’s basement? Did Earnest secretly run a potato farm? Were they buying them in bulk? Did potatoes really did grow on trees? Were they actual potatoes? The answers to those questions were yes, no, no, almost — Bucky did try — but no, and yes.

But the source of the potatoes wasn’t the real mystery here. What baffled Jimmy more was why the attacks were happening so frequently. Sure, the jocks hated the nerds, and they hated him too, but something was driving them to invade the observatory at such a relentless pace. He found out one night, and the answer was far simpler than he’d imagined.

“The jocks are coming… to kill us! Oh, thanks to you and your sordid little pictures.”

Oh. That.

The nerds doing their best to hide evidence of Earnest's crimes during the Yugoslav Wars.

At some point during their adventures, Earnest had tasked him with collecting some… special pictures of the school’s head cheerleader, Mandy, as part of some weird plan of his to take down the basketball-heads. Perhaps he was too trusting of him; not only did he find out he was a depraved human being, he also leaked the pictures to not only the rest of the school, but also the rest of the city. How he got away with this was beyond him.

“You wanted them! You’re the sicko!”

“A natural interest in sexual matters is natural during puberty, bud…”

He was not doing this crap again. He beat him once, and would beat him as many times as he wanted to prove that point that he was not gonna do everything him or the other nerds wanted. Part of them seemed aware of this; also in the Observatory that night were Thad and Cornelius, carrying boxes left and right. Thad was freaking out, and Cornelius — who just like Romeo in that fantastic Romeo and Juliet play he did — just wanted to die.

Eh. Jimmy was sure they’d handle the jocks this time. They didn’t need his help today. Worst-case scenario, he’d wake up the next morning to find someone like Algie strung up on the campus flagpole. And honestly, that wasn’t his problem, right? He had bigger things to worry about.

As he left the observatory, he could still hear Earnest’s panicked screams echoing behind him. “I’m gonna die! I’m gonna die!” Jimmy knew he wouldn’t — Earnest was too stubborn for that. Besides, they had a lot of work to do that week, and the nerds would get it done, one way or another. When he was younger, a wise man told him and his mother — back then with her third boyfriend — that there was zero difference between good and bad things. Sure, he might’ve been wrong and also spit on his face — but maybe there was a grain of truth to this, too.

On his way back to the dorm, he spotted a group of jocks marching toward their usual hangout. He pretended not to see them, but naturally, he noticed Kirby — of all jocks, probably the one he was closest to — though not that it meant much. Jimmy gave him a smile, and Kirby, clearly focused on the task at hand, managed a weak smile in return before turning his attention back to the mission. Maybe they’d talk later.

Back at the dorm, Jimmy eventually fell asleep in front of the common room TV, with some documentary on illegal immigration playing at a barely audible volume. As the night wore on, a figure that arrived at the dorm hours later — entering using a window near the poker table — stared at him for a moment before proceeding back to its own dorm room.
