Another day in paradise.

Despite the chaos he’d recently gone through with the nerds, Jimmy found himself — against all odds — enjoying things a little. Sure, he was still dead-set on wrapping up this whole mess as quickly as possible, but it surprised him to realize that not all the nerds were as lame as he’d thought.

Earnest, after enduring multiple beatings and nearly having a nervous breakdown (Jimmy could swear the kid was one meltdown away from a psych ward), finally came up with a plan that wouldn’t land them on a seat at The Hague or on an electric chair in an Oklahoma federal prison. He called it “Operation Trojan Cow.” The goal? Sabotage the jocks’ daily routines, humiliate them, and set Jimmy up for a grand showdown with their leader, right in front of the entire school on game day. Naturally, Jimmy agreed. He’d already toppled four cliques in a row, and besides, this time, he didn’t have to do everything himself — just the hard parts, of course. It was kinda funny — the same modus operandi being applied to every clique — the power of destroy, build, destroy.

The downside? Earnest and the rest of the nerds were getting weirdly attached to him, all mush and sappy over his presence in their lives — like he had been single-handedly saving their social lives (impossible) or would make them accepted among their peers (also impossible). He wasn’t their hero, they were just working toward a common goal. Still, maybe, just maybe, he could use this newfound adoration to his advantage. A few extra pawns wouldn’t hurt, after all.

Of course, there was also the lingering fallout of what he mentally dubbed “Mandygate”. The number of compromising pictures of Mandy that had been spread throughout town was… astronomical. Jesus, how many of these images did they spread through the city? (The answer was at least 22.) Despite Mandy’s snobby attitude, he felt bad for her. She might’ve been obnoxious, but she was still a person. She didn’t deserve this. Deep inside, she still had a heart. A beating one, too; it’s not like she was dead.

His following days ended up being filled with espionage, sabotage, and fortunately not many other words that ended with -age. It quickly became clear that Jimmy couldn’t just waltz into the jocks’ turf, give everyone cookies, and pretend everything was fine and dandy. He needed a disguise — something to get close enough to cause real damage! His Halloween costume? Too obvious. His greasy Burgers uniform? Yeah, no. If he wanted to infiltrate, he’d have to literally take the bull by the horns.

The Bullworth Bullhorns had a mascot — a big, dopey-looking bull (whoa!) costume that Constantinos, of all people, wore during games. It made some sense; the role was fit for annoying people, so it fit him well. Jimmy figured if he could get that mascot suit, he’d be golden. The only issue? Convincing him to give it up. Jimmy, being Jimmy, considered following his friend Petey’s tried-and-tested principles of compromise and just being nice™, but changed his mind and instead went for the simplest solution: teach him a lesson in the gym’s swimming pool.

The fact that it was drained that day worked in Jimmy’s favor; he had no plans to drown the poor guy, just scare him straight. The nerds also helped him with this; the jocks that went after him ended up being hit by a comical amount of fireworks on the way.

With the bull costume in hand, Jimmy was ready. He had his disguise, and now it was time to do what he did best—kick ass and hope for the best. The jocks wouldn’t know what hit them. (Well, at some point they would, since he was gonna reveal himself. But he didn’t want to be overanalytical about this; he was sounding too much like little Petey.)

For Pete, everything that had been happening lately felt so surreal.

He’d been through plenty of crazy experiences — some with Gary, some on his own — but things seemed different now with Jimmy around. Jimmy wasn’t like everyone else at Bullworth. He wasn’t the smartest guy in the room, and he definitely wasn’t the toughest, no matter how hard he tried to make everyone think otherwise. But, like Gary once told him, there was something about Jimmy. Something set him apart — maybe his lack of naïveté, maybe something else — from everyone else.

Bullworth had always been a war zone — people bullying each other left and right and all that jazz. Jimmy’s arrival hadn’t stopped it from happening, but as he climbed his way up through the cliques, the bullying, at least for Pete, started to ease. Russell kept his word and left him alone, and Pete couldn’t deny that it felt great not getting stuffed into lockers every other day. For everyone else? It was hit-or-miss.

Honestly, Pete hadn’t believed Jimmy could pull it off at first. Sure, Jimmy was the big bad wolf of the place, but there were so many obstacles. So many variables. Sometimes, Pete even questioned what Jimmy really wanted; though he never fully bought into the “James betrayed me and will take over the school” crackpot theory Gary would mumble in his sleep over and over, well, he couldn’t shake the feeling that maybe Gary wasn’t entirely wrong.

After all, Jimmy had done it — at least four times. And he was doing it again.

The jocks had been especially pissed off that day. They’d been humiliated—pranks everywhere, from glue on their benches to marbles scattered across the gym floor to the awfully accurate “jocks play with their balls” message on the scoreboard. Pete had to admit, the nerds knew how to pull off some epic pranks. With their tech know-how, they could really wreak mayhem. Perhaps he’d underestimated them; if they could take down the jocks like this, imagine the others?

The real shock came when the guy pulling off all these stunts turned out to be… the team mascot. Okay, that made some sense — the mascot role wasn’t exactly the most glamorous gig, and Pete could see why someone might use it as a cover for revenge. But who is in the mascot suit now? That role had a rather high turnover rate and had been passed around like crazy — he knew Ivan and Trevor had both worn it before, and eventually Constantinos was picked for the position that would, one day, kill him viciously and painfully. But Pete wasn’t even sure if he still had the job.

But then Jimmy ripped off the bull’s head, revealing himself. Wow. That was… honestly predictable, but he still couldn’t believe it. He’d be a good mascot, for sure, but not for longer, it seemed — he was challenging the jocks to a fight.

Watching Jimmy take them down, one by one, was honestly hilarious. It wasn’t entirely surprising — everyone knew, or at least said, Jimmy was tough, and he’d only been proving them right — but the way he hurled the jocks’ own footballs back at them had Pete cheering. At one point, he even went to one of the lower benches to take a drink from a cooler — only for Jimmy to knock the cup right out of his hand before he could sip anything. Huh. I always thought he liked mango juice…

What really surprised Pete was how quickly Ted went down. After all his teammates got destroyed, Ted just… gave up. Jimmy barely had to throw a punch before Ted threw in the towel, ran away, and got sacked. It all ended so fast that it seemed weird. But hey, the important thing was, it was over.

“You did it! You did it! You beat them all in front of everybody,” Pete said, practically bouncing on his heels.

Jimmy gave him a pat on the back. “Thanks for your help, Petey.”

“Ahh, this is gonna be great!” Pete grinned. Finally, with Jimmy in charge, there’d be peace — at least for a little while. That meant more time for adventures. Adventures he could finally be a part of! Maybe, if things stayed good, he could even convince Gary to chill out and realize Jimmy wasn’t a threat. They could finally be friends again.

But his enthusiasm faded a little when Jimmy brushed him off. After Pete asked if he wanted to watch the game together, he just waved him off. “Sorry, Petey, I’ve gotta talk to everyone else—Mandy, Ted, Kirby, Damon. You know how it is. Maybe later.”

“Oh, o-okay.”

It made sense, from a technical standpoint, to ensure the jocks were following the line — his line, of course. But he wondered why he couldn’t come along. This had happened so many times in the past, and he couldn’t get it. Sometimes, it almost felt like he needed Pete to get things going — but less when it came to everything else.

No point in staying here then, Pete thought. It would’ve been less embarrassing if it hadn’t started raining. He hadn’t brought a jacket or an umbrella, and by the time he made it back to the dorm, the rain was coming down hard. He was pretty much soaked to the bone and his clothes sticking uncomfortably to his skin.

As usual, Pete found Gary lounging in their dorm. He wasn’t surprised — Gary hadn’t been anywhere near the football field that day, and there would be no reason for him to attend it. Actually, since the whole ordeal with Russell, Gary had a habit of skipping Jimmy’s fights with the other clique leaders. Maybe it was better that way; he didn’t exactly have the most reliable temperament. Besides, it looked like he’d been having more fun on his own anyway; he was doodling something weird on a notepad. Pete wasn’t much of an artist, even though he tried, but Gary’s sketches weren’t like his at all — they were abstract, messy. Like whatever was going on in Gary’s head was just spilling onto the page.

When he noticed Pete walk in, he didn’t say anything at first, and just watched him with an amused smirk. Pete, who almost looked like he just went out for a swim, barely noticed Gary either. He kicked off his shoes, water pooling around them, and started peeling off his wet clothes, tossing them into a soggy pile in the corner. No surprise there — Pete had always been the skinnier of the two. His hair clung to his forehead in wet strands, and he shivered, teeth nearly chattering from the cold.

Gary broke the silence first. “Oh hey, I found Nemo.”

Pete shot him a look, and Gary’s grin widened.

“Rough night, eh?” he asked, trying — failing — to suppress a laugh.

Shrugging, Pete reached for a towel from his drawer. “Guess you could say that.”

Although Gary didn’t push for more, his eyes lingered on Pete a moment longer, scanning him. “You’re soaked,” he said, his tone light but teasing.

Pete muttered under his breath as he started drying off. “You don’t say.”

He studied Pete’s face a little longer, half-expecting some miracle — maybe that Jimmy had actually lost to Ted. As much as he’d love that, and it would save him some resources, he knew better. Jimmy was too persistent, too damn lucky. Every time Gary had tried to turn the tables, it had backfired — Jimmy-boy would declare it duck season, Gary would say it was wabbit season, duck season, wabbit season, wabbit season, duck season, and he ends up getting shot in the face. It was just his luck. Bad luck.

Pete threw on a dark red long-sleeved shirt — Gary’s, if he had to guess — and a pair of sweatpants he grabbed from the floor, then flopped down on his bed. Gary watched for a second, pretending not to care, but then slid off his own bed and went to lay beside him.

“So,” he said, leaning back slightly, his voice unusually thoughtful, “What are we gonna do now?”

Pete blinked at him. “W-what do you mean? I thought you were gonna bring up Jim—”

“Pfft,” Gary interrupted him, rolling his eyes. “Can we just do something else for once, Petey? It’s always ‘Jimmy this, Jimmy that.’ God, he’s not gonna get laid with you.”

“S-shut up,” he muttered, cheeks flushing, but there wasn’t any bite to it.

Gary laughed. “You know, I’ve actually got a surprise for us.” He hopped off the bed and went to a corner, pulling out two carefully hidden parfaits, handing one to Pete.

“You bought these for us?”

“Tempting, but no.” he smirked. “I just… borrowed them from our waspy friend upstairs.”

“Who?”

“Gord,” Gary said, taking a spoonful from his parfait. Naturally, he was not gonna take it back. Maybe the spoon, but nothing else.

“No way.” Pete shook his head in disbelief. He couldn’t help but laugh a bit. “He wasn’t gonna eat all these, right?”

“How would I know?” his roommate shrugged, scooping another bite. “But it’s five o’clock somewhere.”

Pete stared at him for a moment, trying to wrap his head around Gary’s sudden shift in attitude. One minute, he was a complete wildcard, all erratic behavior and unsettling grins. The next, he was casually offering the very best of whatever he found on people’s dorms or trashcans — almost like they were back in elementary school, before everything went sideways. Pete sometimes wondered if it was the medication, or the lack thereof, that affected this; but he was no psychiatrist. All he knew was that the Gary he grew up with — the one who’d dared him to eat a live grasshopper and send pebbles flying at passing cars — had become someone else entirely.

But that never stopped him from being nice at times — and also from sharing his finest work with him.

“What’s this?” Pete pointed to a random doodle, a weird mix of sharp lines and strange shapes that vaguely resembled a dog. It looked like a dog, maybe. Maybe a bike. A dog on a bike. A bike on a dog. A bike wearing at least three layers of a dog costume, or some shit. Pete chuckled.

“Well… it’s a dog. More or less. See, it’s a work in progress…”

They went through page after page, Pete pointing out the various shapes, from the silliest ones to the actual serious works of his, and laughing at Gary’s half-serious explanations. Some of the drawings were genuinely good, others just a chaotic mess. But for once, they weren’t talking about Jimmy, the school, or any of the usual drama. It felt… normal. Even fun.

“This one is a giraffe,” he said, pointing at a squiggly figure that looked out of this world.

Pete glanced over and laughed, shaking his head. “No… that’s definitely not a giraffe. It looks more like… a snake wearing a hat?”

“Snakes can wear hats, Petey.”

Funny was one way to describe their conversation. They spent the next while going back and forth, pointing out the strange figures, cracking terrible jokes, talking about life. It felt… almost normal. For once, they weren’t caught up in some scheme or whatever. At some point, Gary even let Pete borrow his iPod — he never would’ve guessed Gary had a soft spot for Prince’s Musicology. The night wore on like that, and eventually, exhausted from the day, Pete fell asleep. Gary dozed off soon after.

When he woke up later, it was dark. The only sound was the low hum of the rain outside, still tapping against the window. Not as heavy as it was earlier, so that was good, but it meant it was not yet time for him to wake up. Gary stirred, groggily blinking his eyes open. He was… warm. Really warm. (Not a bad thing — if he was really cold, he’d have probably been dead.) And for some reason, he wasn’t in his bed. (He could recognize his mattress from miles away.) He frowned, his brain taking a moment to catch up. If he slept on the floor again, he’d probably kill himself. It wasn’t until he glanced down that he realized he was in Pete’s bed.

With Pete.

Somehow Petey had curled up against him, his head resting on his chest, one arm draped over his waist. He stared down at the scene, his first instinct being to pull away; Gary was not an endorser of this Hopkins-approved sex-positive behavior. But no, this was actually kinda comfortable. He was warm too, so at least he wasn’t dead or something.

Gary kinda liked it. Not that he’d ever admit that out loud, of course. He pinched the bridge of his nose, trying to clear his head. His eyes darted to the door — locked. Good. Then to the curtains — closed. Whew. He exhaled softly and relaxed a bit. Stirred and half-awake, Pete nestled a bit closer. “It’s okay,” he murmured, inching closer, his body curling up against Gary’s.

He sighed, staring up at the ceiling. “Yeah, whatever…” He wondered how many crimes he had committed in his past life had caused him to end up here. Many, naturally. But how many crimes in this life have led him here? At least one — befriending this kid.

Maybe that wasn’t a bad thing. Gary closed his eyes again, trying to let the warmth between them lull him back to sleep. He would be awoken half an hour later with many sounds of fireworks set off by a certain Hopkins on the football team’s field — he was now enjoying the good life.
