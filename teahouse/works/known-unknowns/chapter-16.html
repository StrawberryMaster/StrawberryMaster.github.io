Chapter Summary

Gary has a brief talk wth Crabblesnitch. Meanwhile, Jimmy enjoys his new life as king of the school.

It was morning. Dr. Crabblesnitch sat in his dimly lit office, his reading glasses perched on the bridge of his nose, as he scanned the day’s newspaper. The headlines were, unfortunately, predictable — another incident of student violence, another scandal involving the school’s reputation, yada yada yada. He sighed heavily, setting the paper down with a thud. What is wrong with these children? Bullworth had always had its share of problems, of course, but lately the chaos seemed unmanageable.

When he was younger, none of this happened. There was always a kid or two who couldn’t keep their mouth shut, but nothing a beating wouldn’t solve. In his father’s days, the children would yearn for the mines. Nowadays, they can’t even work straight! This formula was repeating at Bullworth; fights broke out daily, cliques at each other’s throats, and now Jimmy Hopkins — their so-called “hero” — had dared to take on the football team, the pride of Bullworth Academy. And somehow, he won.

Crabblesnitch had never trusted Hopkins, not from the day he’d set foot on Bullworth grounds. Essentially half-orphaned, a boy like that — rough around the edges, prone to violence — was bound to bring trouble. And trouble he had! Sure, the other students seemed to worship him now, like he was some sort of leader. “Daddy,” they called him, as if he ruled the school (which made sense, to an extent) or was their gorilla pimp. But after attacking the school’s most prestigious team, and setting off a comical amount of fireworks at 1 in the morning? That was a line too far; only one of them ever brought the school much needed money, and it was not Hopkins. The skepticism Crabblesnitch felt toward him only deepened.

He rubbed his temples, thinking of the complaints from the faculty — at least from Hattrick —, the concerned letters from parents — which he carefully swept under the carpet —, the constant chaos he had to reign in. Jimmy was corrupting them. He needed a solution, and he needed it fast.

A sharp, almost too-loud knock at the door interrupted his brooding. Before he could respond, the door creaked open, and in stepped a student, dressed rather impeccably in his school uniform, and his shoes polished to a mirror shine. Gary Smith. The only thing out of place was the overwhelming smell of cologne, wafting into the office as he entered. What’s this kid even doing?

“Dr. Crabblesnitch,” Gary said, his voice smooth and polite. “I hope I’m not interrupting.”

Crabblesnitch looked him over, not entirely pleased to see him. He could’ve sworn Miss Danvers was there to prevent most students from getting to him unless it was important, or unless they were important to him. Although he had a cordial relationship with his father, it was different with his son. There was something about this boy that always made his skin crawl — too slick, too controlled. But at the same time, Gary had always been one of the more disciplined students on the surface — at least compared to the average student —, rarely stepping out of line, at least in the obvious ways.

“Where is Miss Danvers?”

“She had an, erm, issue with Sheldon in the corridor.”

“Sheldon? Huh. I never really liked that kid.”

“Me either!”

He gestured for Gary to sit. “What is it, Smith? Make it quick. I’m not in the mood for games.”

Gary smiled, a rather unsettling grin flickering his face. “I was actually here to help you, sir. I’ve been thinking… you’ve got quite a problem on your hands. You know, the fighting, the chaos, students like Hopkins running wild…”

Crabblesnitch’s eyes narrowed. “What about Hopkins?”

“Well, it’s no secret that Jimmy has… taken control of the school. At least, that’s what everyone thinks, but I know better.” his voice lowered, leaning in just slightly as if sharing a secret. “I know Jimmy. Better than anyone else here. He’s unstable, violent, he acts like he’s doing all of this for the students, but really? He wants to take everyone down with him.”

The principal listened, his fingers steepled in front of him. Gary continued.

“He’s already attacked our beloved cough football team. That’s just the beginning. If he keeps going unchecked, there won’t be a school left for m- for you to run. But—” Gary paused for dramatic effect, “—I can help you control him. And everyone else who’s starting to follow him.”

“And why would you offer to help me, Smith? What’s in it for you?” Crabblesnitch raised an eyebrow.

Gary smiled again — it seemed like a more genuine, albeit rather unnerving, smile. “Sir, I don’t want to see this school go to hell. I care about it, I really do. And, well, let’s just say I have a vested interest in keeping things… orderly.”

“But how would you do it? I can’t let you go around beating other boys into submission.”

“Just leave that to me. I have my ways, my connections with the students. All I need is your support. You let me handle it, and I promise you, Dr. Crabblesnitch, that Bullworth will be running smoother than it ever has.”

Tapping his fingers against the desk and weighing his options, Crabblesnitch eventually spoke up. “Alright, Smith,” he said slowly. “I’ll give you a chance. But mark my words—if this goes wrong, it’ll be your head.”

“Yes, sir.”

“Also, just one more thing—”

“Yes?”

He leaned in closer. “About those barnyard animals…”

Being popular was something Jimmy had never really experienced — at least not for the right reasons. In his previous schools, he was famous for all the wrong reasons, all the misdeeds he did — but it’s not like he wanted to do it just to stand out; ironically, he just wanted to fit in. But now, standing at the top of the social pyramid, it was starting to feel pretty good.

No more running errands for nerds, preps, greasers, or jocks, no more trying to win their respect only to get bitchslapped around for it. Finally, he had some peace — and he was reaping the benefits. It felt like everywhere he walked, the ground was covered in mink carpet; everyone liked him, and even those who didn’t had no choice but to respect him.

This wasn’t what he’d planned when he first came to Bullworth. All he’d wanted was to keep his head down, maybe make a few friends, and survive. He couldn’t exactly say he’d nailed the first two, but that third one? Yeah, he nailed it. He won. Now, Jimmy was the king of the school, and he was starting to understand why Gary had been so obsessed with this idea. Having people do things for you, wanting to hang out with you, and — most importantly — respecting you? That was the real prize. Maybe he wasn’t as crazy as he thought he was. He should’ve done this much earlier!

As for Gary? He honestly didn’t matter anymore. All that talk about getting back at him? Jimmy had thought about it, sure, mostly because Gary kept throwing punches from the shadows — and forgetting they weren’t in a dark, moonless night. But it wasn’t worth it. Gary had burned every bridge, and Jimmy wasn’t about to destroy his reputation by dredging up a feud that was old news. Let Gary rot in his own mess. Or dumpster food, since it’s what he liked.

The only issue was Pete.

His buddy had gotten more and more insistent that they should be keeping an eye on Gary, that he wasn’t out of the picture. At first, Jimmy shrugged it off, but Pete just wouldn’t let it go. Every conversation seemed to circle back to Gary. It was getting to the point where it was, erm, uncool. Why risk everything — his reputation, his control, the respect he had earned — over some old grudge? Pete didn’t seem to get that Gary was irrelevant now. He was in charge, and their dumb trio didn’t mean anything anymore. It was over.

Instead, Jimmy had started spending more time with his new crew. Well, most of them. Johnny had been more distant, dealing with his own stuff, but Jimmy had earned the respect of the cliques, and it felt good to see guys like Derby, Earnest, and Tad hanging out with him like they were old friends. (Only one missing here was Russell, but he, as a good friend and ally, had other people to pick on anyway.) They’d been talking for a while, shooting the breeze and tossing around unfunny jokes for half an hour, when Derby suddenly leaned forward with a grin.

"And so he asked Ted, 'what's 9 + 10?', and he said, '21'!" [uncontrollable laughter] [audience explodes]

“You know what, Jimmy? Forget this school. The whole town should know about you. You’re like… the mayor.”

He had a good point. Sure, the students knew who ran things here, but what about the adults? What about the rest of the town? It was time for him to show them who Jimmy Hopkins really was. If anything, he was more of a grown-up than they’d ever be.

The idea fired him up. He told the others to hang tight while he went off to get his message across. The excitement was already bubbling in his chest! He could barely hear Pete, who was pleading with him again, begging to come along, to at least talk to him before he left. But Jimmy just brushed him off; Pete didn’t even know where he was going or what the plan was. It didn’t matter. Jimmy had bigger things to handle.

Seeing that chasing after Jimmy would be pointless, Pete turned back and headed inside the dorm. Curiously, the four clique leaders were still there, but their conversation wasn’t as friendly as it had been.

“I don’t see why we bother backing Hopkins anymore,” Derby’s voice rang out, haughty and clipped. “He’s crass, impulsive, and frankly, as daddy would tell me, beneath us.”

“Yeah? You weren’t sayin’ that when he took care of the Greasers for ya,” Ted shot back, clearly annoyed. Impressive, too; Pete hadn’t expected Ted to come up with a coherent sentence, but later recalled this would be something Damon would most likely do. “We were all on board when he took down Russell. What’s changed?”

Earnest chuckled darkly, cutting in. “Jimmy? He… he doesn’t even know what he’s doing half the time. Now, we’re supposed to follow him? I’ve got more brains in one finger than he has in his whole skull!”

Pete couldn’t help but partially agree—at least about Jimmy being, well, not the smartest guy around. But Earnest wasn’t as clever as he thought either; he’d just been defeated by Jimmy, using the same weapons his nerds had made, and that wasn’t enough for him?

Then came a pause, and Derby spoke again, quieter but also a bit sharper. “Maybe Gary was right about him.”

Oh no, not this shit again, Pete thought, his heart pounding, as he pressed himself closer to the doorframe.

“What do you mean?” Ted asked, confused.

Derby sighed, as if explaining the obvious to a child. To him, it was very much as plain as day — he was sure Earnest would agree, but Ted seemed just as dumb as the rest of those greasers. “Think about it. What has Hopkins really done? He’s disrupted the balance, sure, but has he improved anything?”

“Hmm… no.”

“All he’s done is stir up chaos, and now we’re left to clean up the mess. Gary warned us. He said Jimmy would burn everything down, and we laughed it off.”

A heavy silence fell over the room — then Earnest broke it. “Fine. If Jimmy’s out, then it’s every clique for themselves. We’ll settle this the way we know best.”

Suddenly, Pete heard footsteps storming out of the room. He ducked into the shadows as the leaders split, each heading in different directions — some to their respective dorms, others outside. He could’ve sworn Earnest shot him a pitying glance, which would make sense given it wasn’t really dark in the dorm, but it didn’t matter. They were gone.

Gary was right.

Pete’s stomach twisted. If the cliques turned on Jimmy, the whole school would spiral into chaos; there would be a nonzero chance the Harrington junta would execute him the next day. Worst of all, if Gary had somehow orchestrated this, things could get ugly fast. Pete had to find Jimmy, warn him, and get him to fix things. If they acted fast, maybe they could salvage something — maybe Earnest could even be convinced to stick with them. Otherwise, it would be funny if he tested that spud gun on him!

The problem was, well, finding Jimmy. Pete didn’t know, so he started searching the campus. The dorm was empty. The girls’ dorm, as amusing as it would be, wasn’t an option. The library was quiet, the gym was empty, the auto shop abandoned, and no hints of him inside the school. It was kinda funny; it reminded him of when he went like crazy searching for Gary months ago, only to find out he was hidden in the most random of places. But Jimmy was no Gary; he was not gonna hide when he wanted to be seen, to be known. By the time he reached the parking lot, he was winded from running and fast-walking, trying to stay out of the prefects’ sight.

He has to be somewhere, Pete thought, frustrated. Shit. Jimmy could’ve been in town, but Pete knew by the time he found him there, it might be too late. Defeated, he trudged back to the dorm and hesitated outside Jimmy’s door. It was unlocked — as usual —, so he pushed it open and stepped into the usual mess. Sighing, he sat on the bed, waiting.

About an hour later, Jimmy strolled in, looking more triumphant than usual. His hands were covered in spray paint, and he was carrying a bag of something that smelled surprisingly good.

“I did it, Petey! I’m the king, man. The king!”

“Great,” Pete muttered, “but we’ve got a bigger problem now.”

Jimmy laughed. “What problem? I’ve taken care of everything. Even bought myself some… uh…” He peered into the bag, clearly forgetting what he’d bought. “…tacos.”

Pete sighed. “All kinds of problems. Remember that promise you made about restoring law and order and improving the school?”

Drooling over the tacos instead of staring at his friend, Jimmy gave a half-confident “yeah”, forcing Pete to wait a minute or so for him to finally stare at him, instead of his food, so he could continue.

“Well, I think Gary must be behind it, but trust me, everyone wants a fight.”

Unimpressed, Jimmy raised an eyebrow. “What do you think I’ve been doing this entire time? ‘Fighting’ is like my middle name now, Petey. Like, check this out.”

He grabbed a camera of his — a rather new-ish digital camera — and showed Pete a photo from his roll. There it was — the very top of the city hall, with “Bullworthless” scrawled with bright, purple-ish all over the way. It was unbelievable. For all the jokes he made about Jimmy being dumb, he was now terrified to know that not only it was true, but he could also get dumber. Hadn’t he learned anything from the “Jimmy wuz here!” incident? His handwriting hadn’t, for example.

He sighed.

“W-what? Did you seriously think this would work? L-look I-I-I’ve gotta go, talk to you later.”

Pete turned to leave but then stopped, retracing his steps and glancing back at the bag in Jimmy’s hand. “Oh… those are enchiladas, not tacos. Can I grab one?”

"For real real, not for play play."

“Sure,” Jimmy mumbled, still trying to figure out what went wrong.

Pete grabbed an enchilada and walked out, leaving Jimmy sitting there, confused, disappointed, and with one less item to eat. As he sat on his bed, while glad Pete kept the spot warm, he realized if what Pete said was true, he’d have to fight a whole lot of people to keep his throne. And that, as far as Jimmy was concerned, was going to be a whole new battle. He’d have to do everything all over again.

“Aw, man…”

Later that night, Pete decided to confront Gary about everything that had been happening. He found him in their dorm, leaning back lazily, feet propped up on a chair, looking like he didn’t have a care in the world. To be fair — it’s not like he usually did.

“Gary,” Pete started cautiously, “you know what’s been going on, right? With Jimmy? The cliques?”

He barely glanced up. “If it’s about his body count, then no Petey, I don’t wanna know what’s going on.” Dismissive as always.

Pete narrowed his eyes. “I know you’re behind it, y-you’ve gotta be. Jimmy didn’t just lose control of the school overnight.”

But Gary just shrugged, his expression annoyingly neutral. “I told you, Petey. It’s not my fault that Jimmy-boy is a bootleg Brad Renfro. I mean, sure, I hate his guts, but I’m not that desperate to take him down anymore.”

That wasn’t what Pete wanted to hear. He pushed Gary again and again, but all he got were the same denials. Gary insisted that whatever was going on wasn’t his doing.

And technically, that was true, even though he didn’t want to say the quiet part out loud. Gary hadn’t orchestrated the recent chaos — at least not directly. The townies had started doing some of the dirty work for him, slow but steady. He didn’t even have his hands in every part of the operation. But whatever they were up to, it was working. Although Gary was pleasantly surprised to hear the cliques had turned on Jimmy, he was confused as to how it happened.

Like, out of all his rumors, out of all his schemes, the thing that made them turn against Jimmy — or paved the way for it — was him saying he was a trash humper? Unbelievable — though not for them, apparently. Perhaps the idea of doing it all on the same day instead of individually, every couple of weeks or so made it more effective than usual. Whatever the reason, it made his day.

Despite everything going on, Gary worked hard to keep a low profile, trying not to let on that he knew more than he was letting on. With each strange event that rocked the school, though, it became harder to maintain that act. One day, he heard the library was infested with rats, which he didn’t find amusing given how many “rats” frequented the place every day. Then there were whispers about Johnny — leader of the Greasers — being sent to Happy Volts Asylum, which meant one less nuisance in Gary’s world. And then the gym caught fire. Well, that definitely hadn’t been in his plans, but he wasn’t about to complain at this point.

The best news came on a cloudy morning, when the day started off perfectly for Gary. He was jolted awake by the sound of frantic knocking on his door. Pete stirred beside him, groaning in confusion, as Gary stumbled over to open the door.

“Hang on!” Gary shouted.

He opened the door to find no one other than Melvin standing there, wide-eyed and jittery. What a surprise. Gary looked disheveled, his shirt half unbuttoned, hair a mess, and his expression blank.

“H-hi Gary!” he stammered, nervously eyeing Pete, who was now sitting up in bed, rubbing his eyes. Wait, were they sleeping together?

“…hi,” Gary replied flatly, waiting for whatever disaster Melvin had come to deliver. “What’s it?”

“I—I bring news! Big news, my liege! It’s Jimmy. He… he was expelled!”

Gary froze for a moment, before a slow grin spread across his face.

He had won.
