Chapter Summary

Gary enjoys a nice swim. Meanwhile, seeing his reputation being ruined, Jimmy decides to counter-attack.

Gary floated on his back in the gym’s swimming pool, the cool water lapping gently against his body and soothing his tired muscles. The flickering fluorescent lights above the pool — a rather forgotten side effect of the school’s low funding as of late — made the white tiles shimmer. Would’ve been worse had that area been more affected by the recent fires. It was quiet, serene — a rare moment of peace. He closed his eyes, letting the water carry him as he drifted aimlessly.

I won.

That thought had been echoing through his mind for days now. After all the stress, after months of battling Jimmy, of plotting and scheming, after feeling like the school was suffocating him, he had finally won. Jimmy was gone. Expelled. There was no one left to challenge him now. For the first time in what felt like forever, he could actually breathe.

It reminded him of the time he first went off his meds. The first few days had been complete hell, but once he broke through, he felt a freedom he’d never known. This felt a little like that. Their war was over, and for now, Gary had a rare moment to bask in his victory.

As much as his victory thrilled him, the stillness of the moment felt… strange. Almost funny, one could say. All the chaos, the drama — it had all stopped, leaving him floating here in silence. For the first time in a while, he didn’t know what to do with himself. Maybe this was what peace felt like. Gary hadn’t had much of that in his life.

“What now?” he mumbled to himself, his voice barely audible over the gentle ripples of the water. That was a good question, one he took about a minute to answer.

Oh yeah, the townies. He had to deal with them eventually. They were getting a bit, uh, reckless with their actions as of late, acting like they ran the place. He didn’t explicitly agree with that, right? I’ll handle them later, he thought. No rush. He had time now, didn’t he? Maybe he’d grab some lemonade after this. In one of the countless books he borrowed from the trashcans of Bullworth, there was this cookbook that had a lemonade with condensed milk recipe in it. Hmm. That was a good idea. After all, he didn’t really need to plot right now. He could enjoy this — just for a little longer.

Unbeknownst to him, his friend Pete sat quietly in the corner of the pool area, his back against the cold tiles, sketchpad in hand. He wasn’t in the water, just observing Gary from a distance, pencil moving steadily across the paper. In fact, he had been watching him for a while now, lost in his own thoughts as he sketched the scene in front of him. Gary, in his rare moment of calm, looked different. Less intense. More human. It was… different.

Pete’s brow furrowed as he drew, though it wasn’t the sketch that bothered him, not at all. It was everything else. Jimmy had been expelled, and the school was just moments away from total chaos. He hadn’t been happy about any of it. Despite how much he wanted Jimmy to actually remember him, to know he was there for other things, at times it felt like their friendship only existed when he was in trouble or needed help against one of the groups in the school; he always said Pete was his “best buddy”, but he had been hearing that less and less as time went on.

He glanced up at Gary, floating so effortlessly, so at ease. How could he be so content after everything? Jimmy wasn’t just another pawn in the game. He was their friend. Or… at least, he had been. He still couldn’t understand what caused him to see Jimmy as a threat, as someone they should’ve hated. It actually made him wonder what would’ve happened had Russell defeated him that day in The Hole. Would’ve just stopped going after him? Would Jimmy defeat Russell at a later point? And what would happen to, well, him?

After a while of sketching, Pete sat back and looked at what he had drawn. He couldn’t help but chuckle — the way he had sketched Gary made him look like a body just floating aimlessly in the sea. Limbs slack, expressionless. It was almost fitting in a way; the supposed “mastermind” of Bullworth, now drifting with nothing left to fight for.

He liked the drawing, though. It captured something real.

Gathering his things, Pete stood up and stared at Gary, who was still floating, eyes closed, blissfully unaware of the world and, luckily, his surroundings. After a moment, Pete kicked off his shoes and socks, walking closer to the pool’s edge. He dipped his feet in, the cold water sending a shock through him, making him wince. Jeez, how cold is this? But he stayed nonetheless, letting himself relax.

Pete had been here a few times — many times, to be fair — but never really used the pool. Most of the time he hid in the pool stands, sometimes to read or study, usually to vent. The pool itself wasn’t used much, unless you happened to be a good swimmer like Justin from the preps or… uhh… what was his name again? Ricky, was it? Must’ve been.

The quiet stretched on for a while longer. Pete kept watching the subtle movement of the water, the faint ripples around Gary. But eventually he decided to speak.

“So this is it, huh?… But why?”

Gary opened his eyes slowly, the sound of Pete’s voice not entirely surprising — he followed him pretty much everywhere now that Jimmy was looking for other hoes — but still enough to draw him out of his moment of peace. He tilted his head slightly to glance over at him, who sat at the edge of the pool, feet dangling in the water. His expression didn’t change much, however.

“Why not?”

Of course, Pete wasn’t satisfied with that answer. He sighed, looking out across the still water. “Look, I get it. You wanted to take over the school and all that. Hell, even I thought it wasn’t such a bad idea at the time. ‘Put the losers in their place,’ right? We agreed on that.”

He slowly nodded. “Yeah, and we did it. We won.”

“But why Jimmy?” Pete’s voice softened. “Why did you have to ruin his life?”

Gary didn’t respond right away. He floated there for a second longer before making his way back to the pool’s ramp, holding onto the ramp’s edges and looking at Pete. He looked serious — and even Gary’s cool, calculated expression he was wearing just a few minutes earlier was beginning to crack a little.

“Jimmy… was always going to be in the way,” Gary said, explaining what he thought was obvious to little Petey. “He wasn’t some harmless guy just trying to get by, no. He wanted control — he just didn’t know it. He was never going to let me- let us have it, Pete. You know that.”

Pete shook his head. “No, I don’t think he ever wanted what you wanted. He just… I don’t know, he wanted to survive? Maybe make a few friends along the way.”

“Friends?” Gary scoffed. “Nope. Can’t you see? The very minute he got power, he became just like the rest of them. Derby, Ted, Earnest, Pee Stain — he wanted to rule. And of course, just like the rest of them, he didn’t have the brains to pull it off.”

“Maybe, but… you didn’t have to ruin him, Gary. He wasn’t a threat to you, to me. N-not like this. Like, did he do something bad to you or-”

“Did he do something good to you?”

“W-what?”

At this point, Gary had climbed out of the pool, dripping water as he reached for a towel he left nearby. He looked down at Pete.

“I mean, why are you defending him so much? What did James ever do that was so great for you, Petey? Did he save you from something? Were you his housewife?”

Pete felt his throat tighten. “No… it’s not like that. He… he was my friend, he was our friend. I wanted us to have fun, see?”

“Pssh, ‘fun’. You know, Petey… why haven’t you ever treated me the way you treated Jimmy? Always praising him, saying he’s this great guy, this nice guy.” his voice was rather cutting. “You know he isn’t.”

Pete bit his lip, then answered quietly, “I did, Gary. You know that.”

That seemed to shut him up. His mouth was twitching, as if he wanted to say something more, but didn’t. Without another word, he turned away, walking over to retrieve his clothes. Pete tried his best not to look.

As he dressed, Pete, trying to lighten the mood, held up the sketch he had been working on. “Hey, check it out.”

Walking next to him again, Gary glanced at it, and after a beat, he laughed. “Do I really look that flat?”

Shrugging, Pete chuckled. “Kinda.”

It was over.

Jimmy was surprised at how far he’d come — and how far he’d fallen. Just days ago, he’d been hanging out with Bullworth’s top clique members, reliving their best moments (most of which hadn’t aged well). Now he was here, alone, with the whole school — except for Pete, Russell, and his henchmen — hating him. Sowing was fun, but reaping really sucked. What the fuck.

Worst of all, he’d gotten expelled. Sure, he wasn’t a saint, but he hadn’t done the things they accused him of. He’d been trying to fix things, not wreck them — it wasn’t his fault that everyone suddenly decided he had things with trashcans or barnyard animals. It made no sense. He never had any sexual relations with that woman, Miss Trash Can — or any other piece of garbage, for that matter. He suspected Gary was behind it, which was obvious, but finding out that the townies were the ones causing most of the trouble? That was a twist.

But no one believed him. Even though he had the evidence — photos of rat crates from Blue Skies, proof that the guy who set the gym on fire was a townie — no one cared. To them, it was all his fault for not doing anything about it. Maybe they had a point, though; he’s the king of the school and yet he just sat back and watched? His opinion wasn’t worth the paper it was printed on.

And why would he care anymore? He had been expelled, and it’d take an unbelievable amount of miracles to even get him back. The good news is that his mother wasn’t yet back from her honeymoon cruise with stepdick, assuming she didn’t throw him out for someone else, which was very likely. Thank God for that. A few months back, during Christmas, she sent him, besides the awfully cute sweater that he often lent to Pete, a postcard of her and that phony. He almost threw it away, but kept it for posteriority; he barely remembered his face and was glad he couldn’t recall his name. It did remind him of how checked out she was from his life.

One day, while hanging around the Blue Skies — he was now starting to realize the name must’ve been ironic, because the guy there was always a shade of white, gray, or black — he met this girl. Tall, short red hair, punk-y vibe. Having also been expelled from the school, thanks to Mr. Burton, she made his Hopkinsinator go wild. (He really needed a better name for that.)

“Why don’t we teach that creep Burton a lesson?”, she asked him one day.

And they did.

"He's meditating."
"No Jimmy, I think we actually killed him."
"Oh. Okay."

Zoe was different from the other people he’d flirted with. She had this spark, this edge. She wasn’t like anyone else from school — no judgment, no BS, the type of person who wouldn’t defend him always but would side with him when it was important. She just got him. A pretty young thing she was.

One day, he convinced her — or well, she convinced him — to have a date of sorts in a nearby warehouse. Technically, it belonged to Tad’s father, but he was pretty sure neither had stepped foot in there for a while. The place was huge, filled with an unimaginable amount of objects, which was rather normal for a warehouse. Like every law-abiding citizen, they broke down everything they could win; he broke more items, so he could say he was the winner.

And yet a part of him felt bad for doing this. Not because he didn’t like her — he definitely did — but because his mind saw it as a distraction from the inevitable. Eventually, his mom and stepdad would take him to another school, farther away from Bullworth — and it would’ve all been to no avail. He’d be abandoned at another school, left to rot, start fresh again. And there was no guarantee that his ninth school would be easier than the other eight, that there would be no Garys coming after him.

It was amazing. Gary had shit on him from such a height that he thought God himself had crapped on him.

Wandering around the school campus felt so weird. People from virtually every group in school looked down on him, like he was the lowest of the low, like one of those hobos that hung around in Bullworth Town. Almost felt funny. One day he happened to find Petey, one of few who wouldn’t drag him down — partially because it was something he’d never do, but partially because if he did, who’d be by his side? He was now starting to understand why he always seemed more defensive of Gary than the others.

By then it was about 7 p.m., and Pete had been going back to the dorm after retrieving some stuff he forgot in History class. He and Jimmy agreed to walk for a bit, even though he didn’t know for sure what to say. It was awkward. They ended up near the fountain, avoiding the judgmental glares from passing students. Hell, even Algie wasn’t looking at them.

“This is it, huh?” Jimmy muttered.

Pete looked at him, clearly worried. His friend looked hopeless. “It shouldn’t be, Jim. N-not like this.”

They stood there in silence for a while, not knowing what else to say. The tension around them felt thick, and even though neither wanted to acknowledge it, they both knew things weren’t going to magically fix themselves.

“I really think we should do something,” Pete said eventually.

“Do what? All we know is townie kids beat up a bunch of Bullworth kids. Why should I care? I was expelled.”

“Well, we’ve got to find out what’s going on- come on. You’re not just gonna let them win, right?”

Jimmy sighed, defeated but not completely broken or something. “Okay. But if we’re gonna take on the townies, we’re gonna need back-up. Someone big, who doesn’t hate me yet.”

They thought about it for a second. Then, at the same time, just like a great idea, they pointed at each other with the same answer.

“Russell!”

Later that evening, when Jimmy arrived at Russell’s house — a big, grayish building that felt bleak even by Vale standards — he was surprised to find Russell still home. But what really threw him off was the police motorbike parked in the garage. Was that Russell’s dad’s? he wondered. Or… had Russell somehow stolen it? All possibilities were on the table with him, but Jimmy decided not to think too hard about it.

Russell grinned as he revved up the bike, and before Jimmy knew it, he was on the back of the police motorbike, gripping Russell’s jacket as they sped off toward Blue Skies. The image of Russell tearing down the streets on a cop bike, with Jimmy hanging on for dear life, perhaps understandably, caught the attention of a few preppies who happened to be out that night. Jimmy noticed their shocked expressions as they passed. And by the time they reached the rundown streets of Blue Skies, Russell had mowed down at least a dozen of them. One of the guys definitely wasn’t a preppy — possibly just a hobo who got hit by a stray rock. Yikes.

“Woah-ho! Russell, slow down a little.” Jimmy yelled, the wind slapping his face. He was amazed that he hadn’t flown off the bike and been flattened by now. He’d known what it was like.

They were just a few hundred feet from their hideout, whose location was… rather obvious, in retrospect, but he got this hint from Zoe after asking where she usually hung out. A simple question, one that still led her to call him a dumbass for 30 minutes straight. Fair enough.

“Okay… oh no!” Russell suddenly yelled.

“What? What do you mean, ‘oh no?’”

As Jimmy bellowed over the roar of the engine, he realized that they were headed straight for the gates of the complex. Too fast. Way too fast. “You can’t stop it?!”

In a blur of motion, they crashed headfirst into the gates. Remarkably, they survived — only slightly bruised but not dead, which unfortunately meant the plans of replacing Jimmy with a second Jimmy clone would need to be delayed indefinitely. Russell, true to form, immediately bolted off into the complex, leaving Jimmy standing there in a daze. He thought about chasing after him, but then decided this was a fight he needed to handle alone — well, almost alone. Zoe would help, sure, but most of the heavy lifting was on him.

Traveling through a series of industrial buildings and places he hoped he’d never have to go through again, he found himself inside a power plant, the heart of the townies’ turf. Jeez, this place is scary. It was dark, cold, and felt like a maze of steel and shadows. But he kept pushing forward until he found Edgar, the guy who supposedly led the pack of dropouts. Of course, he had a rather complicated plan to put into action; he’d march in there and beat the crap out of him.

And by some miracle, Jimmy did just that.

“You still think messing with me is a good idea? What made you think it was a good idea in the first place?”

Edgar lay sprawled on the cold concrete floor of the plant, gasping for breath. He’d been bested on his own turf, a blow to his pride. It was embarrassing. But for Jimmy, it was a small victory that proved he still had it in him. He didn’t fall onto a vat of acid, real or not — he defeated his first non-Bullworth enemy. He still got it.

Winded but still defiant, Edgar glared up at him. “Aww, man. I hate that school. My parents couldn’t afford to send me there, and now I’m stuck in this dump of a town. Gary said we’d make them all pay.”

Jimmy’s eyes widened. “Wait — did you say Gary?”

That night, Gary was in a rare, carefree mood. He’d been graciously invited to Bryce’s birthday party, which was being held at Tad’s mansion. The choice of venue might have raised a few eyebrows — why not Bryce’s own place? — but Gary wasn’t surprised. He knew the truth: Bryce’s family had been hit by financial troubles, something he (and the other preppies) would never admit outright.

The party was pretty lively, though it didn’t take long for the preps to get completely wasted, barely an hour in. Gary had indulged in the finest food — gourmet dishes that he knew he’d likely never taste again, which wasn’t exactly a bad thing. In the middle of stuffing himself with pâté or some other fancy nonsense he couldn’t recall, Gord leaned in with a tip-of.

“Stuff went down in the industrial district. Word is, Jimmy took control of the townies.”

Gary froze, spoon halfway to his mouth. That was not part of the plan.

Pretending to be calm, he excused himself and, with a half-empty glass of cognac in hand, slipped upstairs to one of Tad’s ridiculously posh bathrooms. That was fancy. Once inside, he sat down on the floor, back against the marble tiles, and screamed as loud as he could into the echoing space.

“Fuuuuuuuuuuck!” He cursed at the top of his lungs.

He was, indeed, unbelievably fucked now.

This was bad. Unbelievably bad. His carefully orchestrated takeover was now spiraling out of control, and now Jimmy-boy, out of all people, had pulled a deus ex machina out of his ass, flipped the script and took over the clique he aligned himself with. He was screwed. He had no Plan C, no Plan D, no mysterious plan Z, no backup. This was supposed to be the endgame, and now… now he had nothing.

After some time, he managed to pull himself together, though his nose was runny, and his eyes were puffy from frustration. When the party ended and he returned to the dorm, he tried to keep his composure, hoping Pete wouldn’t notice how disheveled he looked.

Gary was tired. Tired of everything unraveling. Tired of having to come up with more schemes, more lies, more whatevers to take care of one dumbass student. And for the first time in a long time, he wasn’t sure if he could keep up the act much longer. He really needed to do something.
