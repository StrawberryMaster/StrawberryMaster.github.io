Chapter Summary

Jimmy and Gary face each other for their long-awaited, pay-per-view final showdown. Pete tries to not waddle himself along the way.

The football field was unusually quiet that afternoon.

Normally, this place would be buzzing with activity — jocks yelling, balls flying, nerds being thrown out the stairs and beaten in the field. Curiously, none of that had happened so far. The field was eerily empty.

Pete was sitting next to one of the trees near the field. He didn’t mind the quiet — in fact, he liked it. He had come here to sketch, hoping for some inspiration, but his heart wasn’t in it. His pencil moved sluggishly over the paper, tracing a lazy outline of the football field below, but the drawing lacked life, like it wasn’t even his. It looked more like a Gary sketch rather than a Pete sketch, that’s for sure. He stared at it for a moment before sighing, closing the sketchpad, and leaning back against the rough bark.

His mind drifted. Things at Bullworth had changed ever since Jimmy got expelled, and not for the better. Each day felt more off than the last. It was like the school had become a pressure cooker, and everyone was waiting for it to explode. Maybe it already exploded; he just hadn’t seen the effects of it yet. And now, Gary was in charge, just like he’d always wanted. But what did that mean for Pete?

Each day, he felt like he was constantly walking on a tightrope, unsure when it would snap beneath him or who would throw the rock that would crash into him and have him fall down. But now that he was officially the top dog at the school, would he finally calm down? Would their dynamic shift, or would Pete always just be the tagalong? The guy on the sidelines, there to help but never quite part of the action…

Okay, he had to admit this wasn’t bad all the time. Sometimes he just wanted a break, and sure enough, he got many of them. But he always wanted to be there, even if just once, to do something and be rewarded for it — the only time he did anything remotely close to this was when he helped Jimmy take down Johnny in that lonely winter night. Pete closed his eyes for a moment, letting the breeze ruffle his hair. His thoughts were getting more and more repetitive, circling the same points over and over each day. It didn’t help that half his friends had gone off the deep end while the other half had been expelled. Even at times where he felt like he had a lot, it seemed like he had nothing.

Jimmy had his flaws, sure, but at least he’d been something like a friend. He made Pete feel seen, even if it was only when things went wrong. Now, without him, Pete felt… sort of abandoned. He’d never admit it out loud, but part of him missed the mess Jimmy always brought with him. Anything was better than this strange, hollow calm. And being stuck between Jimmy and Gary only made things worse. At this rate, he’d end up with no one.

He opened his eyes and stared at the empty field again. It was getting harder and harder to find something — or anything — that made him feel like he wasn’t just drifting through the days, waiting for someone else to tell him where to go.

Sometimes he just wanted to end it all.

His thoughts were cut off when, out of the corner of his eye, he noticed a group of jocks and nerds stumbling (or laughing?) as they tumbled down the stairs leading to the field. That must’ve hurt. For a second, Pete thought they were playing some weird game, but then he saw it — their movements were off. They weren’t just messing around, he was missing the obvious. They were fighting.

Confused but not exactly surprised — hell, how many times had they tried to kill each other? —, Pete grabbed his things and quietly started making his way back to the dorms, doing his best to remain stealthy (a successful strategy, given that few people even recalled his name) and hoping to avoid whatever was about to go down. But as he moved across campus, it became clear that the whole school was in chaos. Everywhere he looked, students were at each other’s throats. Not that they never weren’t, of course… but why now?

Nerds punching greasers, preps throwing fists with jocks, students swinging at whoever was closest — like the very thin fabric holding them together had snapped. Jeez. Things didn’t even get this bad when the clique leaders betrayed Jimmy less than two weeks ago. It was like there was no order to it, no strategy — just chaos. As Pete neared the gym, he spotted a familiar figure standing by the entrance.

Gary.

Something clicked in Pete’s brain. It was him. It had to be him. Who else could’ve triggered this madness? How could he have been so naïve in not realizing this before? What was he even doing out here, though? Pete was pretty sure “dying in the battle” was not part of Gary’s plan, whatever it meant at this point. Unless he wanted to take him with to their impeding death — which would be admirable, but neither what any of them wanted. He needed to get out of here.

Curiously, when he looked closer, it didn’t seem like Gary was enjoying much of it, as Pete expected him to. Instead, he looked… worried? Or something close to it, at least. It was hard to tell — people were screaming left and right, and as much as he didn’t want to hear those screams in his brain, it all blurred together.

It didn’t take a minute for Gary’s eyes to lock into Pete’s. Without hesitation, he strode over, grabbing Pete’s arm. “Come on,” he muttered, dragging Pete away from the scene. He barely had time to process what was happening as Gary led him through the madness and back to the dorms, his grip firm but not rough.

The dorm was much calmer than the rest of school, it seemed. Once they were inside, Gary shut the door behind them. He turned to Pete, his face unreadable. “Stay here,” he said, his voice unnervingly calm.

“What?” Pete was still rattled by everything he’d just seen. “Gary, what the hell is going on out there? Why is everyone fighting?”

He shrugged, waving his hand like it wasn’t a big deal. “Don’t worry about it. You don’t need to see this part of the story.”

Pete narrowed his eyes, confused. “What do you mean, ‘this part of the story’? The school’s falling apart! I thought things were supposed to change after Jimmy was gone. You said they would-”

Gary sighed, running a hand through his hair. “Look, you don’t need to be a hero today, okay? Just stay inside. Let it play out. It’s like Halloween — just kids fighting over candy. Except this time, I get all the candy. We can split it 50/50 if you want.”

Understandably, Pete wasn’t satisfied with that answer. “What are you talking about? Why are they fighting like this? Did you—”

“Pete,” Gary cut him off sharply. “Stop. I’m telling you to stay inside because you don’t want to see what’s coming. Trust me.”

That word — “trust” — was famously not a part of Gary’s lexicon. He trusted no one — not even himself. And yet, here he was, asking Pete to trust him now. It was worth noting that this wasn’t a one-time thing; Gary did trust him enough to confide in him the idea of taking over the school, and when he moved back to his dorm room, he didn’t seem to care about the chances of Pete telling Jimmy that his biggest rival was there, in that same dorm, living with one of his best friends. (He never told Jimmy about this, and one day he would, but now would be a bad time for it.)

But something in Gary’s tone — something he couldn’t quite place — made him hesitate. This time, it didn’t seem like he was being manipulative, at least not as much as usual, or that it was a mind game of his. It’s like he was actually concerned for him? Protective, one could say. It was confusing.

His gaze softened just a fraction, and he placed a hand on Pete’s shoulder. “I don’t need you out there, okay? Just stay here. Stay safe. I’ll be back.”

Before he could say anything else, Gary turned and left, the door clicking shut behind him.

Pete stood there in the sudden quiet, though the distant sounds of fighting still echoed through the walls. Part of him wanted to run outside, to figure out what was really going on and maybe put a stop to it all. But another part — the part that had often followed Gary’s lead — told him to stay put.

And so he did.

He could imagine Gary giggling and laughing as he left the dorm, only for him to take the school “for good” and Pete would wake up tomorrow to an image of Jimmy hung on the school’s flagpost. And then… there would be no way he would get away from the rest of Gary’s plans. It was terrible. He could already imagine his mocking. Good puppy, good puppy. Rollin’ on over for The Man…

If only.

Jimmy had his work cut out for him, even with the townies on his side.

For starters, Russell was MIA. After the not very legal-ish raid on the townies’ hideout, he’d vanished, only to be found a week later by Jimmy at an old meat factory in the same complex. Naturally, he smelled like — wow! — meat. He even offered Jimmy some of it, which he accepted out of courtesy, although he did not eat it.

“Russell wants… more… salami.” said a rather pixelated version of Russell.
“I think that’s just shit, Russell. Like actual shit.”
“Oh. Bad.”

The place was crawling with cops, and since breaking and entering wasn’t exactly smiled upon, Russell had to make a quick getaway on a bike parked conveniently by a bus stop just a few feet away from the entrance. (The bike was not intended for a person of his size, so chances are the bike rode him home instead.)

With two powerful cliques on his side, the townies and bullies, all he needed to do was get back at Gary. Although easy — kicking people’s asses was commonplace to both groups —, he recalled that he did this to pretty much everyone in that school and yet still got fucked in the ass. Many times, too. At least eleven times; that strategy wouldn’t work. Only thing he could do now was to do what, in retrospect, he should’ve done much earlier: kick Gary’s ass.

It was amazing, really. Had he just clocked him in the dorm, or even thrown him inside that trashcan, none of this would’ve happened. Probably.

By the luck of the draw, today was also the day Gary decided to make his move. Zoe rushed in, panicked, telling Jimmy the school had erupted into chaos. Great. There goes his date with her. But anarchy at Bullworth was even worse than getting stood up, especially if Gary somehow became in charge. So, with a sigh, Jimmy headed to the school with a — luckily — freshly bathed Russell in tow.

When they arrived, Edgar and some townies were already there, oddly concerned but not ready to jump in. Rumors of the violence had reached town, and it wouldn’t be long before the cops showed up. After one of his more creative speeches and using all of his remaining acumen, Jimmy convinced them that now was the time to act, and they, just like a real American would, needed to spread their unique brand of freedom. He just hoped Bushworld would never hear his speech; he had enough problems with the law already.

And oh boy, it would be an understatement to say the place was out of control.

Yeah, he was definitely not stopping all of that by himself. They decided to split up; Jimmy and Russell would take down — again — the other clique leaders, while Edgar and his group would take care of the students (you know, being the nice, kind-hearted people they usually are). Thinking the boys’ dorm would be mostly safe from the chaos outside, their first stop was the girls’ dorm.

If he got a penny every time a boy invaded the girls’ dorm, he’d be rich; if he got a penny every time a greaser did it, this number would be much lower — they had Lola in their hands already, so what else would they do there? (Unless you were someone like Peanut.) If he got a penny every time he saw a greaser in there with the easily most popular girl in school… well, he’d just get one penny. Seeing Johnny there, getting freaky with Mandy in one of the rooms, while the school was just minutes away from blowing itself up as so funny that all he could do was laugh.

And beat him up too, that was also important. Unwilling to fight much, however, Jimmy managed to convince him to get his fellow members under control, in exchange for pretending nothing happened in the last 5 minutes.

“Never seen you, never saw you, never gave you this five-dollar bill as compensation,” Johnny, face slightly bruised from a fight that certainly happened at another moment, stared back at him.

“Pleasure not doing business!”

Business is business. With the greaseballs under control, it was now time to take on their sworn enemy: those rich preppies. Although he’d never been a fixture of Harrington House, especially after arranging an “armistice” of sorts for both groups last winter, he was pleasantly surprised to find out the door was unlocked — and that the place itself was intact. Jimmy assumed, perhaps, that not even the other students wanted to deal with a post-Hopkins Bullworth somehow led by Derby. Not even Gary would like that, which said a lot about how loved he was.

Seeing Derby just standing there like a coward, drinking tea while the other preps battled around like crazy was just insane. On the other hand, this was very well a thing he’d do. To make things easier for him, he let Russell and his towering presence inflict some psychological terror on him, enough to make him shit himself and give them more time to take care of the other cliques.

The nerds weren’t too far from there — when he arrived at the gym building, still showing hints from the time it almost burned down, he found Earnest and a few of his compadres burning down the Mascot’s head, having impaled it. Hot damn, they did that? he wondered. He privately hoped this was a spare costume and not the one he nicely borrowed from Constantinos.

“Y-You don’t get it!” Earnest desperately insisted. “With my brains and his lack of morals, nothing can stop us.”

“What? You’re full of shit.”

Impressive, Earnest, but disturbing. And so he also went down.

That only left the jocks, who, unsurprisingly, decided to wreck the entire library. Unlike the other three, Tad was not willing to go down without a fight, but there was no big football game night to help him out. When all of this ended, regardless of who ended up winning, they’d have to clean up the building regardless. Why even tag the whole place and make it known that they did it? Not even Jimmy was dumb enough to do that. He’d never did that in his life! Never, not even a little bit.

With the school’s cliques under control, he knew it was time to do what he’d been delaying for so long: find Gary. Still thinking he wouldn’t be in the dorm, and seeing how chaotic things had been — where were the teachers, the prefects? —, he went straight to the main building. There, he found out the townies had graciously taken care of some rough bullies that, otherwise, Russell himself would’ve taken care of. That’s it? Wow. It was so easy — now he’d just need to find Gary somewhere in his building, beat him into submission, sing the chorus of We Are The Champions, and freeze mid-jump in the air, like every good movie did.

Except it wasn’t that easy. He was wrong, and the prefects — about three of them — took him down hard, confiscating his trusty slingshot (goodbye, Matilda). They, of course, would think it was pretty easy. Russell chased after them, but it didn’t matter. Jimmy was on the ground, aching.

And as he stared at the ceiling from the cold marble floor, he could hear the echo-y voices from the devil himself.

Your attention, please… Jimmy is complete human trash.

Gary’s voice echoed through the halls. Damn. Where was he? Jimmy dragged himself up and yelled for him, furious. After a minute of shouting, Gary appeared, standing smugly at the top of the staircase.

“Ah, yes,” Gary said, pretending to speak into an invisible phone. “Dr. Crabblesnitch? Sell all my Raytheon stock… I just got an inside tip that Jimmy Hopkins is about to die.”

That fucker was here. That fucker was here to get him.

Gary had been expecting this for a while, but now that the moment had come, he had to uncomfortably admit that he wasn’t as ready as he thought. Mentally, at least.

He never bought into Pete’s whole “b-but Jimmy’s just an angel!” theory. No, Jimmy wasn’t some byproduct of No Child Left Behind or whatever nonsense Pete was peddling. Jimmy was a monster, plain and simple. It had been clear from day one. He wasn’t like the other low-energy nobodies who faded into the background or got smacked down right before reaching their so-called “edge of glory.”

There was no way he was just gonna be a blimp in their lives, no no no. The issue is that it didn’t take long for Gary to notice Jimmy’s hypocrisy — it was blinding. Saying “bullying is bad” was good and all that, but when did he do anything to stop it? Unless you counted Petey, which… no, he hadn’t stopped any bullying besides his own (to an extent; behind his back, it still went on). And yet he continued to push himself as some sort of savior for all their problems, despite failing to solve his own.

It was ridiculous. At least Gary had principles, even if they were a bit twisted. While Jimmy was partying late at night, taking body shots out of Earnest’s concave chest, Gary was using his precious time to refine the details of his plans to strike back at him. Some worked, others did not, but at worst he had a 50% success rate.

By now, Jimmy had fought or screwed over half the school — sometimes both. Usually both. He’d gotten a few lucky breaks, lost everything, and now he was trying to take it all back. Pathetic, really. He should’ve been expelled ages ago, God knows how long this took. If that had happened after he beat Russell, none of this would be happening.

But here they were. And Gary wasn’t about to make things easy for him.

No way he was going to fight Jimmy in that hallway. Not that there was a better spot — the school was probably a war zone by now, and Gary wasn’t keen on getting stomped by an angry mob. He took off up the stairs, toward a door that led to the roof. Perks of being head boy? You had the keys to places like this. (Plus the teachers’ lounge, which was great when you needed snacks and non-diet cola. Out of precaution, he left both doors unlocked.)

As expected, Jimmy followed. The roof, especially the bell tower, was under repair, but delayed funding from the Harringtons had essentially halted construction for months now. It was basically a death trap now, filled with scaffolding and half-finished repairs. Gary hadn’t spent much time up there — except to watch the sunset a few times that week —, but now it felt like the perfect place for their final showdown!

When Jimmy made it up there, rain had started coming down. Great. He could’ve used that spare coat from his closet, so this was a missed opportunity. He saw a rather distraught version of Gary. Not a surprise, because that’s how he had been for a while. He seemed excited — or maybe just desperate, given how everything had gone for him lately. Or maybe it was because he was getting soaked.

“Gary!”

He grinned. “Moron!”

“Why’d you do it?” Jimmy demanded.

Gary chuckled. “‘Oh, Gary, why would you do such a terrible thing? Why don’t we go around and pick flowers and help the poor and needy?’” He rolled his eyes. “Look at yourself, James. You’re pathetic. Everyone in this school is pathetic. Why wouldn’t I do it?”

“You’re sad, man.”

“I might be sad — but I run your world, moron. Just look around!” Gary swept his arm dramatically toward the school grounds, far below. “You beat up losers like Pee Stain, Johnny, and… uh… what’s- what’s his name again?”

“Who?”

Gary tapped his foot impatiently. “That greaser you hang out with. Whatever. You beat them all for nothing, but guess what? That’s not gonna stop me from kicking your sorry ass tonight.”

“What’s with you and this whole vat of acid idea?”
“Who doesn’t want to get thrown into a vat of acid? It’s fake, but it’s genius. Fifteen years from now someone will try it, you bet.”

Jimmy sighed. “Whatever. Let’s finish this.”

Ironically, though, the epic battle they’d been building toward for months didn’t start right away. Neither of them wanted to fight too close to the edge — Gary didn’t wanna turn into pasta the moment he hit the ground, and Jimmy did not want to fight in the middle of all that scaffolding.

Gary tried his best to set things up, make this moment easier for both. If Jimmy got, well, hit by a wheelbarrow and crashed to the floor, well, that’d be pretty sad. Unfortunately, none of the wheelbarrows wanted to cooperate, and when he realized this, he was on top of the bell tower.

Welp. This is it. He thought. There was nowhere left to run. No way out unless he suddenly grew wings… or claws. Yeah, claws. Claws would be way more useful right now.

The rain was coming down harder now, soaking them both to the bone. Gary was trapped in the corner of the bell tower, no easy way out. His back was to the edge, and the only thing between him and a fatal fall was a couple of feet of crumbling stone. Jimmy stood a few paces away, fists clenched. He was still pissed off, and there was no cute face he could make that would make him soften.

“The thing is,” Gary started, trying to regain control of the situation, “if I win, you’re just another punk. If you win, you’ll be sent away even quicker for beating up the head boy!”

“Why did you do it, Gary?” Jimmy asked. His voice was almost lost in the downpour.

Gary’s smirk faltered, but only for a second. “Because I can. Because making little people like you and the morons who run this place eat out of the palm of my hand feels great.”

“But I never did anything to you!”

“You would’ve — if I’d given you the chance!” he shot back, his voice cracking slightly as he backed up further, until his foot grazed the very edge of the platform. Gary looked behind him, panic flashing in his eyes. Below them was the headmaster’s office — way, way down. He wasn’t sure if he could even do a one-toe stand here without toppling over. The thought sent a shiver down his spine, and it was not from the rain. His face was wet, water streaming into his mouth, and when he tried to swallow, he coughed instead. Jimmy saw this.

“What, cat got your tongue?” he stepped closer.

“Fuck you,” Gary spat, but there was no bite to it. He was backing into a corner — literally. The bad news is that his corner was not infinite, and there was nowhere else to go. Jimmy knew it, and Gary could feel it in the pit of his stomach. “Aren’t you the ‘king’? You went all the way from the crackhouse you live in to beat me. Come on, James! I’m waiting.”

Jimmy took another step forward. “Look, man, I didn’t come here to kill you or anything. I just want to know what the hell happened. Just ask Petey what he thought about you.”

“Petey?” Gary’s laugh was hollow. “What, did he say I suck? That I’m bad, that I ‘ruined your life’? Wow, what a great fucking friend he was.”

Jimmy shook his head. “N-no. No, he never said anything like that. Dude said he was worried about you. What did you two even have anyway?”

“Oh shut up,” he snapped, voice barely above a whisper now.

Gary’s mind was gnarling at him now. Hit this sucker already. He needed to keep his hands — both, preferably — on the wheel, at ten and two, and he was struggling to even enter his vehicle. The idea brewing in his head was terrifying to consider — one that could destroy Jimmy’s life forever, but it might just end his too. He hated that he was even considering it. But maybe…

He let out a bitter laugh. “You know… in another life, I would’ve loved playing golf with you — friend,” Gary said, drawing out the word with a strange tone. Jimmy didn’t interpret it as the usual sarcasm — it seemed off. “All the fun we could’ve had…! But I like to think of this as an education.”

Confused, Jimmy’s brows furrowed. “What the hell are you talking about?”

Realizing the Hopkins would be unable to comprehend even the most basic of sentences, Gary stared back at him. He looked scared. “Everything. This whole place, all these people… they’re nothing. You and me… we’re different, Jimmy. You can pretend you’re better than me, but deep down, you know we’re the same. This was never about them, right?”

Jimmy shook his head. “You’re dead wrong. I’m nothing like you, Gary.”

“Maybe not yet.”

Gary was teetering on the edge at this point, his whole body trembling. Technically, if he aimed just right… he’d fall on some scaffolding, probably not getting too much hurt. If James didn’t have such luck, well, that’d be so sad — he’d get away mostly unscathed and Jimmy would be history. And then he could say he finally had a well-deserved victory. If he didn’t have enough luck, he’d be the one shredded at the glass ceiling far below him. If neither had enough luck, well — that was a gamble.

The rain was soaking every inch of him and his hair was matted to his forehead. If he wasn’t liking this moment, he now hated it. Hated how the cold drops stung his skin, hated how the weight of it made everything feel slower, heavier. Hated how he somehow hadn’t yet punched Jimmy — but why hadn’t he done the same yet? They should’ve fought and ended this right here, right now.

A strange, almost serene smile spread across his face as he stared out into the stormy night, his heart pounding in his chest. Honestly, he could barely hear it over the roar of the rain. His eyes flickered back to Jimmy, who was watching him closely, as if trying to figure out what he’d would do next. And for a moment, Gary almost laughed. Of course Jimmy-boy couldn’t predict him. No one ever could — but at least he could, well, do something.

But he didn’t — he just stared there, slightly alarmed, but didn’t go at him like he thought he would. James leaning self-control? Impossible. He uses a few of his famous retorts — barnyard animals here, “your mom is a serial hooker”, asking how many students on campus he had deflowered — which got him riled up, enough to make him just three feet away from him. And yet the punch never came.

Looks like he’d need to do this on his own then.

He slowly — and curiously, hesitantly — pushed himself forward, his body tipping toward the mini-abyss.

“Wait!” Jimmy lunged at him, his hand outstretched. Although he wanted to teach Gary a lesson, he didn’t want him to die. No, no, he wasn’t that thirsty for revenge.

On the other hand, Gary didn’t expect him to catch him so soon — that and account for the sudden panic that took over his own body. As Jimmy’s hand reached for him, Gary jerked back in reflex, his elbow connecting hard with Jimmy’s ribs. The impact wasn’t intentional, but it was enough to throw both of them off balance.

And then they were falling.

“How long are we gonna keep falling for, by the way?”
“At least seven minutes.”

Pete sat on his bed, Gary’s words still echoing in his mind. Stay here. As if curling up in his bed like a child would somehow protect him from everything that was going on outside…

Although he had to admit it worked, it was not by his own actions. As the arguing and loud crashes seemed to die down, he felt a gnawing discomfort. The longer he stayed still, the more anxiety clawed at him. What even happened? What’s Gary doing, exactly? And most importantly, where the hell was Jimmy? The guy was the only one who could put a stop to this, the only one with enough willpower to find out what went down and deal with it.

And Pete… well, he couldn’t just sit there, doing nothing. To be fair, he could — but he wouldn’t.

He pulled himself out of bed and glanced out the window. The rain was coming down hard, making it impossible to see more than a few feet ahead. He sighed and shook his head. He wasn’t exactly built for moments like these — his immune system wasn’t the best, honestly. For years, Gary would joke to him about a time years ago, when they were younger, when there was this rainstorm that lasted almost an entire week. They got a little crazy being stuck indoors for that long; Pete started to cough after the second day, and Gary had a sore throat and a runny nose not too long after.

His parents would call in, occasionally, and he’d insist they were fine. (Gary’s parents couldn’t care less about how their son was doing there, it seemed.) “This is fine” would end up being the standard response to everything his parents asked him something about school, even if it wasn’t.

There were a lot of things he got used to — for good or worse.

Grabbing his raincoat from the bed — he’d left it there for a while now —, he slipped it on and made his way to the front door of the boys’ dorm. The campus was eerily quiet, not a soul in sight. It was almost funny — the school was fairly normal that day, at least until everyone started beating the crap out of each other, and now it’s so… calm? As Pete stepped outside, the cold rain hit his face, seeping into the hood of his coat. He pulled it tighter around him, shivering a bit. Hopefully he didn’t catch a cold this time.

His eyes darted around. There really seemed to be no one in sight, no exaggeration. What the hell. The place looked like it was abandoned, no sign of the students or the prefects. Even the usual lights that lined the school paths seemed dimmer — though maybe they were always like that. It was like the whole school had vanished.

Pete did notice some flashing lights near the school gates. Police cars; their sirens were on but muted. Well, at least they were here to find out what was going on, but he found it unlikely that the 2-4 cops in those cars would take care of the rest of the school. Would a cop even take care of Russell? Nah.

He couldn’t see anyone near them, though. Not a single officer standing guard. Huh. The sight unsettled him further, but it only fueled his urgency to get to the main building. Pete steeled himself and started his way toward the school, the rain beating down harder with every step. That was unfortunate — his shoes sloshed through the puddles, the cold oozed through his socks… yeah, he was definitely getting a cold. But now was not the time for this: something was wrong and Jimmy, if he was still around, could’ve been in danger.

When Pete finally reached the doors of the building and stepped inside, well, what a surprise: the place was also deserted. Wow! No students, no staff, no prefects.

“Hello?” he called out, his voice sounding too small, smaller than it already was. The only thing that his ears received was the echo of his own voice. At least they could’ve turned the lights on. Pete couldn’t help but feel creeped out as he cautiously made his way up the staircase to the second floor.

As he reached the landing, he noticed the main office was lit up. Oddly, there was no sight of Ms. Danvers there, although the chances she would’ve been at school at this time of day even if nothing was going on were very, very slim. The door to Dr. Crabblesnitch’s study stood ajar, the light from inside spilling into the hallway.

Curiosity and unease tugged at him in rather equal measure. Pete swallowed hard and slowly walked over to the door, peeking in. What he saw made his jaw drop — Dr. Crabblesnitch, himself, the top dog of the school, tied up in a chair, looking extremely furious.

“Come and untie me, boy!” Crabblesnitch barked the moment he noticed Pete standing in the doorway. He had been there for a while, Pete assumed — but he had a good idea of who had done this already.

“Uh, sure…” Pete rushed over, fumbling with the ropes. His hands shook, but he managed to loosen the knots, freeing the principal from his binds.

“Thank you!” he groaned, rubbing his wrists as he stood up. “That bastard kid… do I know you?”

Pete blinked, taken aback. “Uh… I guess?”

“Do you know me?”

“Y-yes, you—”

“Good! Now stay put and tidy this up for me, if you can. I have to find that rat and restore order to this place!”

Crabblesnitch straightened his jacket, barely giving Pete a second glance as he stormed out of the office, his shoes clicking against the polished floor. Well, that was fast.

Half dumbfounded, he stood frozen for a moment before glancing around the study. He shook his head and began putting the chair back in place, carefully arranging things as best he could. Things weren’t that out of place, besides a few open drawers here and there. Finally, when the room looked somewhat normal again, he plopped into the chair on the opposite side of the desk — the very one where Crabblesnitch usually sat.

It was funny sitting here. Although he’d been to the office a few times — luckily for nothing too bad — he’d never sat on this very chair. And of course, not under the exceptional circumstances he was in, which almost certainly involved an ugly plan of Gary’s.

“Wow,” Pete muttered under his breath, the absurdity of the situation settling in. He barely had a moment to process it when, suddenly—

CRASH!

And so two bodies came hurtling down from the roof, breaking through the ceiling with a thunderous noise that rattled the entire room.

Pete leapt out of the chair, eyes wide in shock as dust and debris settled. There, tangled in the wreckage, were his friends, Jimmy and Gary, both groaning as they lay sprawled out on the floor.

Shit. “W-what the hell!?” Pete exclaimed, rushing over to them.

His voice was trembling. Hell, he was trembling. Jesus, there is glass everywhere now. What were they doing? How did they even get up there- ugh. Pete’s heart raced as he knelt beside them. “Are you guys okay?”

Jimmy grunted something that vaguely resembled a “yeah.” Gary, somewhat more disoriented, gave Pete a shaky thumbs-up before his arm dropped weakly back to the ground.. “Oh, fuck,” Pete whispered, panic rising in his throat. This was bad.

There were so many questions in his head and for a moment, he had genuinely no idea what to do first. He was barely processing what had just happened when a sharp sting suddenly brought his attention to his own arm — there was a jagged piece of glass embedded in his forearm, some light blood mixing with the rain dripping down his skin.

“Fuck,” Pete hissed under his breath, gripping his arm instinctively, and wincing as the pain surged through him. His head spun slightly at the sight of the blood. He tried not to freak out, but everything was a blur — his friends lying on the floor, the destroyed office-

Maybe being a hero was harder than he thought.

A couple of days had passed since the incident, for lack of a better term for it. Yet Pete felt like he was still reeling from everything that had happened. And he understood this — it was all too much to process.

Jimmy, miraculously, had come out of it mostly unscathed. A few bruises here and there, but nothing serious. Pete couldn’t believe it — he fell through a glass ceiling and almost walked away from it like it was just a normal fight. He wasn’t made out of steel, but he must’ve definitely been made out of aluminum. Gary, on the other hand, wasn’t as lucky — the guy had to be rushed to the hospital (partially because the infirmary was as empty as it could get), for a few nasty cuts and a possible concussion. His right arm had been in a sling since then, and word had it that he’d broken a couple of ribs during the fall. Was it true? He had no clue, but he hoped he didn’t. To be honest, he still hadn’t been able to shake the image of Gary’s shaken (no pun intended), pale face right after it all went down.

And Pete’s arm? Well, it wasn’t great either — he did get bandaged up (and later got some proper stitches) at a local clinic, and his arm still throbbed from time to time, especially when he tried to move it too quickly — but it could’ve been worse. He was mostly fine, though, and for that, Pete was grateful. His parents called him day and night — they heard the news about things going down at the school, but he insisted that everything was okay and under control. If only…

He never got a word back from Jimmy about what they were doing there. Sure, he expected some sort of fight to have happened — it’s not like they would’ve just jumped there for fun! — but it didn’t look like they even threw a punch at each other. Pete actually wondered if something else went down, but Jimmy always deflected from this topic when they brought it up. If he didn’t wanna do it, his tough-as-nuts roommate would probably kill him if he tried to.

Whatever else, his supposed takedown of Gary brought him immediate stardom — the boys’ dorm played Celebration on-loop for a record 49 minutes before someone eventually shut it down. The relief was that Jimmy was no longer expelled, per the head’s orders; he could probably shoot someone on Fifth Avenue and he wouldn’t bat an eye. It also led Pete to become head boy — the same position that drove Gary to madness. It was ironic, and honestly, kinda sad.

Jimmy was now spending time with this red-haired girl he liked called Zoe, which Pete vaguely remembered from last year. She was nice, and patted both of them on the head an unbelievable amount of times. It was cute, it was funny, and he was glad that Jimmy seemed… happy. This also meant they were one of the few who got away from the arduous task of cleaning up the school, which the school cliques had to do — reportedly the nerds passed out at the sight of cleaning up the gym, which was fair, because he would too.

But being the head boy felt… weird. Not because it put him in a high position, but because virtually nothing changed. He assumed that, now that Jimmy had things under control — for now, at least — things would get peaceful again. Of course, this depended a lot on whether Gary would be expelled or not, and if he didn’t, welp. Here we go again.

Speaking of him, he’d actually be out today from the hospital. How did he just get out was beyond him (where were his parents? What about the cops?). Pete didn’t ask him about the specifics — though he insisted he was okay — but he asked him if they could talk somewhere. Thinking that he wouldn’t want to be in the school right now, he suggested the pier; Gary said he’d “think about it”.

It was about 7:30 p.m., and Gary was 45 minutes late.

Bummed out, Pete, thought of just going back to the dorm. He doesn’t wanna see you, he thought. He wasn’t sure why… but considering that they’d barely spoken to each other since that day, besides a 2-minute-long phone call, he likely wouldn’t want to see anyone else.

But just as Pete was about to leave, there was the devil himself. Gary, walking toward him in a black sling, wearing a dark gray button-up shirt and matching slacks. He chuckled to himself — Gary looked way too formal for someone fresh out of the hospital. Pete, by comparison, was just in his everyday outfit, minus his pink school vest. What caught his attention though was not his clothes, but the fact he had a rather ugly looking black eye.

Gary approached slowly, taking a seat next to Pete on the pier — although he hesitated before fully sitting down. The rain from earlier that day had stopped, leaving the air rather cool and damp. Pete watched him, unsure of what to say at first — before breaking the silence.

“Uh… what happened to your eye?” he gestured to the black, swollen bruise that stood out starkly against Gary’s pale skin. He’d expected him to be better — and to an extent he was, assuming he wasn’t medicated until oblivion — but not like this.

“My dad,” he muttered. His graze was drifting down to the wooden planks beneath their feet. “Look, it’s… I’m gonna have to talk to Crabblesnitch about it. Sort some things out.”

This would be much easier if you hadn’t tied him up, dumbass.

“Are you- are you gonna get expelled?”

Gary exhaled sharply through his nose, a bitter laugh escaping his lips. “Oh, I doubt it. I’ll be back there in no time,” he said, not sounding extremely confident. “You know, I have a few contacts of my own… they can clear up some, erm, misunderstandings that have happened.”

Pete’s brow furrowed, confused and more than a little concerned. How could this man, after everything that had happened, all the destruction he’d caused, all the self-hatred he consumed, all the hell they’d gone through this past year, still want to do it all again? Typical Gary, he guessed. But not even Melvin and Sheldon could get him out of the mess he created.

“Why, Gary? W-why all of this? What was the point of everything you did?”

Gary didn’t look at him. His fingers tapped nervously against his leg, eyes still fixed on the ground as if it held some kind of answer that only he and no one else knew. “You know why,” he said quietly. “It was the plan. You… you knew it. You liked it. Everything was going so well.”

“But why did you have to hurt yourself like this? You fell from so high, and y-you could’ve really hurt yourself. You could’ve died,” Pete insisted, in his usual soft voice. “You didn’t need to do this to get what you wanted.”

And maybe he was right. He could’ve just thrown him from up there and let the good times roll. Yet if Jimmy was also facing all these sorts of issues, who couldn’t have said that he wouldn’t face them at some point? It was obvious. Of course, he was much superior to the low-energy Hopkins, but doing this every time felt just dumb. There’s gotta be more to life than this.

Gary didn’t answer right away. His silence stretched out between them, and Pete thought maybe he wouldn’t respond at all. After what felt like an eternity, Gary finally spoke, staring at him — although his voice was barely above a whisper.

“Sorry.”

It was weak, almost like it pained him to say it. Pete could count on his hand the number of times Gary had said that word in a sincere way — most of it happened years ago, when they were still kids. The most recent one was over a year ago, way before Jimmy arrived, and it happened out of the blue. He was in the common room when Gary walked in, sat on the sofa, and started crying — really hard — on his shoulder. Pete never asked why, and he’d honestly forgotten about it — but not his big, sad eyes that day. And it almost made him tear up a little.

He never understood why someone like Gary ended up sticking with someone like him — so childish and weak at the seams. Their friendship was far from healthy, but they never stayed too far away from each other. Closest to this was when he left the dorm after the incident at the Hole, and he didn’t really stop seeing him.

Describing him wasn’t an easy task — he was a bit of a “known unknown”, and filling out the gaps in his knowledge that he knew existed, but didn’t know what they were was unnerving. In fact, there were things about him of which he was so unaware, he didn’t even know he was unaware of. And it was so hard to find out which way to go.

Gary shifted beside him, leaning his head onto Pete’s shoulder. He let out a long, tired sigh. Maybe, he thought, he knew something — regardless of his awareness of it or not — was over for him.

Pete remained quiet, watching the soft shimmer of the water in the distance. With summer fast approaching, he figured he’d be spending more time at his parents’ house than in the dorms. It felt odd, to think of leaving all of this behind for a while, but he knew that he needed this.

“Hey, Pete?” he asked, somewhat less guarded. “Do you think I could… stop by your house? Sometimes, you know. Maybe often,” he added with a tiny hint of awkwardness. “We could play some games or something.”

And Pete couldn’t help but brighten up at that. It wasn’t a grand gesture, but coming from Gary, it felt like a small step toward something — if not better, then at least something different. Familiar, interestingly, and somehow different. He smiled to himself and hummed a quiet “yes.”

They just sat there, listening to the faint sounds of the world outside the pier. Above them, the marquee moon hung high in the sky, glowing brightly — and what a beautiful moon that was.

Pete couldn’t wish for it to be more different.
