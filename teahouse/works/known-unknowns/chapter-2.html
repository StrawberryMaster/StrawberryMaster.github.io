Chapter Summary

Jimmy shows Gary his new slingshot. Gary does some investigating. And Pete suffers a bit more.

The early morning light cast a rather sickly yellow glow across the common room as Pete shuffled in, his hair sticking up at crazy angles, and eyes still heavy with sleep. He paused, blinking in surprise. That room was a mess — whoever was playing poker yesterday got too carried away and threw the table across the room, black and red chips everywhere. He probably stepped on a red 19 himself! But it wasn’t the chips that surprised him; it was the TV. It was on.

Whatever once was on the screen was now replaced by a distorted, but watchable, image of a show. He could definitely see something that looked like a tiger in there. In front of the TV, Gary, with a smug grin plastered on his face, sat sprawled on the floor, a disassembled mess of wires and a screwdriver abandoned beside him. Curiously, he was spotless.

“Well, look who finally decided to show up,” Gary drawled, not taking his eyes off the TV. “Took you long enough, Petey. Didn’t think you’d miss your daily dose of talking heads.”

Pete gaped. “You… you fixed it?”

“Pfft. Of course not, I made Sheldon do it.”

“S-Sheldon?”

“Yup. You ever heard of cheap labor?”

“But how? None of us could get it right.”

Gary scoffed. “Please. All it took was jiggling a loose connection, something any of you drooling morons could have done.” He gestured towards the TV with the screwdriver. “Voilà! Now this dorm can finally shut up.”

Pete stared at the TV, wondering what actually happened. But since it was fixed… he wasn’t going to question it, right?

“I didn’t know you liked Animal Planet.”

“S-shut up.” he moved to the couch, and Pete sat next to him.

Gary’s grin widened as he leaned closer to Pete, his voice lowering. “You know, Petey, I’ve been thinking. Bullworth is a cesspool of idiots and losers, we know that. But I, erm—” he pointed between himself and Pete — “we could run this place. Put all the losers in their place, make them submit to us.”

Pete’s eyes widened in surprise. “Us? But Gary, I don’t know…”

“Think about it,” Gary interrupted, his voice growing more intense. “We could — and will — rule this school. But first, we need to know where everyone stands. Especially Jimmy-boy. I’d keep an eye on him. See if he’s truly an ally or just like everyone else.”

“Gary, I really don’t know…” Pete began, his voice trailing off as he tried to gather his thoughts. Honestly, he didn’t want to run the school; the last thing he wanted right now was attention. But he couldn’t say it was an appealing idea — no more being bullied by Russell, no more being ignored by the girls, the promise of something more than just surviving Bullworth.

Gary leaned in closer. “Come on, this is our chance. We can finally make a difference here, take control over this dump. You and me, we can do it.”

Pete looked into Gary’s eyes, seeing the conviction there. Admittedly, it was hard to resist the pull of his vision — it just seemed too good enough to be true. He took a deep breath, feeling the weight of the decision pressing down on him.

“So it’s a good idea, right?” Gary pressed, his voice eager.

Pete swallowed hard, nodding slowly. “Yeah, OK, alright sure… I’ll do it.”

Right after responding, the door swung open and Jimmy stepped into the room, looking curious.

“Hey man, what’s going on?” Gary greeted him.

“Not much. What’s going on here?” Jimmy asked, glancing between the two.

Gary stood up, his demeanor shifting. “I was just telling Petey here about my idea to take over the school. I mean… my plan for us to take over the school.”

“What plan?”

Pete was nervous, and it was difficult to hide that; he tried to focus on the TV, but Gary’s plans swirled in his mind, making it hard to concentrate. He felt a knot forming in his stomach, but he didn’t want to show it. Gary sat back down beside him, a satisfied smirk on his face.

“…you know what, Petey, you were right. Jimmy is pretty dumb,” Gary said casually.

Jimmy, feeling provoked, gave Pete a sharp look. “What’d you say about me?”

Pete’s eyes widened, and he quickly tried to defuse the situation. “Whoa, nothing, no, no, no! All I said was that you had to be… pretty dumb to get expelled from so many schools, that’s a-all!”

“Relax, James,” Gary said, walking closer to both. “All he said was that you must be dumb… or, maybe you’re all messed up because you came from a broken home.”

Moments before disaster.

Jimmy’s expression darkened as he took another step toward Pete. “What did you say about me, dwarf?”

“Come on, dude, chill,” Gary interjected with a laugh.

Pete stood near one of the room’s windows, his hands raised. This was embarrassing. “No, no, no, no… Gary’s taking everything out of context, man. I didn’t mean it like that-”

Gary’s grin only widened. “Whoa, whoa, whoa. Don’t lie, Petey. Don’t you lie. Because you know what happens to liars?”

Oh-oh.

“No- no, I’m not lying!” Pete protested, his voice rising in panic.

“We kick them in the balls!” he declared, and it happened. Ouch. Pete landed with a grunt, wincing in pain, and Gary laughed as he watched him struggle to sit up. “Come on, Jimmy, let’s see how good you are with this new slingshot I heard you had.”

Jimmy shot a final glare at Pete before following Gary out of the common room.

“Don’t worry, friend, nothing escapes my notice. I hear everything. You and me, we can do things…”

As he heard the dorm’s main doors closing behind him, Pete sat up slowly, rubbing his side where he had hit the couch.

“Ow,” he muttered to himself, reaching for the TV remote. He flipped through the channels, settling on an old rerun of a show from the 90s. Unfortunately, no sounds of laughter or canned applause could ease his pain.

Later that afternoon, as the final bell of the day rang, Jimmy was free from his Gym classroom. Thankfully, nothing much had happened that day — besides the occasional bullying which he had already grown accustomed to. That and finding out Mr. Burton, the gym teacher, sure was a dick — how did the school even let someone like him teach?

Quickly changing into his regular clothes, Jimmy stuffed his gym gear into his bag and left the locker room. It turns out he had left some stuff in his own locker, so he headed towards the main building to retrieve it. As he made his way through the unsurprisingly crowded hallway, weaving past groups of students, he finally reached his locker.

He turned the combination lock with relative ease and opened the locker door. To his surprise, someone tumbled out, faceplanting the floor and landing in a heap at his feet. It was Pete!

“Pete? What the hell?” he exclaimed, staring down at his friend in disbelief. To be fair, it’s not like it was the first time this happened during his stay here. Precisely, it was the ninth — but it was the first time his locker was the chosen one.

Pete scrambled to his feet, dusting himself off. “Sorry, it was Trent,” he explained, rubbing his shoulder where he had hit the locker. “He thought it would be funny to shove me in there.”

“How long were you in there for?”

“Umm… maybe 10 minutes?”

Oh.

He frowned. “Trent, huh? I’ll have a word with him.”

But Pete shook his head. “D-don’t worry about it. It’s not the first time.”

Jimmy sighed, leaning against his locker. “You gonna be alright?”

“Yeah, I’ll be fine,” Pete replied, managing a weak smile. “Hey, are you doing anything later?”

He nodded. “Yeah, but I’ve got plans. Algie needs my help with something, so I’ll probably be busy with them until late tonight.”

Pete’s shoulders sagged a bit, disappointment flickering across his face. “Oh, okay. I get it. Busy day.”

Jimmy clapped him on the shoulder. “We’ll hang out soon, Petey. Just gotta take care of a few things first.”

“Sure, no problem. I understand.”

Jimmy gave him a final nod before heading off down the hall, leaving Pete standing by the open locker. Although he obviously understood his obligations, he wondered how easy it would get to follow him around — he wasn’t even sure if he had to do anything or if Gary had some other things he wanted to extract from the boy.

Pete left the scene shortly after — his shoulder now sore and his face burning from that fall. Luckily for him, the second floor bathroom was just a few feet away.

A few weeks passed, mostly in a blur of classes, conflicts, and some random stuff mostly relating to the jocks. Although Pete found himself hanging out with Jimmy whenever he could, those times were usually overshadowed by Gary’s more domineering presence. Jimmy was often preoccupied with the bullies, and the nerds to a lesser extent — with him being the only person willing to help them, he practically became their savior. And he was also wrapped up in his own adventures with some of the girls, meaning he spent most of his time watching him from afar rather than actually interacting with him. Aside from the occasional “hi” in the dorms, that is.

One late night, Pete was alone in the common room, his nose buried in a thick, dusty book about classical Chinese stories; Gary found that one in a trashcan near the English classroom. The dormitory was quiet, with most students either asleep or sneaking around the campus, which gave him just enough space to read it outside his room. While he read the book, Gary slipped in, carrying a bag stuffed with various items.

“Hey, Petey,” Gary greeted him. “Check out what I got from my supplier.”

Pete glanced up from his book, curious. To his surprise, Gary pulled out a carton of mango juice and some leftover food, placing them on the couch. “Dinner is served,” he announced with a grin.

He couldn’t help but smile as he reached for the juice. “Um, thanks, Gary.”

Gary shrugged, flopping down onto the couch. “Don’t mention it. Figured you’d be starving since you’ve been too busy reading those old books.”

“They’re not that old! And I like them.” Pete frowned, closing his book with a sigh.

Not too interested in hearing that, Gary rolled his eyes. “Whatever. You know, it’s funny how Jimmy’s always hanging out with those loser nerds like Pee Stain and that creepy hobo by the school bus. He’s got the potential to be someone important around here, but no… he wants to waste his time kissing Earnest’s ass.”

“Jimmy’s just trying to figure things out, I guess.”

“Figure things out?” Gary scoffed. “He’s enough of a loser already. You see, Petey, that’s the problem. He’s only interested in hanging out with those freaks and weirdos. It’s like he doesn’t see the bigger picture.”

Pete took a sip of the mango juice. It tasted good, but he was trying to ignore the growing knot in his stomach. He didn’t like the way Gary talked about Jimmy, but he also didn’t want to provoke Gary’s wrath. Many moons ago he tried that and, unfortunately, it did not go well. “J-Jimmy’s a good guy, really. He’s just… different.”

“Different,” Gary echoed mockingly. “Oh no, Jimmy-boy is ‘different’! Yeah, instead of being a ruthless vandal, we’re gonna find out he’s just school shooter material! That won’t cut it for me.”

It’s silent for a moment as Pete drinks more of the juice. He hasn’t even found out what food Gary bought him in the bag he had, and part of him didn’t even want to know.

“Speaking of which, I need to get more info from my… sources. People have been saying things about me, and I want to know what.”

“What kind of things?”

“Things,” Gary said vaguely, leaning back with a sinister smile. “Rumors, gossip, who cares? All I know is that knowledge is power… whoever the quote source is, and I intend to have all of it. Maybe you should think about that next time instead of looking at the shit I find on the floor.”

Pete hesitated, looking down at his book. He really wasn’t going to argue with him today, not about a book out of all things. With a resigned sigh, he placed it on the table.

“Alright, okay,” he said quietly. “But also… why are you dressed like that?”

His outfit looked like it was scavenged from a thrift store clearance bin — or maybe, given his own interests, a trashcan. An oversized, tattered jacket that looked somewhat familiar (maybe his father’s?), a teal sweater, some dark gray slacks, and a pair of worn boots. It’s kind of surprising how no prefect seemed to spot him, apparently.

Gary smirked, glancing down at his clothes. “Pfft. You don’t know what a good disguise is. Maybe soon you will, though. Maybe… now shut up and let’s eat.”

“Yeah, sure,” he replied back timidly.

“Excellent.”
