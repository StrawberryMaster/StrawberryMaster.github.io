Chapter Summary

Pete votes in the school's upcoming class president election. As Halloween nears, Gary prepares a surprise for his two friends.

A few days later, Pete found himself once again in Mr. Hattrick’s Biology class. Hattrick was as strict and stern as ever — which was no surprise to anyone —, and paced slowly at the front of the room, very much like a prison warden, with his sharp eyes were scanning the room for any signs of misbehavior. You’d think they were working with plutonium, not plant cells.

Pete had already finished his tasks and was now left to his own thoughts, drifting off as he absentmindedly doodled in his notebook. He drew a tiny castle perched on a turtle’s back. It was funny. Stupid little things, perhaps, but he liked them more than the dissection instructions they’d ignored all week.

The door creaked open, and Kirby, one of the jocks, entered the room. “Mr. Hattrick,” Kirby began, his tone uncharacteristically polite, “some of us need to leave for early voting in the school’s president elections. You know, t-that- one with Ted and Earnest.”

Hattrick raised a skeptical brow, folding his arms across his chest. “Early voting, now? Ridiculous. But fine. *Only* if you've completed the assignment.”

Pete almost groaned at the mention of the elections. God, that was a mess.

This election pitted Earnest from the nerds against Ted from the jocks in what had become a very dirty campaign. Initially, Pete had half-expected one of the preps to throw their hat in the ring — think of someone like Derby or, alternatively, Bif —, seeing as they practically owned the school, and winning would just be a formality for them. But that hadn’t happened. Since none of the greasers were interested, nor were any of the bullies, and there was *no way* he’d be writing in Constantinos… Ted and Earnest had sufficed, apparently.

Ted had the entire school apparatus — most of the girls — in his corner. Earnest, meanwhile, had a tiny grassroots campaign that failed to make a splash anywhere, except for the times Algie was nearby. The existence of early voting was rather unusual, though. It either meant they wanted to get it done quickly, or maybe Earnest wasn’t polling as badly as Pete had thought.

Soon Hattrick scanned the room, his gaze landing on Pete. “Kowalski, you’re the only one who seems to have completed everything. You can go now — but don’t misbehave!”

Kirby nodded towards Pete, gesturing for him to follow. “Come on, kid. Let’s get this over with.”

Pete gathered his things and followed Kirby out of the classroom. The walk was awkward; Kirby made it clear he wanted no contact with Pete or any of his “creepy friends”. When they reached the auditorium, Pete noticed it had been set up as a makeshift polling station. At the entrance, Peanut, one of the greasers, stood guard. Huh. Maybe he was the only “impartial” observer they could find?

He glanced at them, his expression bored. “You’re here to vote?”

“Yeah,” Kirby replied, nudging Pete forward. “He’s the only one allowed out right now.”

Smirking, Peanut leaned against the doorframe. “Alright, just make sure you vote quick so we can get this done fast.”

“Sure, whatever.” Pete muttered.

Inside, Kirby led him to a curtained booth. There, Pete glanced at the names, and sighed. He cast his vote for Ted — not because he wanted to, but because he imagined he’d be forced to if he didn’t. It’s not like anything would change, to be fair — although the preps ran the school in theory, given their money and social status, no one could argue the jocks had the bigger popularity. Earnest never stood a chance against him, and he thought he was just as authoritarian, if not more, even by Bullworth standards.

On the way out, Peanut gave the two a lazy two-finger salute. “Democracy in action, baby. Now get out of here.”

Pete left the auditorium, his thoughts swirling. That was… fast. He just hoped the other nerds wouldn’t find out about his vote; 1% more sympathy from the jocks in exchange for intense hatred by the nerds would probably kill him one day. A while back, he heard that Earnest and his goons were working on an “experimental” potato-based spud gun, which would be a pretty powerful weapon in the wrong hands. As he walked back to the dorm, he took a look to see if anyone was around.

Supporters for future presidential candidate Earnest do the macarena in solidarity with his campaign.

The common room was deserted — so were the hallways —, save for a lanky greaser sprawled on a worn-out couch, snoring softly. Given his size, it was likely someone like Lefty; Pete considered waking him for a game of checkers, but ultimately decided against it.

Back in his dorm room, the first thing that hit him was the rather disheveled state of Gary’s bed. Unlike Pete’s, Gary’s sheets were tossed aside, blankets bunched at the foot. Ugh. It wasn’t the first time this had happened this month, and it greatly annoyed him because, although not common, when it did happen, he would be the one cleaning it up. Gary could be so inconsiderate sometimes. He sighed, already picturing himself having to remake the bed. Slightly annoyed, he decided to straighten it up.

That’s when he saw it. A glint of plastic amidst the crumpled bedsheets by Gary’s pillow. When moving closer, he found a small, amber pill bottle, nestled amongst some discarded candy wrappers and a slightly crumpled edition of, apparently, “Tintin in the Land of the Soviets”. So he did read, after all…

He picked it up, the weight heavier than the plastic container itself. It was full, almost unused. Strangely, the label was worn.

“Huh,” he breathed. “Why’s this here?”

Okay, he wasn’t concerned about the bottle — he knew Gary was diagnosed with ADHD many years back, so this was likely his — but why wasn’t it tucked away in his drawer instead of half-hidden in the clutter? And why was it full — was he not taking them? Was this just a spare bottle?

There were too many questions for little Pete to answer. Maybe some time later he could think about this again. For now, he thought, maybe he just needed to doze off for a bit.

Election Day arrived a few days later, and Pete volunteered to help count the votes. The process was tedious but straightforward — think of watching paint dry, but taking much less time. The final tally: 22 votes for Ted, a meager 8 for Earnest, and an (un)surprising number of abstentions — a whopping 53% of the student body apparently couldn’t be bothered.

Pete chuckled. Although he already expected a Tad win, he did notice the number of nerds (9) outnumbered the number of Earnest votes (8). He couldn’t help but wonder if they knew one of them had betrayed Earnest — after all, with nine members and only eight votes, someone must have abstained (or worse, voted against their own leader). They would take it well, for sure.

To his surprise, he wasn’t the only one who’d noticed it; although he wasn’t too interested in the election at that point, Gary brought it up to them while hanging out at Jimmy’s dorm.

“So, friends,” he drawled, nudging Jimmy with his elbow. “I heard the nerds are having a meltdown over a missing vote. How pathetic, really.”

Jimmy shrugged, his gaze flickering to Pete. “Doesn’t sound like our problem.”

It was true — it was good gossip, but it was brief gossip, and would fade away after a day or two. If only the school newspaper was still around… he’d see a headline with “Nerds to Earnest: Drop Dead”, and find out one of them would really drop dead the next day.

Pete opened his mouth to agree, but Gary cut him off with a smirk. “Oh, I know exactly which of them didn’t vote for Sergeant Fluffyboots,” he declared, a smug smile splitting his face.

Naturally, both Pete and Jimmy were curious, but Gary was also very sneaky. He simply crossed his arms and said, “Not telling! Let the losers squabble amongst themselves…”

Pete sighed, rolling his eyes. Gary’s love of chaos knew no bounds, apparently. But it’s not like he needed to know who it was anyway; he wasn’t a nerd. On the other hand, he wondered if one of them would figure it out soon — and how would everyone react.

“Anyway, knuckleheads,” he announced, his voice bouncing off the walls of their dorm room. “What are you two gonna be for Halloween?”

Pete and Jimmy exchanged brief glances. Turns out they hadn’t given much thought to costumes — especially Pete, who hated last year’s Little Red Riding Hood costume that he was forced to wear by a rather familiar roommate of his.

“Dunno,” Jimmy shrugged.

“Same here,” Pete admitted.

Gary’s lips stretched into a wide, toothy grin. “Oh, don’t worry, I got you covered, Jimmy,” he said, already heading towards the door. “Just stay put, I’ll be back in a bit.”

He turned to Pete. “And for you, Petey…” he said, wiggling his eyebrows suggestively. “I got a little surprise. You’re gonna love it.”

Before he could question it, Gary was gone, leaving him alone with Jimmy. A beat of awkward silence stretched between them before Jimmy broke it with a low chuckle.

“So,” he said, “looks like you’re gonna be rocking a surprise costume tonight.”

Pete groaned. This would definitely not end well. Moments later, Gary returned, holding a pink bunny costume. Complete with floppy ears and a fluffy tail. Oh, God.

“No way, Gary,” he protested, backing away with a grimace. “Not happening.”

There was no chance he’d enter that fuzzy, slightly cute nightmare fuel. But Gary disagreed.

“It is happening.”

“N-no!”

He clamped his hand around Pete’s wrist, his grip surprisingly strong. “Come on, Petey,” he wheedled. “It’ll be okay this time. Trust me.”

“Gary, I’m serious,” he hissed, trying to yank his arm free. “Let go!”

“Come on, don’t be a wuss. Don’t ruin this night for us, femme-boy.”

Jimmy watched the scene unfold, looking concerned. Deflated, Pete let out a long sigh. “Fine,” he mumbled, the fight draining out of him. “Just for tonight.”

Gary grinned triumphantly as Pete reluctantly and clumsily shoved himself into the costume, the giant pink ears flopping uselessly on his head. Talk about humiliation.

After this, Gary excused himself. “I’m gonna put on my own costume and grab yours, Jimmy. Be right back.” He left the room, leaving Pete and Jimmy alone.

And Pete, rather distraught, just stared at the floor, the absurdity of the bunny costume making him feel even smaller. God, he wanted to disappear so badly. Last year he almost died of embarrassment from having to walk around in such a costume, and this year it would be just as bad. Fuck. Jimmy walked over, trying to offer some reassurance.

“Hey, Pete, don’t worry about it. It’s just one night. We’ll get through this,” he said, his tone gentle.

Pete nodded slowly, but the embarrassment still burned in his cheeks. “Yeah, I guess you’re right,” he mumbled, though he didn’t look up. Maybe, just maybe, things wouldn’t be bad this year.

A moment later, he shuffled back down the hallway to the dorm room. He was kinda surprised that Gary got his size just right, but he was starting to hate the floppy ears that kept drooping over his eyes. That needed to be fixed — anything to salvage a shred of his ever-decreasing dignity.

He reached his dorm room, fumbling with the doorknob and muttering curses under his breath. Just as he pushed the door open, a booming voice shattered the hallway’s tense silence.

“Hey, bunny kid!”

Pete froze, his heart plummeting to his stomach. Standing a few feet away was Russell — the undisputed leader of the bullies —, flanked by one of his goons, who was wearing a… rather funny-looking Edna mask. Well, they had definitely surpassed his expectations. But that was no good…

Pete’s mind raced, desperately searching for an escape route. But before he could even stammer a greeting, Russell’s booming voice filled the hallway again.

“Russell loves bunnies!” he declared, punching his fists together.

All Pete could manage was a weak “Oh.”

He was so dead.
