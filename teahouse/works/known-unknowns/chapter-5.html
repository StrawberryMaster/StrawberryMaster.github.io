There were three entrances to what became known as “The Hole”.

The first entrance, and most well-known, was the door that led to the boiler’s room. Mr. Luntz, surprisingly, rarely stayed there; he hated his job as a janitor and has been especially avoiding the boiler room in favor of hanging out with the “higher ups” in the staff room, to limited success. It was kinda funny how Crabblesnitch, who was desperately trying to find The Hole, couldn’t find something that was just underneath his nose.

At some point, some students managed to make copies of Luntz’s key; this is how they’ve been able to access it. Pete, unfortunately, was not one of them.

The second entrance was a door near the school parking lot that led them to the basement. Lots of boxes, chairs, and even some old appliances were lying around there — but the path to The Hole was also much longer. The advantage of this path is that, assuming you weren’t killed by the live wires, you’d have an easier chance of getting in and out; a dozen or so students would prefer leaving through the basement door rather than fighting their way through the boiler door, and getting paddled by the prefects in the process.

Unfortunately, Pete also did not have a key to the basement door.

The third and final entrance was the most interesting of them: an elevator that took them directly to The Hole! But that was the issue: Bullworth had no elevators, except for one rusty elevator on the roof of the building, near the clock tower. It was inaccessible even for most staff, as Miss Danvers kept the keys locked away for everyone.

Well, except for a few people…

It was about 6:30 pm when Constantinos, who gave him the free ticket, told him to meet up with him near Danvers’ office. The school was quiet at this hour, with most students either in the cafeteria or in their dorms. He was late. Pete fidgeted with the crumpled note in his pocket, the words “Meet me by Danvers – Roof access” scrawled messily across the page. Jesus, his handwriting sucked.

It took about five minutes before he emerged from around the corner, a weird smirk plastered on his face, sauntering towards him.

“Yo, Kowalski,” he greeted him. “Ready to see the coolest place in Bullworth?”

Pete eyed him skeptically. “You got the key?”

He winked. “More than that, my friend. Follow me.” Constantinos chuckled, a low rumble in his chest.

Their walk was short; the access door to the roof was just a few feet ahead of them, near a class. “This is the express route,” Constantinos explained, his voice barely a whisper. “It’s kept locked tight, but a few of us… well, let’s just say we have our ways.”

He rummaged in his pocket, pulling out a rather small, tarnished key. As he watched out for any prefects — fortunately there were none — he unlocked the door. “Hurry up, I don’t wanna be late.” he gestured toward the stairs leading further up the building.

Pete sighed. As they climbed the stairs to the roof, his anxiety grew. Although he had been to The Hole occasionally, the place had been unused for a while — and even then he usually came with Gary, mostly so he didn’t feel left out after missing a fight. Thing is, he was basically going there on his own (Constantinos wasn’t much of a friend, though he was being rather friendly that day, and invited by Gary, if that made any sense.

In fact, he hadn’t even seen Gary that day. At least he was friendly enough to send him a ticket, but couldn’t he have been more, well, direct? That made his stomach churn.

As they climbed, the air grew colder, the scent of dust and disuse clung to the walls. That place hadn’t been opened in a while, likely because work on the clock tower repairs were stalled for about four months now. Rumors had it that Derby’s father, Mr. Harrington, had withheld funding for the clock tower repairs and Crabblesnitch was scrambling to find another source to fund the repairs.

When they reached the top, Constantinos entered an “Employees Only” access door that led him to a rusty, obscure elevator right below the clock tower. It was a single, rickety elevator car, its metal walls adorned with obscure graffiti. It looked somewhat ancient; this had definitely been there since before Pete was born.

“You sure about this?” he stammered.

Constantinos chuckled. “Come on,” he replied, stepping inside. Pete hesitated for a moment before following him into the rather cramped elevator.

With both now in the elevator, he pressed a single, faded button. The rusty machinery groaned to life, the cable creaking ominously as they descended. The brief ride was filled with an unsettling silence, broken only by the groaning of the elevator. Pete didn’t feel like talking; he was still confused about a lot of things that day. A minute or so later, with a shuddering halt, the car lurched to a stop. Constantinos threw the door open, revealing a path to a somewhat dark, circular platform bathed in the moonlight streaming through a series of small windows.

“Welcome to The Hole, kid,” he smirked, leading Pete down the small corridor. The place hadn’t changed much since last time, except it was a lot more crowded now. He could see leaders from all sorts of cliques — from Earnest to Derby, from Tad to Johnny — alongside a few girls and other members of their respective groups. Although the fight wasn’t supposed to start until 7:15, there were three notable absences. One was Russell, a surprise given that he was one of the contestants. One was Jimmy, who, curiously, never told him about the fight either. Why’s that…? He thought they were close enough to talk about these things, unless Jimmy didn’t want him to know.

And the third absence was Gary.

“You know, it’s my first time as a bookie,” Constantinos said with a nervous chuckle, glancing around. He pulled a can of Sprite from his bag and handed it to Pete. “You gonna bet anything?”

Pete took the can, too absorbed in the situation to immediately reply. His mind was racing, trying to process everything that was happening. The anticipation of the fight, whatever was going on between Gary and Jimmy, the overall intensity of the underground arena — he was on edge.

He sat in a nearby chair, watching the scene unfold before him. Or, well, waited for it — most of the people there were chatting, waiting for the two contestants to show up. It was almost kinda boring. If he had the key, he would’ve definitely arrived later; what a bummer it was. When it was 7:05, Russell finally made his entrance. The massive figure of the bully leader moved through the crowd almost like a tank, students parting to let him pass. He looked… okay, surprisingly. He didn’t seem happy, but he didn’t look angry either. But this was not going to be an easy fight for Jimmy.

Five minutes later, to Pete’s surprise, he saw Gary entering The Hole, leading Jimmy by the arm. Jimmy looked rather confused, although he wasn’t sure if he was really confused or if that was his normal face. Gary’s face, however, had a rather sinister glee to it. As they approached the center of the ring, Gary turned to Jimmy with a smirk.

“So, Jimmy-boy, here we are — the Hole — the place where this school separates the men from the boys, the wheat from the chaff and all that nonsense.” His voice echoed through the arena, dripping with malice — and a bit of formality too.

Jimmy looked around, confused but alert. What was he high on this time? “Okay, so what’s that got to do with standing up to people, keeping them in line?”

“This is where I stand up to you, my friend.”

“What are you talking about?”

Gary’s smile turned cold and calculating, more than it usually was. “Oh, I know you hate me, Jimmy-boy. I know you’ve said all that stuff about me behind my back.”

“What are you talking about?”

“Don’t play innocent with me,” Gary sneered. “You wanna run this school, I wanna run this school. Only one of us is gonna make it — and it’s gonna be me!”

Uh-oh.

As he climbed a small ladder, Gary turned to the crowd, raising his voice. “Ladies and gentlemen, boys and morons, I give you... Russell!”

Russell stepped forward, towering over Jimmy with a menacing grin. The crowd roared in anticipation.

“Gary, now I hate you!” Jimmy shouted, his fists clenching.

“I know!” his eyes gleamed with twisted delight. Oh boy, was he loving it. “Russell, go beat that little jerk who said that nasty stuff to me about your mom and those barnyard animals.” Russell’s expression rapidly darkened, and he charged at Jimmy like a raging bull.

Pete watched in horror from his corner, the scene playing out like a nightmare. It couldn’t be right.

The brawl rapidly turned into a dance of punches and swears on the cold, unforgiving platform. There was a small puddle of water below them; chances are both would be sick by the end of it. Or whoever came out alive… and Pete was stunned as he watched Jimmy, thrown into the fray against his will, scrambling to defend himself against Russell’s relentless aggression.

The can of Sprite he was holding almost slipped from his grasp, too. He thought of imagining it rolling across the platform. He hadn’t even opened it. He didn’t even know how to react, or what to do, or… what to not do. He stumbled towards Gary, his legs shaky, without even realizing it.

Gary watched the fight with a rather grim intensity. It was a tough battle, he recognized. He noticed Pete approaching, his eyes flicking briefly to the rather out-of-place can of Sprite in his hand. Without breaking eye contact with the brawl, Gary reached out and picked it up from his hand, popping the tab and holding it out to Pete.

It felt like mocking. He didn’t even want to take it. And he did, the cold metal biting into his palm — but he couldn’t drink from it.

Something happened.

In a flurry of punches and desperate dodges, he managed to land a lucky blow square on Russell’s jaw. The bully stumbled back, momentarily stunned, and Jimmy seized the opportunity. With a final, desperate lunge, he sent Russell sprawling onto the metal platform. Groaning, Russell lay there, defeated, like a dead fish in a pond.

Silence descended. Pete watched, that can of Sprite still clutched in his hand, as Jimmy stood there, chest heaving, a mixture of shock and disbelief etched on his face. It was quite a surprise when he came out alive from it.

He’s meditating, guys.

Gary wasn’t happy about it. He stepped forward, although not entering the mini-arena itself, smirking at Jimmy.

“You happy now, jerk?” Jimmy, panting and bruised, shot back.

“Ecstatic! I love watching two morons beat the crap out of each other.”

“Why did you do it, Gary? I thought we were friends!”

Gary laughed, a cold, rather mirthless sound. He briefly glanced at Pete, then back at Jimmy. “Friends? You and me? I’ve taken dumps that had more brains than you, ‘friend’. No, I’m taking over this school... and you’re just a liability.”

With that, he turned on his heel, heading for the exit. “See you around, moron,” he called over his shoulder, leaving the scene as if it was just another day. Maybe to him it was — but not for everyone else.

Pete, trying to salvage something from the mess, clapped awkwardly. “Well done, Jimmy!” he called, but his attempt to start a round of applause fell flat. Chances are the clique leaders probably just lost all their bets and now had to pay hard cash to Constantinos — and whoever was his boss. Maybe…

Jimmy helped Russell to his feet. “I never insulted you or your mother. Just leave me and Petey alone, alright?”

Russell, still dazed from the fight, nodded. “Okay, Jimmy.”

But Pete didn’t pay attention to the conversation between Jimmy and Russell. When he finally looked behind him, Gary was gone, vanished into one of the corridors that led to the Hole. This was no simple prank or fight anymore — it was about Gary’s warped vision of taking control.

When he climbed out of The Hole alongside the others, using the basement entrance, many minutes later, all he wanted was to get away, curl up somewhere, and ignore the reality of what he’d just witnessed. But then it hit him: Gary was his roommate. They’d inevitably cross paths again.

With a heavy heart, Pete trudged back to his dorm room. As he pushed open the door, he was met with the rather unexpected sight of Gary hurriedly gathering his belongings and stuffing them into a backpack.

“What are you doing?” Pete blurted out, his voice raspy.

Gary spun around, his face contorted in a mix of anger and desperation. “Leaving,” he spat, shoving a crumpled shirt into his bag. “This place is crawling with bastards. I’ve had enough.”

Pete just stared at him with disbelief. What was all that for?. Maybe Gary’s betrayal wasn’t completely personal. Maybe he was just scared, desperate to escape the consequences of his own actions — whatever they were.

“But… the room…” he stammered, his voice barely above a whisper. “Y-you can’t just leave, why did you do that to Jimmy? W-what about us three-”

Gary stopped shoving clothes, his eyes flashing with annoyance. “’Us three’? Petey, you really are pathetic, aren’t you? I expected more from you… but I guess you’re just as useless as the rest of them.”

Well, that hurt.

Pete’s fists clenched at his sides. “I-I trusted you, Gary. You used me.”

“Maybe I used you,” Gary sneered, zipping up his backpack. “You were convenient, a means to an end. But now? Now, you’re just in my way.”

“You’re a monster, Gary.”

Gary slung the backpack over his shoulder, smirking. “Oh, I’m so sorry! I didn’t think that, after months of kissing James’ ass, after weeks of seeing you running around like you’re his fucking lapdog, with no time for your actual friends… that what you really wanted was some diss digg.”

“Diss what?”

“Diss digg. You don’t know?”

“W-what do you mean?”

Menacingly, Gary smirked and grabbed his crotch — a way of provoking his soon-to-be former roommate.

“This dick, motherfucker. It means this dick.” He walked to the room exit, not looking back at Pete. “And don’t follow me.”

He slammed the door, leaving Pete standing there, feeling more alone than ever. He sank onto his bed, burying his face in his hands; he needed some time to reflect on everything he saw that night.
