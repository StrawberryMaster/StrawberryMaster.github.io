Chapter Summary

A concerned Pete goes searching for his roommate Gary. Meanwhile, Jimmy has bigger plans.

A week had crawled by since the events in The Hole. Jimmy, the unlikely victor over the leader of the bullies out of all people, had somehow managed to weasel his way into their circle, now that he had the blessing of Russell and at least tactical support from the rest of the clique. The bullying Pete occasionally suffered from them abruptly stopped, which was quite a relief. Russell did fulfill his promise, after all! An additional relief was that the nerds, who were then mostly ambivalent, were beginning to warm up to him. (Namely Beatrice and… curiously, Melvin.)

Now that things were somewhat calmer, he expected Jimmy to spend more time with him. He was wrong; if anything, he was more busy than usual, doing errands for the nerds, keeping the bullies in line, and doing a surveyance of the preps. That left Pete with a bitter taste in his mouth. Not that it was surprising, but to him, it seemed Jimmy had traded one form of misery for another, setting his sights on Bullworth’s elite rather than his group of friends. Well, what once was a group of friends…

Gary just vanished.

The corner of his room now seemed untouched; it didn’t even look as if anyone had ever slept there. The bed had been neatly made, the pillow firmly in place... it was as if he’d never had a roommate! It was borderline uncanny. Pete pretty much never saw Gary again after that day, not on campus, not in class, and certainly not in the halls. Meanwhile, the school was buzzing with rumors about him: that he was hiding in the bell tower, that he had made “first contact” with the townies, that he was taking drugs with some of the greasers... there were so many wild theories. None of it made any sense. It just… didn’t.

A cynical part of him suspected Gary was still lurking around, brewing some new scheme in the shadows. It was the only explanation for it — it’s not like he could’ve gone that far anyway. But another part wanted to remain firmly lodged in the realm of the unnoticed; it’s not like he could do anything besides look for him, which would have limited results. He could’ve been anywhere in Bullworth, and even if he did find him, he had no idea if he’d be even willing to let him in.

On the other hand… he couldn’t deny that he was curious. Gary, despite the countless insults made at him that he learned to ignore or tried his best to not focus on, wasn’t a terrible roommate (usually), and it’s not like he was now sleeping with his door locked because he was gone. What else would he be capable of? Worst thing he could do is kick his balls again; hopefully not.

After spending so much time looking out for Jimmy, maybe it was time he looked out for Gary too? This could take a while, but he had a few guesses of his own.

Later that day, Pete finally managed to hang out with Jimmy. They met up just outside the boys’ dorm, the late afternoon sun casting long shadows across the campus. Jimmy looked rather energized, even more so than usual.

“Hey, Pete,” Jimmy greeted him with a grin. “Guess what? I’m gonna compete in a boxing contest at the boxing club in town.”

Pete’s jaw dropped. “W-what? A boxing contest? Really?”

“Yeah,” he nodded, flashing a rather toothy grin. “The prize is a home in the Vale worth a million dollars. Can you believe that? I’ll finally be your neighbor.”

He couldn’t help but chuckle. The image of Jimmy living in a mansion was ridiculous. Not that he couldn’t do it, it’s just that it seemed so surreal. Just a few weeks ago he was playing Grottos & Gremlins with the nerds, and now he was headed to a big mansion? It was funny, yet a flicker of hope sparked within him. Maybe, just maybe, things were finally looking up for Jimmy.

“You’re joking, right?”

“Kinda,” he admitted with a smirk, popping his knuckles. “But I’m serious about the contest. It’s not just about the prize, I wanna keep an eye on those rich kids. Make sure they know I’m not backing down, you know.”

Pete’s expression grew more serious. “You think they’ll try something?”

“I… wouldn’t put it past them,” he replied, his tone sharpening. “That’s why I need to stay on top of things. And having you there would help. Contest starts around 7 pm. You in?”

He hesitated for a moment, then nodded. “Yeah, I’ll be there. I wouldn’t miss it.”

Jimmy clapped him on the shoulder. “Thanks, Pete. It means a lot. Plus, it’ll be good to have a friend in the crowd.”

He swaggered off, leaving Pete alone by himself near the dorm entrance. Well, that was interesting. Since he had plenty of time before the contest began, he recalled he had a few other things to take care of first.

It was nearing 5 in the afternoon when Gary woke up and looked at his watch.

“Damn it…” he muttered as he sat up on his cot. He was still pretty tired, but he couldn’t fall back asleep. He was still too wired.

Groggily standing up, Gary stretched as he looked around the room. He saw his surroundings, and frowned for a moment. The lights are dim, and there’s only an old cot, a desk, a small TV, and half a dozen shelves. It’s nothing like staying in the dorms, that’s for sure.

He walked over to the desk, and flopped down on the chair. As soon as he sat down, he groaned. It was stiff and uncomfortable, like everything else down there.

“Of course this damn chair is uncomfortable…” he grumbled as he tried to get situated. Gary sat there for a few minutes, trying to relax, eventually leaning back and staring up at the ceiling. Jesus, this place sucked. Why did he think the school basement was a good place to live in?

Not the best place to be in.

The worst part is that he was exhausted, both physically and mentally. It turns out that planning wasn’t as easy as he thought, especially when he wasn’t sure where to strike next. Being isolated in the basement was good because it kept him away from the criminals in the dorm, including traitors like Jimmy, who happened to be both a criminal and a traitor. Everything was going to plan, and then… Jimmy. That fucking nerd ruined everything.

His mind drifted back to that day in the Hole. How the hell did everything fall apart so quickly? If Hopkins hadn’t tried to backstab him, he wouldn’t be in this situation. Gary had been down there for a week or so and hadn’t seen anything close to real food, just the same snacks that an old hag like Edna stashed at some point.

And yet both situations were eating away at him. Not only did he now have to make plans and keep track of what Jimmy and his newly-loyal luddities — the bullies — were doing, but he also had to find food. Shame burned in his throat — a Smith shouldn’t be reduced to scavenging for scraps. How embarrassing.

He tried to push that thought to the back of his mind for now. He really needed to eat something.

But Gary’s head jolted up from his thoughts as he heads the sound of footsteps. Someone was coming. He froze in place — who the hell was coming down here? Nobody even knew he was here, and there were no fights in the Hole this week. Looking around, he tried to find something — anything — that he could use as a weapon. Apparently, his pillow wouldn’t be enough.

Gary?

Huh. That’s Pete. What the hell is he doing down here?

Gary tried to steady himself, though he remained nervous. He really, really didn’t want to speak to anyone right now, especially one of his supposed allies. But it’s too late now — Pete already knows he’s down here.

He didn’t reply, instead waiting for Pete to say something; he doesn’t. A minute or so later, he lets out a small sigh as he sees Pete. It was definitely him, yeah, no hallucinations or anything like that. But how did he even find him? There’s no way he got a key to here. He was kinda surprised to see him, but tried his best to not show it.

“There you are.” Pete said, approaching Gary.

Gary couldn’t hold back a scoff. “Wow, brilliant deduction, Petey,” he drawled. “You wanted to see how I was doing? Well, I’m keeping my room clean,” he gestured sarcastically to the cramped basement room. “Don’t you have loser friends to hang out with?”

Despite Gary’s hostility, Pete didn’t react with anger. He just stood there, his face somewhat unreadable. Gary soon found himself scrutinizing him. Pete looked… thin. Not that he wasn’t thin, but maybe he looked thinner. And definitely more tired than usual.

This made more sense when he realized that Pete was basically relying on him to scavenge something for them. Oh, he must’ve been enjoying Edna’s sweet casseroles this entire time. Yum!

“Look,” Pete started cautiously, “I know things have been… weird.”

Gary chuckled. “Weird? You could say that. ‘Oh no Gary, things were weird’. Petey, is that the title of your sex tap-”

“But,” he continued, ignoring Gary’s interruption, “I-I just… wanted to see if you were alright.”

Gary scoffed again, but it lacked its usual conviction. “You really are a dumbass, you know that?”

“M-maybe so. But a-at least I’m trying to care about you, even when you’re being an ass. That’s gotta count for something, right?”

Well, that was going as well as he could’ve expected. Hopefully he wouldn’t ask how he got there — Sheldon is a jack of all trades, who would’ve guessed? — or try to lock him down there. That’d be cruel, assuming he didn’t know about the elevator. That or, well, the part about getting down to the ground…

He looked at Gary a bit closer, his eyes scanning over him. He… didn’t look like he was in a good shape. Slight bags under his eyes, a bit of hollowness in his cheeks, he looked like he was on the verge of a breakdown. Pete could smell a faint odor coming off of him and God, he didn’t smell good. He probably wasn’t washed in days. Couldn’t he have taken a bath elsewhere, at least?

“Hey, um, I gotta ask you something.” Pete said, tentatively.

Gary just grunted, looking at him with an uninterested expression.

“What is it.”

There was no way Pete could say something like “Hey dude, you kinda stink!” without being walloped by him and being thrown out the Hole’s elevator shaft.

“Have you eaten anything lately?”

That was a stupid question, Gary knew that. Of course he was eating, unless Pete decided that he was dead and that he’d need to channel his spirit instead. That’d explain some stuff.

“Yeah, I’ve been eating.” he muttered back sarcastically. “What about you?”

“N-no, what exactly have you been eating?”

Gary just shrugged. “Food, dumbass.”

Looks like he really didn’t want to have this conversation. Pete looked around the basement, taking in the quite pitiful surroundings. He noticed some snacks and other packaged food scattered around the room. That’s all Gary’s been eating? Not that he expected a full kitchen down here, but still.

“I’m not a child, Petey. I don’t need you to take care of me.” Gary said gruffly. “I can manage on my own.”

Pete wasn’t so sure about that. “You look like hell, man. I’m gonna make a soup for us or whatever, if I can find something to use down here.”

He slumped back into his cot, grumbling. “Fine.”

With that being said, Pete scurried around the dimly lit basement — he spotted a forgotten shelf tucked away in a corner, its surface cluttered with an assortment of odds and ends. He rummaged through it, finding a dusty can of diced tomatoes, a dented box of chicken broth, a crumpled packet of instant noodles. It wasn’t much, but it was more than enough. Even better: there was water (treated water, he hoped) and also a hotplate he could use.

Pete set to work with surprising dexterity. Although not a chef at heart, his mom taught him the very basics of cooking — including how not to burn the entire house. No monoxide poisoning happening on his watch! He filled a chipped enamel pot with water and tossed in some diced tomatoes. As the water boiled, he emptied the chicken broth and the noodles, stirring the concoction with a rusty spoon he found nestled amongst the half-dozen forgotten utensils.

About 25 minutes or so later, he was done. Pete ladled it carefully into an also chipped mug.

“Here,” he said, extending the mug towards Gary. “It’s… tomato soup. I h-hope you don’t mind.”

Although Gary hesitated for a moment, there was no denying that it looked surprisingly inviting. Huh. So he did know how to cook. With a grunt, he took the mug from Pete’s hand.

The first sip was heavenly. It was warm and savory, and it’s the first real food he’s had in days. Hell, maybe in weeks, if he ignored the usual dumpster food or whatever crap Edna was making. He could practically feel his body thanking him as he swallowed it. And then he took another spoonful, then another, silently eating as he tried his best to ignore Pete’s watchful eye and show him no indication that he was enjoying it. That would be demonstrating weakness — which he would never do.

Standing there, Pete just silently watched him eat. When he was done, he finally looked up at him — much more alive, for better or worse — and muttered a gruff “thanks.”

“No problem, I guess.”

He laid back down on the cot, exhaustion maybe catching up with him. “Now shoo. We can talk some other time,” Gary murmured, his voice barely a whisper. “Not now.”

Though a part of Pete didn’t wanna leave him down there, he didn’t want to argue with him about it; it’s not like he’d convince him to go back to the dorm anyway. That and Jimmy would be at the very least wary of his intentions — why hang out with the guy who just betrayed me?

As he headed back up the basement steps, he took one last look back at Gary. He seemed vulnerable, for sure, but as he had known for a while and had personally seen it recently, he was not someone to keep an eye on. Funny for Pete; he’d have to keep an eye on him another time. This evening, he had a boxing contest to watch.
