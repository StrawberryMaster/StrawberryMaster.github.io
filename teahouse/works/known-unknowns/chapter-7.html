Chapter Summary

Pete spends his time thinking about stuff.

Chapter Notes

If Pete got a penny for every time Gary had mysteriously vanished over the last few weeks, he’d have a total of two pennies. Admittedly, not very much, but it was weird that it’d happened twice.

When he came by to visit him the next day, he wasn’t there. He tried again another time, only for nothing to appear. Then the next day, and voila, nothing. He’d searched for him everywhere, retracing his steps to the basement (many times), even venturing out the campus at night with a flimsy flashlight… but nothing happened. Eventually it turned into a game of Where’s Waldo?, except this time he had to find Gary and, unfortunately for him, he was usually never seen in a large crowd. No easy way to spot him either; although Pete was very familiar with his teal vest, he hadn’t seen a glimpse of it. Only thing he was able to find was a half-eaten bag of stale chips in the basement, and he was definitely not eating that. His insistence on finding his former roommate later reached the ears of Jimmy, who, annoyed by this, asked Pete to tone it down. No more fun for today.

It felt… weird being all alone in his dorm room, however. Sure, he now had peace and quiet — and no more chances of waking up to an obscure-ish nightcore tune — but it was a woozy feeling for him. At least other things happened while Gary was around, but with Jimmy it was a bit different. Not that Jimmy let him in every adventure of his, of course, but between the two, only one of them told everything that happened with excruciating details…

But maybe he was thinking too much about it.

And he tried to move on, or at least not focus on it as much. One day, he managed to dodge dodgeball during gym class thanks to a suspiciously well-placed trip wire, so that was fun. He even found himself acing a surprise chemistry quiz, after a lucky spill of baking soda neutralized a particularly noxious potion — thanks, Peanut — that was brewing at the lab table next to him. Maybe Gary was just synonymous with bad luck and he never realized it.

One particularly dull Tuesday, he was in English class with the perpetually inebriated Mr. Galloway. Honestly, he felt kinda bad for him, but he also didn’t wanna get into his personal affairs. In the meantime, he doodled absentmindedly in his notebook: today’s drawing was a misshapen dragon battling a rather ugly stick figure knight. He shouldn’t have given him hair.

Galloway, his face flushed crimson and voice thick, was attempting (and failing) to dissect the intricacies of metaphor — or whatever he was supposed to discuss that day. By the time Pete paid attention to him, he was talking about something completely different.

“And then,” he hiccupped, “she asked me if I was far-left or far-right on the political spectrum? C-Can you believe it?”

The classroom remained silent. Seemingly oblivious to the lack of response, he let out a braying laugh. “Me? Far-left? Far-right? Ha… bitch, I’m farting.”

Pete stifled a snort. It was a classic Galloway moment, equal parts sad and hilarious. Admittedly, it was the funniest thing he’d said in days — but it was kinda saddening because the non-drunk Galloway was much funnier. There was just no easy way for him to tell him this, though.

As Galloway glanced around the empty classroom, a sudden suspicion dawned on him. He squinted at Pete, who just so happened to be the closest student to him.

“Oh, hi Peter… where… where is everyone?” he slurred, pointing at the little boy with a shaky finger.

Oh, yeah. The classroom was empty, save for the two. Pete could’ve sworn there were more people in there that day — but maybe at some point they just went out. Either that or class had ended and none of them noticed it, which would explain a lot. He had no way of guessing, and honestly, didn’t want to think about it.

Pete cleared his throat. “I… well…” he stammered. “Since attendance is a little… low today, would it be alright if I read a book or something?”

The drunken teacher’s face contorted in confusion. Galloway blinked, his eyes focusing on Pete for a moment. Then, a slow smile spread across his face. “Read a book? In my class? Oh… yeah… that’s brilliant, yes.”

He lurched towards his desk, his movements unsteady, and soon came back clutching a not-so-dusty book in his hand. He thrust it towards Pete with surprising vigor. “You know… Deirdre gifted me this for my birthday… uh… I forgot when exactly, but it was a good book. I think I can lend you it for a bit?”

Pete gingerly accepted the book. “Great Expectations,” it read. He flipped through the pages, the faded yellow giving him a rather strange sense of comfort — it reminded him of the random stuff Gary found in the local dumpsters. Oh yeah, Gary…

“Just… try not to fall asleep,” Galloway muttered, leaning back in his chair with a sigh. “I don’t even know how many pages this had.”

Pete nodded, a small smile tugging at the corner of his lips. It was better than nothing, that’s for sure — although a part of him was nagging him for doing the thing he criticized himself for: not having many friends, and doing whatever he could to ignore that.

He loaned the book to Jimmy.

He wasn’t exactly sure why he wanted it — hell, he’d barely finished Chapter 2 when he asked for the book. And he couldn’t deny that the thought of Jimmy actually reading anything was faintly amusing — all he had done this month was beat random people around! Thinking it would help him get his mind away from the Preps and all other Bullworthian nonsense, he agreed.

It just so happened that Galloway wanted the book back. Turns out he wanted to do something with the class with the book, which was a surprise, but he didn’t really question it. In fact, he actually forgot about the book in the first place! So this was embarrassing for him. He had to get the book back. Stopping by Jimmy’s dorm, he found the door locked. At first he thought his roommate had locked it — then he realized Jimmy never had a roommate. Huh. Why did he get his own dorm room for free? Not that he was the only one without a roommate, of course, but it made little sense to him.

That did remind him — a while back he recalled Jimmy bragging about winning a “sweet beach house” at the boxing match, which turned out to be a consolation prize since he wasn’t eligible for the million-dollar home in the Vale. Looks like they wouldn’t be neighbors so soon.

And given how “cool and exciting” (in his own words) it was, it would be no surprise if he was spending his time there. It was less exciting when, one early morning, just before class, he went to the clubhouse himself retrieve the book. Jesus, these mornings were getting colder and colder — he was just a few days away from walking around Bullworth wrapped around in a blanket. Knocking on the door, he was taken aback for a moment when, instead of Jimmy, he instead saw Trent — the blonde-haired bully — standing before him. Did he get the wrong address?

He looked a bit disheveled, like he had just woken up — a white tank top, a pair of plaid pajama pants — nothing too unusual here. Well, besides the fact he was there. This was no bully territory, unless Russell happened to be a real estate agent. Would be a good role for him, honestly.

“H-hey, umm… is Jimmy around?”

Rubbing his eyes, still looking a bit out of it, he took a moment before responding to Pete.

“Uh… yeah, he’s here.”

“Can… can I talk to him?”

Trent just grunted, stepping aside so he could let Pete in. He nodded gratefully and stepped inside, glancing over at him. Trent looked confused. What was he doing there so early? It’s not like Jimmy was fighting against him or the other bullies anymore, and he found it unlikely that he was gonna ask for their help to take down the preps. Something was missing; he couldn’t help but notice the dazed expression on his face.

“Are you… okay, dude?” he asked tentatively.

He shrugged, running a hand through his messy hair. “Just had a bad night.” Trent muttered, avoiding eye contact with him.

Well, he was probably not gonna get much info from him, but this was odd. Trent wasn’t acting like his usual brash, confrontational self. He was quiet and withdrawn, which was a bit unnerving. On the other hand, it was barely 8 a.m. — what else would he expect? At best, he was just being grumpy. At worst, he had a bender last night. Too many possibilities for him to analyze.

“Ah, gotcha.” Pete replied, trying not to pry too much. Luckily for him, he was easily able to find Jimmy — he was longing on a chair next to a- whoa. Was that a Future Street Racer 3D machine? At this time of year, at this time of day, in this part of the country, localized entirely within his clubhouse?

Whaaaaat.

What a place, huh?

What were the Preps thinking? These were super rare — only 50 of those were made worldwide, and the closest one to him was all the way down in Bridgeport. Who knew what else would’ve been there?

Pete briefly looked around the beach house. Despite its age, Jimmy had made it his own. There was a poker table, quite similar to the one at the dorm (except in much better shape), an interesting fireplace, and he even had a bar of his own! It felt more like a home than he had expected, and not exactly a cheap one.

Jimmy soon spotted him and waved hello. Pete, trying his best to ignore these precious discoveries, waved back.

“Nice place you’ve got here. You’ve got that book I lent you?”

“Petey!” he grinned, scratching his head. “Yeah, I read a bit of it. It's good, but man, it’s heavy stuff. You can take it back if you want.”

Hoping he wouldn’t get any spoilers from him over the next few days, he took the book from Jimmy’s outstretched hand.

“And thanks,” he replied. “It’s not the mansion I was hoping for, but it’s better than nothing.”

“Yeah, I guess so,” Pete said, flipping through the pages of the book. Had he even read anything? It looked just like it was when he lent it to him. “I'm glad you’re doing okay though.”

Jimmy gave a nod. “Yeah. You holding up alright? I know things have been… uh, rough.”

They were — sorta. Pete’s actual thoughts on the disappearance, reappearance, and second disappearance of Gary were getting too confusing even for a nerd’s brain; if someone like Algie tried to dissect his, they would likely find nothing of value. And that’s the way the world goes…

Pete sighed. “Yeah, it’s been tough. I haven’t seen Gary in a while, and I’m not sure if that’s a good thing or a bad thing.”

This was half-true — but Pete didn’t want to tell him about the basement encounter. It was personal, after all, and there would be nothing of value gained (besides some chuckles) about Gary eating canned rat food and not showering for half a millennia.

Cautiously, Jimmy placed a hand on his shoulder. “Hey, don’t worry about him. We’ll deal with whatever comes our way. Just keep your head up, alright?”

He smiled weakly. “Thanks, Jimmy. I’ll try.”

They briefly continued chatting as Jimmy mentioned his plans for the night. Tonight, he was planning on egging Mr. Hattrick’s house. A funny idea, though he was kinda surprised he didn’t think of it earlier. If anything, it sounded a lot like a Gary plan — in a past world, maybe, this is what they would’ve been doing right now.

Pete chuckled. “Why? I mean… I can see why, but any reason in particular?”

“You know, him being a prick and all to Galloway and the other kids. So I’m meeting up with Tad and some of the preps tonight. You in?”

“I don’t know, Jimmy,” Pete mumbled. Though he wasn’t opposed to the idea in principle, he wasn’t in the mood for it. It wasn’t even about the preps, too; even though he was ignored by most of them and was bullied by a few not too far back, they had moved on to other, weaker targets. The closest prep to him was like, Parker? And even then, they were at least one Tour de France away from each other. But right now wasn’t the time for it.

“There’s a lot on my mind, you see. M-maybe we can catch up later today if you want?”

“Sure thing,” Jimmy shrugged. “Just let me know. I could hear more from you sometimes, see…”

He would — as soon as he got his hands on that Street Racer machine. The power of 3D… stereo… Dolby… talk about fascination. As Pete turned to leave, he noticed Trent silently heading to the only bed in the room, flopping down without a word. Perchance…?

Nah. Maybe it was best if he didn’t know. He was snooping around too much. It was weird. This month had been the most peaceful in his life in many years, perhaps, and yet it also seemed the most hectic. Very few things were happening to him, but many things were happening to others — including his friends.

The worst part is that he didn’t know how to solve it, get things back to the way they were. A few weeks back, they were having fun on Halloween, baking cookies and having tea; today, they were all split — sorta — and he was getting too focused on anything that wasn’t one of his problems. This was not healthy.

As he walked along the beach, the sound of the water was somewhat soothing to his turbulent thoughts. He absentmindedly kicked at a pebble as he walked — soon recalling he had a pebble collection at home. It wasn’t very special or anything, but he collected them with a friend over the years. He wondered where he stored it. Lost in thought, Pete didn’t hear the approaching footsteps; when he realized this, a hand clamped over his eyes, plunging him into darkness.

“Guess who?”

“Huh?” Pete yelped, startled. “Who is it? A hobo?”

That was a childish guess, Pete knew that. It came out a bit too strained, too — his attempts at humor would never land him a spot on Comedy Central. Although the hand remained firmly in place, a few moments later it was slowly removed. He turned, his breath catching in his throat.

Standing before him, a rather unremarkable smile playing on his lips, was Gary.

“It’s me, the hobo!” he waved, mimicking jazz hands. “Got five bucks to spare, Petey?”

Pete’s laughter died in his throat, as did the rest of his willingness to live. This was not something he had on his bingo card for this year. But any reaction coming from him also died as he saw a serious look on Gary’s face.

“Seriously. We need to talk.”
