Chapter Summary

Gary encounters Pete. Pete also has an encounter with a game he really likes.

Well, that was a weird conversation.

Their talk was fairly brief. Gary, who was completely serious after his stunt just moments earlier, asked him about Jimmy, his whereabouts, and what he was up to. Pete innocently filled him in.

He almost couldn’t even mutter anything back — he didn’t know what he’d ask first. Where were you? What are you even trying to do? Why did you betray us? Have you been showering lately? Many questions came into his mind at the moment — but he chose to pick the first one. It was the simplest, but also the least likely to get him riled up.

What he got was a simple “no comment”, and this also meant the other three questions would likely remain unanswered. Two of them, to be fair — his hand smelled nice, surprisingly. Maybe even one, because it was kinda obvious that something had gotten into his head, forcing him to betray Jimmy and maybe half a dozen people while at it.

But what was Gary trying to do? “Revenge, maybe,” Pete thought. The problem is that, whatever he was meaning or was trying to do, it was developing in a really weird way, almost like it wasn’t a Gary plan after all. Jimmy wasn’t the only “new kid” they saw in Bullworth; most who didn’t enter a clique, however, were either beaten into submission or worse. He could’ve killed Jimmy in nine different ways if he wanted to — why was it taking him so long to do it?

Unless, perchance, that wasn’t his plan. But when this popped up into Pete’s mind, it was too late. Gary had vanished as quickly as he appeared. He stood alone on the beach, the early sun casting a shadow on him that mirrored the growing concern in his mind.

But he didn’t even have time to think about it then — he was worried about getting late to class. And when he remembered this event later that day, he realized he didn’t tell Gary that Jimmy was, well, right behind them. He’d probably disturbed Jimmy enough that morning, too; if Gary came inside the beachhouse and noticed Trent just chilling there, he would die of laughter.

Speaking of that beachhouse… hmm… Future Street Racer 3D. Pete was gonna get his hands on that machine.

To his relief, Jimmy was not opposed to giving him access to the place — mi casa, su casa — and even gave him a spare set of keys, too. He couldn’t deny it: this really looked like a nice base, even if he excluded the game machine, weird furniture and whatever else was in here. The groups at Bullworth had their own bases, naturally, although his friend group never did. The bullies had The Hole for themselves until it was later repurposed as a fighting club of sorts. The nerds had the Observatory, but given its location — right next to the football field — it was to be expected for them to use the Library instead.

Speaking of the field, the jocks had their clubhouse next to the field. On the other hand, they never really used it, and he never got inside it; he knew of someone in the dorm who had a spare key, but couldn’t remember who. The greasers had the Autoshop, though he genuinely had no idea what went there. And of course, the rich preppies needed a walled community of their own, aka Harrington House. God, that place was beautiful — and yet filled with some of the worst people he’d ever met.

If only he had more people to hang out with nowadays, though…

When he got there later that afternoon, the first thing he did was turn on the huge machine. Hopefully the preps wouldn’t mind their slightly higher electricity bill later this month — but since their fathers would be paying for it anyway, who cares? Pete wasn’t even sure which of them previously owned this place.

Oh boy. It really was in 3D. It took some time for his eyes to get adjusted to it; when was the last time he even saw a machine with such powerful graphics? The answer was earlier this year at home, playing the hit PS2 game The Urbz: Sims in the City. The soundtrack was so good that Gary even added his favorite songs to his old iPod (since when he had one?) and let Pete borrow it — for about 4 hours. Sure, the quality was insufferable, but it was the closest thing to a birthday present he got from him.

Future Street Racer happened to be one of his favorite games in the dorm, mainly because it happened to be the only one. In this new version, Pete expected a lot of new features and cool stuff to appear, but to his surprise, none of that was there. It was the same game, except in 3D. The sound quality sounded worse too, which was a bummer. He was no audiophile, but he was sure a former roommate of his would be bashing his head on the floor right now if he heard anything close to a compression artifact. But not an issue, hopefully.

After many, many, many playthroughs, he decided to take a break. He looked at a clock in the wall next to the unused bar; it was 7 p.m. Whoa. How did time go by so fast- he felt like he had barely arrived in there. And there was no way he’d be leaving for now, right? That game must’ve had something hidden in it! Hopefully something new, whether an easter egg he was unaware of or whatever. There was no way they just slapped the 3D on top of the thing. Okay, maybe they did — but couldn’t they have done something else, too?

What a bummer.

With his stomach growling and his thumbs aching from repeatedly mashing the goddamned thing, Pete thought of extending his break by a few more minutes. He stretched, popping his back with a satisfying crack, and shuffled towards a nearby cooler. The place had no fridge, curiously, and the closest thing to one was a plastic monstrosity overflowing with soda cans (of which some were empty!) and questionable-looking hot dogs. Maybe this was the place Gary was grabbing food from…

Interestingly, it was getting harder and harder for him to concentrate on what was happening. He felt like his brain cells were slowly melting away — side effects of watching cars speed by him frame-by-frame, he guessed. Or maybe he was just tired. Good news is that he found something to eat: a lone lukewarm soda and a rather sad-looking sandwich wrapped in greasy paper. Maybe things weren’t looking up to him after all, but it’s not like he was used to fancy gourmet feasts.

He unwrapped the sandwich with a sigh, and took a hesitant bite; it was salty and kinda sour, so he was starting to question if this was even edible or who made it. (Hopefully Jimmy — who knows what else the other preps were doing here.) His stomach lurched, but the thought of facing the game again on an empty stomach was worse; he choked the rest down, washing it down with the soda. He must’ve lost 10 years of his life just by doing this.

Perhaps it was time to just call it a night. Pete had some fun, but this wasn’t as relaxing as he thought it would be. Stepping away from the cooler, he went to a mattress in the corner — the closest thing to a bed in the room, to be fair — and laid down. It wasn’t comfortable, but hey, at least it wouldn’t turn into a The Princess and the Pea situation. Or so he hoped. Maybe it would be alright to take a brief nap.

And so he did.

When he woke up hours later, now sightly less disoriented, he slowly began to realize that he’d spent almost the entire day over there. Great. And to add insult to injury, there was a rather unexpected weight on the mattress next to him. A voice muffled by the sheets soon broke that silence.

“Oh, hey Petey. Shouldn’t you be… er… asleep?”

Right. This was Jimmy’s place. Not exactly the most relaxing nap spot. His hair had grown a bit, no longer the same-old buzzcut he had been used to for the past few months, but it was funny seeing it this close. It looked nice.

“W-well,” Pete stammered. “S-sorry. How long was I out for?”

“Uh… exactly 2 hours and 13 seconds.”

Pete shot Jimmy a look. “You were counting?”

“…nah.” he chuckled. “You can stay if you like, though.”

“Thanks. So… how did the egging go?”

“Got jumped,” Jimmy mumbled, pulling the sheet tighter around him. “Set up by that little weasel, Gary. One of the preps almost clocked me good.”

Ohhhhh.

Pete’s eyes widened. “Oh, that’s… bad.”

“I mean, how would I know Tad had webbed toes?” A flicker of shame crossed Jimmy’s face. “Still, I didn’t expect him to show up. How did he find out?”

Well, this was something. Pete rapidly recalled his conversation with Gary earlier that day — unintentionally, maybe he helped set him up. Uh-oh. Jimmy probably wouldn’t hold it against him anyway, to be fair. But the fact that Gary had struck tonight, and that he’d targeted Jimmy at all — that was news.

He didn’t think it’d happen today — or that he would strike in the first place. He had been a pawn and hadn’t realized it. “I… I don’t know. Maybe Gary overheard something?”

“Maybe,” Jimmy sighed. “Look, let’s figure it out together tomorrow. Sound good?”

“Yeah,” Pete agreed. “Sounds good.”

A moment later, he spoke again, his voice a bit more hesitant. “Oh, you think you could grab a spare blanket from under the mattress?”

Pete reached down automatically, but froze for a moment. “Why? Isn’t this one big enough?”

Jimmy peeked out from under the covers, then glanced back at the mattress, and finally met Pete’s gaze with a look that spoke volumes.

“You don’t wanna know,” he chuckled. “I don’t usually sleep clothed, you see.”

“Oh.”

Realizing that Gary was a bigger threat than he’d imagined, Jimmy was now determined to take him down as fast as possible. The complicating factor was that Gary, well, was somewhere unknown and, unlike little Petey, he was unwilling to hunt him down. At least not yet.

The good news is that he now had targets of his own: the waspy preps who had turned against him. Oh well — hopefully they wouldn’t mind some of the chaos. It was fairly easy to get them to hate him, though. Beating the preps in their own race tournaments was good, but not good enough. Getting revenge on one of their most influential members, Tad, by egging his house? Much better. (In retrospect, he should’ve bought more eggs. That would’ve made him crack up, no puns intended.)

As a bonus, he even managed to have a date of sorts with Pinky, who just so happened to be Derby’s cousin. (He decided to not comment on their supposed relationship.) Although Jimmy was certainly exploring all sorts of options at this point, he was not very interested in her. But as she seemed like a valuable asset in his campaign, why complain? And so he didn’t.

Yet none of that worked; sure, they now hated him, but they didn’t feel pressured or intimidated by him. As awesome as it was, he was not so willing to go with the tried-and-tested strategy of kicking everyone’s ass anymore. He needed to squeeze his brain and think of something else. If he was supposed to target someone, who, exactly, would it be?

Luckily, Pete had some advice.

[incoherent mumbling]

“…you gotta beat one of them publicly. You gotta prove to them that you’re better than their best man. Why don’t you box that dumb jerk Bif?”

They were at the pier, staring at the sea. It was getting dark, and the weather was getting pretty bad. It looked like it would rain soon. Worse, it looked like it would snow soon. Not sure what else he was expecting; winter would (officially) start in just a few weeks, but it was already here. He was wearing the most formal outfit he’d worn in weeks — a blue Aquaberry shirt, cream slacks, brown loafers — all part of his initial attempt to make the preps befriend him. In an alternate timeline, one could call him a promising white-collar worker. Still, it was a mediocre outfit for fall, and a terrible outfit for winter.

“Not bad, Pete. Not bad at all. Alright… see ya around.”

He didn’t even listen to what Pete said back. Probably something like “Good luck Jim, you’re the best!”. Good ol’ Pete was always someone he could trust. He’d never hurt a fly! Talk about a faithful companion. The starry-eyed boy grabbed his skateboard and skedaddled into the streets of Bullworth.

Still on the pier, a rather distraught Pete looked at the sky. He should’ve grabbed his coat from the dorm before venturing out here; it was nearing 8 p.m., and there was no reason for him to be outside right now. Well, there was — but not anymore. Sometimes he wanted to lay down and close his eyes forever. He wouldn’t do that on the bench, for sure.

But there was no adventure he’d go on today. Sure, he could just follow Jimmy — but to what avail? Stuff happened and he was always the last one to find out. And yet he was so used to seeing things this way, living this way. He got used to this, maybe, to spare his life from the brunt of pain. He got so used to this that his very own willingness to live wore out — and the only thing keeping him alive were his friends.

But it seems like no one was reaching out for him this time.
