Chapter Summary

Pete gets a visit.

Not much had changed since that day at the pier.

Well, not for Jimmy — against all odds (well, most people’s odds), he managed to defeat Bif and become the boxing champion! Not only that, but he also got a key chance at defeating Derby and whoever was pretending to be the co-leader that day; they were his now, and that’s what mattered.

For starters, this now meant he had control — or at least a strong influence — on three cliques: the bullies, who were safely under his control; the preps, although only time would tell how long someone like Gord would try to screw up things; and the nerds, who were so scared by the other cliques that they found Jimmy as the savior they all wanted. He may not have controlled the entire school — but he was gonna be their king.

Jimmy was well aware that one clique in particular — the jocks — knew of this and had their own hostility against him. It wasn’t as bad as everything else he had seen until then, but he knew he’d have to take care of them too.

That’s where the greasers stepped in.

They had been fairly neutral despite everything going on, maybe intentionally, maybe because they didn’t care. (Probably both.) It was a surprise when Johnny, the supposed big bad leader of the brunch, wanted his help with something. To him, it sounded dumb — but perhaps it would be worth a try. Right now though, he was busy helping some hobo guy who claimed to be Santa. Maybe it was really him. In any case, why not show himself as a good boy?

In the meantime, this trusted friend Pete was rotting in the dorm.

God hates a coward, his dad would always tell him. Little Pete would always sound like a broken clock, complaining about the same things over and over and not doing anything to fix them. And when he tried to fix them, he just made it worse. That’s what he remembered, at least — and it was one of the things that came along with him in life.

Why couldn’t he just go out and make new friends? It wasn’t stupid. But it was hard — he tried many times and usually failed, miserably. The fact that the only lasting friends he had during his stay at Bullworth were Gary and Jimmy was troubling enough. Sure, he liked Jimmy. He thought he liked Gary, although he didn’t know if this was reciprocal. But their newfound rivalry killed off all chances of them having fun for the rest of the year — and it was unlikely things would change next year.

The 2D version of Future Street Racer had the Pete Stamp of Approval, unlike many students at Bullworth.

Feeling apathetic, Pete took his time of the day to do some leftover English homework; he knew Galloway wouldn’t mind, but he really didn’t want to not do it. He could’ve sworn he had submitted it already, but so much had happened lately that he had lost track of things. It’s not like he had much to do at this point — besides everything else he had been doing since. It was the only thing he could do to entertain himself at this point.

Just as he was about to finish, he heard a knock on the door. Probably Jimmy or one of the marauders in the dorm; he didn’t say a thing. With a sigh, he put down his pen and trudged over to the door. He peered through the peephole, and standing on the other side was Gary. Huh.

He creaked open the dorm, revealing Gary himself. He was carrying a brown shoulder bag, for whatever reason, though he didn’t say a thing about it. He just saw it was really him. Gary stood there, his hands stuffed in his pockets, grinning at him.

“Femme-boy,” he greeted, his voice low. “It’s been a while. Missed me? What are you doing?”

Pete gestured vaguely at the open book on his desk. “Homework. English,” he mumbled, his mind racing. What did he want this time? If he wanted to hear something about Jimmy, he wouldn’t get it this time, at least not from him.

Gary stepped into the room, his gaze scanning the cluttered space. To his surprise and pleasure, Pete had kept the dorm fairly clean — although he was not sure if he was just cleaning less now that he was gone, or if he just wasn’t staying there as much anymore. A strong possibility, given he had gone around kissing Jimmy’s ass for more than he needed to. He paused, his eyes landing on the unfinished assignment on the desk. “Looks like you’re working hard,” he commented.

Pete shrugged, feeling a wave of self-pity wash over him. “Gotta keep up appearances, you know,” he muttered back. This was getting uncomfortable, to say the least. They hadn’t been in this space — together, at least — in months. Things rapidly devolved into a dog-eat-dog world, and unfortunately for him, he was one of the main targets.

“So, uh,” Gary attempted to break the silence between them, “Why are you inside? Don’t you have parents to spend Christmas with?”

It was kinda painful for him to admit that it had been so long since he’d thought about his family, or the concept of Christmas, for that matter. “Uh, y-yeah, I will,” he stammered, trying to sound less awkward than he felt. “At some point.”

Gary was not convinced of this answer; he raised an eyebrow and tried to hide a grin. “You know, you could try being a little more festive, Petey. Get into the spirit of the season or some crap.”

“Well… it’s not like I’m gonna dress up as Buddy the Elf or something,” he retorted. “I’m just not really feeling the Christmas cheer right now.”

But Gary just shrugged back. He sat at the end of his neatly made bed, which had been that way for at least two months already, it seems. Well, at least he had kept it nice and clean. Would’ve been bad if he just showed up in here and found someone else à la Hopkins smearing their shoes in it.

“Oh, I know the feeling,” he admitted. “You know, my parents are too busy with their own little world to even notice I exist. And from what I hear… your ‘friends’ like James are going making snow angels in dog pee-covered snow.”

“What?”

“Yep.”

Though he hated thinking about it, even Gary had to admit that he kinda missed hanging out with Pete. Sure, when they first met, most of his commentary used to be irrelevant to whatever they were discussing, but at least the guy was a good listener. It was different from world-famous dickologist Hopkins, who had smart ideas but executed them terribly — leading to him paying the price or taking action before it reached that point. It’s like he had a reverse Midas touch that, unfortunately for him, made his plan of taking over the school much harder.

Plus he had a good music taste. Maybe slightly too kooky for him, though; no human being his age would voluntarily listen to Hüsker Dü. (Not that he could complain much, since he knew he would be the victim of extreme humiliation if everyone found out he liked the AMVcore-like music that just so happened to be the favorite genre of Pee Stain & Friends.)

“I’m sorry, Gary,” Pete said softly, “U-uh, well, I guess we can both be overlooked sometimes. It’s tougher than it sounds. But uh…” Attempting to break the tension between them was hard, but they’d known each other for a little over a decade. It shouldn’t have been this hard, even if the conversation looked like it was moving along.

“U-um, so, where have you been hiding out all this time?”

Gary hesitated at first. Yeah, there was no way he’d just be telling to Pete where he was all this time; if he did, there would be a non-zero chance he’d end up smacked by Russell or second-amendmented by Jimmy himself. His eyes were already darting around the room as if searching for an escape. But since he wasn’t there anymore, maybe there was not much of a point in hiding it. At least not from him.

“Harrington House,” he whispered.

Pete’s eyebrows shot up in surprise. What? How did he even get there? He never had the key to it a few months back, and he didn’t have super close connections with the preps either. Worse, how was he so close to him, and he never found out? Before deciding that stalking him was a good idea, it almost looked like he was dead and vanished.

But he rapidly realized there was an explanation for it: if the preps betrayed Jimmy, and Gary was there during their argument, then logically, he must’ve caused it to happen. Given how he wasn’t seen in the basement after that day, he could as well assume he moved there that same day. And now that the preps were friends with Jim — even if they were forced to — there was no place for him there.

“You were staying there?” Pete asked. He was mesmerized, to put it simply.

A rather sheepish grin creeped onto Gary’s face. “They had a spare room upstairs. Why not? It was convenient,” he explained. “I got what I wanted, they got what they wanted… I don’t usually betray my allies, see.”

I don’t know about that.

Although Pete was vaguely aware of Gary’s “take over the school” idea, curiously, he was not aware of the specifics. Taking the school was no easy feat, since you needed at least strong support from all cliques — or let them fight each other until death and take over the ruins. Looks like he was doing both, but this was his very own hidden agenda, one that only he could be aware of.

Before Pete could reply, he heard a rather loud rumble. Apparently, that came from Gary’s stomach — that was funny. Hopefully, he thought, he wasn’t eating canned food or whatever he was eating until then.

“Uh… you didn’t hear that.” he muttered, his cheeks flushing slightly. That was… slightly embarrassing for him.

Pete couldn’t help but laugh. “Don’t worry about it,” he said, “I’m starving too.”

The cafeteria was still open at this time of day, and he heard from Jimmy that Edna’s cooking skills had greatly improved recently; on a scale of 0 to 10, it increased half a point to 1. Still, he wasn’t willing to go there and try it out. He might’ve had something else to eat in his room, maybe.

It was then that, to his surprise, Gary picked up a wrapped package from the handbag he was holding. “I took this before I left,” he explained, holding up a piece of cake. “Some rich kid’s birthday went horribly wrong, but hey, I got cake.”

Huh. It was definitely cake — vanilla with a layer of chocolate frosting and sprinkles on top. Pete took the cake, eyeing it suspiciously before taking a small bite. It’s surprisingly good. The vanilla was rich and buttery, while the chocolate frosting added a rather sweet contrast. No wonder why the preps were easily the most well-nourished compared to the rest of the students. It was like having cake and eating it too — no pun intended.

“Wow. It’s good,” Pete said, savoring the taste. Chances are it was someone else who made the cake, though he’d be pleasantly surprised if it was one of the preps.

Gary nodded, taking a bite of his own slice. “Could’ve been worse.” he mumbled. “Happy Christmas, Pete.”

His voice was filled with a rather fuzzy warmth that surprised even him. But the sentiment was appreciated either way.

“Happy Christmas, Gary.”

It was like the weight of the world seemed a little lighter at that moment. By the time the last crumbs of the cake had vanished, his stomach was begging for him to do the very wise and reasonable action of laying down and doing nothing — which he did.

That didn’t stop him from glancing at Gary, who also happened to be lying down in his bed. He must’ve missed it after being away for so long — when was the last time he got a good night’s sleep? He seemed to be lost in thought, his eyes fixed on the ceiling.

“Well,” Pete began, breaking the silence. “Um, are you going back to the dorm now? Or…?”

There was no reply from him. Maybe he shouldn’t have asked — at least not yet — maybe he was being too demanding. Maybe too insistent. Or maybe he was just being too hard on himself. He spent so much time pondering this that he didn’t even realize Gary was mumbling about other things — some stuff about “Jimmy and ice” he couldn’t properly react to. Then came something something “barnyard animals”. He drifted off to sleep just a few minutes later.

When Pete took a shower later that night, the hot water was a refreshing slap to his senses. As he stepped out, wrapped in a worn bathrobe, he soon glanced at his reflection in the bathroom mirror. His hair was a mess, his eyes vaguely bloodshot from lack of sleep. He could’ve sworn he was sleeping well! Sure, he kept track of many more things than he actually needed to, but he was fine.

Going downstairs and back to his dorm, he entered it to the sound of silence. Understandably, Gary wasn’t here. He shouldn’t have expected something different, to be fair. But something was poking at the back of his head. There had to be something he was planning next — and he’d be screwed if he discovered he was part of it.
