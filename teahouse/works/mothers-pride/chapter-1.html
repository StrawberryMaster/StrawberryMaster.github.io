As the rain came down in sheets against the treehouse, its familiar noise tugged at Finn, filling him with an ache in his chest he couldn’t quite name.

He was sitting cross-legged on the wooden floor with his back against the couch, and there was a mess of cards spread between him and Jake. It was Card Wars! Or well, uh, whatever this was; at some point, neither of them was really keeping score and Jake kept making up rules that didn’t exist. It was fun, though. “Flooping the pig cancels out the hustle,“ Jake had said about ten minutes ago, placing a card face-down with his usual seriousness. Of course, he could floop the pig, but he would flip when anyone else did it to him. And Finn had just said, “Sure, man.”

That was the last thing either of them had said.

Jake was curled up now on the other end of the couch, his body stretched and pooled into a shape that was honestly more blanket than dog. (How did that work?) One of his ears twitched every now and then when the thunder cracked. His eyes, as expected, were closed. His breathing was slow; generally, Jake did not struggle to fall asleep. Finn envied that; he’d always envied that.

BMO was on the windowsill, face-down, their little screen dark except for a faint green pulse that blinked every couple of seconds. He wasn’t sure if that was normal. Neptr… uh, Neptr wasn’t there. Maybe he was in the kitchen or something…? Either way, this meant it was just him, Finn. Him and the rain and the cards.

Hmm. He picked one up. Cerebral Bloodstorm. This one was a real card, he thought. He turned it over, seeing the back was blue with gold trim. Huh. He didn’t remember the backs being gold. Finn set it down, and picked up another. Cool Dog. That one had a picture of a dog in sunglasses. Okay, that was a cool dog, for sure. But that wasn’t a real Card Wars card. He’d played this game a thousand times, and he was pretty sure this wasn’t real!

He looked at the spread of cards fanned out on the floor, and something cold briefly moved through him; not necessarily in his body, but behind his eyes, in that one place where thoughts started before they became thoughts. Weird, he thought. Thinking of it, some of the cards had pictures he recognized. Some of them had pictures he was sure he’d never seen, too! And one of them was blank, just pure white. When he tilted it, it stayed white.

Oh?

Oh.

He looked at Jake. Jake’s chest rose and fell. Nothing particularly unusual here.

“Jake,” he said quietly. His voice came out strange; it was a bit thin and echoey, as if he were speaking into a jar. “Hey, Jake. You asleep?”

Jake’s ear twitched and he let out a long breath through his nose. Nothing.

“Yeah,” Finn said. “Okay.”

He set the card down carefully, face-up, aligned with the others. Finn sat there for a while, just looking at the room. It was a nice room, especially for the treehouse. Maybe only a tree could make a house truly alive… there was a smell to it, a deep green smell, sort of like sap and old rain, maybe something underneath those things, that just made it home. And a lot of other things, too. The floorboards had grooves where they’d dragged furniture, there was a stain near the ladder where Jake had dropped an entire pot of spaghetti three… or four… or five years ago, or however long ago it had been. Finn couldn’t remember exactly. He remembered the spaghetti, though! Oh boy. He remembered laughing so hard his stomach hurt.

The window rattled. Lightning flashed, and for a second the whole room went blue-white, and Finn could see every detail: the scratches on the floor, every knot in the wood, and all the dust motes frozen in the air like tiny planets. That was funny. Then it was gone, and the room was warm and dim again, lit only by the candles on the table.

God, he should go to bed. He really, really should just go to bed.

But he stood up instead and walked to the bathroom.

Their bathroom was small. It had always been small, which was hardly surprising. The mirror above the sink was cracked in one corner; he honestly didn’t remember how that had happened, but the crack had been there for as long as he could remember. Tired, Finn leaned on the sink with both hands and looked at himself.

Seventeen.

He was seventeen years old and he looked… he didn’t know what he looked like. Okay, sure, he looked like, uh, well, him, but still. His blond hair was longer than he used to wear it, falling past his jaw now, out of the hat. In fact, he wasn’t sure where the hat was. Upstairs, maybe, but these days, he never wore it anymore, even in dreams. His face was thinner than it used to be. Less of a kid, naturally. There was also a light mustache coming in above his lip; barely there, though, as it was more fuzz than hair, and a few pale wisps on his chin that couldn’t decide if they wanted to be a beard or not. He ran his thumb across them without thinking, the way he’d started doing lately, just feeling them there, and seeing this as a quiet little proof that his body was doing things without asking him first.

His eyes were the same, though. Blue… big… maybe not so big, actually. Not quite small either, but definitely bigger. Thinking of it, maybe that was the worst part; his eyes still looked like a kid’s eyes, set in a face that was trying hard not to be a kid’s face anymore, and the whole thing didn’t quite fit together yet. It was strange, really. Finn felt, at times, as if he were like a drawing somebody had erased halfway through and started over on without using a fresh page. That explained a lot of things.

After a moment, he looked at his right arm. Oh, yeah, right, his arm, the one that had grown back. He flexed his fingers. Hmm, pale skin, knuckles, fingernails. Those were normal fingers. Luckily, they worked, but seeing them sent a sudden, cold spike through his chest. No, wait. He shouldn’t have this arm. In the real world, his arm was pretty much cold metal and wires, all courtesy of the doctor, Bubblegum. But here, it seeems that forwhatever reason, his brain had given him the old one back. The, uh, the gift, or the replacement, or the, um… prosthetic made of flesh and grass magic.

Finn stared at his hand. He felt a terrible distance from it, a creeping realization that it belonged to someone else and he was just borrowing it. Which, to an extent, was entirely true, he couldn’t really deny that. It was just weird to think that he’d lost an arm, of all things, had it replaced by magic, and then watched that very same arm tear itself away from his body to become somebody else. And people had carried on fairly normally.

But he didn’t want to think about it. So he just unzipped and took a piss. The sound of it was loud in the small room, and he thought: If this is a dream, did I just piss myself? Which was— okay, that was a real concern, actually. How awkward it would be! He was too old to piss himself in his sleep. Jake would never let him live it down, and if he found out, which he always did, he’d tell BMO. And BMO, well, would probably make a little file about it and store it somewhere in their data brain. In other words, it would be thing, a known thing, a real-real, not-for-play-play thing that everyone would know. Hopefully this wasn’t that… hopefully he was just dreaming and was fine. He finished, flushed, and washed his hands. The water was warm. In his dreams, water was always warm or always cold, never in between.

He sat down on the closed toilet lid.

The rain was louder in here. The bathroom had a small window, high up, and the rain hit it at an angle that made it sound like fingers drumming. He listened to it. He put his hands on his knees.

Did he even deserve to be alive?

Finn blinked. God, where had that thought come from…? He turned it over, almost annoyed at it— like, okay, wow, thanks brain, great timing. Fantastic timing, really appreciate that one… maybe it’d want some fries with that. Alright, he had saved people, kingdoms, all that crap, and had fought things that didn’t really have names and he’d won, mostly, or at least survived, which was a kind of winning. He was good. He was a good person. He, uh, tried to be a good person. Well, he’d been told he was a good person by enough people… so, statistically at least some of them had to be right.

But Finn had also hurt people. Over the years, he’d been selfish, or been cruel in small ways that he was only now old enough to recognize as cruelty. At best, he’d made mistakes that couldn’t be undone.

So, um, yeah. He didn’t know.

He thought about his mom. Oh, Margaret… there’d been this one night, many many nights ago — he must have been pretty small, of course —, when a storm had come in bad and fast, and he’d been pretty scared. Mom sat with him on the floor of the bathroom— this same bathroom, actually. No no no wait, no it couldn’t have been this one. It had no windows. What was he even—

And then she’d sat with him, sang something low and tuneless until the storm passed and he’d fallen asleep against her side. He hadn’t thought about that in a long time. She was gone. Joshua, dad, was gone. He was here, in a dream, sitting on a toilet. And the more Finn thought about this, the sadder he’d felt.

He exhaled slowly through his nose.

What was the question he was thinking of again…? Hmm, wait. No. He’d think about it some other time. Actually, he’d think about it never. He stood up, washed his hands again, even though he’d already washed them, and opened the bathroom door.

The living room was exactly as he’d left it. It seemed no one had even moved an inch, and so Finn walked to the window and looked out.

Hmm. The land of Ooo stretched out below the treehouse in the dark and the rain, and he couldn’t see much of it… just the shapes of a few hills, the faint suggestion of the Candy Kingdom’s towers in the distance, what looked like the black ribbon of a river? That was it. Lightning flashed again and he saw the grass, bright green for an instant. It was impossibly green, the kind of green that probably hadn’t existed until now, and then it was dark again.

How much had changed…

Finn pressed his forehead against the glass. It was cool. The rain ran down the other side in long, wandering lines, and he watched one droplet race another to the bottom of the pane. Oh hey, the left one won. He didn’t know why he’d picked the right one to root for. He always picked the wrong one. Bad luck.

The treehouse was gone in real life. It was sad, and he knew that. The treehouse had been destroyed, and he’d grieved it, and he’d moved on, and yet strangely, he was standing inside it again. By all accounts, it seemed his brain had built it back for him while he slept. Just about every plank, every nail, every stain and scratch and dent it could remember. It didn’t have a complete blueprint, but it was mind-boggling.

But it was wrong in small ways, for that same reason. The crack in the bathroom mirror was on the wrong side… or maybe it wasn’t; maybe he’d just remembered it wrong for so long that the dream had adopted his error, in some way. The kitchen table had five chairs, but he was pretty sure they’d only ever had four. The ladder to the upper levels had a rung missing that he didn’t think had ever been missing. Well, these were all small things. They certainly didn’t matter, at least until they were all you could think about.

Either way, that was the thing about memories. They didn’t stay, but they gained shape and lost details. Especially details that hadn’t been there in the first place… and eventually, if enough time passed, you couldn’t tell the difference between what you remembered and what you’d invented, and the memory became something new… not, say, a record of the past, but a story you told yourself about the past, and the story was always a little bit wrong.

He could feel it happening. He could feel the treehouse slipping away from him, year by year, detail by detail. The exact color of the walls in afternoon sunlight… the sound the front door made when you opened it… was it a creak or a groan? Both? Neither? Or the smell of Jake’s bacon pancakes at seven in the morning. (Which were, thankfully, still pretty good.) Or, that is, the way the whole structure of the tree would sway in a strong wind, just slightly, just enough that you could feel it in your stomach if you were lying in bed.

God, he was losing it.

Finn turned away from the window. The cards were still on the floor.

The green of the grass was still behind his eyes. Jesus, that green from the lightning flash. And it pulled something up into him.

Fern.

He really, really didn’t want to think about Fern, but thought about him anyway. As Finn sat down on the couch, the cushion sighed under his weight. He leaned forward, elbows on his knees, and stared at the floor between his feet.

Fern, who had been him. It was weird to think there once was a copy of Finn, or at least some sort of grass-y translation. Fern had wanted so badly to be the Finn that the wanting had turned into something dangerous. And Finn had—

Alright, he could dress it up. He could say it was an accident, which kind of was, or it was necessary, which probably wasn’t, or something like Fern was already gone by then or whatever. Some of those things were even true, too. But underneath the words, the justifications and the narrative he’d constructed to make it livable, there was a simple fact: Fern had been alive, or at least close enough to it, and then Fern had not been alive, and Finn had been the reason for that.

He’d never apologized, had he? Oh, he’d never said I’m sorry. He’d never looked Fern in the eye and said, ”I’m sorry I couldn’t save you.”

And now he couldn’t. Because the moment was gone, and this moment probably wouldn’t come back, not even in his dreams. Well, not really. In dreams, you got shapes that looked like people. You got a Jake-shaped dog and a BMO-shaped thingmajig, which was pretty funny, but it wasn’t really them.

Fern had been so like him, and that was the worst part. Not that Fern had been a stranger or an enemy, exactly, but that Fern had been him… had felt what he felt, had feared what he feared, and had wanted what he wanted. And Finn had failed him…

The thought sat there for a while. It didn’t leave, and Finn just sighed.

He knew he couldn’t just sit here with this. Not alone, at least. As the rain filled the silence, he realized he would need to talk to someone.

And so Finn picked up the banana phone.

It was sitting on the counter. Did they even have a banana phone? Maybe Jake did. They had their own phones and all, but Finn wasn’t really willing to look after his, or Jake’s, for that matter. Either way, he had this banana phone. It was yellow and curved, like a normal banana. How exciting…

He held it for a moment, feeling the weight of it; it was heavier than a real banana, but lighter than a real phone. After thinking for a moment, he dialed Bubblegum’s number. He knew it by heart; hell, he’d known it by heart since he was thirteen. Some things you didn’t forget, even when you forgot everything else.

It rang and rang and rang. The signal crackled and popped; it must have been the storm, probably. Or the dream he was in, or maybe both. Then there was a click, and then a pause, and finally her voice, though tinny and distant, as if she was speaking from the bottom of a well.

“Finn?”

“Oh hey, Bonnie.”

“It’s… um, what time is it?”

“I don’t know. I think it’s, uh, late. Sorry.”

A pause. He could hear something in the background on her end. It sounded like a bit of machinery, but maybe a bit of music too. Sure enough, PB was always doing something, or at least in the middle of something. He’d never once called her and had her full attention, and he’d stopped expecting it years ago.

“Is everything okay?” she asked, but the way she said it told him she expected the answer to be yes.

“Yeah. I mean… uh, yeah. I just…” He leaned against the counter. “I-I don’t know. I was just sitting here and I thought I’d call.”

“You’re bored,” she said. Not a question. Ultimately, it was true.

“I guess. Kind of.”

“Finn, it’s the middle of the night.”

At least her time estimates were probably more accurate than his, which probably was a good thing. What the hell was he doing at this time of night, anyway? If only his dreams were more coherent… though this one was, by far, the most coherent of them all.

“I know. I know, I just— like everyone’s asleep here, and the rain’s been going for hours, I think, and I couldn’t—” He stopped, and started again. “Do you ever just, like… sit somewhere you’ve sat a million times and feel like you’ve never been there before?”

Another pause. Finn heard the sound of something being set down on her end: glass on metal, or close enough to it. “Hmm. You know, that’s a very philosophical question for—wait, what did you say, the middle of the night?”

What? “Uh, I didn’t say anything about that. You said that.”

“Oh. Uh, right. Well.” He could hear a drawer opening and closing. She was in her lab, probably, given all the noise; she’d never let it disrupt her beauty sleep. “I think what you’re describing is a form of what is called dissociative spatial—actually, no, never mind. Are you sure you’re okay? You sound weird.”

“Weird how?”

“Well, you’re calling me at whatever time it is to ask me about déjà vu.”

He almost smiled. “Oh, it’s not like that, it’s more like… uh, the opposite. Like everything’s familiar, but still wrong. I’m in the treehouse but it’s not—” The signal crackled sharply, as a burst of static ate the rest of his sentence. He waited for it to pass. “—not quite right.”

“You cut out,” Bubblegum said. “What about the treehouse?”

“Nothing. Just that it’s… I don’t know how to explain it. I think this might be a—” Static again, but longer this time. It was this weird, low, warbling hum that rose and fell like breathing. When it cleared, Bubblegum was mid-sentence.

“—rometric pressure from the storm is probably affecting your signal. I can call you back on the—”

“N-no no, don’t. It’s fine, really! I just wanted to hear a voice.” He said it, and immediately wished he hadn’t. It sounded needier than he meant it, and he could feel her recalibrating on the other end.

“Okay,” she said. A bit softer now, he thought. “I’m here. What’s going on?”

And the funny thing was… he didn’t know how to answer that. It was hard for Finn to even guess what was going on in his mind right now. What he did know, though, was that he was standing in a kitchen that didn’t exist, holding a phone shaped like a fruit, talking to someone who might not be real, about feelings he couldn’t name. So he said the thing that was closest to the surface for him, the thing that had been sitting in his chest all night like a stone in a shoe.

“Do you ever think about Fern?”

There was silence. Not static silence, but actual silence. And then: “Fern?”

“Yeah.”

“I… yes. Sometimes, uh, I mean, in a scientific context, mostly. You see, the intersection of botanical and—”

“Not in a scientific context, Bonnie.”

“Oh.” He heard her sit down and exhale for a moment. “The other Fern, you mean. Well… yes. I think about Fern. What about him?”

“I killed him.”

“Finn—”

“I know, I know it’s more complicated than that. It was the Green Knight and the grass demon and all that junk, but uh— but at the end of it, at the bottom of it— he was me, and I—” He gripped the banana phone tighter. “He was me, Bonnie. He had my face and my memories and my— and I never even said sorry to him. I never just looked at him and said, ‘Hey, man. I’m sorry this happened to you. I’m sorry being you was so hard.’”

“Finn, you know that Fern’s situation was, well, it wasn’t something an apology could have fixed.”

“I know that.”

“There weren’t many nuances to it. He was an inherently unstable and, uh, composite entity. And then there was the whole thing with the grass—”

“I know that, PB.”

She stopped. He could hear her breathing, thinking, or at least choosing the right words to say this time. Nothing unusual here; Bubblegum was always choosing words, picking them up and examining them like specimens before she decided which ones to use.

“You’re right,” she said, and her voice had changed again; it was more quieter now. “You’re right that it’s… that it’s not about whether an apology would have changed the outcome. Maybe it would have, but perhaps it’s about whether you needed to say it.”

“I think so.”

“And you didn’t get to.”

“No.”

“And that’s…” She trailed off. On her end, it seemed like something beeped softly; a timer, maybe, but she ignored it. He could tell she ignored it because there was a tiny pause where her attention flickered away and then came back, and that small act of choosing him over the beep meant more to him than he could say. “…that’s a hard thing to carry around, Finn. Right?”

“Yeah.” Finn’s voice came out thick, and he swallowed. “I just keep thinking— what if he was scared? At the end, you know? What if he was scared, and he was looking at me, and all he saw was the guy who was supposed to be him, the real deal, the one who got to keep going, and I just— I was right there and I couldn’t—”

The static came back. God. It was not a crackle, but like a wall of it, dense and white, filling his ear like water. He pulled the phone away, stared at it, put it back.

“—inn? Finn, are you—”

“I’m here.”

“You cut out again.”

“I know. It’s the storm.”

“What were you saying?”

He closed his eyes. What had he been saying? The specifics were already blurring into his head, the way they always did when he tried to talk about the things that actually mattered. It was unnerving. The words were dissolving before they reached his mouth, and his feelings seemed too big for the language available; an ant in high heels could never climb a wall. “Just… do you think he forgave me?”

“I think…” Bubblegum started, and then stopped, and he could hear her wrestling with the answer. Sure enough, the scientist in her would say something such as “forgiveness is a complex neuroemotional process”, or that Fern’s cognitive architecture was fundamentally different from his and so the question itself may be malformed. But the part of her that was Bonnibel, that was his friend, that was the person who had known him since he was a dumb, brave kid and full of a light that she sometimes envied… well, that part wanted to say something true, and so it did. “I think Fern understood you better than anyone else ever could. Because he was you, and… and if you would forgive you—”

“I don’t know if I would.”

“—then maybe that’s the thing we should work on.”

Finn opened his eyes. It was still raining outside. “Yeah,” he said. “Maybe.”

And then more quiet. Not silence this time, but just quiet. The difference between nothing and the choice to not fill the nothing… he could still hear her lab in the background. It was amazing to hear the sound of a kingdom being maintained even at this hour, even in the dark, even in the rain.

“Bonnie?”

“Mm?”

“Am I… do you think I’m okay?”

“In what sense?”

He laughed, a small, airless thing. “In any sense. I don’t know. I just feel…” He ran his free hand through his hair. It was long, maybe too long. “I feel old. I know I’m young, but I feel like I’ve been alive for a hundred years, and I can’t remember most of them, and then the ones I can remember— it’s like I’m watching them through a window, a flashback or something. Like they happened to someone else and I just… saw?”

“That sounds like—”

“Don’t. No, don’t. Please. Don’t tell me what it sounds like, don’t give it a name. I don’t want it to have a name.”

She paused. “Okay.”

“I just want to know if I’m okay.”

Another pause on her end. A bit longer, actually. Then, carefully: “I think you’re a person who has been through a lot. More than most people, uh… more than most anyone, let’s be frank. And I think you’re dealing with it the way you deal with everything, which is…” she chuckled, “by sitting alone in the dark and calling people on a banana phone instead of sleeping.”

“Oh, that’s not—”

“But it’s not a criticism, Finn. You’ve never been good at sitting still. Even when you’re sitting still, you’re not sitting still. But it’s not a bad thing. I think you’re thinking, or worrying, or—” The signal wavered, her voice dipping in and out like a radio losing its station. “—and that’s what makes you you, but it’s also—” Static. “—exhausting. For you, not for—” More static. “—just for you.”

“Bonnie, you’re breaking up.”

She sighed. “I said it’s exhausting, being you. It looks exhausting, that is.”

He let out a breath. “Yeah.” He paused. “Yeah, it is.”

“Then maybe stop trying to be you for one night and just… let it be. Just let the rain be rain, and let the night be night. You don’t have to figure everything out right now.”

“What if I’m not actually—” The static swelled again, now sharp and sudden, and cutting the word clean off. He grimaced, held the phone away, brought it back. “What if this isn’t—” Static. He tried once more. “What if none of this is r—” White noise, just white noise. Jeez.

“Finn? I lost you there.”

He stopped trying. “Nothing. Never mind… I’m sorry.”

“Get some rest, okay? You sound tired.”

I am tired, he thought. I am so tired.

“Yeah,” he said. “Okay. Hey, Bonnie?”

“Mm?”

“Thanks for picking up.”

A brief pause.

“Always, Finn. You know that.”

He didn’t know that, or at least wasn’t so sure of it. But it was nice to hear.

“Night, Bonnie.”

“Goodnight.”

The line went dead. He held the banana phone against his ear for a few more seconds, listening to the static, the rain, and the nothing. Then he set it down.

She wasn’t wrong, honestly. He was bored, admittedly. But perhaps bored wasn’t the right word; it was too small, and too simple. What he felt was something larger and more shapeless, something that boredom was a part of, though not exactly the whole of. It was as if Finn felt he was in a room full of sleeping things and beings, and knowing that he was the only one awake, while also knowing that being awake was a kind of punishment… even if he didn’t know what he was being punished for. That made sense, right?

He wondered if this was a dream after all. He’d tried to say it, tried three times, and each time it seemed the static had swallowed the words like something didn’t want them said out loud. Maybe it was a dream, as twisted as it appeared to be.

Finn wondered, and the wondering was itself a kind of answer, because in real life he didn’t wonder. Well, not in the way he was doing so here. In real life, things were solid and they stayed where you put them… usually. For example, Card Wars cards didn’t come blank, bathroom mirrors cracked on the correct side (or not at all), and some kitchen tables had four chairs, not five. Either way, phone calls didn’t cut out every time you tried to say the word real. He was sure of it.

But if this was a dream, then eventually he would wake up. And waking up meant—

No. He didn’t finish the thought. Finn stood at the counter with his hands flat on the surface and he breathed (naturally; he wasn’t just going to suffocate himself), and he didn’t know if he could stand it. Hmm, waking up. How normal, one would think, but… opening his eyes to whatever room he was actually in; whatever bed, whatever ceiling, whatever version of the world existed outside this warm, wrong, beautiful treehouse that his brain had built for him out of scraps and longing…

He didn’t know if he could stand it again.

He went back to the window.

The rain was lighter now. Or maybe it wasn’t? Maybe it was the same and Finn had just gotten used to it. At that point, he should just stop asking so many questions, given how few to no answers he had. He sat on the floor beneath the window with his back against the wall and his knees drawn up to his chest, and for a moment, he thought about Margaret and Joshua again.

Oh, Joshua, who had been stern and loving… though more loving than stern, thankully. And Margaret, who used to sing when it rained. A lot, actually, even if he couldn’t remember the words anymore, but he could remember the melody, vaguely. She used to hold him in her lap and sing, and the rain would hit the windows, and he would feel so safe that safety, in some way, became the default setting of his entire worldview. If anything, it was also the thing that let him charge into danger without hesitation — after all, some part of him believed he would always be caught, and that there would always be arms to hold him.

They hadn’t had to keep him. He was just a weird human baby crying on a leaf in the woods. But they chose to, and that choice ended up being the foundation of everything Finn was: every good thing about him, every act of courage and kindness, and also his occasional stubborn, stupid heroism. Funny to think all of it traced back to a dog and his wife finding a baby in the woods and saying, ”This one’s ours, baby.”

But there was no lullaby or arms to help him now, unfortunately. Finn was seventeen, but he definitely felt a hundred. Hell, he felt like he’d lived too long and, paradoxically, not long enough. He felt like he’d done too much, and also not nearly enough. He felt like he’d been given a life that was enormous, surely: full of magic, food, adventure — okay, honestly, more food would be nice right about now —, and other impossible things, and that he’d spent it recklessly, and now, he was looking at what was left and while it wasn’t nothing, it wasn’t what he thought it would be, either.

Oh, his arm. His stupid arm. Finn held it up in the candlelight and looked at it. Every finger, every knuckle, and also the lines on his palm… what had that cost him? Not in pain, that is; pain was, usually, temporary, pain went away. But in… well, in something else, in the thing that made him Finn, if that made any sense…

Thinking of it, he was looking at the very flesh that had become Fern. Had he lost a piece of his soul along with the limb? Had the grass magic that grew it back once filled the gap with something different, pulling a whole other person out of him, just like the way his brain had filled this treehouse with slightly wrong details?

Was he still him? Or was the real him the one who had died in the grass?

Oh, this is a bad question. These are bad, bad questions.

Was he still the kid who’d gone on a billion adventures? He had no clue. Was he still that kid, or was he someone else now, that is, someone who looked like that kid and sounded like that kid but who was, underneath, as much a copy as Fern had been? He didn’t know.

He pressed his face against his knees.

Mom, he thought. The thought that came into his head was not that of Margaret, or even his birth mother, Minerva. It was just… mom. The concept, that is, or at least the general idea of what “mom” meant to Finn. To him, it was someone who would hold you and tell you it was okay, a feeling that lived in the arms, and the warmth, and also the heartbeat you could hear through their chest.

He wanted that. He wanted it so badly that the wanting was like a thickness in his throat. Finn may have been a hero, and he may have saved the world, but he just wanted his mom.

It feels so long since my body’s been warm.

Finn sat with that thought for a while; he let it be true. Because, to be fair, it was true. Even here, in this dream, surrounded by candlelight, wooden walls and people that may or may not have been real, he was cold. Not physically, naturally; the room was warm, the floor was warm, even the goddamned rain on the other side of the glass sounded warm. But inside, he felt that something that had been there for a long time, longer than he’d known, was growing slowly the way the crack in the mirror grew. Millimeter by millimeter… until one day you looked at your reflection and it was split in two.

Finn felt something on his cheek. He touched it; it was wet. He was crying. Oh no.

His tears were cold. They ran down his face in thin lines, and they, unlike the rest of the room, were cold the way rain is (usually) cold, the way glass is (also usually) cold, and the way the space between stars is (surely?) cold. But Finn let them come, he didn’t wipe them away. He sat on the floor of the treehouse and, tired, let himself cry for a moment. It was so weird. He held himself the way Margaret used to hold him, with his arms wrapped around his own knees, chin tucked, and eyes closed. He held himself and he rocked, just slightly, just barely, a motion so small that no one would have noticed even if anyone had been awake to see.

Thinking about it, he stayed like that for a long time.

Outside, the rain began to slow a bit. The thunder moved off to the east, and through the water still streaking the glass, Finn could see the faintest suggestion of light on the horizon. Maybe there would be sunrise soon, or at least the idea of it. The world was getting ready to do the thing it always did, whether he wanted it to or not.

And he would wake up soon, he knew that. The dream was already starting to thin at the edges, and it was something he hadn’t personally experienced, at least in such a way, until now. The colors were less vivid, the sounds less crisp, and he was pretty sure the walls of the treehouse were becoming somehow both more and less solid, as if it were a painting he could see the brushstrokes in.

But he didn’t want to go. As strange as it soundned, though, he also didn’t want to stay. If there was some third option between waking and sleeping, between remembering and forgetting, between being Finn and being no one at all, he’d gladly take it.

Yet there was no third option. There never was.

He opened his eyes and briefly looked at the room. Sure enough, there was Jake, BMO, all the cards on the floor, a series of other items, and the window with its thinning rain and its faint light. Finn looked at all of it, and he tried to hold it, tried to press it into his memory. The closest to this once was when he pressed a flower into a book; he knew, of course, that had he kept the flower there, the flower would dry, its colors would fade, and eventually he would open the book and find only a brown, brittle shape that might have once been a flower. The same thing could happen to all of this.

“I’m sorry, Fern,” he said to the empty room.

No one answered.

Finn closed his eyes and breathed in. The treehouse still smelled like sap and rain. What’s the word Bonnie used for the smell of rain again…? Petrichor, right, right. Ah…

He held the breath and let it go.

And somewhere, in a room he couldn’t see, in a bed he couldn’t feel, Finn opened his eyes. The treehouse was gone, and so was the rain, and so was all that warmth.

But finally, he was awake.
