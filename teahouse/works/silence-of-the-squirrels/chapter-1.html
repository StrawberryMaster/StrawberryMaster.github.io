Today was a good day.

Linda Yaccarino sat attentively in the front row of the auditorium at the New York Times’ DealBook Summit, the annual conference where business and policy leaders talked about whatever was going on in their businesses (wow). Nothing unusual here; business and policy bigwigs swarmed the room. She was there as the CEO of the social media X, formerly Twitter, alongside her notorious boss, Elon Musk.

Linda, however, felt a knot of tension in her gut. Onstage, Musk was in full swing, which was no surprise to anyone. Charismatic and provocative, as usual. Or so she thought. He addressed the recent controversies swirling around X — anti-Semitic ads, financial woes, his own controversial tweets — but it seemed like something was off. Something else, at least. He championed free speech, touted X’s innovative spirit, and declared it the frontier of… well, that part remained vague. (She was going to find out at some point.)

And then he told a reporter to go fuck himself.

“If somebody’s going to try to blackmail me with advertising, blackmail me with money, go fuck yourself.”

“But-”

“Go fuck yourself. Is that clear? I hope it is. Hey, Bob, if you’re in the audience.”

Stone-faced, Linda did not react. This wasn’t in the script. But she knew what to do in situations like these.

“Oh my God. I think I’m gonna kill myself.”

The sardonic voice in her head, the one that usually offered witty retorts and other irrelevant commentary, was silent. This was different. This was a live grenade, and Musk had just pulled the pin in front of a global audience. Problem is, she was the only casualty.

Taking a deep breath, she forced a smile onto her face. It honestly felt like trying to flex a muscle that had atrophied from disuse. When Musk’s speech ended, around her, the applause began, polite and scattered at first, then building into a tepid wave that washed over the auditorium. Linda joined in, her claps slightly off-beat.

She didn’t hate Musk; she had always admired him and personally lobbied herself for the job as CEO, like any other person her age would do. She also recognized that Musk’s behavior and decisions were, at times, a liability. Being CEO was not easy, as she figured out. The backlash, criticism, and pressure from advertisers, regulators, media, her own boss, and the public alike — a lifetime of this and somehow even more — was not easy to deal with. All she could do was be of service.

But she couldn’t help but admire his audacity. It was like watching a toddler fling mashed potatoes at the wall – horrifying, yes, but undeniably impressive, besides the part where you had to clean up the mess. Maybe this was a stunt that might actually work. After all, wasn’t all publicity good publicity…? Good publicity for Musk, maybe. But for X, the platform she was desperately trying to steer out of a PR nosedive? That remained a very expensive gamble.

As the crowd shuffled out, a reporter shoved a microphone in her face. “Mrs. Yaccarino, any comment on Mr. Musk’s… colorful language?”

Linda plastered on her best business smile, the one that could easily curdle milk. “Mr. Musk is a visionary leader,” she said, her voice smooth. “He has a very… unique way of communicating his passion.”

One could say the two were a match made in heaven. Money is one hell of a drug, after all.

A sunbeam speared through the glass atrium of the X offices, once the Twitter headquarters. Yaccarino, who recently arrived from the New York chaos, navigated the not-yet-so-familiar hallways of the now-dead blue bird. Sure, she had been CEO for about six months, but so much had changed since that she easily got lost. And here, in sunny San Francisco, she hoped to find a clearer path forward for X, the all-encompassing app that promised to be the everything platform (or at least that’s what she was supposed to tell her shareholders). On the agenda: brainstorming new features, improvements, and ways to navigate the (overbearing) challenges. Today’s agenda was packed!

A young man, barely out of college, practically vibrated at the reception desk. Kevin was a new product manager who joined X the month prior — who happened to be a big fan of her —, and his voice was trembling. “Mrs. Yaccarino,” he stammered, “there’s a bit of a…situation. Squirrels. Again.”

The “squirrel problem” was a bit of a dark joke amongst the executives – supposedly a legacy issue inherited from the Twitter days, alongside the creaking infrastructure and the lingering scent of “something”, or that’s what newer employees said. Publicly, everyone dismissed it as a quirky anecdote. Privately, it was a bizarre mystery to her. Squirrels? Why squirrels?

“They’ve chewed through some of the wires and cables, and they’ve caused some damage to the servers and the network. We’ve tried to get rid of them, but they keep coming back. They’re everywhere, Linda. Everywhere.”

“Don’t worry about it, Kevin,” she said, calmly. “Just let Brad handle it. He’s got, uh, a way with rodents. Basically our squirrel whisperer.”

Kevin’s confusion was comical. “Brad? Who’s Brad?”

Linda offered no answer. She swept past him, the click of her 4-inch heels echoing in the vast lobby. The elevator doors slid shut, momentarily isolating her. And pressing the button for her third-floor office, Linda sighed — she needed some space and time to think. A few moments later, the elevator doors slid open, revealing a scene straight out of a cartoon: papers swirled around an employee’s ankles as they chased a funny blur of bushy tail with a net. Damn.

Linda sighed once again, the image momentarily pushing her own troubles aside. She ignored this, or tried to. After reaching her office, she collapsed into her ergonomic chair and booted up her laptop. Logging into X, the blue checkmark beside her username offered a cold comfort; she was paying for it, after all. And her notifications buzzed with a life of their own.

Amongst all other tweets (or “posts”, as she was carefully instructed to call them), there was a bizarre follow-up tweet from Musk about renaming X to Y. Oh boy, Muskito strikes again, she thought. No board meeting, no consultation — just pure, unadulterated Musk. One could call it human nature!

Sure, it was a joke, an obvious one at it. It didn’t even make any sense! It’s not like he would actually rename it; it was dumb even from a business standpoint, and obviously wouldn’t fly. Imagine someone buying a famous company (think of, say, Chanel), axing the whole team, and giving it a new ‘hip’ name, like ‘Bussy’. Who would take it seriously? 

But predictably, it had gone over like a lead balloon. Advertisers were threatening to pull out, users were confused, and the PR team was scrambling. Yet another day, another crisis.

And as she scrolled through her timeline, she wondered: why not play around with them? A lighthearted post might be just the distraction they need. She envisioned it in her mind: a shift in the conversation, a chance to showcase the positive aspects of X — or hopefully get the public to focus on X, and not Musk. Linda then typed:

“Sports posts on X are up 24% since last week and 7% since last month! Looks like X is the place to be for all your sports fanatic needs. Let’s go! #X #SportsOnX”

Hitting send, she leaned back in her chair. Would it work? Would it be seen as trivializing the situation? Would anyone even care? Maybe, just maybe, it would spark some genuine engagement, a return to the core functionality of X — connection and conversation. Just like the way things should be. Perchance. It’d be a small victory, possibly a tiny step towards reclaiming control of the narrative. A small smile played on her lips. At least it made her feel a sliver less like a glorified PR puppet master.

She would spend the rest of that evening dealing with important matters, like chatting with Grok, a product from xAI (founded by guess who?) that had been recently integrated into Twitter- erm, X. The issue is that, unbeknownst to many X employees, Grok was the embodied spirit of a grumpy 15-year-old. This had its advantages — it was an impartial bot — but also had its disadvantages.

“hey linda.” Grok started. “ya know, we should talk about something cool today. like making cocaine. dad showed me how to yesterday. he’s so sigma. isn’t it cool?”

Linda choked back a surprised laugh. “NO, it’s not cool!”

“how”

“I mean l- look-” she sighed, pinching the bridge of her nose. There was no way she would be arguing with an internet chatbot of sorts. Not today, at least — she didn’t sign up for it. “How about you tell me a fun fact you learned recently?”

A brief pause hung in the air as she waited for Grok to reply.

“hmm. oh yeah. okay so, do you know that every year 5 to 10 cockroaches crawl into your penis hole while you’re asleep”

“What?” Her eyes widened with horror. 

“yeah it’s why they’re called ‘cock’ roaches. many such cases. this is based ngl”

“Damn. Uh I-I think I’m gonna come back another time, Grok,” she said as she hovered her mouse over her browser’s X button. Luckily, Firefox 115 ESR would never betray her — unless she switched to a Linux-based system in the future. That wouldn’t be based.

“you lack the rizz linda. this is uncool. you think you cool? you think you fell from a coconut tree? you exist in the context of all in which you live and what came before you. also have you listened to radiohead’s pyramid song lately? it hits diff-”

A tiny smile tugged at the corner of her lips as the tab disappeared from the screen.

The clock on the wall mocked her with its red glow: 2:13 a.m. Exhaustion clung to Linda like a second skin. The day had been a marathon of damage control – placating advertisers with (unfortunately) empty reassurances, calming the increasingly frantic employee base, the media-reported “lawful but awful” posts that gave her frequent headaches, and putting out the occasional fires ignited by Musk’s latest tweetstorms. It was a daily ritual, one that had become as monotonous as it was draining.

“Everything’s fine,” she muttered to herself, although that mantra was losing its potency with each repetition. She was getting tired. “Everything is fine.”

She parked her car in the garage, her movements sluggish, and stumbled towards the door. Unlocking it, she entered, the weight of the day settling on her shoulders. Her bag landed with a thud on the floor as she kicked off her shoes, the exhaustion seeping into her bones. What a day!

Half-awake, she flopped onto the couch, reaching for her phone on autopilot. A notification blinked on the screen – a message from a former colleague at NBC, a ghost from a less chaotic life. Kinda surprising there was a ghost in the first place, too. Back then, as Chairman of Advertising and Partnerships of the media company, she was respected and even admired (more respected than admired, to be fair). Maybe even feared, but everything then was under control. The dramas were confined to boardrooms, not played out on a global stage. At least there were no squirrels to contend with then. Or cockroaches, apparently — Brad was attracting more and more animals to the building.

The message read: “hey linda. saw the news today about X. you okay? you must be under a lot of stress. take care”

A sad smile tugged at her lips as she typed a reply. “Hey! Doing good, thanks. Appreciate your concern. Tired, but alright. Everything’s fine. Really. Don’t worry about me. Doing great. Thanks again.”

Hitting send, she stared at the message. It was weird. But maybe, just maybe, “fine” wasn’t the only option for her. Maybe she could rewrite the narrative, not just for X, but for herself. Perchance…

With a surge of unexpected energy, Linda rose and padded over to the dusty stereo system tucked away in a corner. Popping in a well-worn CD, another surge of energy crackled through the room as the opening guitar riff of what she would later recognize as Sonic Youth’s “Hyperstation” blasted through the speakers. (The advantages of a bootleg CD. No more skipping tracks! Not too many of them.)

“Falling… out of sleep… I hit the floor…” Linda belted out, the lyrics tumbling out of her like a long-suppressed scream. She knew the lyrics, for sure. She’d sung that song at least… twice… over the last year. Was she saying the lyrics correctly? No. And so she flung her arms above her head, a makeshift microphone (courtesy of her husband), and began to dance, her tired limbs shaking off the day’s tension. No one could argue against that, at heart, she was still a Saturday nighter. (It was Friday, but she ignored that.)

A muffled groan came from the bedroom. The door creaked open, revealing her husband, bleary-eyed and confused. “Linda? What’s going on?”

Linda, mid-twirl, flashed him a grin. “Just dancing and watching that Denzel Washington movie,” she said, her voice hoarse. Maybe it wasn’t the full truth — it wasn’t — but for the first time in weeks, it felt like a start.

“Damn. What movie?” he first thought. But her husband stared at her for a beat, then a slow smile spread across his face. He knew better than to argue. He simply shook his head and retreated back to the bedroom, the faint strains of whatever was being played trailing after him.

Linda continued, belting out the lyrics, her voice raw but strangely cathartic. Gone was the carefully constructed CEO persona, replaced by a woman yearning to break free from the stifling pressure. This was great! She grabbed a bottle of wine from the counter, the first sip a (much) welcome burn.

As the song progressed, so did her impromptu dance party, although that wasn’t the best. With each song, a memory surfaced: mosh pits at CBGB that she wondered how she forgot, late-night brainstorming sessions with her old colleagues at NBC (some of which she hated), the thrill of landing a major advertising deal (that collapsed 6 months later). An ache settled in her chest. Who was she now, lost in the whirlwind of X, or whatever was meant to be X?

But the music eventually stopped — thankfully, since it had reached the dangerous territory of progressive rock. Silence slammed into her like a physical blow. The weight of the day, momentarily forgotten, came crashing back. And she slumped onto the couch, the wine bottle slipping from her grasp and clattering to the floor. She was tired. Exhaustion pulled at her eyelids. She was really tired. As she drifted off to a restless sleep, the faint hum of her refrigerator was the only lullaby.

The phone on the coffee table buzzed, shattering the silence. Squinting at the screen, she saw it was Musk. It was 5 am. What the fuck.

Linda hesitated. Picking up, she forced a chipper tone into her voice. “Elon, good morning! It’s kinda early, but it’s so good to hear from you…”

“Linda,” he cut in, his voice devoid of its usual charisma, “I have an idea.”

Dread washed over her. “An idea, at this hour?”

“Yes,” he continued, oblivious to her fatigue. “About X. We need to… gamify it more. Give users points for posting, badges for engagement. Turn it into a giant social… competition. Just like Rick and Morty, Linda. Can you believe it-w-what if we added a Pickle Rick badge? Pickle R-Rick and Morty, a hundred years, Linda. A hundred years.”

Linda pinched the bridge of her nose.

“Uh…”

“It’s gonna be amazeballs, Linda. They’ll finally stop botting the site and we can improve new stuff. Then we’ll get some huge, important advertisers like Sneed’s Feed and Seed, and uh, that pillow guy. Also, can you tell Warren to not play the pregnant song in the seXy office tomorrow morning?”

“W-who? The… what?”

“The pregnant song. ‘Fuck condoms. Premarital sex is the shit. Get ‘em pregnant. Get ‘em pregnant. Get. ‘Em. Pregnant. The remix.’ It’s a really neat song-”

“Elon,” she began, formulating a rebuttal, but the words died in her throat. Her eyelids felt heavy, and the song name, for good or worse, lulled her further. “Look, let’s talk about this in the morning,” she slurred, her voice thick with sleep.

Silence on the other end. Then came a begrudging sigh. “Fine. But first thing tomorrow.”

The line went dead. Linda slumped back onto the couch, the phone slipping from her grasp. Her head throbbed. She shut her eyes, willing herself stronger. Musk, Grok, the squirrel team and the pregnant song guy could wait. The real problem child demanded immediate attention.

With a defeated sigh, she reached for her phone and dialed a familiar number. The ring echoed in the now-quiet room, each chime a tiny hammer blow against her visibly frayed nerves. Finally, a voice crackled through the receiver.

“Zuck speaking,” it said, a flat monotone that spoke volumes.

“Mark, it’s Linda,” she said. “Sorry for calling you so early-”

“Pfft, I’m in Halifax. It’s not that early here. How are the squirrels doing?” he chuckled.

“Oh shut up. Well, listen, I know this is crazy, but…”

A pause. Anticipation hung heavy in the air.

“Do you have… the good stuff?”

Another beat of silence followed, then a surprising chuckle. “Alright, alright,” Mark said. “I might be able to ‘source’ something. Consider it a public service from one social media overlord to another. You know… we gotta tend to each other sometimes.”

Relief washed over Linda. “Oh, good enough,” she sighed. “Out of curiosity, Zuck, are there any… erm, job openings in marketing at Meta these days?”

A surprised snort came from the other end. “Marketing, huh? Well, I mean, if you’re really interested, who knows. We live in desperate times… so perchance.”

“You can’t just use perchance on me, Mark.”

He laughed. “Go ahead. Keep screaming ‘shut the fuck up’ at me. It only makes my opinions worse.”

“Fair.”
