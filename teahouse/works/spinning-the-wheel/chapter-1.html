“Man, I think I’ve seen that movie enough to quote it in my sleep.”

The dim light from the flickering TV cast long shadows around the room, revealing the haphazard movie posters on the wall — low-quality prints of cult classics, and their corners were peeling slightly. A shame, given that there weren’t many in the first place, and yet they were already showing its age. Andre’s basement smelled faintly of old popcorn, remnants of a rather underwhelming movie marathon. On the screen, Outside Providence scrolled through its credits for what felt like the hundredth time.

Cal glanced over at Andre, whose eyes were barely open. The guy was fighting a losing battle with sleep, slouched quite awkwardly in the corner of the couch. To be fair, so was he — he had enough of… uh… what was the main character’s name again? Dumbo or something like that. He let out a long sigh, breaking the silence, before looking at his friend again.

“Hey, Andre,” he murmured, giving him a gentle shove. “You falling asleep on me over there?”

Andre snapped to attention, blinking as if he’d just been caught. “Huh? No, no man. I’m good. Just… resting my eyes, you know?”

“Uh-huh.” Cal raised an eyebrow, not buying it for a second. “Well, since you’re so awake, do you have anything else to watch? I’m getting a little tired of this one, believe me.”

He let out a sheepish chuckle, rubbing the back of his neck. “Uh… to be honest? I’m not even sure where half my movies are anymore. I kinda forgot where I put a bunch of them.” Andre gestured vaguely around the cluttered basement. “This place is a mess. Sorry, dude.”

Surveying the disaster, Cal could agree; it really was a mess. Andre had promised a lineup of good movies for this exact situation, but clearly, future Andre had dropped the ball. There were no new-ish movies to speak of, too — Next Friday was loaned to some cousin of his in upstate New York, He Died with a Felafel in His Hand wasn’t out for a few more months, and neither of them was desperate enough to sit through Shakespeare in Love again. At least not yet. The rest were either scratched beyond repair, lost in the chaos, or worn out from a trillion rewatches. What a bummer.

There was a rumor floating around about some rare, alternate cut of Outside Providence where Mousey — played by the ever-dapper Jonathan Brandis — and Drugs — Jon Abrahams’ character — had their roles reversed. Andre had heard about it, but naturally, he didn’t have a copy. He wasn’t even sure if it was real, half-hoping it was. It was the kind of thing you wanted to believe in, but knowing Andre’s luck, he wasn’t about to stumble across it. This didn’t stop him from buying a seventh copy of the movie and insisting this one was the real deal. (It wasn’t.)

Andre glanced over at Cal, who was rubbing his hands together like he was about to present a grand idea. “You wanna watch something else or…? I mean, I have Children of the Corn 666—”

“Actually…” Sighing dramatically, Cal leaned forward. “What if we did something else?”

Andre raised an eyebrow. “Like what?”

“I don’t know, man. Your parents aren’t home, right? We could… make something. Like, cook something.”

His friend blinked, surprised. “A recipe? Seriously? You want to cook?”

“Yeah, why not? It’s not like we’ve got anything better to do,” Cal said, a faint grin forming in his face. “Could be fun, I guess.”

Staring at the TV for a moment longer, even though the credits rolling by seemed more distant than ever, Andre thought about this. Sure, whatever attachment he had to the characters on screen was gone, especially since he had missed half of the credits already, so no point in complaining about that. His usual stoic expression cracked into a half-smile.

“Alright,” Andre said, pushing himself up from the couch. “But I’m not responsible if we burn the house down.”

Cal laughed. “Deal. So, what’s on the menu, Chef Andre?”

“Pfft.” He stretched his arms lazily as he led the way into the kitchen. “I’ve got some stuff in the kitchen. Let’s see what we can throw together.”

The kitchen was far more organized than the basement, almost like a different universe altogether. Clear countertops, neatly arranged appliances — his mom’s doing, no doubt, amen for that. Andre rummaged through a cabinet, pulling out random items until he finally emerged with a single packet of tomato soup mix. He held it up for inspection.

“Tomato soup,” declared a rather enthusiastic Andre. “One packet.”

Cal squinted at the packet, raising an eyebrow. “Uh… but that’s just one packet.”

“Indeed.”

They both stared at the soup packet for a rather uncomfortable two minutes. Cal even poked it at one point, hoping it would grow legs and arms and get up from the table, which it didn’t. After a few more moments of silence, Andre shrugged, tossing the packet onto the counter. “It’s all we’ve got. You know, I used to love this stuff when I was a kid.”

Cal picked it up, flipping it over to read the instructions. “Not exactly gourmet, but I’m game.” Chuckling, he turned the packet in his hands, studying the back. “Alright, let’s see what we need.”

As Cal gathered the sparse ingredients — a pot, some water, and a wooden spoon —, Andre’s attention drifted to something else. He spotted an old crossword puzzle lying on the kitchen counter, something his dad had started months ago but never bothered to finish. It was an old habit of his; buying the crosswords yet never filling them out. Dude barely even tried. The pages were yellowed slightly, the pen lines abruptly ending mid-clue. Hmm.

Andre grabbed the crossword and a pen, leaning back against the counter as Cal measured out the water and poured it into the pot. A faint scent of tomato soup began to fill the kitchen as the water began to warm. Absentmindedly, Andrew scanned the crossword clues, none of the clues really stumping him that much.

“‘Sinatra employer of the early 40s,’” Andre muttered to himself, pen hovering over the page. He frowned, tapping the pen against his chin. “Uhh… who the hell knows that?”

From the stove, where Cal was stirring the soup, he chuckled. “Sinatra, huh? Oh, you mean Dorsey.”

“What?” he blinked. “H-how do you know that?”

“My mom,” Cal shrugged, still stirring. “She owned an LP, I think. Don’t think I listened to it much, b-but I definitely remember reading the fine print.”

Andre stared at the crossword for a few more seconds, the letters starting to blur. Ugh. He finally dropped the pen with a sigh. “I give up. It’s all just pointless trivia.” Of course, that didn’t stop him from trying a few more clues, failing miserably at each one.

It was funny, though. Perhaps noticeably, Andre’s knowledge on filling these crosswords had waned over the years. Which was ironic — one of his jobs for the school newspaper was making the crosswords. They were pretty hard, too; most people left theirs unfilled. And no one realized they were all crosswords from the New York Times. Cal couldn’t say he was much better at it; he couldn’t know for shit what dilettantish meant, could never recall Crusoe’s supposed prototype, and had no idea “what made Dr. Dre” meant. (He later figured the answer was ‘ane’, as in Andre. Whoa…) These clues were all in the same crossword, and yet none of his guesses made it.[a]

“Hmm, ‘today’s teens’… eleven letters… uh… how about ‘destructive’… or ‘stupidshits’—”

“It’s ‘generationy,’ smartass.”

Andre snorted at his two half-decayed brain cells and eventually pushed the crossword aside. He watched as Cal finished stirring the soup, the kitchen filling with that rich, savory aroma. Sure, the entire process seemed like it took half a millennia, but God, the smell of it — it was pretty damn good.

Cal poured the soup into two small bowls and handed one to his friend. “Hey,” he said, “where are your parents, anyway? It’s kinda weird how quiet it is here.”

Andre paused, spoon halfway to his mouth. “It is?”

“Yeah, I mean— I don’t know. It feels more empty than it should be.”

It really was — for a genuine moment he expected Mel to have eaten them both. (This was a plausible outcome, and he would not discard this theory.)

Blowing on the hot soup, Andre replied, “They’re at some work conference thing. Mom’s giving a presentation or something, I dunno. They’ll be back Sunday night.”

“At least you’ve got someone to hang with until then.”

“Yeah.”

He grinned. “It’s so much more friendly with two.”

Andre blinked, almost chuckled. He could not believe that Cal actually thought he’d pull that Winnie the Pooh quote on him and get away with it. (At least he tried.)

“Did you seriously just—”

“Don’t make it weird,” Cal interrupted, still grinning. “I just like it here, that’s all. It’s quieter, you know? At my place, there’s just… always something going on. I like having a break from that.”

“…yeah. I get that.”

They ate in comfortable silence for a while. The soup was good — basic, admittedly, but warm and filling. Andre’s spoon scraped the bottom of the bowl as he finished. Cal, on the other hand, was more distracted. His eyes wandered to the window, where rain had begun to patter softly against the glass. Was kinda funny, too — they had sun for so long and then now it was pouring. Mother nature’s bipolar, I guess.

Hmm, that smell. Petrichor, it was called — one word he did memorize from the crosswords — the earthy scent that rose from the ground whenever the rain hit dry soil. Cal always thought it was just the smell of dirt, though it was more complex than that. Yet it was a smell he had always loved, something that reminded him of simpler times, when the world seemed more straightforward… less complicated. He smirked.

Andre noticed the rain too, but it pulled at a different thread of memory for him. He remembered a time, back when he was ten, when it had rained like this — no, more like poured. It had been one of those freak storms that turned the streets into rivers, the sky flashing violently with thunder and lightning. Or so he thought, at least.

He had been terrified that night. Every crash of thunder made his heart race. And then he remembered his dad watching him tremble by the window, and suddenly saying, “Come on Andre, let’s go outside.” At first, he thought he was joking — who in their right mind would go out in a storm like that? But his father wasn’t kidding. He’d pulled him into the downpour, no umbrella, no raincoat. Just the two of them standing in the driveway, obviously soaked within seconds because it was still raining. Yet all his dad did was laugh. “You feel it?” he’d said. “Sometimes you’ve got to face the storm head-on. It’s what they told me at the BND.”

They were both sick for about a week after this.

Cal sighed, still staring at the rain as if he could see more than just water falling. “It’s really coming down,” he muttered.

Andre nodded but didn’t say anything, letting the storm speak for itself. It was rain — of course it had to come down. If only rain could float, though… he had actually planned to go to the store later; there were a few things — many things, actually — they were running low on, and he didn’t want to hear his mom gripe about it when she came back from the conference. With the rain coming down hard, he decided against it. And yet, he wasn’t in the mood to deal with the wet streets or the mess of driving through the storm. At least not yet.

Shifting on the counter, Cal crossed his hands. “No point in going out in this. I think I’ll stick around for a bit… at least until the rain lets up. Soon, maybe?”

“Maybe… uh, hey, remember when you used to make those pillow forts during thunderstorms?”

Cal chuckled softly, his face warming with the memory. “I could never forget Fort Gabriel, yeah.” Every time there was a storm, he’d hide in it and hoped it would go away soon. He was a paranoid little thing.

“That was a dumb name.” Andre said, and he knew it was true. Any other kid would’ve named it after something more exciting — think of say, Fort… uh… he’d find a good alternative one day.

“I worked for it, I named it,” his friend shot back, half-laughing. “I mean, I never got struck by lightning, so that counts for something, right?”

Andre shook his head, but there was a faint smile on his face. “I guess.”

After finishing their soup and waiting for the rain to let up — which, naturally, it didn’t — Andre and Cal eventually decided to trudge upstairs to Andre’s room. The sound of rain was still steady, and both could hear the occasional rumble of distant thunder. Thinking it might turn into a full-on thunderstorm, Andre hesitated to turn on his PC. That poor thing would take forever to load up, and with the risk of the power flickering, it wasn’t worth the risk. Sure, it had always been somewhat slow, but things had gotten worse a week before, when, testing some old CDs in his drawer, he inserted Janet Jackson’s Rhythm Nation to give it a try, see if it was still working. It was; five minutes in and when the title-track came in, the thing crashed and almost burned.[c]

“Man, I was hoping to finish CyberJudas tonight,” Andre said as they walked in. He gave a slight nod toward his clunky, dust-covered PC sitting on the desk. Bobert, as he called it, desperately needed an extended vacation. “You know, it’s a miracle this thing still runs.”

“Cyber-what?” Eyebrows raised, Cal flopped onto Andre’s bed without a second thought, the mattress creaking under his weight.

“CyberJudas. It’s this, uh, political thriller game where you play as the U.S. president, and you have to manage all these crises. Nuclear threats, spies, economic collapse… y’know, real uplifting stuff.” He dropped his weight into the desk chair. “I started a playthrough a couple weeks ago, but I’ve barely made progress.”

Cal rolled onto his back, looking up at the ceiling. “Why not? Is it hard?”

“Uh. Well. No,” he shrugged. “No, no. It’s just that, uh, it takes forever to load, and half the time it crashes when I’m in the middle of something important. I love it, but it’s a nightmare.”

Funny way of saying you can’t play the game for shit, dude. This would never happen to him, even in real life — if say, an extra 537 voting ballots fell from the sky, he’d be Al Gore’s strongest soldier. And no one could say he hadn’t tried… but he couldn’t even drown his sorrows in the White House website now.

Looking again at the room, Cal noticed it was surprisingly spotless. A miracle, quite frankly, given how Andre usually let things pile up until it became a total mess. A few books were neatly stacked on a shelf — had he ever read them? —, a couple of old CDs were in a small pile next to his stereo, and aside from a few scattered clothes in the corner, the space was shockingly organized. Cal kicked off his shoes and made himself comfortable, sinking deeper into the bed.

“So, what’s our plan for the rest of the night?” Cal asked, still lounging on the bed, eyes half-closed.

It wasn’t yet night, but soon it would be. Andre glanced out his room’s window, watching the rain trickle down the glass. “I was hoping the storm would chill out, but I guess we’re stuck for a bit.” He spun around in his chair once, then stopped. “You cool staying here for a while longer?”

“Hmm. Yeah, sure, why not.”

And they did.

At some point Cal wandered over to Andre’s bookshelf. As he saw earlier, most of the books were covered in a fine layer of dust, showing how rarely they were touched. He saw one that was cleaner than the others — The Porcupine, it was called — and slid it off the shelf.

“Huh,” he muttered to himself as he flipped through the first few pages. He actually knew this book! In fact, Cal got it as a gift from Rachel out of all people — alongside five bucks which he greatly appreciated and a promotional CD of UK duo Moloko — on his 17th birthday. It had a cute dog on the cover, though. That was so cool.[b] Where was he again… oh yeah, the book.

He’d read it a while back, called it brilliant after finishing it. A political novel, sure, but it made him reflect on so much more — guilt, judgment, the fine lines people draw between power and morality and all that sort of crap. But it kinda stuck with him over time. He had no clue Andre had a copy of it; he made a mental note to re-read it again. Maybe… right now.

And so he did.

By the time he looked up from the pages, the room was nearly dark. Thankfully, no one needed to know about his night vision powers — at least not just yet.

He glanced over at Andre. In the past hour (or two. or three?) he’d been spinning in his desk chair, half-talking, half-mumbling bits of random conversation. The poor kid was having so much fun that at some point he just drifted off. His head lolled back, his mouth slightly open — for a moment Cal thought he was dead. But nah — he was probably just meditating. And he definitely wouldn’t mind seeing that Cal lent his book to himself for a while.

And yet the stillness of the room struck him then — it was almost too quiet, besides for the faint sound of rain outside, naturally. The rest of the world felt so far away, and he liked this. Cal got up from the bed and walked over to the window, sliding it open just a bit and letting the cool breeze sweep in. Ahh…

A raindrop landed softly on his cheek, sliding down his face. He almost chuckled a bit, and didn’t bother wiping it away; it was oddly comforting, like it connected him to the storm outside. Sometimes you just needed that — to withdraw from everything, to feel the world in simpler ways. That was a quote, right? From whomever the source was, he forgot. (It was Kierkegaard.) To be humble, you had to withdraw from the turmoil of the world or whatever. It made sense, actually. It was better than thinking about the aspects of life — of his life — that he never wanted to bring up, too.

Cal at some point extended his hand out of the window, letting the rain fall onto his skin. The drops were, naturally, cold, but he didn’t pull away. He just watched as the water slowly trickled down his arm, pooling in the lines of his palm before dripping off his fingertips. Funny. It was the wettest his hand had been from the standpoint of water. Rachel actually liked doing this a lot — she’d get out in the rain and let herself get soaked without a care in the world. And yet she never got sick from it, somehow. It’s almost like she was invincible — and every time he pointed this out to her, she made sure he spelled out every syllable in it. In-vin-ci-ble. Huh. How so? He had no clue, but he wished he had a bit of this invincibility himself. Oh well.

After a few minutes of quiet, he heard a soft voice behind him.

“Uh, you okay?”

Oh yeah, Andre. He was in his room, after all.

Cal didn’t turn around at first, just nodded slightly, staring at his almost drenched hand. “Yeah… probably.”

Andre moved to sit beside him, before realizing that if he tried that he’d faceplant the floor, as there were no chairs close to the window and the chair he was in was closer to the door than to the window. Good thing — he couldn’t imagine seeing that thing getting wet. He wished he named it. Giving up that idea, instead he stared out the window too. His gaze quickly fell to Cal’s hand, now resting on the windowsill, fingers unbelievably wet. What the flying fuck is he doing now.

He just clasped his own hands behind his back, leaning forward slightly.

“Uhh— y-you want something else to eat? I could scavenge the fridge or something. Maybe we could drive out and grab some snacks or… something.”

Of course, Cal just kept staring outside. There was nothing out there besides nature, rain, and human constructions he’d been long accustomed too. He shrugged. “What else could we eat?”

Scratching his head, Andre paused for a moment. “I dunno… I mean, grilled cheese, cereal, eggs maybe? It’s slim pickings, but I can throw something together.”

“You pick, man. I’ll be down in a bit.”

So there was something else to eat down there… but no harm, no foul. As Andre left, Cal stood by the window just a little longer, watching the rain blur the world outside. What an oddly soothing experience. I should do this more often, he thought. But it’d probably be better if he ate a bit — his stomach was not very receptive to water coming from acid rain, honestly. As he prepared to leave, he smirked a bit and looked around the room. It’s very much dry, thankfully. As a parting gift, he comically wiped his wet hand on Andre’s bedsheets — a little souvenir, one would say — before shutting the window and heading out.

Cal closed the door behind him with a soft click. It’s not like those sheets would be wet forever anyway. Besides the years of urine embedded within that mattress, but he’d rather not think about it. It was just like that quote from… uh… who was it again? Nixon, probably. “You miss 100% of the shots you don’t take”, he’s got to have said that once. Great quote.

He just hoped he wouldn’t miss his shots tonight.
