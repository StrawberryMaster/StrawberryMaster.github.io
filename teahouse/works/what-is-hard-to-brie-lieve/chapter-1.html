It was evening in America.

Outside, thick, slate-gray clouds sagged low over the rooftops, blotting out the last traces of daylight. Inside, Trip was sprawled across an armchair, humming faintly to some indie track trickling out of his iPod speakers — another obscure band Grace wouldn’t know, or care to know. The sound was tinny, too, barely cutting through the quiet hum of the apartment. She wasn’t paying attention to it anyway. Her gaze had drifted across the living room, settling on one of the cluttered bookcases they’d shoved against the wall years ago. In fact, her eyes were fixed on a familiar spot: that damn brass bull. It sat there, squat and smug, and at some point it became one of many wedges that were driven deeper into their marriage with every passing day.

“Trip,” she said, her voice sharp enough to slice through his humming, “when are you going to get rid of this thing?”

Trip’s head jerked up from the travel magazine he’d been flipping through, his fingers pausing mid-page. He followed her glare to the bull — it was a tacky little souvenir from his ill-fated bachelor party in Spain years back — and then flicked his eyes back to her, lifting his eyebrows in the process. “What, Grace… this?” He leaned forward slightly, resting an elbow on his knee as he gestured toward it. The bull glinted dully under the lamplight, and he had to admit its stubby horns looked more comical than majestic. Truth be told, it looked out of place. Not that Trip would ever say so, of course. “This quirky little guy?”

“Quirky?” Grace shot back, crossing her arms. “It looks like something you’d fish out of a junk drawer and mistake for a busted doorknob.” She didn't look very happy. “You know how I feel about it.”

He straightened up and tossed the magazine onto the coffee table. “I know, I know,” he said, too fast, trying to dismiss it before it grew into another argument. “I’ll take care of it right now, alright?” He didn’t move, though. He just sat there, watching her, hoping the promise would be enough.

It wasn’t.

Grace’s lips pressed into a tight line. “You know I’ve had to bring this up, what, half a dozen times already—” she started, her words gathering steam, but Trip waved a hand, cutting her off.

“Jesus, get off my back! I said I’ll do it in a minute!”

Their bickering was interrupted by a knock at the front door. Trip’s attention shifted immediately. “Uhp, he’s here!”

“What?! You told me it’d be an hour from now!” Grace muttered, the change of pace already catching her off guard. Jesus, did he forget the time?

“No, no, he’s supposed to be here now!” Trip insisted, already halfway to his feet, brushing off her confusion as he strode toward the door.

The door swung open, and there stood Ben, their oldest friend, looking rather unfazed by whatever he’d just walked into. He was dressed down in a faded purple sweatshirt — its sleeves slightly stretched from years of wear — and a pair of chinos that, honestly, had seen better days. His easy grin faltered for a split second as he took in the scene, but he recovered quick. “Hey, guys! Everything good?”

“Ben!” Grace’s face lit up. “It’s so great to see you.” She stepped forward, pulling him into a quick hug before ushering him inside.

Ben had been part of their lives for over a decade now; he practically family at this point! He’d roomed with Trip back in college, survived their late-night antics, and later played matchmaker when he introduced Trip to Grace at some dive bar junior year. The three of them had a messy, tangled history: sloppy toga parties (minus the togas — Trip still cringed at the memory), backyard barbecues in Ben’s old place in Utica, and too many nights that ended in laughter or disaster, sometimes both. Usually both.

“Come in, get comfortable,” Grace said, waving him toward the couch. “Make yourself at home.”

Their guest chuckled, letting her warmth settle over him as he stepped further into the room. “Thanks, Grace. Feels good to be here.”

Trip reappeared from the corner, clutching a framed photo he’d snagged off a side table. “Hey, Ben, check this out—us in Italy a couple weeks back.” He held it out, the glossy image catching the light: the two of them grinning in front of some sun-bleached ruin. They looked happy.

“Italy, huh? Looks amazing,” Ben said, leaning in to get a better look. “See anything worth writing home about?”

“Oh, yeah, thanks, man,” Trip replied, a faint defensive note creeping into his voice. “It was a blast. We had a great time.”

Grace gave Ben a playful eye roll and nudged his arm. “Oh, Ben, you’re always so nice. Isn’t he sweet?”

Trip cleared his throat, feeling rather overshadowed. “Anyway,” he said, gesturing toward the couch, “come sit with us.”

As Grace patted the cushion next to her, Ben eased onto the couch, glancing between them as he settled in. The air felt a little tight, though. “So,” he ventured, keeping his tone light, “how’s life been treating you two?”

Slumping back into the armchair, Trip let out a short scoff. “Life? Eh, it’s fine.” He flicked a hand dismissively, like he was brushing off dust. “We’re good. Everything’s good.”

“How’s it been since we last saw each other?” Ben prompted gently.

It had been a while. Sure, he’d bumped into Trip a few days ago outside a deli, exchanged a quick “hey,” and spotted Grace last week at a coffee shop, but those were just quick blips. A proper hang-out? That was two, maybe three years ago. Last time they did something together was at a Michael Jackson concert at the Apollo Theater. It wasn’t perfect — Trip lost his car keys and spent a hilarious half hour waddling around like a lost duck after the show, only to discover them tucked into his waistband — but it was a night they all laughed about. A damn good show, too. Good times.

Ben had spent the last year crisscrossing around the country, working for Encyclopædia Britannica’s International Publishing board. Unfortuntely, this also meant that trips to see Trip and Grace had dwindled to postcards and the occasional text. He’d only settled back in town a few months ago, and when Trip invited him over tonight, it felt like a chance to stitch things back together. What could go wrong? Nothing, right?

“Here’s a fun one,” Trip said, leaning forward, elbows on his knees. “It was almost exactly ten years ago, tonight, that you introduced us. Senior year of college!”

“Oh, yeah! You two hit it off like fireworks… couldn’t keep you apart, I guess.” he chuckled.

Grace’s smile tightened, just for a second. “Aw, geez…” she murmured, but Trip steamrolled right over her.

“We really wanted to thank you for all these years of…” he trailed off, fishing for the right word.

“Pain?”

“Heh heh heh… agony.”

“Love,” she corrected him.

Ben frowned, thrown. “Agony?”

Trip’s face went pink, and he stumbled over himself. “Uh— I— I mean, uh, it’s, uh— it’s great you could be here tonight.”

The conversation limped along, but as the evening stretched, the cracks between Trip and Grace started to show more clearly. Ben felt like a referee at a match he hadn’t signed up for! Caught in the middle of the couple’s domestic drama, he could only offer his presence as a comfort. Just like last time. “This is a nice room, honestly,” he said, trying to divert the conversation to something less contentious.

It wasn’t a lie, to be fair; the place had some nice things: a three-seat couch that looked suspiciously like the one they “borrowed” from a friend of his during their senior year (with a suspicious red wine stain to match), and some framed travel posters and pictures that documented their previous vacations. Ben could remember one of them, the day they went to Perpignan, in southern France — those were the good days, apart from the fact they ended with Ben sorting out their lost passports and maxed-out credit cards.

Trip mumbled something about grabbing a drink and quickly shuffled off to the kitchen. Ben glanced out the window — city lights smearing through the glass, the air outside thick with that pre-rain smell he forgot the name of. Gonna rain soon, I guess.

Grace sighed, breaking the quiet. “I… you know, this—this is all just another miscommunication, I think…”

Ben nodded, unsure of what to say. He fidgeted a little, watching her gather herself.

After a beat, she called out her husband. “Um, Trip, if— if you have a second, could you— could you brush some truffle butter on the crostini? It’s all in the fridge… just be careful with the truffle butter this time…”

No response.

“Could you do that for me? You have to do it manually, I lost the remote.”

She tried again, raising her voice slightly.

Still no response. Moments later, Trip emerged, frustration etched on his face, not looking very happy.

“Nevermind,” he muttered, rubbing the back of his neck. “I can’t find the brie. Where did you hide it?”

She blinked. “Brie? We don’t have any brie.”

“What?! Grace, I specifically asked you to buy brie!”

Turning her head to dodge his stare, Grace let her fingers twist the hem of her sweater. “I’m sorry, okay? It slipped my mind.”

Ben sat there, perched on the edge of the couch, watching the whole exchange ping-pong back and forth. He was quite confused. They were in rare form tonight, apparently.

“Jesus, do you just not hear me when I talk to you? Just this morning I asked you to buy brie!”

Grace glared at him, then turned to their guest. “Look, I’m sorry I forgot the goddamn brie, alright?”

Ben tried to ease the tension with a light-hearted response. Maybe it’d work this time. “Hey, no worries. Brie would’ve been nice, but it’s really not a big deal,” he said, offering a reassuring smile.

But Trip was on edge. “Uhh, God! What the hell has been going on in here?” he exclaimed, looking from Ben to Grace and back again.

“Huh… should I just go back to the kitchen?” he half-joked.

“No, Trip, no, everything’s fine; our friend is just teasing…” Grace tried to smooth things over, hoping it had worked.

“You can stay, Trip,” Ben chimed in. “We don’t mind.”

“Alright…” he replied back, though his tone suggested anything but agreement.

Grace took a breath, and then shifted gears to something she’d be much more interested in talking about. “You know, ever since we got married, I’ve been obsessed with redecorating. I can’t help it, I’m forever buying new furniture and redecorating. It’s like I need to keep tweaking things,” she confessed.

“Yeah, it’s in your blood, you grew up with this stuff…”

“It sounds interesting,” Ben added, trying to show interest in their conversation.

Grace was about to respond when Trip cut in. “See, try to understand, even after a full day’s work designing magazine ads, Grace somehow finds the time to decorate and redecorate—”

And she interrupted him, her frustration bubbling to the surface. “I could never do this, but sometimes I want to start fresh and paint free-form all over the walls with a big, messy brush…” Her voice trailed off with a frustrated sigh.

Trip responded with an annoyed sigh of his own, and Ben, sensing the growing tension, asked, “Are you two okay?”

“Umm…”

Ben tossed him a quick wink, a little lifeline of reassurance.

“Oh, ha ha, I—” Trip started, but was interrupted by Grace’s pointed comment. “Oh, well… you seem to remember how much Trip likes getting attention.”

Trip’s frustration erupted. “Uhh, you’re driving me insane!” he shouted.

“Trip is a nice guy, really!” Ben, ever the mediator, interjected, trying to defuse the situation.

But his anger, however, was not so easily quelled. “Your… goddamn decorating…”

Grace huffed, turning into her guest. “Ben, can you believe this?”

“Oh, yeah, you…” Trip started. “I know what you’ll say… the way you keep trying to be so supportive! You and your goddamn brie…”

Trip’s voice was, unsurprisingly, laced with sarcasm. And Grace knew that. “Trip, all this stuff you do, you’re just trying to make us look like we’re some kind of perfect happy couple! Like we’re so happy!”

“Oh, Christ…!”

“Wait—are you two not happy?” Ben's brows knitted together and he finally asked a question that, honestly, he should have asked much earlier.

“Okay, you know what,” Trip said, turning to face their guest. “I’m going to ask you something.”

“Trip—”

“Ben… when you’re married, can you try to change the other person to be even ‘better’ than who yourself are?”

“No,” Ben answered simply. “Sometimes, but uh, usually, no.”

Grace’s reaction was immediate. “What…?! Oh, okay… fine…” Grace said, her tone resigned. “Don’t you see, Trip?! Our marriage… is a sham, Trip! Don’t you see that? It’s all fake, all pretend…”

Trip’s response was one of denial. “Yeah, go on, get out of my face! I can’t fucking take this! Shit!”

She didn’t say another word. Grace just stood, brushing past him, and disappeared into the kitchen. Trip stayed put however, his back to the room, staring at one of his portraits from his trip to Italy. Oh boy, what a ride!

“Umm… do you two want a drink or something?” Ben offered, trying to calm the situation.

“What are you saying? A drink? What?” Trip asked, his confusion evident.

Ben gestured them toward the little bar cart shoved in the corner.

“You’ve got… let’s see, some beer, vodka, gin—oh, is this rum? Neat. And uh… oh wait, chocolate milk?” he held up a carton he’d grabbed from the edge of the cart, giving it a curious shake. “Anything with mel-”

Trip blinked. “W-we-I don’t have chocolate milk.”

“No chocolate milk? Then what the hell have I been sucking on for the past ten seconds?”

“What?” Grace’s voice rang out from the kitchen, just as confused as Trip was moments earlier. “I can’t hear you — are you talking to me?”

“I was not talking to you!” Trip shouted back.

He looked back at Ben, getting worried. “Ben, this is not normal, this has never happened, I swear.”

Ben didn’t know what to do with that, so he fiddled with a bottle of red wine on the table — picking it up, setting it down, like he was testing its weight. Nice. Trip, meanwhile, lunged for a distraction. “Y-you know, I just got a hold of this rare imported Icelandic vermouth I want you to try!” He snatched a sleek bottle from the cart and thrust it toward his friend.

“Icelandic vermouth, huh?” Ben said, taking the bottle tentatively. “Never heard of it. Sounds…unique. I’m gonna stick with wine this night, though.”

He grabbed the red wine again, popping the cork with a quick twist and pouring himself a glass, the liquid glugging softly into it. As the faint scent of the wine hit the air, Ben hesitated, then asked, “So, uh… was today a good time for me to drop by, or…?”

Trip’s shoulders slumped, his hand dragging through his hair. “Look, if—if you want to go, just…”

“I mean, if you’d rather I—” Ben started, but Trip cut him off.

“No, no, I mean—Jesus Christ…” his voice cracked, frustration bubbling over as he ran a hand through his hair.

“What? If you’re talking to me, I can’t hear you!” Grace shouted back, having no idea what was going on anymore.

“I didn’t say anything!”

Ben, at a loss, picked up the wine bottle again — up, down, up, down — like some malfunctioning machine stuck on a loop. Hell yeah. Grace swept back into the room then, wiping her hands on a dish towel.

“Ben, from what you said before, about helping your spouse…” she trailed off, eyeing him expectantly. “I think that tells us something about Trip.”

He frowned. Did I say that? He genuinely couldn’t remember, but Trip jumped in before he could clarify.

“Oh, we’re talking about me again, are we?”

“Damn it, Trip, you and my parents are always planning these stupid trips I have to go on!”

“They’re vacations! Expensive ones at that!” he countered, his voice rising.

“Of course, nobody thinks to ask me what I want…”

“But what do you want, Grace?” Ben asked, his question simple, as many others in the night had been.

Grace paused, the question catching her off guard. “What?”

“Ah, you want to know more. Thank you, that helps her,” Trip said, his sarcasm evident. “Grace, you never want to go on vacation with me and your parents — so I have to plan everything!”

“Uhh…” Grace’s response was noncommittal, her mind elsewhere.

Turning the conversation back to his wife, Trip continued. “Grace, I don’t think you really do want us to quit our jobs, lead a simpler lifestyle.”

“What? Trip, you know what I—” Grace started to respond, but Trip cut her off.

“Come on! You grew up with a silver spoon in your mouth. You can’t live any other way.” His words landed hard, sharper than usual, and Ben shifted uncomfortably in his seat. Trip didn’t usually bite like that — at least, not in front of him…

…who, once again, picked up the red wine bottle, and put it down.

“Ben, come on, I don’t feel like fixing drinks at this moment!”

“Sorry.”

Grace, however, was insistent. “No, let’s—let’s focus on us, our relationship,” she urged, steering the conversation back to more pressing matters. “I think Trip’s afraid of visiting his parents. He does everything he can to avoid seeing them…”

“Afraid of my parents?! My parents are idiots,” he retorted.

“Is it, Trip?” Ben asked, looking for confirmation.

“Yes?” Trip responded, although his answer was more of a question. “I mean—you know— that’s ridiculous. Why would I—why would I be afraid of their stupid meatloafs and their tacky house and their crappy wine…?”

He laughed. “And look around here. We’re doing well, really well,” he said, gesturing to their surroundings.

“God, Trip, I can’t believe even now you’re still…”

“Oh yeah, you’d never admit that I’ve created a good life for us.”

Ben, trying to find some common ground, saw his gaze drifted to the bookcases lining the far wall. The shelves were crammed with an eclectic mix of books and trinkets, so nothing unusual there. There were well-worn paperbacks filled with the works of Rimbaud and Baudelaire (probably owned by Grace), and a cute collection of mini-sculptures (also probably owned by Grace). A handful of seashells sat next to a lumpy clay figure he couldn’t really recognize. Finally, he picked up a brass bull in one of the shelves. “This looks cool,” he said, hoping to (once again) defuse the tension. “Patriotic, too.”

He was ignored.

Trip let out a small sigh, his frustration growing. “Ben, I, I—I’m not—there’s—there’s nothing…” he said, his voice trailing off into a frustrated sigh.

Grace echoed his frustration with a sigh of her own.

“Grace, we don’t—we don’t have to do this… things are okay, we can just—”

“Trip, I can’t stand the pretending anymore… I can’t take it!”

“No, Grace, we can… I mean, come on, don’t make it out to be so bad… I mean why? Why is it that anytime I want to do something nice for you, you resist it?”

Ben, trying to stay tethered to the mess unfolding, wandered a few steps away and let his fingers brush over the answering machine on the side table. He pressed a button — beep — no messages. No way to duck out of this one, it seemed. He lingered there, half-listening, half-dreading.

 

He was fucked.

“Trip…”

“Like I always have to convince you?”

“You know Ben, I can’t say Grace never acts loving towards me… she’s all over me at our goddamn parties, trying to look good in front of the guests!”

“Do you think she loves you?” Ben asked, his question being short and straightforward.

For a moment, there was silence, until a lightbulb lit up in Trip’s head.

“Aha!”

“What…? Are you trying to say… I might not love Trip? Oh, god, I—is that… is that what you think is happening here?!”

“Well-”

“Oh God, no, to suggest that I don’t love Trip is… it’s going too far! Uhh… I don’t know what to do…” she said, turning to Ben for guidance.

“I mean, are you happy with your marriage?” he asked, his question cutting to the heart of the matter.

“See, Ben, I… uhh… I’ve been very lonely,” she admitted, her voice barely above a whisper.

Ben’s eyes darted to the wall, landing on a big, abstract painting splashed with jagged blues and reds. He pointed at, looking like a kid tugging at a sleeve to stop his parents from all the yelling. Maybe it’d work? “Hey, what about this?”

Grace followed his gesture, her expression softening for a second. “Uhh, this painting? Well… mmm… see, all I really wanted was something personal and, uh… beautiful and special…” She trailed off, her face hardening again. “But I just… mmm… no, I—I just get so depressed and angry when I look at it!”

“Jesus…! No, look, there’s nothing wrong with the stupid painting!” Trip argued.

“Well, it makes me feel like shit.”

“Uhh… you feel that for no reason!”

“The painting is nice,” Ben interjected. “It’s really, erm, vintage.”

“Jesus, Ben, you keep obsessing about Grace’s stuff, you know that?”

He sighed. It really was vintage — but apparently Trip wasn’t too fond of that.

“Grace, even though you’re now a creative director at work, the truth is… you’re not an artist,” Trip said, his words blunt and unkind. Not the first of its type, especially that night, however.

“What!?” Her voice spiked. She was truly incredulous.

“Is that true, Trip?” Ben asked, seeking confirmation.

“Yes?” Trip replied back.

“You can both go to hell,” Grace said, as she stormed towards the exit door. Ouch. “Look, do you want me to leave? I can’t take this. I’m going to leave,” she threatened, her patience at its end.

“You don’t need to leave,” Ben pleaded, with his calm and reassuring voice.

“No? No, don’t, don’t even talk to me… ugh…”

Trip’s patience had reached its limit. “Okay, I’ve had enough of this bullshit!” he said, his voice rising in anger. “Ben, I know what you’re hinting at, okay?!” He paced the room, his movements erratic as he spoke about their lifestyle, about the weird feeling that hanged over them like a dark cloud. It was odd.

“Do you two want the truth? Huh?” Trip challenged, his eyes locked onto Ben’s. “Ben, are you ready to hear it?”

“Yes, I think,” Ben replied, his voice steady.

“Grace, huh?” Trip turned to his wife.

“Yes!”

Trip took a deep breath. “See, Ben, I’ve been paying attention to what you’ve been saying tonight. You’ve really been pushing me tonight. It makes me wonder if you’re really my friend or not.”

“Trip!”

But he continued, undeterred. “Ben, I ask you this—yes or no… do you really think that praising us…. and yet suggesting that Grace doesn’t love me… that we’re supposed to now understand something about ourselves from all that? Is what you’ve said tonight supposed to add up somehow, to something?”

“Hmm. Yes,” he answered. “Yes, I do think that way.”

“Alright.” he nodded, as if Ben’s response had unlocked something within him. “Grace… uhh… see… I’m… ashamed of my parents.”

“What!? Your parents are great. I love seeing them.”

“I know, I know. But compared to yours… they’re so… ignorant,” Trip confessed, his voice low.

“But they’re real. I hate how my parents are pretending to be something—” Grace began, but Trip cut her off for the tenth time that night.

“I’ve tried hard to be like your family.”

“Trip… really? I don’t—”

“But I feel like a phony. I feel like I can never get rid of my past. It’s kind of like I don’t know who I am anymore,” he said, his voice breaking.

Grace stared at him, then found her own truth. “I’m going to give you the truth too, Trip. Through our whole marriage… I’ve let you dominate me.” she shook her head, stunned at her own words.

“Dominate you…? What? I don’t—”

“As a kid, my parents took care of everything, bought me whatever I wanted, made decisions for me… Trip—I’ve used you—and our apartment full of expensive crap—to hide. To avoid responsibility for myself. I can’t avoid growing up forever.”

“Jesus… I, uhh… this changes everything…”

“Yes, it does change everything,” Grace agreed. “I think it’s over.”

“Grace, w—what—”

Trip reached for her, his hand hovering awkwardly in the air.

“No! It’s over, Trip, don’t you see that?”

Ben, caught up in the weight of it, moved toward Grace, arms open for a hug. She sidestepped him, barely registering it. And Trip stood frozen, at a loss for words.

“Wha… I—I just told you—” he stammered.

“You just told me something about us you should have said a long time ago,” she choked out. “You’re really ashamed of your past… and you wish you had mine… and no, Ben, hold on… and about me… from the beginning, I’ve let you control me.”

“Grace, I—”

“No, Trip, this is about me, and it’s about us, but it’s not about you…”

“Not about me…? Hey, no, wait!” he protested, but Grace was already walking towards the door.

“Ben, I’m sorry,” she said over her shoulder. “Thank you, though. You really helped me tonight.”

“Thanks?”

“Grace!” Trip called out.

But it was too late. The door shut with a soft click, as she stepped out and closed it. Ben could hear the sound of the elevator being called just outside.

“Jesus Christ…” Trip whispered. The moment was finally sinking in.

Ben, feeling for his friend, offered a hug to Trip, who was now grappling with everything that had happened in the last 10-15 minutes.

“I should have told her that…” Trip said, regret coloring his voice.

“Wow,” Ben said, the only word that seemed to fit the gravity of the situation. “Just… wow.”

“I—I could have told her that…” Trip repeated, quieter now, lost in the echo.

He sighed, then straightened up, his face flushed with embarrassment. “I think… I think this evening is cut a little short, Ben. You, uh, you gotta head out. I—”

The shame hit him hard — Ben, of all people, witnessing this trainwreck. This was terrible; talk about a bad feeling. He couldn’t even look at him. “I’ll be fine. Really. Just… go.”

Ben’s face flickered with disappointment, but he got it — or at least thought he did. With a small nod and a pat on Trip’s shoulder, he slipped out, the door closing with a muted thud.

The silence in the apartment was deafening, for sure. Trip sank down onto the living room floor, defeated. He reached for the half-empty bottle of wine on the couch — he wasn’t sure whether he left it there or if it was Ben messing around —, tipping it back and taking a long, desperate swig. Some wine dribbled down his chin, staining his olive shirt, his favorite. A hollow laugh escaped his lips.

“I just wanted to be adored,” he mumbled to himself. It was ironic, pathetic, and maybe even hard to believe. Maybe he was a bit of a phony after all. Sleep wouldn’t come easy tonight.

As Grace navigated the streets, the reality of the situation sunk in. She still needed to get her clothes, belongings and God knows how many sculptures and paintings from the apartment. Maybe even today, depending on how her conversation with Trip would go. Briefly, a petty thought flickered across her mind — she could rent the vacant apartment directly above theirs! It would be the ultimate power move, but practicality settled in. If that wasn’t feasible, she had a friend who lived a few blocks away… what was her name again? She had a Mozenrathian-sounding name that she could never properly remember. Regardless, a part of her knew this was truly the end. Trip tripped on himself and Grace fell from grace, no puns intended.

Descending in the elevator, Ben grappled with a nagging sense of unease. Sure, his life wasn’t terrible — but damn. Was he so out of touch with Grace and Trip? Had he misead them? Had they changed? Had he changed? To be loved is to be changed, he thought. Maybe they changed each other so much to the point where they destroyed themselves, self-destructed. Maybe he was just the fallen domino, the last one to fall down in their slow-motion collapse; he just never noticed it. A day or two could pass and maybe that situation would linger for a while. He’d wake up tomorrow and the world would keep spinning, as if nothing unusual happened that day.

In a parallel universe, Ben would be dissecting something entirely different. U.S. economic collapse; the Japanese nuclear weapons program; president Wesley Clark; a Super-Iraq controlling half of the world’s oil reserves; the Hollywood heartthrob they all watched as teenagers tragically end their life; the next Smash Mouth album, whichever was more relevant. But one thing probably wouldn’t have changed: he’d have missed the feeling of being loved. And maybe, just maybe, that was something Trip and Grace had lost sight of too. All he could do was guess. Just like last time.

It was evening in America.
