Chapter Summary

Worried about his friend Jennie, Fidget searches for her whereabouts.

The faint buzz of a cheap alarm clock cut through the quiet of Fidget’s rather cramped bedroom. Its red digital numbers were glowing: 7:13 a.m. He sighed.

Rolling over on a mattress that had seen better days, he rubbed his eyes before sliding out of bed, both of feet hitting the cold floor with a wince. Ouch. He forgot how cold linoleum could be at this time of year. But at that point, it was too late to complain; he’d gotten used to it.

His room was illuminated by a pale blue light spilling through the blinds, which helped him notice his room was quite of a mess — some clothes thrown over a chair, a dozen or so empty soda cans on top of more than a dozen skate magazines, and what looked like a couple of crumpled cigarette packs scattered on the floor. I should probably get rid of that, he thought.

Quickly shuffling to the bathroom, almost stepping over a pair of Airwalks on the way, he turned the faucet until the pipes groaned and somewhat lukewarm water splashed against the sink. Splashing water on his face, he tried to piece together the night before. Where was he again? Oh yeah, the club. That was cool — the music, he thought. Some of his friends were there, albeit not many of them. One of them that he did recall was Jennie — what was she doing there again? Hmm. Fidget thought for a moment. She’d been looking out for Telly or whatever. He hadn’t seen her after that.

He could look into this.

Grabbing his toothbrush, he scrubbed at his teeth, although his mind was still foggy. The minty burn of the toothpaste jolted him slightly more awake. He could eat it if he really wanted to, but that probably wouldn’t be a good idea. After spitting, Fidget stripped down and stepped into the shower, letting the warm water run over him. It helped — if only a little. Every now and then he found out that taking a hot shower would fix all of his problems — well, besides all others that couldn’t be fixed. He stood there for a few extra moments, eyes closed, breathing deep. Then, after drying off, he pulled on a fresh long-sleeved shirt and jeans, feeling — marginally — more human. He liked it, too — it was actually a brand new “goddamn this noise inside my head” shirt he’d bought a week ago or two. This one looked great on him, too.

Back to… oh yeah, Jennie. He wondered if she went to Steven’s party. Maybe? It wouldn’t have been surprising — that kid had a fuckload of money and could get anyone in there if he wanted to.

“Gotta… uh, gotta check on her. Check-in. Check-in. Check-in.” he said.

Now in the kitchen, Fidget grabbed the cordless phone from the counter, dialing her pager number and waiting. He was pretty sure hers had voicemail, and so he left a short message asking if she was alright and to call him back as soon as possible. Five minutes later, nothing. Impatient, he would punch in Jennie’s number again and add a quick “143” (I love you) for good measure. Again, nothing. About twenty minutes later, he finally accepted that he wouldn’t get a response so soon.

Maybe she was still asleep. Maybe she didn’t want to talk? Either way, it left him with a nagging feeling. Fidget chewed his lip and bounced his knee more times than he could imagine.

Hmm. Maybe he could call Steven instead, right?

Unlike the others, Steven happened to have a cell phone of his own: a very early version of what was apparently called the Motorola StarTAC. He’d never heard of it, and it likely wouldn’t come out until next year, but he had seen it in person, and wow — it was so light it didn’t even look like a mobile phone. No way he’d be able to get this at launch. Besides… he wasn’t ready to abandon his Nokia 2110 so soon. When he turned 18, his father gave it to him as a birthday present. It was his first gift on many birthdays, too… and right now, it was the closest thing he had to him.

His stomach growled. He grabbed a bag of chips from the counter, tearing it open and eating a handful while leaning against the fridge. The chips were decent, he thought. He wasn’t exactly hungry (even if his body disagreed), but hey, at least eating gave his hands something to do.

Fidget’s parents weren’t around often, which was nothing new. His mom worked long hours at IBM, and always talked about how they were developing “the next big thing” in tech. (She had been saying this for about two years now.) His dad, on the other hand, worked construction upstate in Albany, coming home maybe once a month if he was lucky. But he didn’t mind the quiet, though. On the contrary; it meant he could blast his music and hang out with his much cooler friends without anyone yelling at him.

He soon finished off the handful, wiped his fingers on his jeans, and tossed the bag aside.

If Steven had a phone, well, he could definitely call him. He should, perhaps. Fidget dialed the number, listening to its ring until it kicked over to voicemail.

“Yo, it’s Steve. Uh… I’m probably not home right now… so leave a message or whatever.”

What a bummer.

“Um- hey Steve, it’s a- me, Fidget. Can-can you hit me back when you wake up? Tryna find Jennie. Later.”

Click. Well, nothing to do now but go there himself.

Before heading out, he glanced out the window and groaned — the sky was gray, and there was a steady drizzle tapping against the glass. It was raining! Shit. He’d need a coat.

He dug through his closet, eventually pulling out a mustard-yellow raincoat that almost certainly belonged to his dad. How did that even get here? Eh. He zipped it up, tucked his phone into his pocket, and scurried away.

Outside, the rain was light but kinda annoying. It was only a matter of time before things got worse, he assumed. The city was hardly quiet this early, so all he hoped was that he wouldn’t get splashed by a passing car.

He wondered if Jennie was alright.

It didn’t take long for Fidget’s sneakers to slap against the wet pavement of the Lower East Side, with the rain coming down in grayish sheets. He had to admit — his dad’s coat, probably a relic from the ’70s, hung off his bony frame like a deflated balloon. He didn’t look cool, he looked like shit. Plus his hood kept slipping, but he didn’t really care.

He carried with him a dark cocoa-colored Walkman — specifically the WM-EX511, which he found gorgeous —, which, honestly, he should’ve used more often. He called this bad boy “Robbie,” which might’ve been a good name for a boy, but admittedly was not a good name for a bad boy, nor a Walkman. But it would suffice. It wasn’t waterproof either, so at least the coat would help with that.

PJ Harvey’s voice snarled through his headphones. Hell yeah. He could never say no to another listen of his favorite song, Long Snake Moan, from his new favorite album. Fidget loved its sounds thrumming in his skull. It helped him relax, too. The cassette had actually been a gift from his mom’s IBM coworker for his birthday, some random middle-aged dude who thought he was “alternative” enough. Fidget hated the guy, but the tape? Fucking perfect.

The rain continued to pick up as Fidget approached the apartment building where Steven lived. It was right on the edge of Alphabet City, and while not quite a luxury building, it was definitely nicer than the walk-ups most of their friends crashed in. A bored-looking doorman barely glanced up from his newspaper as Fidget slipped past, ignoring the water dripping from his oversized yellow coat onto the faded lobby carpet. From the standpoint of water, fortunately, his coat was not the wettest.

He jabbed the elevator button and waited. Boooring. The elevator creaked its way down to the lobby, and finally inside, Fidget pressed 14, watching the numbers illuminate slowly as the cab ascended. The elevator smelled vaguely of cigarettes and something kinda floral, too. Hmm. Nice.

Steven’s apartment was at the end of a long hallway with identical doors on either side. Fidget first knocked twice to no response. He then tried the handle — to his surprise, it was unlocked. The door swung open to reveal a scene that looked like something from one of those college movies his mom wouldn’t let him watch. (All of which he did watch eventually, of course.)

Bodies. Everywhere. Not dead, thank God, but they were sprawled across couches, chairs, and the floor in various states of consciousness. It looked like a Guess Who? board, for crying out loud! The only good thing in that room was the unmistakable sweet-skunky smell of weed.

“Holy shit,” Fidget muttered, pulling his headphones down around his neck.

He recognized a few faces among the sleeping forms, too. There was… uh… Harold? That looked like him. Harold (or a lookalike) was passed out on the couch with his arm dangling to the floor, and he could’ve sworn he saw Casper curled up in a ball by the window. He didn’t know if he felt good for not coming or bad for not being invited; he just heard of it from passing conversation at the club. Either way, it looked like he’d missed quite the rage.

Fidget blinked, trying to take it all in. These were his friends — sort of. Though he was farcloser to Steven than most of the people here, it’s not like he was on his VIP list. To everyone else, he was probably just one of Steven’s extended friends, and it’s how he even got in most of their encounters and stuff. But not this one. Speaking of that guy…

“Steven?” he called out.

No response. As he walked through the house and entered the hallway, trying to search for Jennie in some of the rooms, he noticed one of the doors was wide open. Inside, here was Telly — whom Fidget unfortunately recognized —, sprawled naked across the sheets, tangled up with some girl who looked just as out of it. Typical. Fidget always thought the guy was weird. He couldn’t pinpoint exactly when these negative feelings toward him started, but they had been there for a while. The guy was just too obsessed with girls, even more than everyone else. It was weird. And of course, he really didn’t need to see the shit he had been doing; he quickly shut the door. Now he’d have to get this moment out of his brain somehow.

Ignoring them, he pressed on, weaving through room after room until he finally spotted Jennie.

She was curled up in an armchair, her knees drawn to her chest, staring at nothing in particular. One could think she was dead; her face was quite pale. She looked rough. Fidget’s chest tightened a little, and he hesitated before stepping closer.

“Jennie?” he asked quietly, crouching down beside her. She stirred, cracking one eye open with a groan. “You okay?”

It was sorta half-assed, honestly, because he didn’t know how else to say it. At first, Jennie just blinked, as if registering him for the first time. She didn’t say anything right away, just stared at him as if she were trying to recognize who that was. Then, finally, she let out a breath and nodded, albeit it was far from convincing.

Fidget ran a hand through his hair. He wanted to say something more, but what?

“I-I can give you a ride home if you want,” he offered.

The good news is that he had just enough money for his situation. Her home wasn’t too far away from here, if he remembered correctly — but given her state, it would be best if he just paid a cab for her. It would be safer than having him take her home instead. It’s not like he had two coats, too…

For a moment, she didn’t respond. Then, slowly, she nodded again. “Yeah… okay.”

He exhaled. Though he didn’t know what had happened to her last night — she just seemed too weird, for the lack of a better word —, whatever it was, she didn’t need to be here anymore.

Maybe he shouldn’t have given her that depressant.

Fidget supported Jennie as they stepped into the elevator, with his arm looped around her waist to keep her steady. That descent felt like it dragged on forever. The fact neither of them said a word just made it worse.

When the doors opened to the reception, Fidget guided her across the tiled floor to the desk. The receptionist barely glanced up from his book. “Letters from London,” or whatever it was called, but it was a pretty damn good cover.

“Sir, can we get some water?”

The man sighed, handed over a small paper cup, and went back to reading. Fidget filled it at the fountain nearby and pressed it into Jennie’s hands. Although she drank slowly, she seemed satisfied with it.

They settled onto a worn bench by the entrance. Knowing that she was in little condition of getting home safely, and he didn’t have a way of carrying her home by himself — especially with everyone else just watching! — Fidget opted to call a cab. The two of them remained quiet for quite a bit, until Fidget finally spoke.

“Uh, Jennie, about last night… I-I shouldn’t have given you that. I don’t know if-if it messed you up more or-”

“It’s fine,” she interrupted him.

Fidget nodded, though the guilt still gnawed at him. “What happened at the party? You don’t have to say if you don’t want to, of-”

“I don’t wanna talk about it,” she said quickly, looking away.

“…ah.”

After a minute or two of silence, she turned back to him. “But there’s something else.”

“Hm?”

“I’m HIV positive.”

Fidget froze. It felt like his stomach had dropped — metaphorically, of course. But it could’ve very well been real with what he had just heard. “W-What? How?”

“From Telly.”

Whoa. Well, I guess that made some sense. Wait, what was he thinking? This wasn’t right.

“But when did you find out?” Fidget’s mind was pretty close to reeling.

“Yesterday.”

He stared at her in complete silence for what looked like minutes. It was actually just 15 seconds, but he was in shock. Fidget had no clue they were even in a relationship of sorts. He had no idea they even had sex. Hell, he didn’t even know Telly was seropositive.

All he could manage to say was, “I’m sorry, Jennie.”

She met his gaze, surprisingly unshaken. “You shouldn’t be. It’s not your fault.”

Before he could say more, the cab rolled up outside and honked; conversation time was over. Fidget helped her to her feet and walked her to the door. As she slid into the backseat, she gave him a small wave. “Bye, Fidge.”

“Bye, take care,” he said, shutting the door. He stood there, watching the cab pull away until it vanished from sight.

Wow. Just… wow.

Fidget felt this deep, aching sadness in his heart that he couldn’t even properly describe. Jennie wasn’t even his closest friend, but she was someone he really liked and wanted to be alright with. Like — Jesus, what happened? How had everything gone so wrong so fast? He didn’t have answers. It’s not like he could’ve prevented this, but he still felt bad.

Maybe if he was just a bit more open to her about why he didn’t like Telly, maybe he could’ve avoided this. Or at least helped avoid this. Assuming she’d listen to him, naturally — which he thought she would. But Fidget wondered: did she see him the same way he saw her?

Am I even a good friend to her?

He kicked at a puddle, watching the water ripple out. Telly. That asshole. How long had he known? Did he even know? If he did, had he even cared? Probably not — guys like him never did. They just took what they wanted and left everyone else to deal with the fallout. And Jennie — God, Jennie — caught in the middle of it all. This sucked. He should go back up just to punch that fucker in the face. Yet it wouldn’t really make things better, even if he’d feel satisfied for doing it.

She said yesterday. Yesterday, when he was screwing around at the club, oblivious. Like… had she known then, while they were all laughing and drinking? Was she sitting there with that bomb in her head, not saying a word? It’s not like he could’ve guessed, but maybe he should’ve noticed something. And yet he hadn’t caught up in his own bullshit. Some friend he was.

Fidget tilted his head back, letting the rain hit his face. It was cold — duh — but he didn’t move. He exhaled again. Running against the run of play would not be easy for Jennie. She could die, even with all advancements in AIDS/HIV research over the last five, ten years. He’d hate to see a friend of his go out so, so soon. It happened once to a childhood friend of his around three years ago, and although the circumstances were very different, the pain he felt was the same.

He felt useless. Jennie probably felt something similar.

The least he could do was help her heal the pain.
