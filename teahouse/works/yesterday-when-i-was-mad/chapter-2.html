Chapter Summary

Fidget pays Telly a visit.

Chapter Notes

Per popular demand, a second chapter! (In retrospect, I should've written it from the start.)

The day dragged on very much like a funeral procession — his, probably. Fidget had a hard time focusing on anything. His mind kept circling back to Jennie’s revelation, each loop tightening around his chest until he could barely breathe.

Hearing “I’m HIV positive” from a friend, any friend, let alone someone you knew, just wasn’t easy to grapple with. Although he wasn’t even fully sure at this point — whether from his internal paranoia or the severity of the situation — if they were friends or just close acquaintances, those words never fully dissipated from his brain. Fidget was in despair.

He’d paced so much that his downstairs neighbor had banged on their ceiling with a broom handle. Twice, in fact. (Mrs. Lawler was not the nicest neighbor, admittedly.) By late afternoon, he’d chewed his thumbnail until it bled, and the rain outside had turned from a drizzle to a proper downpour. It suited his mood perfectly, one could say. But he felt much worse than the rain ever did.

What was he supposed to do? It wasn’t his news to share, sure. But Telly... that fucker was out there, probably still at Steven’s place, maybe even with another girl by now. The thought made Fidget’s stomach turn.

“Jesus Christ,” he muttered, running his hands through his hair for what felt like the hundredth time that day. “W-what about that girl?”

He almost forgot; Telly was sleeping with a girl earlier this morning at Steven’s. Who was she? Were they friends? Did they-

Ugh.

Fidget grabbed the cordless phone again, dialing some random number that he thought belonged to Harold. No answer. After some thinking, he tried Casper; he’d probably be home right now, he assumed. Again, nothing. He went back to Steven’s. Straight to voicemail. His fingers hovered over the keypad, tempted to call Jennie again, but again, what would he even say?

Pestering her would be the worst thing he could do right now, too.

By evening, the rain had stopped, but the city glistened under streetlights like it was coated in glass. Fidget had made up his mind. He couldn’t just sit here.

Telly’s place was a two-floor walk-up in a building that smelled of boiled cabbage and weed. (And honestly, other stuff too.) Fidget had only been there once before, crammed onto a floor with a dozen others after some party in Central Park. Telly lived with his mother and a cute baby he could not recall the name of. He never heard of his father, but judging by the tight quarters and Telly’s general attitude, Fidget assumed that dude was long gone.

He knocked about seven times, being met with nothing but silence. Was this kid even home? Given that in all of his seven knocks, he’d knocked on the door also seven times, this meant he’d done 49 knocks already. Not a world record, but it was enough to annoy him.

Fidget knocked again, much harder this time, ignoring the pain in his knuckles.

“Yo, who is it?”

Finally.

“It’s Fidget.”

A pause, then footsteps. The door swung open to reveal Telly in basketball shorts and no shirt, looking like he’d just woken up despite it being 8:30 p.m.

“Fidget? The fuck you want?” He didn’t sound angry, just confused, maybe a little annoyed. This was fair — they weren’t the closest of friends, and there would be no real reason for him to show up for a visit.

“Can I come in?”

Telly shrugged and stepped aside. “Whatever.”

The apartment was surprisingly clean compared to Steven’s post-party disaster, that was for sure. It looked packed, yes, but it didn’t seem bad. Just maybe too crowded. The walls were lined up with several pictures of young Telly and also family pictures in general. There was one slightly bigger picture of a young Telly holding an anteater plushie. It looked cute.

There were also bookshelves cramming with books that he was pretty sure Telly never read. If he did, maybe they wouldn’t be in this situation right now.

“Didn’t know you knew where I lived,” Telly said, flopping back onto the futon and reaching for a half-smoked joint in the ashtray.

Fidget remained standing, his hands stuffed into the pockets of his jeans. “Oh, uh, Harold told me once.”

“So what’s up? You looking for weed or something? Cause I’m almost out.”

His heart was pounding so hard he was sure Telly could hear it. He hadn’t planned what to say, and definitely hadn’t rehearsed this confrontation on the walk over. Now that he was here, the words wouldn’t come.

“I.. uh… well… I saw Jennie today,” he finally managed.

Something flickered across Telly’s face. Whether it was interest, concern, or something else, he had no idea; it was gone before Fidget could identify it.

“Yeah? How is she?”

“Not good.”

Telly took a drag, exhaling slowly. “What’s that got to do with me?”

The casual dismissal in his tone made something snap in Fidget’s chest. “She’s HIV positive,” he blurted out, louder than he’d intended. “She got tested yesterday, see.”

Telly’s hand froze halfway to his mouth, leaving the joint burning untouched between his fingers. For a long moment, he just stared at Fidget, who had to admit he was a bit spooked.

“That’s not funny, man,” he finally said, but his voice had lost its edge.

“I’m not joking.”

“Well, she’s lying. She probably got it from someone else.”

Fidget took a step forward. “Well, she said she got it from you.”

The joint fell from Telly’s fingers onto the futon. He didn’t seem to notice. “T-that’s bullshit. I don’t have… I’m not…” He stood up suddenly, pacing to the window and then back. “She’s fucking lying.”

“Why would she lie about that?”

“I don’t know! To fuck with me? Girls do that shit.” At this point, Telly’s voice had risen an octave, and there was something rather wild in his eyes now.

“You should get tested,” Fidget said, trying to keep his voice steady. He didn’t think he was succeeding.

Telly just laughed.

“I’m not getting tested because some bitch is spreading rumors about me.”

The slur against Jennie made Fidget’s fingers curl into fists, but he forced himself to stay calm. There was no way he would leave this fight unharmed; it was more likely he’d fall from two rows of staircases than that. He tried changing the topic. “Telly... how many girls have you been with since Jennie?”

He stopped pacing.

“That’s none of your business.”

“If you have it—”

“I DON’T HAVE IT!” Telly shouted, and slammed his palm against the wall. The sudden violence made Fidget flinch. Ouch.

“Y-you don’t know that.”

“I know I’m not sick! Do I look sick to you?” Telly spread his arms wide, as if his physical appearance was proof enough.

“That’s not how it works, dude. You can have it for years before symptoms show up.”

“Oh, who are you now, a fucking doctor?”

“I’m just saying—”

“Get out.” Telly pointed to the door, though his arm was trembling slightly. “Get the fuck out of my house.”

Fidget didn’t move, however. “There’s a free clinic on St. Mark’s. Anonymous testing, see. You could know in—”

“I SAID GET OUT!” Telly lunged forward, shoving Fidget hard enough to make him stumble back. That hurt a bit.

For a moment, Fidget thought about shoving back, about letting all the anger and frustration of the day explode through his fists. But he knew that wouldn’t work. Instead, he straightened up and looked Telly in the eye.

“If you don’t get tested, I’m telling everyone. A-all your friends. And every girl at the next party. Everyone.”

It was a courageous act, for sure. It’s not that he wanted to do it, but if he had no choice, he would. It was the right thing, right?

Meanwhile, Telly’s face immediately drained of color. “You wouldn’t.”

“Try me.”

They stared at each other in rather tense silence. Fidget almost thought he would shit himself right there.

Finally, Telly stepped back, running a hand over his face. “Just… get out. Please.” His voice had lost all its fire.

Although Fidget hesitated at first, he nodded once and turned to leave. At the door, he paused. “The clinic opens at nine tomorrow. I’ll be there.”

Telly didn’t respond, just stood there, looking suddenly very young and very lost.

Outside, the night air felt electric against Fidget’s skin. He hadn’t realized how hot it had been in that tiny apartment, or how hard he’d been breathing.

He didn’t know if Telly would even go to the clinic tomorrow. His bet was on “no,” but this was a serious situation. He didn’t know if his threat would work, or if he’d even have the guts to follow through on it. In fact, he didn’t know what any of this meant for Jennie, or for all the other girls they knew.

But Fidget just hoped that maybe, just maybe, his newfound courage had saved the day. He’d done something. For once in his life, he hadn’t just watched from the sidelines.

It wasn’t much. But it was better than nothing.

And tomorrow… well, tomorrow would have to take care of itself.

Fidget hadn’t slept. Not really. Well, maybe an hour or two of fitful dozing before his alarm blared at 8:15 a.m. His eyes felt like they were full of sand, and his mouth tasted like something had crawled in and died there overnight. (He made sure to brush his teeth to get rid of that, which was successful.)

The St. Mark’s clinic was nothing special from the outside — just another storefront between a deli and a used bookstore, with a small laminated sign reading “Community Health Services.” As Fidget approached, he checked his watch: it was 8:57 a.m. He wasn’t even sure why he’d come. He’d had sex exactly twice in the past year, both times with condoms. (With whom was not important.) The rational part of his brain knew his chances of being positive were astronomically low.

But rationality had taken a backseat over the last twenty-four hours. And Telly might’ve needed some emotional support anyway.

As he neared the entrance, he stopped short. There, huddled against the wall, hood pulled up despite the morning being clear and mild, was Telly. He was smoking a cigarette with trembling hands, not looking up as people passed.

Fidget hesitated. He’d half-expected Telly not to show. Hell, after their confrontation last night, he’d been nearly certain of it. The fact that he was here was enough of a surprise, though a pleasant one at it.

“Hey,” Fidget said, approaching cautiously.

Telly looked up, and Fidget did notice how different he seemed from the cocky kid at Steven’s party the other. Not that he’d be here with the same level of energy he had there, of course; it was morning! But it didn’t look like he’d slept much either.

“Didn’t think you’d actually come,” Telly muttered, dropping his cigarette and grinding it under his heel.

“Yeah, well. I mean… I figured I should get checked too. Just in case.”

Telly gave him a look that said he wasn’t buying it — he definitely wasn’t — but didn’t have the energy to call him out. Instead, he just nodded toward the door. “They open?”

“Should be. It’s nine.”

Inside, the clinic was brighter than expected — white walls adorned with health posters, plastic chairs arranged in rows, a small TV in the corner playing what looked like a morning talk show with the volume down low. Only one other person was there, an older man engrossed in a newspaper.

The receptionist, a woman with kind eyes and gray-streaked hair, looked up as they entered. “Good morning. How can I help you?”

Fidget glanced at Telly, who had been looking at some of the other posters. “Uh, we’re here for HIV testing,” Fidget said, keeping his voice low.

If the woman found anything unusual about two teenagers getting tested first thing on a Wednesday morning, she didn’t show it, thankfully. She just handed them each a clipboard with forms attached. “Fill these out. We do anonymous testing if you prefer — just check the box on the top right. The fee is sliding scale, pay what you can. We’ll call you when it’s your turn.”

They sat in chairs at opposite ends of the small waiting room, neither of them speaking. There wasn’t really much they could say, honestly; though there were many things they could chat about, they weren’t in the best place to chat. Fidget filled out his form quickly, checking the “anonymous” box without hesitation. When he looked up, Telly was still staring at his blank form, pen hovering over the paper. Poor guy.

Twenty agonizing minutes later, a nurse called, “Number 17?”

Fidget looked down at his ticket. That was him. As he stood, he caught Telly’s eye. The other boy gave him a tense nod.

The actual test was surprisingly quick. A clean, sharp prick of his finger, a few drops of blood on something that looked a lot like a pregnancy test, and then waiting. The nurse, a guy probably not much older than them, made small talk about the weather and a movie that would come out later that fall — Dead Presidents or whatever was its name. Fidget couldn’t focus on any of it.

When the nurse announced, “Negative,” Fidget felt a relief so profound that his knees actually weakened. He hadn’t realized just how scared he’d been until that moment. Even though he was sure he never had the virus, he wasn’t certain — though now he was.

“Remember, if you’ve been exposed within the last three months, you should get tested again in another three, just to be sure,” the nurse added, handing him a pamphlet. “And here’s some information about safe sex practices.”

Fidget nodded numbly, taking the pamphlet without really looking at it. He could read these when he got home anyway.

Back in the waiting room, Telly was being led through a different door by another staff member. They locked eyes briefly as they passed; Fidget gave him a quick smirk, although he didn’t know if he caught it.

Now that he was done, Fidget considered leaving. He’d done what he came to do — gotten tested, made sure Telly had shown up. He could walk out now, go home, try to put this whole nightmare behind him, and let things be. Whatever happened next wasn’t his business, right? Yet the fact he had gone so far just to do something for a kid that, maybe, would’ve never gotten tested otherwise — or at least not so soon — kept him anchored to the plastic chair, flipping through months-old magazines without registering a single word.

Forty-five minutes crawled by before Telly emerged. His face gave away nothing, but his eyes — they looked a bit different. He walked straight past him and out the door without a word.

Fidget quickly followed him outside, where Telly had already lit another cigarette and was taking deep, desperate drags.

“Telly—”

“Not here,” he cut him off, already walking away from the clinic with quick, jerky steps.

They walked in silence for several blocks until they reached Tompkins Square Park. It was still early enough that the park was relatively empty — just a few dog walkers and the occasional jogger here and there. Telly found a bench partially hidden by overgrown bushes and sat down.

Fidget sat beside him, leaving space between them; he wasn’t really sure what to say. And so he waited, watching a squirrel dart up a nearby tree. Oh hey, I found Alvin. Heh. Squirrels.

“It was positive,” Telly finally said, his voice flat. “In case you were wondering.”

Oh.

Even though Fidget had suspected as much, hearing it confirmed made his stomach twist. It was real. He almost couldn’t believe it. “Did… did you know? Before?”

Telly’s head snapped up. “No! I didn’t fucking know. You think I’d—” He broke off, running a hand over his face. When he spoke again, his voice was a tad softer. “I didn’t know.”

“I’m sorry, man.”

“Yeah. Me too.”

His voice cracked on the last word.

They sat in silence for another long moment. Unlike with Jennie, Fidget somehow found it harder to engage with Telly; the courage he had shown yesterday just wasn’t really there. If it was bad enough for her, it’d be just as bad for him. He gave her the virus without even knowing it.

“What happens now?” Fidget asked quietly.

Telly gave him a rather humorless laugh. “Fuck if I know. T-they gave me all these… papers, referrals, medications I can’t afford and shit.” He pulled a crumpled bundle of pamphlets from his pocket and stared at them like they were written in a language he couldn’t read. “I’m supposed to see some doctor there next week.”

“T-That’s good, right? There’s stuff they can do now. It’s not like… uh…”

“Not like a death sentence?” Telly finished what Fidget meant to say. “That’s what they kept saying. ‘With proper treatment, you can live a full life.’ Whatever that means.”

Another silence. A young mother pushed a stroller past them, her baby babbling happily. If she knew what they were talking about, she would’ve strolled faster.

“What about the girls?” Fidget asked a question that had been weighing on him since yesterday. Jennie was certainly not the only one he’d had relations with, considering that other girl at Steven’s was sleeping with him. Not that he was sure of anything the two had, but it seemed likely. There could’ve been many others too; Telly was always a bit of a womanizer.

But Telly’s face crumpled. For a moment, Fidget thought he might punch something, or him. Instead, a choked sound escaped his throat, and to Fidget’s shock, Telly began to cry. It was not an outright sob, but there were clear tears that carved tracks down his cheeks.

“Oh god. Oh fuck. I didn’t… I didn’t know.” He buried his face in his hands, his shoulders shaking like crazy. “How many? How many did I…?”

Instinctively, Fidget reached out, awkwardly patting Telly’s back. When Telly didn’t pull away, he let his hand rest there. He wasn’t sure what else to do.

“We’ll figure it out,” Fidget said, though he had no idea how. “W-we’ll find them, tell them to get tested.”

Telly looked up, his face streaked with tears. “Why are you even helping me? You barely know me. You don’t even like me.”

It was a fair question, one Fidget wasn’t sure he had an answer to. Fidget did know just the minimum details about him, and given how they had a similar friend group, they were closer than he’d think. But they weren’t really “friends,” and Fidget didn’t really like him either. But how could he just abandon him after all of this?

“I don’t know. I guess… we’re all kinda in this together, right? You, me, Jennie… our friends, see.” He shrugged. “What happens to one of us happens to all of us, in a way.”

Telly wiped his face with his sleeve, looking suddenly much younger than he was. “I fucked up. I fucked up so bad.”

“Yeah,” Fidget agreed, because there was no point denying it. “But you didn’t know.”

Impulsively, he wrapped an arm around Telly’s shoulders in an awkward half-hug. Telly tensed for a moment, then leaned in, accepting the gesture. It was nice. They stayed like that for a minute or two. It was really nice.

When they finally pulled apart, Telly looked a bit drained, though steadier. “I should go. I need to… think. I gotta figure out what to do next.”

Fidget nodded. “Call me if you need anything, alright?”

“Yeah. Thanks.” Telly stood, hesitated, then added, “I-I honestly don’t know if I would’ve, uh, come here today… I don’t know what I would’ve done.”

As Fidget watched Telly walk away, he couldn’t shake the feeling that something fundamental had shifted. Their little crew — not quite friends, though more than acquaintances — was fracturing in ways he couldn’t fully grasp yet.

Jennie had it. Telly had it. And all the nameless, faceless girls who might be carrying the virus without knowing it — they were part of this too, now. This was getting real serious. It was weird to think that, had he not come out after Jennie that day, maybe he would’ve never heard of it. Telly likewise wouldn’t have known, and everyone would go on like normal. As if nothing happened — when it still did.

Fidget didn’t bring his Walkman this time. For once, he wanted to hear the city around him — car horns and distant sirens, pigeons cooing, the rustle of leaves… normal sounds, maybe. It could distract him a bit from everything going on.

He thought about calling Jennie and telling her about what would happen. But what good would it do? It wouldn’t change her diagnosis. It wouldn’t turn back time either — but maybe she could’ve been aware of it either way.

Tired, Fidget began the long walk home. Shit was insane. Their group was crumbling, yes, but maybe something else could rise from the debris?

It wasn’t much of a silver lining. But for now, it was all he had.

And somehow, it would have to be enough.
